**Anatolia** ([Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_language "Turkish language"): *Anadolu*), also known as **Asia Minor**,[^1] is a [peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peninsula "Peninsula") in [West Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Asia "West Asia") that makes up the majority of the land area of [Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey"). It is the westernmost protrusion of [Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia "Asia") and is geographically bounded by the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") to the south, the [Aegean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_Sea "Aegean Sea") to the west, the [Bosporus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosporus "Bosporus"), the [Propontis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propontis "Propontis") and [Hellespont](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellespont "Hellespont") to the northwest, and the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea") to the north. The eastern and southeastern limits have been expanded either to the entirety of Asiatic Turkey [^3] or to an imprecise line from the Black Sea to the [Gulf of Alexandretta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Alexandretta "Gulf of Alexandretta").[^2] Topographically, the [Sea of Marmara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_of_Marmara "Sea of Marmara") connects the Black Sea with the Aegean Sea through the Bosporus and the [Dardanelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dardanelles "Dardanelles"), and separates Anatolia from [Thrace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrace "Thrace") in [Southeast Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Europe "Southeast Europe").

During the [Neolithic period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic_period "Neolithic period"), Anatolia was an early center for the [development of farming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Development_of_farming "Development of farming") after it originated in the adjacent [Fertile Crescent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertile_Crescent "Fertile Crescent"). Beginning around 9,000 years ago, there was a major migration of [Anatolian neolithic farmers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolian_Neolithic_Farmers "Anatolian Neolithic Farmers") into [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic_Europe "Neolithic Europe"), with their descendants coming to dominate the continent as far west as the [Iberian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula") and the [British Isles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Isles "British Isles").

The [earliest recorded inhabitants of Anatolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ancient_peoples_of_Anatolia "List of ancient peoples of Anatolia"), such as the [Hattians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hattians "Hattians"), who were neither [Indo-European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-European_languages "Indo-European languages") nor [Semitic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semitic_languages "Semitic languages"), were gradually absorbed by the incoming Indo-European [Anatolian peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolian_peoples "Anatolian peoples"), who spoke the now-extinct [Anatolian languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolian_languages "Anatolian languages"). The major Anatolian languages included [Hittite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hittite_language "Hittite language"), [Luwian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luwian_language "Luwian language"), and [Lydian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lydian_language "Lydian language"); other local languages, albeit poorly attested, included [Phrygian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrygian_language "Phrygian language") and [Mysian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mysian_language "Mysian language"). The [Hurro-Urartian languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurro-Urartian_languages "Hurro-Urartian languages") were spoken throughout [Mitanni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitanni "Mitanni") in the southeast, while [Galatian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galatian_language "Galatian language"), a [Celtic language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celtic_languages "Celtic languages"), was spoken throughout [Galatia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galatia "Galatia") in the central peninsula. Among the other peoples who established a significant presence in [ancient Anatolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prehistory_of_Anatolia "Prehistory of Anatolia") were the [Galatians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galatians_\(people\) "Galatians (people)"), the [Hurrians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurrians "Hurrians"), the [Assyrians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assyrian_people "Assyrian people"), the [Armenians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenians "Armenians"), the Hattians, and the [Cimmerians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cimmerians "Cimmerians"), as well as some of the [ancient Greek tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ancient_Greek_tribes "List of ancient Greek tribes"), including the [Ionians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionians "Ionians"), the [Dorians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dorians "Dorians"), and the [Aeolians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aeolic_Greek "Aeolic Greek"). In the era of [classical antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_antiquity "Classical antiquity") (see [Classical Anatolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Anatolia "Classical Anatolia")), the Anatolian languages were largely replaced by the [Greek language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language"), which came to dominate a large region during the [Hellenistic period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_period "Hellenistic period") and the [Roman period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire").

The [Byzantine period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire") saw the height and eventual decline of Greek influence throughout the peninsula as the [Byzantine–Seljuk wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine%E2%80%93Seljuk_wars "Byzantine–Seljuk wars") enabled the incoming [Seljuk Turks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seljuk_dynasty "Seljuk dynasty") to establish a foothold in the region. Thus, the process of Anatolia's [Turkification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkification "Turkification") began under the [Seljuk Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seljuk_Empire "Seljuk Empire") in the late 11th century and continued under the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") until the early 20th century, when the [Ottoman dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_dynasty "Ottoman dynasty") collapsed in the [aftermath of World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aftermath_of_World_War_I#Ottoman_Empire "Aftermath of World War I"). Between 1894 and 1924, millions of non-Turkic peoples and [Christians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_in_the_Ottoman_Empire "Christianity in the Ottoman Empire"), especially [Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greeks "Greeks") (about two million) and [Armenians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenian_Genocide "Armenian Genocide") (est. 1.5 million),[^8] were [killed by the Ottoman Turkish authorities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Ottoman_genocides "Late Ottoman genocides") or expelled from the bulk of the area of modern-day Turkey. Nonetheless, a variety of non-Turkic languages continue to be spoken by ethnic minorities in Anatolia today, including [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic"), [Kurdish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurdish_language "Kurdish language"), [Neo-Aramaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assyrian_Neo-Aramaic "Assyrian Neo-Aramaic"), [Armenian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenian_language "Armenian language"), the [North Caucasian languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Caucasian_languages "North Caucasian languages"), [Laz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laz_language "Laz language"), [Georgian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgian_language "Georgian language"), and [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language").

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Weichsel-W%C3%BCrm-Glaciation.png/250px-Weichsel-W%C3%BCrm-Glaciation.png)

Europe during the Last Glacial Maximum, c. 20,000 years ago. Anatolia was connected to the European mainland until c. 5,600 BCE, 9 10 11 when the melting ice sheets caused the sea level in the Mediterranean to rise around 120 m (390 ft), triggering the formation of the Turkish Straits. As a result, two former lakes (the Sea of Marmara and the Black Sea ) were connected to the Mediterranean Sea, which separated Anatolia from Europe.

Traditionally, Anatolia is considered to extend in the east to an indefinite line running from the [Gulf of Alexandretta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Alexandretta "Gulf of Alexandretta") to the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea"),[^12] coterminous with the [Anatolian Plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolian_Plateau "Anatolian Plateau"). This traditional geographical definition is used, for example, in the latest edition of *[Merriam-Webster's Geographical Dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merriam-Webster%27s_Geographical_Dictionary "Merriam-Webster's Geographical Dictionary")*.[^2] Under this definition, Anatolia is bounded to the east by the [Armenian Highlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenian_Highlands "Armenian Highlands"), and the [Euphrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euphrates "Euphrates") before that river bends to the southeast to enter [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia").[^13] To the southeast, it is bounded by the ranges that separate it from the [Orontes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orontes_River "Orontes River") valley in [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria_\(region\) "Syria (region)") and the Mesopotamian plain.[^13]

Following the [Armenian genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenian_genocide "Armenian genocide"), [Western Armenia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Armenia "Western Armenia") [was renamed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Anatolia_Region#Etymology "Eastern Anatolia Region") the [Eastern Anatolia region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Anatolia_region "Eastern Anatolia region") by the newly established Turkish government.[^14] [^15] In 1941, with the [First Geography Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Geography_Congress,_Turkey "First Geography Congress, Turkey") which divided Turkey into [seven geographical regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographical_regions_of_Turkey "Geographical regions of Turkey") based on differences in climate and landscape, the eastern [provinces of Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provinces_of_Turkey "Provinces of Turkey") were placed into the Eastern Anatolia region,[^16] which largely corresponds to the historical region of Western Armenia. Vazken Davidian terms the expanded use of "Anatolia" to apply to territory in eastern Turkey that was formerly referred to as *Armenia* (which had a sizeable [Armenian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenians_in_the_Ottoman_Empire "Armenians in the Ottoman Empire") population before the Armenian genocide) an "ahistorical imposition" and notes that a growing body of literature is uncomfortable with referring to the Ottoman East as "Eastern Anatolia".[^17] [^14] [^15]

The highest mountain in the Eastern Anatolia region (also the highest peak in the [Armenian Highlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenian_Highlands "Armenian Highlands")) is [Mount Ararat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Ararat "Mount Ararat") (5123 m).[^18] The [Euphrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euphrates "Euphrates"), [Aras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aras_\(river\) "Aras (river)"), [Karasu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karasu_\(Euphrates\) "Karasu (Euphrates)") and [Murat rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murat_river "Murat river") connect the Armenian Highlands to the [South Caucasus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Caucasus "South Caucasus") and the [Upper Euphrates Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_Euphrates_Valley "Upper Euphrates Valley"). Along with the [Çoruh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%87oruh "Çoruh"), these rivers are the longest in the Eastern Anatolia region.[^19]

## Etymology

The English-language name *Anatolia* derives from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") Ἀνατολή (*Anatolḗ*) meaning "the East" and designating (from a Greek point of view) eastern regions in general. The Greek word refers to the direction where the sun rises, coming from ἀνατέλλω *anatello* '(Ι) rise up', comparable to terms in other languages such as " [levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant") " from Latin *levo* 'to rise', " [orient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orient "Orient") " from Latin *orior* 'to arise, to originate', [Hebrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_language "Hebrew language") מִזְרָח *mizraḥ* 'east' from זָרַח *zaraḥ* 'to rise, to shine', [Aramaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aramaic "Aramaic") מִדְנָח *midnaḥ* from דְּנַח *denaḥ* 'to rise, to shine'.[^20] [^21]

The use of Anatolian designations has varied over time, perhaps originally referring to the [Aeolian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aeolians "Aeolians"), [Ionian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionians "Ionians") and [Dorian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dorians "Dorians") colonies situated along the eastern coasts of the [Aegean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_Sea "Aegean Sea"), but also encompassing eastern regions in general. Such use of Anatolian designations was employed during the reign of Roman Emperor [Diocletian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diocletian "Diocletian") (r. 284–305), who created the [Diocese of the East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diocese_of_the_East "Diocese of the East"), known in Greek as the Eastern Diocese, but completely unrelated to the regions of Asia Minor. In their widest territorial scope, Anatolian designations were employed during the reign of Roman Emperor [Constantine I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantine_I "Constantine I") (306–337), who created the [Praetorian prefecture of the East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture_of_the_East "Praetorian prefecture of the East"), known in Greek as the Eastern Prefecture, encompassing all eastern regions of the [Late Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Roman_Empire "Late Roman Empire") and spanning from [Thrace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diocese_of_Thrace "Diocese of Thrace") to [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diocese_of_Egypt "Diocese of Egypt").

Only after the loss of other eastern regions during the 7th century and the reduction of [Byzantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire") eastern domains to Asia Minor, that region became the only remaining part of the *Byzantine East*, and thus commonly referred to (in Greek) as the Eastern part of the Empire. At the same time, the [Anatolic Theme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolic_Theme "Anatolic Theme") (Ἀνατολικὸν θέμα / "the Eastern theme") was created, as a province (*[theme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theme_\(Byzantine_district\) "Theme (Byzantine district)")*) covering the western and central parts of Turkey's present-day [Central Anatolia Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Anatolia_Region "Central Anatolia Region"), centered around [Iconium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iconium "Iconium"), but ruled from the city of [Amorium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amorium "Amorium").[^22] [^23]

The Latinised form " Anatolia ", with its *\-ia* ending, is probably a [Medieval Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_Latin "Medieval Latin") innovation.[^21] The modern Turkish form *Anadolu* derives directly from the Greek name Ἀνατολή (*Anatolḗ*). The Russian male name [Anatoly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatoly "Anatoly"), the French [Anatole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatole_\(given_name\) "Anatole (given name)") and plain [Anatol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatol "Anatol"), all stemming from saints [Anatolius of Laodicea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolius_of_Laodicea "Anatolius of Laodicea") (d. 283) and [Anatolius of Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolius_of_Constantinople "Anatolius of Constantinople") (d. 458; the first [Patriarch of Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriarch_of_Constantinople "Patriarch of Constantinople")), share the same linguistic origin.

## Names

The oldest known name for any region within Anatolia is related to its central area, known as the "Land of [Hatti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hatti_\(land_of_the_Hattians\) "Hatti (land of the Hattians)") " – a designation that was initially used for the land of ancient [Hattians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hattians "Hattians"), but later became the most common name for the entire territory under the rule of ancient [Hittites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hittites "Hittites").[^24]

The first recorded name the Greeks used for the Anatolian peninsula, though not particularly popular at the time, was [Ἀσία](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%88%CF%83%CE%AF%CE%B1 "wikt:Ἀσία") (*Asía*),[^25] perhaps from an Akkadian expression for the "sunrise" or possibly echoing the name of the [Assuwa league](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assuwa_league "Assuwa league") in western Anatolia. The Romans used it as the name of their [province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia_\(Roman_province\) "Asia (Roman province)"), comprising the west of the peninsula plus the nearby [Aegean Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_Islands "Aegean Islands"). As the name "Asia" broadened its scope to apply to the vaster region east of the Mediterranean, some Greeks in [Late Antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Antiquity "Late Antiquity") came to use the name Asia Minor (Μικρὰ Ἀσία, *Mikrà Asía*), meaning "Lesser Asia" to refer to present-day Anatolia, whereas the administration of the Empire preferred the description Ἀνατολή (*Anatolḗ*; lit. 'the East').

The [endonym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endonym "Endonym") Ῥωμανία (*Rōmanía* "the land of the Romans, i.e. the Eastern Roman Empire") was understood as another name for the province by the invading [Seljuq Turks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seljuq_Turks "Seljuq Turks"), who founded a [Sultanate of Rûm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultanate_of_R%C3%BBm "Sultanate of Rûm") in 1077. Thus (land of the) [Rûm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%C3%BBm "Rûm") became another name for Anatolia. By the 12th century Europeans had started referring to Anatolia as *Turchia*.[^26]

During the era of the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"), many mapmakers referred to the mountainous plateau in eastern Anatolia as [Armenia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenia "Armenia"). Other contemporary sources called the same area [Kurdistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurdistan "Kurdistan").[^27] Geographers have used *[East Anatolian plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Anatolian_plateau "East Anatolian plateau")*, *[Armenian plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenian_plateau "Armenian plateau")* and the *[Iranian plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iranian_plateau "Iranian plateau")* to refer to the region; the former two largely overlap.[^28] While a standard definition of Anatolia refers to the entire Asian side of Turkey, according to archaeologist Lori Khatchadourian, this difference in terminology "primarily result\[s\] from the shifting political fortunes and cultural trajectories of the region since the nineteenth century".[^28]

Turkey's [First Geography Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Geography_Congress,_Turkey "First Geography Congress, Turkey") in 1941 created two [geographical regions of Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographical_regions_of_Turkey "Geographical regions of Turkey") to the east of the Gulf of Iskenderun-Black Sea line, the [Eastern Anatolia region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Anatolia_region "Eastern Anatolia region") and the [Southeastern Anatolia region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeastern_Anatolia_region "Southeastern Anatolia region"),[^29] the former largely corresponding to the [western part of the Armenian Highlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Armenia "Western Armenia"), the latter to the northern part of the Mesopotamian plain. According to [Richard Hovannisian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_G._Hovannisian "Richard G. Hovannisian"), this changing of toponyms was "necessary to obscure all evidence" of the [Armenian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenians "Armenians") presence as part of the policy of [Armenian genocide denial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenian_genocide_denial "Armenian genocide denial") embarked upon by the newly established Turkish government and what Hovannisian calls its "foreign collaborators".[^30]

## History

### Prehistoric Anatolia

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/G%C3%B6beklitepe_%C5%9Eanl%C4%B1urfa.jpg/250px-G%C3%B6beklitepe_%C5%9Eanl%C4%B1urfa.jpg)

The henges in Göbekli Tepe were erected as far back as 9,600 BCE.

Human habitation in Anatolia dates back to the [Paleolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleolithic "Paleolithic").[^31] Neolithic settlements include [Çatalhöyük](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%87atalh%C3%B6y%C3%BCk "Çatalhöyük"), [Çayönü](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%87ay%C3%B6n%C3%BC "Çayönü"), [Nevali Cori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nevali_Cori "Nevali Cori"), [Aşıklı Höyük](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A%C5%9F%C4%B1kl%C4%B1_H%C3%B6y%C3%BCk "Aşıklı Höyük"), [Boncuklu Höyük](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boncuklu_H%C3%B6y%C3%BCk "Boncuklu Höyük"), [Hacilar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hacilar "Hacilar"), [Göbekli Tepe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe "Göbekli Tepe"), [Norşuntepe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nor%C5%9Funtepe "Norşuntepe"), [Köşk Höyük](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%B6%C5%9Fk_H%C3%B6y%C3%BCk "Köşk Höyük"), and [Yumuktepe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yumuktepe "Yumuktepe"). Çatalhöyük (7,000 BCE) is considered the most advanced of these.[^32] Recent advances in archaeogenetics have confirmed that the [spread of agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_agriculture "History of agriculture") from the Middle East to Europe was strongly correlated with the [migration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-modern_human_migration "Pre-modern human migration") of [early farmers from Anatolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_European_Farmers "Early European Farmers") about 9,000 years ago, and was not just a cultural exchange.[^33] Anatolian Neolithic farmers derived most of their ancestry from local [Anatolian hunter-gatherers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolian_hunter-gatherers "Anatolian hunter-gatherers"), suggesting that agriculture was adopted in site by these hunter-gatherers and not spread by [demic diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demic_diffusion "Demic diffusion") into the region.[^34] Anatolian-derived Neolithic Farmers would subsequently spread across Europe, as far west as the Iberian Peninsula and the British Isles,[^35] [^36] as well as to the [Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maghreb "Maghreb").[^37] Most modern Europeans derive a significant part of their ancestry from these Neolithic Anatolian farmers.[^38] [Levantines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levantines "Levantines") also have significant Neolithic Anatolian farmer ancestry from post- [Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") migrations.[^39] About 6,500 years ago and thereafter, Anatolians became more genetically homogeneous due to eastern inflow. Earlier forms of Anatolian and non–Indo-European languages such as Hattic and Hurrian were likely spoken by migrants and locals participating in this great mixture. Steppe ancestry is also absent in Anatolians until the Bronze Age.[^40]

[Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") Anatolia has been [proposed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolian_hypothesis "Anatolian hypothesis") as the [homeland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urheimat "Urheimat") of the [Indo-European language family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-European_languages "Indo-European languages"), although linguists tend to favour a [later origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurgan_hypothesis "Kurgan hypothesis") in the steppes north of the Black Sea. However, it is clear that the [Anatolian languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolian_languages "Anatolian languages"), the earliest attested branch of Indo-European, have been spoken in Anatolia since at least the 19th century BCE.[^41] [^42]

### Ancient Anatolia

Anatolia's historical records start with [clay tablets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clay_tablets "Clay tablets") from approximately around 2000 BC that were found in modern-day [Kültepe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%BCltepe "Kültepe").[^43] These tablets belonged to an [Assyrian trade colony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karum_\(trade_post\) "Karum (trade post)").[^43] The languages in Anatolia at that time included Hattian, Hurrian, [Hittite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hittite_language "Hittite language"), [Luwian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luwian_language "Luwian language"), and [Palaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palaic_language "Palaic language").[^44]

#### Hattians and Hurrians

The earliest historically attested populations of Anatolia were the [Hattians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hattians "Hattians") in central Anatolia, and [Hurrians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurrians "Hurrians") further to the east. The Hattians were an indigenous people, whose main centre was the city of [Hattush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hattush "Hattush"). Affiliation of [Hattian language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hattian_language "Hattian language") remains unclear, while [Hurrian language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurrian_language "Hurrian language") belongs to a distinctive family of [Hurro-Urartian languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurro-Urartian_languages "Hurro-Urartian languages"). All of those languages are extinct; relationships with indigenous [languages of the Caucasus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Languages_of_the_Caucasus "Languages of the Caucasus") have been proposed,[^45] but are not generally accepted. The region became famous for exporting raw materials. Organized trade between Anatolia and [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia") started to emerge during the period of the [Akkadian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akkadian_Empire "Akkadian Empire"), and was continued and intensified during the period of the [Old Assyrian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Assyrian_Empire "Old Assyrian Empire"), between the 21st and the 18th centuries BCE. Assyrian traders were bringing tin and textiles in exchange for copper, silver or gold. Cuneiform records, dated c. 20th century BCE, found in Anatolia at the Assyrian colony of [Kanesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%BCltepe "Kültepe"), use an advanced system of trading computations and credit lines.[^46] [^47] [^48]

#### Hittites, Luwians, and Palaians

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Sphinx_Gate%2C_Hattusa_01.jpg/250px-Sphinx_Gate%2C_Hattusa_01.jpg)

The Sphinx Gate in Hattusa

[Hattian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hattians "Hattians") rulers were gradually replaced by [Hittite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hittites "Hittites") rulers.[^43] The Hittite kingdom was a large kingdom in Central Anatolia, with its capital of [Hattusa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hattusa "Hattusa").[^43] It co-existed in Anatolia with [Palaians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pala_\(Anatolia\) "Pala (Anatolia)") and [Luwians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luwians "Luwians"), approximately between 1700 and 1200 BC.[^43] As the Hittite kingdom was disintegrating, further waves of Indo-European peoples migrated from southeastern Europe, which was followed by warfare.[^49] It is not known if the [Trojan War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_War "Trojan War") is based on historical events.[^50] [Troy's Late Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age_Troy "Late Bronze Age Troy") layers matches most with *[Iliad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iliad "Iliad")* 's story.[^51]
