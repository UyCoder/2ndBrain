The marked territories on this global map from the United Nations are mostly of countries which are sovereign states with full international recognition (brackets denote the country of a marked territory that is not a sovereign state). Some territories are countries in their own right but are not recognized as such (e.g. Taiwan ), some few marked territories are disputed about which country they belong to (e.g. Kashmir ) or if they are countries in their own right (e.g. Western Sahara (territory) or the state known by the same name ).

A **country** is a distinct area of land in the [world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") with a government that functions as a [sovereign state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state "Sovereign state"), an [unrecognized state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unrecognized_state "Unrecognized state"), a [constituent country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constituent_country "Constituent country"), or a [dependent territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_territory "Dependent territory").[^9] [^10] [^11] [^12] A country may consist of multiple nations, a structure known as a [multinational state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multinational_state "Multinational state").[^13] Countries are often distinguished as [developing countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country") or [developed countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country").[^14]

There is no universal agreement on the number of "countries" in the world.[^15] [^16] This ambiguity is a result of several countries not being recognized as sovereign states by the UN system, but are recognized by at least one UN member, known as " [diplomatic recognition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_recognition "Diplomatic recognition") '. These countries often have [disputed territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_dispute "Territorial dispute") and have only [partial recognition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_states_with_limited_recognition "List of states with limited recognition").[^16] A number of entities without recognition are also commonly considered a country.[^16] [^17]

Areas other than a [political entity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polity "Polity") may be referred to as a "country," such as the [West Country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Country "West Country") in England, "big sky country" (used in various contexts of the [American West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_West "American West")), "coal country" (used to describe [coal-mining regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal-mining_region "Coal-mining region")), [cottage country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cottage_country "Cottage country") or simply "the country" (used to describe a [rural area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rural_area "Rural area")).[^18] [^19] The term "country" is also used as a qualifier descriptively, such as [country music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Country_music "Country music") or [country living](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rural_lifestyle "Rural lifestyle").[^20]

The definition and usage of the word "country" has fluctuated and changed over time. *[The Economist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economist "The Economist")* wrote in 2010 that "any attempt to find a clear definition of a country soon runs into a thicket of exceptions and anomalies." [^21]

## Definition

In English, the word has increasingly become associated with political divisions, so that one sense, associated with the [indefinite article](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indefinite_article "Indefinite article") – "a country" – is now frequently applied as a synonym for a state or a former sovereign state. It may also be used as a synonym for "nation." Taking as examples [Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada "Canada"), [Sri Lanka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sri_Lanka "Sri Lanka"), and [Yugoslavia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yugoslavia "Yugoslavia"), cultural anthropologist [Clifford Geertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_Geertz "Clifford Geertz") wrote in 1997 that "it is clear that the relationships between 'country' and 'nation' are so different from one \[place\] to the next as to be impossible to fold into a dichotomous opposition as they are into a promiscuous fusion." [^22]

Areas much smaller than a political state may be referred to as countries, such as the [West Country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Country "West Country") in England, "big sky country" (used in various contexts of the [American West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_West "American West")), "coal country" (used to describe [coal-mining regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal-mining_region "Coal-mining region") in several sovereign states) and many other terms.[^18] The word "country" is also used for the sense of [native sovereign territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aboriginal_title "Aboriginal title"), such as the widespread use of [Indian country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_country "Indian country") in the United States.[^23] The term "country" in English may also be wielded to describe [rural areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rural_area "Rural area"), or used in the form "countryside." [Raymond Williams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Williams "Raymond Williams"), a Welsh scholar, wrote in 1975:[^24]

> 'Country' and 'city' are very powerful words, and this is not surprising when we remember how much they seem to stand for in the experience of human communities. In English, 'country' is both a nation and a part of a 'land'; 'the country' can be the whole society or its rural area. In the long history of human settlements, this connection between the land from which directly or indirectly we all get our living and the achievements of human society has been deeply known.

The unclear definition of "country" in modern English was further commented upon by philosopher [Simon Keller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Keller "Simon Keller"):[^25]

> Often, a country is presumed to be identical with a collection of citizens. Sometimes, people say that a country is a project, or an idea, or an ideal. Occasionally, philosophers entertain more metaphysically ambitious pictures, suggesting that a country is an organic entity with its own independent life and character, or that a country is an autonomous agent, just like you or me. Such claims are rarely explained or defended, however, and it is not clear how they should be assessed. We attribute so many different kinds of properties to countries, speaking as though a country can feature wheat fields waving or be girt by sea, can have a founding date and be democratic and free, can be English speaking, culturally diverse, war torn or Islamic.

—  *New Waves In Political Philosophy*, "Making Nonsense of Loyalty to Country", page 96

[Melissa Lucashenko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melissa_Lucashenko "Melissa Lucashenko"), an [Aboriginal Australian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aboriginal_Australians "Aboriginal Australians") writer, expressed the difficulty of defining " [Country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Country_\(Indigenous_Australians\) "Country (Indigenous Australians)") " in a 2005 essay, "Unsettlement":[^26]

> ...What is this thing country? What does country mean?... I spoke with others who said country meant Home, but who added the caveat that Home resided in people rather than places – a kind of portable Country... I tried to tease out some ways in which non-Indigenous people have understood country. I made categories: Country as Economy. Country as Geography. Country as Society. Country as Myth. Country as History. For all that I walked, slept, breathed and dreamed Country, the language still would not come.

## Statehood

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Limited_Recognition_States.svg/330px-Limited_Recognition_States.svg.png)

UN member states that at least one other UN member state does not recognise Non-UN member states recognised by at least one UN member state Non-UN member states recognised only by other non-UN member states

When referring to a specific [polity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polity "Polity"), the term "country" can refer to a [sovereign state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state "Sovereign state"), [state with limited recognition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_states_with_limited_recognition "List of states with limited recognition"), [constituent country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Country_\(disambiguation\)#Administrative_divisions "Country (disambiguation)") and [dependent territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_territory "Dependent territory").[^10] [^11] A sovereign state is a political entity that has supreme legitimate authority over a part of the world.[^27] There is no universal agreement on the number of "countries" in the world since several states have disputed sovereignty status, and a number of non-sovereign entities are commonly called countries.[^17]

Some countries, such as [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan"), [Sahrawi Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic "Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic") and [Kosovo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kosovo "Kosovo"), have limited recognition or actively disputed sovereignty from sovereign states.[^28] [^29] Breakaway states, such as [Transnistria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transnistria "Transnistria") have no recognition.[^30]

Other sovereign states are unions of separate polities, each of which may also be considered a country in its own right, called constituent countries. The [Danish Realm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danish_Realm "Danish Realm") consists of [Denmark proper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark "Denmark"), the [Faroe Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faroe_Islands "Faroe Islands") and [Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland "Greenland").[^31] The [Kingdom of the Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands "Kingdom of the Netherlands") consists of the [Netherlands proper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands"), [Aruba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aruba "Aruba"), [Curaçao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao "Curaçao") and [Sint Maarten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sint_Maarten "Sint Maarten").[^32] The [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") consists of [England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/England "England"), [Scotland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scotland "Scotland"), [Wales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wales "Wales") and [Northern Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Ireland "Northern Ireland").[^33]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Dependent_territories.svg/330px-Dependent_territories.svg.png)

Dependent territories and their sovereign states. All territories are labeled according to ISO 3166-1 1 or with numbers. 2 Colored areas without labels are integral parts of their respective countries. Antarctica is shown as a condominium instead of individual claims.

Some dependent territories of sovereign states are sometimes referred to as "countries" due to their [autonomous administrative divisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomous_administrative_division "Autonomous administrative division").[^34] These include the [overseas territories of New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomous_administrative_division#New_Zealand_overseas_territories "Autonomous administrative division"), the [dependencies of Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependencies_of_Norway "Dependencies of Norway"), the [British Overseas Territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories "British Overseas Territories") and [Crown Dependencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crown_Dependencies "Crown Dependencies"),[^34] the [territories of the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States "Territories of the United States"), the [external territories of Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_and_territories_of_Australia "States and territories of Australia"), the [special administrative regions of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_administrative_regions_of_China "Special administrative regions of China"), the autonomous regions of the Danish Realm, [Åland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%85land "Åland"), [Overseas France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_France "Overseas France"), and the [Caribbean Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caribbean_Netherlands "Caribbean Netherlands").[^35]

A few dependent territories are treated as a separate " [country of origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Country_of_origin "Country of origin") " in international trade,[^36] [^37] such as [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong"),[^38] [^39] [^40] [Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland "Greenland"),[^41] and [Macau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macau "Macau").[^42]

### International law

There are debates over whether states can exist as a fact independent of recognition or whether recognition is one of the facts necessary to bring states into being.[^43] [^44] [^45] [^46] Although international law defines "sovereignty" as not under another,[^47] no definition is binding for members of the United Nations on the criteria for statehood.[^48] [^49] [^17] State practice relating to the recognition of a country typically falls somewhere between the *declaratory* and *constitutive* approaches.[^50] [^51] [^52] [^53] [^54]

The [declarative theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state#Declarative_theory "Sovereign state") outlined in the [1933 Montevideo Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montevideo_Convention#Contents_of_the_convention "Montevideo Convention") describes a "state as a person of international law" in *Article 1*:[^55] [^56]

1. Having a permanent population
2. Having a defined territory
3. Having a government
4. Having the ability to enter into [relations with other states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_recognition "Diplomatic recognition")

The Montevideo Convention in *Article 3* implies that a sovereign state can exist even if no other countries recognise it.[^55] [^57] As a restatement of [customary international law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customary_international_law "Customary international law"), the Montevideo Convention merely codified existing legal norms and its principles,[^58] and therefore does not apply merely to the signatories of international organizations (such as the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations")),[^17] [^59] [^56] but to all subjects of international law as a whole.[^60] [^61] A similar opinion has been expressed by the [European Economic Community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Economic_Community "European Economic Community"),[^62] reiterated by the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union"), in the principal statement of its [Badinter Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Badinter_Committee "Badinter Committee"),[^63] and by Judge *[Challis Professor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Challis_Professorship "Challis Professorship")*, [James Crawford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Crawford_\(jurist\) "James Crawford (jurist)").[^59]

According to the [constitutive theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state#Constitutive_theory "Sovereign state"), a state is a legal entity of international law if, and only if, it is recognised as sovereign by at least one other country.[^64] Because of this, new states could not immediately become part of the international community or be bound by international law, and recognised nations did not have to respect international law in their dealings with them.[^65] In 1912, [L. F. L. Oppenheim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L._F._L._Oppenheim "L. F. L. Oppenheim") said the following, regarding constitutive theory:

> International Law does not say that a State is not in existence as long as it is not recognised, but it takes no notice of it before its recognition. Through recognition only and exclusively a State becomes an International Person and a subject of International Law.[^66]

In 1976 the [Organisation of African Unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_African_Unity "Organisation of African Unity") define state recognition as:[^67]

> ...the recognition of an independent and sovereign state is an act of sovereignty pertaining each member of the international community, an act to be taken individually, and it is, therefore, up to member states and each OAU power \[to decide\] whether to recognise or not the newly independent state.

## Identification

Symbols of a country may incorporate [cultural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture "Culture"), [religious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion "Religion") or [political](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics") symbols of any nation that the country includes. Many categories of symbols can be seen in flags, coats of arms, or seals.[^68]

### Flags

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Flickr_-_%E2%80%A6trialsanderrors_-_Johnson%27s_new_chart_of_national_emblems%2C_1868.jpg/250px-Flickr_-_%E2%80%A6trialsanderrors_-_Johnson%27s_new_chart_of_national_emblems%2C_1868.jpg)

Johnson's new chart of national emblems, published c. 1868. The large flags shown in the corners are the 37-star flag of the United States (flown 1867–1890), upper left; the Royal Standard of the United Kingdom, upper, right; the Russian Imperial Standard, lower left; and the French tricolore with inset Imperial Eagle, lower right. Various other flags flown by ships are shown. The Flag of Cuba is labelled "Cuban (so called) ". The Chinese dragon on the Flag of China was drawn mistakenly as a western dragon.

Originally, flags representing a country would generally be the personal flag of its rulers; however, over time, the practice of using personal banners as flags of places was abandoned in favor of flags that had some significance to the nation, often its patron saint. Early examples of these were the [maritime republics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maritime_republics "Maritime republics") such as [Genoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa "Republic of Genoa") which could be said to have a national flag as early as the 12th century.[^78] However, these were still mostly used in the context of marine identification.[^79]

Although some flags date back earlier, widespread use of flags outside of military or naval context begins only with the rise of the idea of the [nation state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation_state "Nation state") at the end of the 18th century and particularly are a product of the [Age of Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Revolution "Age of Revolution"). Revolutions such as those in [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution") and [America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution "American Revolution") called for people to begin thinking of themselves as [citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizens "Citizens") as opposed to [subjects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commoner "Commoner") under a king, and thus necessitated flags that represented the collective citizenry, not just the power and right of a ruling family.[^80] [^81] With [nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalism "Nationalism") becoming common across Europe in the 19th century, national flags came to represent most of the states of Europe.[^80] Flags also began fostering a sense of unity between different peoples, such as the [Union Jack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_Jack "Union Jack") representing a union between [England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/England "England") and [Scotland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scotland "Scotland"), or began to represent unity between nations in a perceived shared struggle, for example, the [Pan-Slavic colors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan-Slavic_colors "Pan-Slavic colors") or later [Pan-Arab colors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan-Arab_colors "Pan-Arab colors").[^82]

As Europeans [colonized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonized "Colonized") significant portions of the world, they exported ideas of nationhood and national symbols, including flags, with the adoption of a flag becoming seen as integral to the [nation-building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation-building "Nation-building") process.[^83] Political change, social reform, and revolutions combined with a growing sense of nationhood among ordinary people in the 19th and 20th centuries led to the birth of new nations and flags around the globe.[^84] With so many flags being created, interest in these designs began to develop and the study of flags, [vexillology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vexillology "Vexillology"), at both professional and amateur levels, emerged. After World War II, Western vexillology went through a phase of rapid development, with many research facilities and publications being established.[^85]

### National anthems

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Handschrift_Brussel_p-37-38.jpg/250px-Handschrift_Brussel_p-37-38.jpg)

Early version of the "Wilhelmus" as preserved in a manuscript of 1617 (Brussels, Royal Library, MS 15662, fol. 37v-38r) 86

A national anthem is a [patriotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriotism "Patriotism") [musical composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_composition "Musical composition") symbolizing and evoking eulogies of the history and traditions of a country or nation.[^87] Though the custom of an officially adopted national anthem became popular only in the 19th century, some national anthems predate this period, often existing as patriotic songs long before designation as national anthem. Several countries remain without an official national anthem. In these cases, there are established *[de facto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_facto "De facto")* anthems played at sporting events or diplomatic receptions. These include the United Kingdom (" [God Save the King](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/God_Save_the_King "God Save the King") ") and Sweden (*[Du gamla, Du fria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Du_gamla,_Du_fria "Du gamla, Du fria")*). Some sovereign states that are made up of multiple countries or constituencies have associated musical compositions for each of them (such as with the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom"), [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia"), and the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union")). These are sometimes referred to as national anthems even though they are not sovereign states (for example, " [Hen Wlad Fy Nhadau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hen_Wlad_Fy_Nhadau "Hen Wlad Fy Nhadau") " is used for Wales, part of the United Kingdom).[^88]

## Patriotism

A positive emotional connection to a country a person belongs to is called [patriotism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriotism "Patriotism"). Patriotism is a sense of love for, devotion to, and sense of attachment to one's country. This attachment can be a combination of many different feelings, and language relating to one's homeland, including ethnic, cultural, political, or historical aspects. It encompasses a set of concepts closely related to [nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalism "Nationalism"), mostly [civic nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_nationalism "Civic nationalism") and sometimes [cultural nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_nationalism "Cultural nationalism").[^89] [^90]

## Economy

Several organizations seek to identify trends to produce economy country classifications. Countries are often distinguished as [developing countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country") or [developed countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country").[^91]

The [United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Department_of_Economic_and_Social_Affairs "United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs") annually produces the *World Economic Situation and Prospects Report* classifying states as developed countries, economies in transition, or developing countries. The report classifies country development based on per capita [gross national income](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_national_income "Gross national income") (GNI) [^92] and identifies subgroups within broad categories based on geographical location or ad hoc criteria. Geographic regions with developing economies are Africa, East Asia, South Asia, Western Asia, and Latin America and the Caribbean. Economies in transition are in the former Soviet Union and South-Eastern Europe. Regions with developed countries are in Northern America, Europe, and Asia and the Pacific. The majority of economies in transition and developing countries are found in Africa, Asia, Latin America, and the Caribbean.[^93]

The [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank") also classifies countries based on GNI per capita. The *World Bank Atlas method* classifies countries as low-income economies, lower-middle-income economies, upper-middle-income economies, or high-income economies. For the 2020 fiscal year, the World Bank defines low-income economies as countries with a GNI per capita of $1,025 or less in 2018; lower-middle-income economies as countries with a GNI per capita between $1,026 and $3,995; upper-middle-income economies as countries with a GNI per capita between $3,996 and $12,375; high-income economies as countries with a GNI per capita of $12,376 or more.[^94]

It also identifies regional trends. The World Bank defines its regions as East Asia and Pacific, Europe and Central Asia, Latin America and the Caribbean, Middle East and North Africa, North America, South Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa. Lastly, the World Bank distinguishes countries based on its operational policies. The three categories include [International Development Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Development_Association "International Development Association") (IDA) countries, [International Bank for Reconstruction and Development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Bank_for_Reconstruction_and_Development "International Bank for Reconstruction and Development") (IBRD) countries, and Blend countries.[^94]
