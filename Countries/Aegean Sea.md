The **Aegean Sea** [^1] is an elongated [embayment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embayment "Embayment") of the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") between [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe") and [Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia "Asia"). It is located between the [Balkans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balkans "Balkans") and [Anatolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolia "Anatolia"), and covers an area of some 215,000 km <sup>2</sup> (83,000 sq mi).[^4] In the north, the Aegean is connected to the [Marmara Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marmara_Sea "Marmara Sea"), which in turn connects to the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea"), by the straits of the [Dardanelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dardanelles "Dardanelles") and the [Bosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosphorus "Bosphorus"), respectively. The [Aegean Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_Islands "Aegean Islands") are located within the sea and some bound it on its southern periphery, including [Crete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crete "Crete") and [Rhodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhodes "Rhodes"). The sea reaches a maximum depth of 2,639 m (8,658 ft) to the west of [Karpathos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karpathos "Karpathos"). The [Thracian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thracian_Sea "Thracian Sea") and the [Sea of Crete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_of_Crete "Sea of Crete") are main subdivisions of the Aegean Sea.

The Aegean Islands can be divided into several island groups, including the [Dodecanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecanese "Dodecanese"), the [Cyclades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclades "Cyclades"), the [Sporades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sporades "Sporades"), the [Saronic islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saronic_Islands "Saronic Islands") and the [North Aegean Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Aegean_islands "North Aegean islands"), as well as Crete and its surrounding islands. The Dodecanese, located to the southeast, includes the islands of Rhodes, [Kos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kos "Kos"), and [Patmos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patmos "Patmos"); the islands of [Delos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delos "Delos") and [Naxos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naxos "Naxos") are within the Cyclades to the south of the sea. [Lesbos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lesbos "Lesbos") is part of the North Aegean Islands. [Euboea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euboea "Euboea"), the second-largest island in Greece, is located in the Aegean, despite being administered as part of [Central Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Greece_\(administrative_region\) "Central Greece (administrative region)"). Nine out of twelve of the Administrative regions of Greece border the sea, along with the [Turkish provinces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provinces_of_Turkey "Provinces of Turkey") of [Edirne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edirne "Edirne"), [Çanakkale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%87anakkale "Çanakkale"), [Balıkesir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bal%C4%B1kesir "Balıkesir"), [İzmir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%B0zmir "İzmir"), [Aydın](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayd%C4%B1n "Aydın") and [Muğla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu%C4%9Fla_Province "Muğla Province") to the east of the sea. Various Turkish islands in the sea are [Imbros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imbros "Imbros"), [Tenedos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tenedos "Tenedos"), [Cunda Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cunda_Island "Cunda Island"), and the [Foça Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fo%C3%A7a_Islands "Foça Islands").

The Aegean Sea has been historically important, especially regarding the civilization of [Ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece"), which inhabited the area around the coast of the Aegean and the Aegean islands. The Aegean islands facilitated contact between the people of the area and between Europe and Asia. Along with the Greeks, [Thracians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thracians "Thracians") lived along the northern coasts. The [Romans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") conquered the area under the Roman Empire, and later the [Byzantine Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire") held it against advances by the [First Bulgarian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Bulgarian_Empire "First Bulgarian Empire"). The [Fourth Crusade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_Crusade "Fourth Crusade") weakened Byzantine control of the area, and it was eventually conquered by the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"), with the exception of Crete, which was a [Venetian colony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Candia "Kingdom of Candia") until 1669. The [Greek War of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_War_of_Independence "Greek War of Independence") allowed a Greek state on the coast of the Aegean from 1829 onwards. The Ottoman Empire held a presence over the sea for over 500 years until it was replaced by modern [Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey").

The rocks making up the floor of the Aegean are mainly limestone, though often greatly altered by volcanic activity that has convulsed the region in relatively recent geologic times. Of particular interest are the richly colored sediments in the region of the islands of [Santorini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santorini "Santorini") and [Milos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milos "Milos"), in the south Aegean.[^4] Notable cities on the Aegean coastline include [Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athens "Athens"), [Thessaloniki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thessaloniki "Thessaloniki"), [Heraklion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heraklion "Heraklion"), [Volos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volos "Volos"), [Alexandroupolis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandroupolis "Alexandroupolis") and [Kavala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kavala "Kavala") in Greece, and [İzmir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%B0zmir "İzmir") and [Bodrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bodrum "Bodrum") in Turkey.

Several issues concerning sovereignty within the Aegean Sea are disputed between Greece and Turkey. The [Aegean dispute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_dispute "Aegean dispute") has had a large effect on [Greece-Turkey relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece%E2%80%93Turkey_relations "Greece–Turkey relations") since the 1970s. Issues include the delimitation of [territorial waters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_waters "Territorial waters"), [national airspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_airspace "National airspace"), [exclusive economic zones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone "Exclusive economic zone"), and [flight information regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_information_region "Flight information region").[^5]

## Name and etymology

The origin of the name Αἰγαῖον πέλαγος (neuter) or Αἰγαῖος πόντος (masculine) [^6] has been explained by ancient grammarians through various theories:

**Figure-Based Origins**

- [Aegeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegeus "Aegeus"), the father of [Theseus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theseus "Theseus"), who is said to have drowned himself in the sea. According to the story, Aegeus instructed Theseus to raise white sails if he succeeded in killing the [Minotaur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minotaur "Minotaur"), when Theseus forgot to do so, Aegeus believed his son had died and leapt into the sea.[^7] [^8] [^9]
- Aegaea, an [Amazon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazons "Amazons") queen who was said to have perished there.[^8] [^9]
- Aegaeon, a sea deity.[^8]
- [Poseidon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poseidon "Poseidon"), known by the epithet "Aigaios".[^10]
- Ruler Aigaion. [Stephanus of Byzantium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephanus_of_Byzantium "Stephanus of Byzantium") wrote that one of the legends is that [Karystos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karystos "Karystos") was formerly called Aigaia, after the ruler Aigaion, who also lent his name to the Aegean Sea.[^11]
- The [Mysian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mysian "Mysian") hero Aigaion.[^10]
- Giant [Briareus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Briareus "Briareus"), also called Aigaion.[^10]
- Briareus, a primeval thalassocrat, also known as Aigaion.[^10]

**Place-Based Origins**

**Linguistic and Interpretive-Based Origins**

- From αἐί γαῖα, meaning "because the land is always in sight".[^10]
- From αἰγίς, meaning a violent squall, referring to the sea's stormy nature.[^8]
- From αἶγες ("goats"), because the many islands looked like a herd of goats rising from the water.[^13]
- The metaphorical use of αἶγες (aiges; "goats") for large, violent waves, as noted by the [Suda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suda "Suda"), suggesting a link between the sea's name and its fearsome character.[^14]

Ancient Greek authors also called it the "Greek Sea".[^15]

The sea was known in [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") as *Mare Aegaeum* while under the control of the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire"). [Pliny the Elder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder") write that the Romans used the terms "Macedonian" for the northern part near [Macedonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macedonia_\(Roman_province\) "Macedonia (Roman province)") and [Thrace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrace_\(Roman_province\) "Thrace (Roman province)") provinces, and "Grecian" for the part near [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece_in_the_Roman_era "Greece in the Roman era") itself.[^12]

In medieval Latin, the Aegean Sea was known as *Egeopelagus*, a form attested as late as 1419 in [Venetian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Venice "Republic of Venice") state papers (Ducatus Egeopelagi) and derived from the Greek αἰγαιοπέλαγος / αἰγαῖον πέλαγος.[^16] The term *Arcipelago* also appears, for instance in a treaty on 30 June 1268 between the Venetians and the emperor [Michael Palaeologus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_VIII_Palaiologos "Michael VIII Palaiologos").[^16]

This term subsequently gave rise to *[Archipelago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archipelago "Archipelago")* ([Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language"): αρχιπέλαγος), which in many European languages originally referred specifically to the Aegean Sea.[^17] [^9] The terms *Greek Archipelago* or *Grecian Archipelago* were also used.[^9] Later, as archipelago came to be used increasingly as a generic term for island-studded seas,[^18] the terms Greek Archipelago or Grecian Archipelago continued to be used [^19] [^20] [^21] [^22] [^23] alongside the ancient name, Aegean Sea.[^23] [^18]

In some [South Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Slavic_languages "South Slavic languages"), the Aegean is called *White Sea* ([Bulgarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulgarian_language "Bulgarian language"): Бяло море, [romanized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Bulgarian "Romanization of Bulgarian"): *Byalo more*; [Macedonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macedonian_language "Macedonian language"): Бело море, [romanized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Macedonian "Romanization of Macedonian"): *Belo more*; [Serbian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serbian_language "Serbian language"): *Belo more* / Бело море).[^24]

The [Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_language "Turkish language") name for the sea is *Ege Denizi*, which is derived from the Greek name, and *Adalar Denizi* meaning "Sea of Islands". In the past, Turks also referred to it as the "White Sea", to differentiate it from the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea").[^8] [^13]

## Geography

The Aegean Sea is an elongated [embayment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay "Bay") of the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") and covers about 214,000 square kilometres (83,000 sq mi) in area, measuring about 670 kilometres (420 mi) longitudinally and 390 kilometres (240 mi) latitudinal. The sea's maximum depth is 2,639 metres (8,658 ft), located at a point west of [Karpathos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karpathos "Karpathos"). The [Aegean Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_Islands "Aegean Islands") are found within its waters, with the following islands delimiting the sea on the south, generally from west to east: [Kythera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kythera "Kythera"), [Antikythera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antikythera "Antikythera"), [Crete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crete "Crete"), [Kasos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kasos "Kasos"), [Karpathos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karpathos "Karpathos") and [Rhodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhodes "Rhodes"). The Anatolian peninsula marks the eastern boundary of the sea, while the Greek mainland marks the west. Several seas are contained within the Aegean Sea; the [Thracian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thracian_Sea "Thracian Sea") is a section of the Aegean located to the north, the [Icarian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icarian_Sea "Icarian Sea") to the east, the [Myrtoan Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myrtoan_Sea "Myrtoan Sea") to the west, while the [Sea of Crete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_of_Crete "Sea of Crete") is the southern section.

The Greek regions that border the sea, in alphabetical order, are [Attica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attica "Attica"), [Central Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Greece_\(administrative_region\) "Central Greece (administrative region)"), [Central Macedonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Macedonia "Central Macedonia"), Crete, [Eastern Macedonia and Thrace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Macedonia_and_Thrace "Eastern Macedonia and Thrace"), [North Aegean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Aegean "North Aegean"), [Peloponnese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peloponnese_\(region\) "Peloponnese (region)"), [South Aegean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Aegean "South Aegean"), and [Thessaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thessaly "Thessaly"). The traditional Greek region of [Macedonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macedonia_\(Greece\) "Macedonia (Greece)") also borders the sea, to the north.

The Aegean Islands, which almost all belong to [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece"), can be divided into seven groups:

1. [Northeastern Aegean Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Aegean_Islands#The_North_Aegean_Islands "List of Aegean Islands"), which lie in the [Thracian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thracian_Sea "Thracian Sea") [^25]
2. [East Aegean Islands (*Euboea*)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euboea "Euboea")
3. Northern [Sporades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sporades "Sporades")
4. [Cyclades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclades "Cyclades")
5. [Saronic Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saronic_Islands "Saronic Islands") (or *Argo-Saronic Islands*)
6. [Dodecanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecanese "Dodecanese") (or *Southern Sporades*) [^26]
7. [Crete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crete "Crete")

Many of the Aegean islands or island chains, are geographical extensions of the mountains on the mainland. One chain extends across the sea to Chios, another extends across Euboea to Samos, and a third extends across the Peloponnese and Crete to Rhodes, dividing the Aegean from the Mediterranean.

The bays and gulfs of the Aegean beginning at the South and moving clockwise include on Crete, the [Mirabello](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirabello_Bay "Mirabello Bay"), [Almyros](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Almyros_Bay&action=edit&redlink=1 "Almyros Bay (page does not exist)"), [Souda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Souda_Bay "Souda Bay") and [Chania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Chania "Gulf of Chania") bays or gulfs, on the mainland the [Myrtoan Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myrtoan_Sea "Myrtoan Sea") to the west with the [Argolic Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argolic_Gulf "Argolic Gulf"), the [Saronic Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saronic_Gulf "Saronic Gulf") northwestward, the [Petalies Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petalies_Gulf "Petalies Gulf") which connects with the [South Euboic Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Euboea "Gulf of Euboea"), the [Pagasetic Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pagasetic_Gulf "Pagasetic Gulf") which connects with the [North Euboic Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Euboea "Gulf of Euboea"), the [Thermian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermian_Gulf "Thermian Gulf") northwestward, the [Chalkidiki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalkidiki "Chalkidiki") Peninsula including the [Cassandra](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cassandra_Gulf&action=edit&redlink=1 "Cassandra Gulf (page does not exist)") and the [Singitic Gulfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singitic_Gulf "Singitic Gulf"), northward the [Strymonian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strymonian_Gulf "Strymonian Gulf") and the [Gulf of Kavala](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gulf_of_Kavala&action=edit&redlink=1 "Gulf of Kavala (page does not exist)") and the rest are in [Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey"); [Saros Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saros_Gulf "Saros Gulf"), [Edremit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edremit_\(District\),_Bal%C4%B1kesir "Edremit (District), Balıkesir") Gulf, [Dikili Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dikili_Gulf "Dikili Gulf"), [Gulf of Çandarlı](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_%C3%87andarl%C4%B1 "Gulf of Çandarlı"), [Gulf of İzmir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_%C4%B0zmir "Gulf of İzmir"), [Gulf of Kuşadası](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Ku%C5%9Fadas%C4%B1 "Gulf of Kuşadası"), [Gulf of Gökova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_G%C3%B6kova "Gulf of Gökova"), [Güllük](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%BCll%C3%BCk "Güllük") Gulf.

The Aegean Sea is connected to the Sea of Marmara by the [Dardanelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dardanelles "Dardanelles"), also known from Classical Antiquity as the Hellespont. The Dardanelles are located to the northeast of the sea. It ultimately connects with the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea") through the [Bosporus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosporus "Bosporus") strait, upon which lies the city of [Istanbul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Istanbul "Istanbul"). The Dardanelles and the Bosporus are known as the [Turkish Straits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_Straits "Turkish Straits").

### Hydrography

Aegean surface water circulates in a counterclockwise [gyre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_gyre "Ocean gyre"), with [hypersaline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersaline "Hypersaline") Mediterranean water moving northward along the west coast of Turkey, before being displaced by less dense Black Sea outflow. The dense [Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean "Mediterranean") water sinks below the Black Sea inflow to a depth of 23–30 metres (75–98 ft), then flows through the [Dardanelles Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dardanelles_Strait "Dardanelles Strait") and into the [Sea of Marmara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_of_Marmara "Sea of Marmara") at velocities of 5–15 cm/s (2–6 in/s). The Black Sea outflow moves westward along the northern Aegean Sea, then flows southwards along the east coast of Greece.[^28]

The physical oceanography of the Aegean Sea is controlled mainly by the regional climate, the fresh water discharge from major rivers draining southeastern Europe, and the seasonal variations in the Black Sea surface water outflow through the [Dardanelles Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dardanelles_Strait "Dardanelles Strait").

Analysis [^29] of the Aegean during 1991 and 1992 revealed three distinct water masses:

- **Aegean Sea Surface Water** – 40–50 metres (130–160 ft) thick veneer, with summer temperatures of 19–24 °C and winter temperatures ranging from 8 °C (46 °F) in the north to 13 °C (55 °F) in the very south.
- **Aegean Sea Intermediate Water** – Aegean Sea Intermediate Water extends from 40–50 m (130–160 ft) to 200–300 m (660–980 ft) with temperatures ranging from 11–18 °C (52–64 °F).
- **Aegean Sea Bottom Water** – occurring at depths below 500–1,000 m (1,600–3,300 ft) with a very uniform temperature (13–14 °C (55–57 °F)) and salinity (3.91–3.92%).

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Greece_map_of_K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification_%28new%29.svg/250px-Greece_map_of_K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification_%28new%29.svg.png)

Climate map of Greece. Most of the landmass surrounding the Aegean Sea is classified as Csa, with the northern region being BSk.

The climate of the Aegean Sea largely reflects the climate of Greece and Western Turkey, which is to say, predominantly [Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_climate "Mediterranean climate"). According to the [Köppen climate classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification "Köppen climate classification"), most of the Aegean is classified as Hot-summer Mediterranean (*Csa*), with hotter and drier summers along with milder and wetter winters. However, high temperatures during summers are generally not quite as high as those in arid or semiarid climates due to the presence of a large body of water. This is most predominant in the west and east coasts of the Aegean, and within the Aegean islands. In the north of the Aegean Sea, the climate is instead classified as [Cold semi-arid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-arid_climate "Semi-arid climate") *(BSk)*, which feature cooler summers than Hot-summer Mediterranean climates. The [Etesian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Etesian "Etesian") winds are a dominant weather influence in the Aegean Basin.

## Biogeography and ecology

### Protected areas

Greece has established several [marine protected areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_protected_area "Marine protected area") along its coasts. According to the Network of Managers of Marine Protected Areas in the Mediterranean ([MedPAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MedPAN "MedPAN")), four Greek MPAs are participating in the Network. These include [Alonnisos Marine Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alonnisos_Marine_Park "Alonnisos Marine Park"), while the [Missolonghi–Aitoliko Lagoons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Missolonghi%E2%80%93Aitoliko_Lagoons "Missolonghi–Aitoliko Lagoons") and the island of [Zakynthos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zakynthos "Zakynthos") are not on the Aegean.[^33]

## History

### Ancient history

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Cycladic_figurine%2C_female%2C_2800-2300_BC%2C_AM_Naxos_%2813_01%29%2C_143205.jpg/250px-Cycladic_figurine%2C_female%2C_2800-2300_BC%2C_AM_Naxos_%2813_01%29%2C_143205.jpg)

Female figure from Naxos (2800–2300 BC)

The current coastline dates back to about 4000 BC. Before that time, at the peak of the [last ice age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_glacial_period "Last glacial period") (about 18,000 years ago) sea levels everywhere were 130 metres (430 ft) lower, and there were large well-watered coastal plains instead of much of the northern Aegean. When they were first occupied, the present-day islands including [Milos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milos "Milos") with its important [obsidian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obsidian "Obsidian") production were probably still connected to the mainland. The present coastal arrangement appeared around 9,000 years ago, with post-ice age sea levels continuing to rise for another 3,000 years after that.[^34]

The subsequent [Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") civilizations of Greece and the Aegean Sea have given rise to the general term *[Aegean civilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_civilization "Aegean civilization")*. In ancient times, the sea was the birthplace of two ancient civilizations – the [Minoans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minoan_civilization "Minoan civilization") of Crete and the [Mycenaeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece "Mycenaean Greece") of the [Peloponnese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peloponnese "Peloponnese").[^35]

The Minoan civilization was a Bronze Age civilization on the island of Crete and other Aegean islands, flourishing from around 3000 to 1450 BC before a period of decline, finally ending at around 1100 BC. It represented the first advanced civilization in Europe, leaving behind massive building complexes, tools, stunning artwork, writing systems, and a massive network of trade.[^36] The Minoan period saw extensive trade between Crete, Aegean, and Mediterranean settlements, particularly the Near East. The most notable Minoan palace is that of [Knossos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knossos "Knossos"), followed by that of [Phaistos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phaistos "Phaistos"). The Mycenaean Greeks arose on the mainland, becoming the first advanced civilization in mainland Greece, which lasted from approximately 1600 to 1100 BC. It is believed that the site of [Mycenae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycenae "Mycenae"), which sits close to the Aegean coast, was the center of Mycenaean civilization. The Mycenaeans introduced several innovations in the fields of engineering, architecture and military infrastructure, while trade over vast areas of the Mediterranean, including the Aegean, was essential for the Mycenaean economy. Their [syllabic script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabic_script "Syllabic script"), the [Linear B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_B "Linear B"), offers the first written records of the Greek language and [their religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycenaean_religion "Mycenaean religion") already included several deities that can also be found in the [Olympic Pantheon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twelve_Olympians "Twelve Olympians"). Mycenaean Greece was dominated by a warrior elite society and consisted of a network of [palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palace "Palace") -centered states that developed rigid hierarchical, political, social and economic systems. At the head of this society was the king, known as *[wanax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wanax "Wanax")*.

The civilization of Mycenaean Greeks perished with the [collapse of Bronze Age culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age_collapse "Late Bronze Age collapse") in the eastern Mediterranean, to be followed by the so-called [Greek Dark Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_Dark_Ages "Greek Dark Ages"). It is undetermined what cause the collapse of the Mycenaeans. During the Greek Dark Ages, writing in the Linear B script ceased, vital trade links were lost, and towns and villages were abandoned.

### Ancient Greece

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Greek_Galleys.jpg/250px-Greek_Galleys.jpg)

A fleet of Athenian trireme

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Celcius_library.jpg/250px-Celcius_library.jpg)

Library of Celsus, a Roman structure in important sea port Ephesus

The [Archaic period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaic_Greece "Archaic Greece") followed the Greek Dark Ages in the 8th century BC. Greece became divided into small self-governing communities, and adopted the [Phoenician alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet "Phoenician alphabet"), modifying it to create the [Greek alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_alphabet "Greek alphabet"). By the 6th century BC several cities had emerged as dominant in Greek affairs: Athens, Sparta, [Corinth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Corinth "Ancient Corinth"), and [Thebes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thebes_\(Greece\) "Thebes (Greece)"), of which Athens, Sparta, and Corinth were closest to the Aegean Sea. Each of them had brought the surrounding rural areas and smaller towns under their control, and Athens and Corinth had become major maritime and mercantile powers as well. In the 8th and 7th centuries BC many Greeks migrated to form [colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonies_in_antiquity "Colonies in antiquity") in [Magna Graecia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magna_Graecia "Magna Graecia") ([Southern Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Italy "Southern Italy") and [Sicily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sicily "Sicily")), Asia Minor and further afield. The Aegean Sea was the setting for one of the most pivotal naval engagements in history, when, on 20 September 480 B.C., the Athenian fleet gained a decisive victory over the Persian fleet of the [Xerxes II of Persia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerxes_II_of_Persia "Xerxes II of Persia") at the [Battle of Salamis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Salamis "Battle of Salamis"). Thus ending any further attempt of western expansion by the [Achaemenid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire").[^37]

The Aegean Sea would later come to be under the control, albeit briefly, of the [Kingdom of Macedonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macedonia_\(ancient_kingdom\) "Macedonia (ancient kingdom)"). [Philip II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_II_of_Macedon "Philip II of Macedon") and his son [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") led a series of conquests that led not only to the unification of the Greek mainland and the control of the Aegean Sea under his rule, but also the destruction of the [Achaemenid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire"). After Alexander the Great's death, his empire was divided among his generals. [Cassander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassander "Cassander") became king of the Hellenistic kingdom of Macedon, which held territory along the western coast of the Aegean, roughly corresponding to modern-day Greece. The Kingdom of [Lysimachus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lysimachus "Lysimachus") had control over the sea's eastern coast. Greece had entered the [Hellenistic period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_period "Hellenistic period").

### Roman rule

The Macedonian Wars were a series of conflicts fought by the [Roman Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic "Roman Republic") and its Greek allies in the eastern Mediterranean against several different major Greek kingdoms. They resulted in Roman control or influence over the eastern Mediterranean basin, including the Aegean, in addition to their hegemony in the western Mediterranean after the [Punic Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punic_Wars "Punic Wars"). During Roman rule, the land around the Aegean Sea fell under the provinces of [Achaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaea_\(Roman_province\) "Achaea (Roman province)"), [Macedonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macedonia_\(Roman_province\) "Macedonia (Roman province)"), [Thracia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thracia "Thracia"), [Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia_\(Roman_province\) "Asia (Roman province)") and [Creta et Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crete_and_Cyrenaica "Crete and Cyrenaica") (island of Crete).

### Medieval period

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Emirate_of_Crete_Map.svg/250px-Emirate_of_Crete_Map.svg.png)

Emirate of Crete, after early conquest of Arabs

The [fall of the Western Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire "Fall of the Western Roman Empire") allowed its successor state, the [Byzantine Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire"), to continue Roman control over the Aegean Sea. However, their territory would later be threatened by the [early Muslim conquests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Muslim_conquests "Early Muslim conquests") initiated by [Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad "Muhammad") in the 7th century. Although the [Rashidun Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashidun_Caliphate "Rashidun Caliphate") did not manage to obtain land along the coast of the Aegean Sea, its conquest of the Eastern Anatolian peninsula as well as Egypt, the Levant, and North Africa left the Byzantine Empire weakened. The [Umayyad Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") expanded the territorial gains of the Rashidun Caliphate, conquering much of North Africa, and threatened the Byzantine Empire's control of Western Anatolia, where it meets the Aegean Sea.

During the 820s, Crete was conquered by a group of [Berbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berbers "Berbers") [Andalusians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Andalus "Al-Andalus") exiles led by [Abu Hafs Umar al-Iqritishi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Hafs_Umar_al-Iqritishi "Abu Hafs Umar al-Iqritishi"), and it became an independent [Islamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic "Islamic") state.[^38] The Byzantine Empire launched a campaign that took most of the island back in 842 and 843 under [Theoktistos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoktistos "Theoktistos"), but the re-conquest was not completed and was soon reversed. Later attempts by the Byzantine Empire to recover the island were without success. For the approximately 135 years of its existence, the emirate of Crete was one of the major foes of Byzantium. Crete commanded the sea lanes of the Eastern Mediterranean and functioned as a forward base and haven for Muslim corsair fleets that ravaged the Byzantine-controlled shores of the Aegean Sea. Crete returned to Byzantine rule under [Nikephoros II Phokas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikephoros_II_Phokas "Nikephoros II Phokas"), who launched a huge campaign against the Emirate of Crete in 960 to 961.[^39]

Meanwhile, the [Bulgarian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Bulgarian_Empire "First Bulgarian Empire") threatened Byzantine control of Northern Greece and the Aegean coast to the south. Under [Presian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presian_of_Bulgaria "Presian of Bulgaria") and his successor [Boris I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boris_I_of_Bulgaria "Boris I of Bulgaria"), the Bulgarian Empire managed to obtain a small portion of the northern Aegean coast.[^40] [Simeon I of Bulgaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simeon_I_of_Bulgaria "Simeon I of Bulgaria") led Bulgaria to its greatest territorial expansion, and managed to conqueror much of the northern and western coasts of the Aegean. The Byzantines later regained control. The [Second Bulgarian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Bulgarian_Empire "Second Bulgarian Empire") achieved similar success along, again, the northern and western coasts, under [Ivan Asen II of Bulgaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivan_Asen_II_of_Bulgaria "Ivan Asen II of Bulgaria").

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Aegean_Sea_by_Piri_Reis.jpg/250px-Aegean_Sea_by_Piri_Reis.jpg)

A 1528 map of the Aegean Sea by Turkish geographer Piri Reis

The [Seljuk Turks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seljuk_dynasty "Seljuk dynasty"), under the [Seljuk Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seljuk_Empire "Seljuk Empire"), invaded the Byzantine Empire in 1068, from which they annexed almost all the territories of Anatolia, including the east coast of the Aegean Sea, during the reign of [Alp Arslan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alp_Arslan "Alp Arslan"), the second [Sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan "Sultan") of the Seljuk Empire. After the death of his successor, [Malik Shah I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malik_Shah_I "Malik Shah I"), the empire was divided, and Malik Shah was succeeded in Anatolia by [Kilij Arslan I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilij_Arslan_I "Kilij Arslan I"), who founded the [Sultanate of Rum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultanate_of_Rum "Sultanate of Rum"). The Byzantines yet again recaptured the eastern coast of the Aegean.

After [Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantinople "Constantinople") was occupied by Western European and Venetian forces during the [Fourth Crusade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_Crusade "Fourth Crusade"), the area around the Aegean Sea was fragmented into multiple entities, including the [Latin Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_Empire "Latin Empire"), the [Kingdom of Thessalonica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Thessalonica "Kingdom of Thessalonica"), the [Empire of Nicaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_Nicaea "Empire of Nicaea"), the [Principality of Achaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principality_of_Achaea "Principality of Achaea"), and the [Duchy of Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_Athens "Duchy of Athens"). The Venetians created the maritime state of the [Duchy of the Archipelago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_the_Archipelago "Duchy of the Archipelago"), which included all the Cyclades except [Mykonos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mykonos "Mykonos") and [Tinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tinos "Tinos"). The Empire of Nicaea, a Byzantine rump state, managed to affect the [Recapture of Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recapture_of_Constantinople "Recapture of Constantinople") from the Latins in 1261 and defeat Epirus. Byzantine successes were not to last; the Ottomans would conquer the area around the Aegean coast, but before their expansion the Byzantine Empire had already been weakened from internal conflict. By the late 14th century, the Byzantine Empire had lost all control of the coast of the Aegean Sea and could only exercise power around their capital, Constantinople. The Ottoman Empire then gained control of all the Aegean coast with the exception of Crete, which was a Venetian colony until 1669.

### Modern period

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/German_tanks_in_Rhodes.gif/250px-German_tanks_in_Rhodes.gif)

German Tanks in Rhodes during the WW2

The [Greek War of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_War_of_Independence "Greek War of Independence") allowed a Greek state on the coast of the Aegean from 1829 onward. In the aftermath of the [Balkan Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balkan_Wars "Balkan Wars") (1912–1913) Greece expanded northwards along the coast, while [Bulgaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bulgaria "Kingdom of Bulgaria") gained access in the northernmost section of the sea. The Ottoman Empire held a presence over the sea for over 500 years until its [dissolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Ottoman_Empire "Dissolution of the Ottoman Empire") following [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), when it was replaced by modern Turkey. After the war, Greece gained control over the area around the northern coast of the Aegean, including the territory held by Bulgaria. By the 1920s, Greece and Turkey had about resumed their present-day borders.

In the [Italo-Turkish War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italo-Turkish_War "Italo-Turkish War") of 1912, Italy captured the Dodecanese islands, and had occupied them since, reneging on the 1919 [Venizelos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eleftherios_Venizelos "Eleftherios Venizelos") – [Tittoni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tommaso_Tittoni "Tommaso Tittoni") agreement to cede them to Greece. The Greco-Italian War took place from October 1940 to April 1941 as part of the [Balkans Campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balkans_Campaign_\(World_War_II\) "Balkans Campaign (World War II)") of [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"). The Italian war aim was to establish a Greek [puppet state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puppet_state "Puppet state"), which would permit the Italian annexation of the Sporades and Cyclades islands in the Aegean Sea, to be administered as a part of the [Italian Aegean Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Aegean_Islands "Italian Aegean Islands"). The German invasion resulted in the [Axis occupation of Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_occupation_of_Greece "Axis occupation of Greece"). The German troops evacuated Athens on 12 October 1944, and by the end of the month, they had withdrawn from mainland Greece. Greece was then liberated by Allied troops.

## Economy and politics

Many of the islands in the Aegean have safe harbours and bays. In ancient times, navigation through the sea was easier than travelling across the rough terrain of the Greek mainland, and to some extent, the coastal areas of Anatolia. Many of the islands are [volcanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcano "Volcano"), and marble and iron are mined on other islands. The larger islands have some fertile valleys and plains.

Of the main islands in the Aegean Sea, two belong to Turkey – [Bozcaada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bozcaada "Bozcaada") (Tenedos) and [Gökçeada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6k%C3%A7eada "Gökçeada") (Imbros); the rest belong to Greece. Between the two countries, there are [political disputes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_dispute "Aegean dispute") over several aspects of political control over the Aegean space, including the size of territorial waters, air control and the [delimitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_boundary_delimitation "National boundary delimitation") of economic rights to the [continental shelf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_shelf "Continental shelf"). These issues are known as the [Aegean dispute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_dispute "Aegean dispute").

### Transport

Multiple ports are located along the Greek and Turkish coasts of the Aegean Sea. The [port of Piraeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_Piraeus "Port of Piraeus") in Athens is the chief port in Greece, the largest passenger port in Europe [^41] [^42] and the third largest in the world,[^43] [servicing about 20 million passengers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_busiest_cruise_ports_by_passengers "List of busiest cruise ports by passengers") annually. With a throughput of 1.4 million [TEUs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twenty-foot_equivalent_unit "Twenty-foot equivalent unit"), Piraeus is placed among the top ten ports in container traffic in Europe and the top container port in the [Eastern Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean "Eastern Mediterranean").[^44] Piraeus is also the commercial hub of [Greek shipping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_shipping "Greek shipping"). Piraeus bi-annually acts as the focus for a major shipping convention, known as Posidonia, which attracts maritime industry professionals from all over the world. Piraeus is currently Greece's third-busiest port in terms of tons of goods transported, behind [Agioi Theodoroi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agioi_Theodoroi "Agioi Theodoroi") and [Thessaloniki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_Thessaloniki "Port of Thessaloniki").[^45] The central port serves ferry routes to almost every island in the eastern portion of Greece, the island of Crete, the Cyclades, the Dodecanese, and much of the northern and the eastern Aegean Sea, while the western part of the port is used for cargo services.

As of 2007, the Port of Thessaloniki was the second-largest container port in Greece after the port of Piraeus, making it one of the busiest ports in Greece. In 2007, the Port of Thessaloniki handled 14,373,245 tonnes of cargo and 222,824 [TEU's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twenty-foot_equivalent_unit "Twenty-foot equivalent unit"). Paloukia, on the island of [Salamis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salamis_Island "Salamis Island"), is a major passenger port.

### Fishing

Fish are Greece's second-largest agricultural export, and Greece has Europe's largest fishing fleet.[^46] Fish captured include [sardines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sardines_as_food "Sardines as food"), [mackerel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mackerel_as_food "Mackerel as food"), [grouper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grouper "Grouper"), [grey mullets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grey_mullet "Grey mullet"), [sea bass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_bass "Sea bass"), and [seabream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sparidae "Sparidae"). There is a considerable difference between fish catches between the pelagic and demersal zones;[^47] with respect to pelagic fisheries, the catches from the northern, central and southern Aegean area groupings are dominated, respectively, by [anchovy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anchovy "Anchovy"), [horse mackerels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trachurus "Trachurus"), and [boops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boops "Boops"). For demersal fisheries, the catches from the northern and southern Aegean area groupings are dominated by [grey mullets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mullet_\(fish\) "Mullet (fish)") and [pickerel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spicara_smaris "Spicara smaris") (*Spicara smaris*) respectively.

The industry has been impacted by the [Great Recession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Recession "Great Recession"). Overfishing and [habitat destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_destruction "Habitat destruction") is also a concern, threatening grouper, and seabream populations, resulting in perhaps a 50% decline of fish catch.[^48] To address these concerns, Greek fishermen have been offered a compensation by the government. Although some species are defined as protected or threatened under EU legislation, several illegal species such as the molluscs *[Pinna nobilis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinna_nobilis "Pinna nobilis")*, *[Charonia tritonis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charonia_tritonis "Charonia tritonis")* and *[Lithophaga lithophaga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithophaga_lithophaga "Lithophaga lithophaga")*, can be bought in restaurants and fish markets around Greece.[^49]

### Tourism

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Chora_of_Mykonos_%289%29.jpg/250px-Chora_of_Mykonos_%289%29.jpg)

Tourists in the town of Mykonos, part of the Cyclades

The Aegean islands within the Aegean Sea are significant tourist destinations. Tourism to the Aegean islands contributes a significant portion of [tourism in Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourism_in_Greece "Tourism in Greece"), especially since the second half of the 20th century.[^50] A total of five UNESCO [World Heritage sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Site "World Heritage Site") are located the Aegean Islands; these include the [Monastery of Saint John the Theologian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monastery_of_Saint_John_the_Theologian "Monastery of Saint John the Theologian") and the [Cave of the Apocalypse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cave_of_the_Apocalypse "Cave of the Apocalypse") on [Patmos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patmos "Patmos"),[^51] the [Pythagoreion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoreion "Pythagoreion") and [Heraion of Samos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heraion_of_Samos "Heraion of Samos") in [Samos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samos "Samos"),[^52] the [Nea Moni of Chios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nea_Moni_of_Chios "Nea Moni of Chios"),[^53] the island of [Delos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delos "Delos"),[^54] and the [Medieval City of Rhodes.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhodes_\(city\) "Rhodes (city)")[^55]

Greece is one of the [most visited countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Tourism_rankings#Europe "World Tourism rankings") in Europe and the world with over 33 million visitors in 2018,[^56] and the tourism industry around a quarter of Greece's Gross Domestic Product.[^57] The islands of Santorini, Crete, Lesbos, Delos, and Mykonos are common tourist destinations. An estimated 2 million tourists visit Santorini annually.[^58] However, concerns relating to [overtourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overtourism "Overtourism") have arisen in recent years, such as issues of inadequate infrastructure and overcrowding.[^59] Alongside Greece, Turkey has also been successful in developing resort areas and attracting large number of tourists,[^60] contributing to [tourism in Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourism_in_Turkey "Tourism in Turkey"). The phrase " [Blue Cruise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_Cruise "Blue Cruise") " refers to recreational voyages along the [Turkish Riviera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_Riviera "Turkish Riviera"), including across the Aegean.[^61] The ancient city of [Troy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troy "Troy"), a World Heritage Site, is on the Turkish coast of the Aegean.[^62]

Greece and Turkey both take part in the [Blue Flag beach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_Flag_beach "Blue Flag beach") certification programme of the [Foundation for Environmental Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundation_for_Environmental_Education "Foundation for Environmental Education"). The certification is awarded for beaches and [marinas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marina "Marina") meeting strict quality standards including environmental protection, water quality, safety and services criteria.[^63] As of 2015, the Blue Flag has been awarded to 395 beaches and 9 marinas in Greece. Southern Aegean beaches on the Turkish coast include [Muğla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu%C4%9Fla "Muğla"), with 102 beaches awarded with the blue flag, along with [İzmir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%B0zmir "İzmir") and [Aydın](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayd%C4%B1n "Aydın"), who have 49 and 30 beaches awarded respectively.[^64] [^65]
