**Libya**,[^2] officially the **State of Libya**,[^3] is a country in the [Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maghreb "Maghreb") region of [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa"). It borders the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") to the north, [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") to [the east](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt%E2%80%93Libya_border "Egypt–Libya border"), [Sudan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudan "Sudan") to [the southeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya%E2%80%93Sudan_border "Libya–Sudan border"), [Chad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chad "Chad") to [the south](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chad%E2%80%93Libya_border "Chad–Libya border"), [Niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger "Niger") to [the southwest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya%E2%80%93Niger_border "Libya–Niger border"), [Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria "Algeria") to [the west](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria%E2%80%93Libya_border "Algeria–Libya border"), and [Tunisia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunisia "Tunisia") to [the northwest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya%E2%80%93Tunisia_border "Libya–Tunisia border"). With an area of almost 1.8 million km <sup>2</sup> (700,000 sq mi), Libya is the [fourth-largest country in Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_African_countries_by_area "List of African countries by area") and the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world"), and the [16th-largest in the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_outlying_territories_by_total_area "List of countries and outlying territories by total area").[^10] The country claims 32,000 square kilometres of southeastern Algeria, south of the Libyan town of [Ghat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghat,_Libya "Ghat, Libya").[^4] [^11] The capital and [largest city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Libya "List of cities in Libya") is [Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripoli,_Libya "Tripoli, Libya"), located in the northwest and containing over a million of Libya's seven million people.[^12]

Libya has been inhabited by [Berbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_people "Berber people") since the late [Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") as descendants from [Iberomaurusian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberomaurusian "Iberomaurusian") and [Capsian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capsian "Capsian") cultures. In classical antiquity, the [Phoenicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia") established city-states and trading posts in western Libya, while several [Greek cities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrenaica "Cyrenaica") were established in the East. Parts of Libya were variously ruled by [Carthaginians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Carthage "Ancient Carthage"), [Numidians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numidia "Numidia"), [Persians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire"), and [Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemaic_Kingdom "Ptolemaic Kingdom") before the entire region became a part of the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire"). Libya was an [early centre of Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Christianity#Libya "Early Christianity"). After the [fall of the Western Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire "Fall of the Western Roman Empire"), the area of Libya was mostly occupied by the [Vandals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vandal_Kingdom "Vandal Kingdom") until the 7th century when [invasions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_the_Maghreb "Muslim conquest of the Maghreb") brought [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") to the region. From then on, centuries of [Arab migration to the Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_migration_to_the_Maghreb "Arab migration to the Maghreb") shifted the demographic scope of Libya in favour of [Arabs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabs "Arabs"). In the 16th century, the [Spanish Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Empire "Spanish Empire") and the [Knights Hospitaller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller "Knights Hospitaller") occupied Tripoli until [Ottoman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") rule [began in 1551](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Tripolitania "Ottoman Tripolitania"). Libya was involved in the [Barbary Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_Wars "Barbary Wars") of the 18th and 19th centuries. Ottoman rule continued until the [Italo-Turkish War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italo-Turkish_War "Italo-Turkish War") in 1911, which resulted in [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy "Kingdom of Italy") occupying Libya and establishing two [colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_colonization_of_Libya "Italian colonization of Libya"): [Italian Tripolitania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Tripolitania "Italian Tripolitania") and [Italian Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Cyrenaica "Italian Cyrenaica"), later unified in the [Italian Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Libya "Italian Libya") colony from 1934 to 1943.[^13]

During [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), Libya was an area of warfare in the [North African Campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_African_Campaign "North African Campaign"). The [Italian population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_settlers_in_Libya "Italian settlers in Libya") then went into decline and Libya became independent as a [kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Libya "Kingdom of Libya") in 1951. A [bloodless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonviolent_revolution "Nonviolent revolution") [military coup in 1969](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1969_Libyan_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1969 Libyan coup d'état"), initiated by a coalition led by Colonel [Muammar Gaddafi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi "Muammar Gaddafi"), overthrew [King Idris I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idris_of_Libya "Idris of Libya") and created a [republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic "Republic"). Gaddafi was often described by critics as a [dictator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictator "Dictator"), and was one of the world's longest serving non-royal leaders. He [ruled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Libya_under_Muammar_Gaddafi "History of Libya under Muammar Gaddafi") for 42 years until being overthrown and [killed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_of_Muammar_Gaddafi "Death of Muammar Gaddafi") in the [2011 civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Libyan_Civil_War "First Libyan Civil War"), which was part of the wider [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring"), with authority transferred to the [National Transitional Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Transitional_Council "National Transitional Council") then to the elected [General National Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_National_Congress "General National Congress").

Since 2011, Libya has been involved in a [political and humanitarian crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libyan_crisis "Libyan crisis"), and by 2014, two rival authorities claimed to govern Libya, which led to a [second civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Libyan_Civil_War "Second Libyan Civil War"), with parts of Libya split between separate governments, based in [Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripoli,_Libya "Tripoli, Libya") and [Tobruk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobruk "Tobruk"), as well as various tribal and [Islamist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamism "Islamism") militias. The two main warring sides signed a permanent ceasefire in 2020, and a [unity government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_National_Unity_\(Libya\) "Government of National Unity (Libya)") took authority to plan for democratic elections, though political rivalries continue to delay this.[^14] [^15] [^16] [^17] [^18] [^19] [^20] [^21] In March 2022, the [House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Representatives_\(Libya\) "House of Representatives (Libya)") ceased recognising the [Government of National Unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_National_Unity_\(Libya\) "Government of National Unity (Libya)") and proclaimed an alternative government, the [Government of National Stability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_National_Stability "Government of National Stability") (GNS). Both governments have been functioning simultaneously since then, which has led to dual power in Libya. The international community continues to recognise the unity government as the legitimate government of the country.

Libya is a developing country ranking 115th by HDI, and has the [10th-largest proven oil reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_proven_oil_reserves "List of countries by proven oil reserves") in the world. Libya has the highest level of greenhouse gas emissions per person in Africa, but has made little progress toward developing climate commitments. Libya is a member of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), the [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement"), the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union"), the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League"), the [Organisation of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation"), and [OPEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OPEC "OPEC"). The country's official religion is [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Libya "Islam in Libya"), with 96.6% of the Libyan population being [Sunni Muslims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunni_Islam "Sunni Islam").[^4] The official language of Libya is [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic "Modern Standard Arabic"), with vernacular [Libyan Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libyan_Arabic "Libyan Arabic") being spoken most widely. The majority of Libya's population is [Arab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabs "Arabs").[^22] [^23] [^24]

## Etymology

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Archaeological_Site_of_Sabratha-108976.jpg/250px-Archaeological_Site_of_Sabratha-108976.jpg)

Archaeological site of Sabratha, Libya

The origin of the name "Libya" first appeared in an inscription of [Ramesses II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramesses_II "Ramesses II"), written as *[rbw](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rbw "wikt:rbw")* in [hieroglyphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hieroglyphic "Hieroglyphic"). The name derives from a generalised identity given to a large confederacy of ancient east "Libyan" [Berbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berbers "Berbers"), [North African](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa") people(s) and tribes who lived around the lush regions of [Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrenaica "Cyrenaica") and [Marmarica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marmarica "Marmarica"). An army of 40,000 men [^25] and a confederacy of tribes known as "Great Chiefs of the [Libu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libu "Libu") " were led by King [Meryey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meryey "Meryey") who fought a war against [pharaoh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharaoh "Pharaoh") [Merneptah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merneptah "Merneptah") in year 5 (1208 BC). This conflict was mentioned in the [Great Karnak Inscription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Karnak_Inscription "Great Karnak Inscription") in the western delta during the 5th and 6th years of his reign and resulted in [a defeat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Perire "Battle of Perire") for Meryey. According to the [Great Karnak Inscription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Karnak_Inscription "Great Karnak Inscription"), the military alliance comprised the [Meshwesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meshwesh "Meshwesh"), the [Lukka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lukka "Lukka"), and the "Sea Peoples" known as the [Ekwesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ekwesh "Ekwesh"), [Teresh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_Peoples "Sea Peoples"), [Shekelesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shekelesh "Shekelesh"), and the [Sherden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sherden "Sherden").

The Great Karnak Inscription reads:

> "... the third season, saying: 'The wretched, fallen chief of Libya, Meryey, son of Ded, has fallen upon the country of Tehenu with his bowmen—Sherden, Shekelesh, Ekwesh, Lukka, Teresh. Taking the best of every warrior and every man of war of his country. He has brought his wife and his children—leaders of the camp, and he has reached the western boundary in the fields of Perire."

The name "Libya" was brought back into use in 1903 by Italian geographer Federico Minutilli. It was intended to supplant terms applied to [Ottoman Tripolitania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Tripolitania "Ottoman Tripolitania"), the coastal region of what is today Libya, having been ruled by the Ottoman Empire from 1551 to 1911 as the Eyalet of Tripolitania.[^26]

Libya gained independence in 1951 as the [United Libyan Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Libya "Kingdom of Libya") (المملكة الليبية المتحدة **al-Mamlakah al-Lībiyyah al-Muttaḥidah**), changing its name to the Kingdom of Libya (المملكة الليبية **al-Mamlakah al-Lībiyyah**), literally "Libyan Kingdom", in 1963.[^27] Following a coup d'état led by [Muammar Gaddafi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi "Muammar Gaddafi") in 1969, the name of the state was changed to the Libyan Arab Republic (الجمهورية العربية الليبية **al-Jumhūriyyah al-'Arabiyyah al-Lībiyyah**). The official name was "Socialist People's Libyan Arab Jamahiriya" from 1977 to 1986 (الجماهيرية العربية الليبية الشعبية الاشتراكية), and "Great Socialist People's Libyan Arab Jamahiriya" [^28] (الجماهيرية العربية الليبية الشعبية الاشتراكية العظمى,[^29] **al-Jamāhīriyyah al-'Arabiyyah al-Lībiyyah ash-Sha'biyyah al-Ishtirākiyyah al-'Udmá** [listen](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/2/2a/Al-Jamahiriyyah_al-Arabiyyah_al-Libiyyah_ash-shabiyyah_al-Ishtirakiyyah_al-Udhma.ogg/Al-Jamahiriyyah_al-Arabiyyah_al-Libiyyah_ash-shabiyyah_al-Ishtirakiyyah_al-Udhma.ogg.mp3 "Play audio")) from 1986 to 2011.

The [National Transitional Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Transitional_Council "National Transitional Council"), established in 2011, referred to the state as simply "Libya". The [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") (UN) formally recognised the country as "Libya" in September 2011 [^30] based on a request from the Permanent Mission of Libya citing the [Libyan interim Constitutional Declaration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libyan_interim_Constitutional_Declaration "Libyan interim Constitutional Declaration") of 3 August 2011. In November 2011, the [ISO 3166-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_3166-1 "ISO 3166-1") was altered to reflect the new country name "Libya" in [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language"), *"Libye (la)"* in [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language").[^31]

In December 2017, the Permanent Mission of Libya to the United Nations informed the UN that the country's official name was henceforth the "State of Libya"; "Libya" remained the official short form, and the country continued to be listed under "L" in alphabetical lists.[^32]

## History

### Ancient Libya

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Leptis_Magna_Arch_of_Septimius_Severus.jpg/250px-Leptis_Magna_Arch_of_Septimius_Severus.jpg)

Leptis Magna

The coastal plain of Libya was inhabited by [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") peoples from as early as 8000 BC. The [Afroasiatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages "Afroasiatic languages") ancestors of the [Berber people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_people "Berber people") are assumed to have spread into the area by the [Late Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age "Late Bronze Age"). The earliest known name of such a tribe was the [Garamantes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garamantes "Garamantes"), based in [Germa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germa "Germa"). The [Phoenicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia") were the first to establish trading posts in Libya.[^33] By the 5th century BC, the greatest of the Phoenician colonies, [Carthage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carthage "Carthage"), had extended its [hegemony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hegemony "Hegemony") across much of North Africa, where a distinctive civilisation, known as [Punic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punic "Punic"), came into being.

In 630 BC, the [ancient Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") colonised the area around [Barca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barca_\(ancient_city\) "Barca (ancient city)") in Eastern Libya and founded the city of [Cyrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrene,_Libya "Cyrene, Libya").[^34] Within 200 years, four more important Greek cities were established in the area that became known as [Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrenaica "Cyrenaica").[^35] The area was home to the renowned philosophy school of the [Cyrenaics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrenaics "Cyrenaics"). In 525 BC the [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_people "Persian people") army of [Cambyses II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambyses_II "Cambyses II") overran Cyrenaica, which for the next two centuries remained under Persian or Egyptian rule. [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") ended Persian rule in 331 BC and received tribute from Cyrenaica. Eastern Libya again fell under the control of the Greeks, this time as part of the [Ptolemaic Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemaic_Kingdom "Ptolemaic Kingdom").

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Roman_emperor_Septimius_Severus%2C_200-11%3B_Altes_Museum%2C_Berlin_%2826307681078%29.jpg/250px-Roman_emperor_Septimius_Severus%2C_200-11%3B_Altes_Museum%2C_Berlin_%2826307681078%29.jpg)

Septimius Severus, the first Roman emperor native to Roman Africa, was born in Leptis Magna.

After the fall of [Carthage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carthage "Carthage") the Romans did not immediately occupy [Tripolitania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripolitania "Tripolitania") (the region around Tripoli), but left it instead under control of the kings of [Numidia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numidia "Numidia"), until the coastal cities asked and obtained its protection.[^36] [Ptolemy Apion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy_Apion "Ptolemy Apion"), the last Greek ruler, bequeathed Cyrenaica to Rome, which formally annexed the region in 74 BC and joined it to Crete as [a Roman province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creta_et_Cyrenaica "Creta et Cyrenaica"). As part of the [Africa Nova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa_Nova "Africa Nova") province, Tripolitania was prosperous,[^36] and reached a golden age in the 2nd and 3rd centuries, when the city of [Leptis Magna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leptis_Magna "Leptis Magna"), home to the [Severan dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Severan_dynasty "Severan dynasty"), was at its height.[^36]

On the eastern side, Cyrenaica's first Christian communities were established by the time of the Emperor [Claudius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudius "Claudius").[^37] It was heavily devastated during the [Kitos War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kitos_War "Kitos War") [^38] and almost depopulated of Greeks and Jews alike.[^39] Although repopulated by [Trajan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajan "Trajan") with military colonies,[^38] from then started its decline.[^37] Libya was early to convert to [Nicene Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicene_Christianity "Nicene Christianity") and was the home of [Pope Victor I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Victor_I "Pope Victor I"); however, Libya was also home to many non-Nicene varieties of early Christianity, such as [Arianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arianism "Arianism") and [Donatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donatism "Donatism").

### Islamic Libya: 647–1556

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Awjila_%28Libia%29_-_The_Mosque_of_Atiq.jpg/250px-Awjila_%28Libia%29_-_The_Mosque_of_Atiq.jpg)

The Atiq Mosque in Awjila is the oldest mosque in the Sahara.

Under the command of [Amr ibn al-As](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amr_ibn_al-As "Amr ibn al-As"), the [Rashidun army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashidun_army "Rashidun army") conquered [Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrenaica "Cyrenaica").[^40] In 647 an army led by [Abdullah ibn Saad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdullah_ibn_Saad "Abdullah ibn Saad") took Tripoli from the Byzantines definitively.[^40] The [Fezzan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fezzan "Fezzan") was conquered by [Uqba ibn Nafi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uqba_ibn_Nafi "Uqba ibn Nafi") in 663. The Berber tribes of the hinterland accepted Islam, however they resisted Arab political rule.[^41] For the next several decades, Libya was under the purview of the [Umayyad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") [Caliph of Damascus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caliph_of_Damascus "Caliph of Damascus") until the [Abbasids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate") overthrew the Umayyads in 750, and Libya came under the rule of Baghdad. When Caliph [Harun al-Rashid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harun_al-Rashid "Harun al-Rashid") appointed [Ibrahim ibn al-Aghlab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibrahim_I_ibn_al-Aghlab "Ibrahim I ibn al-Aghlab") as his governor of [Ifriqiya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ifriqiya "Ifriqiya") in 800, Libya enjoyed considerable local autonomy under the [Aghlabid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aghlabid "Aghlabid") dynasty. By the 10th century, the Shiite [Fatimids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimids "Fatimids") controlled Western Libya, and ruled the entire region in 972 and appointed [Bologhine ibn Ziri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bologhine_ibn_Ziri "Bologhine ibn Ziri") as governor.[^36]

Ibn Ziri's Berber [Zirid dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zirid_dynasty "Zirid dynasty") ultimately broke away from the Shiite Fatimids, and recognised the Sunni Abbasids of Baghdad as rightful Caliphs. In retaliation, the Fatimids brought about the migration of thousands from mainly two Arab Qaisi tribes, the [Banu Sulaym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banu_Sulaym "Banu Sulaym") and [Banu Hilal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banu_Hilal "Banu Hilal") to [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa"). This act drastically altered the fabric of the Libyan countryside, and cemented the cultural and linguistic Arabisation of the region.[^36]

Zirid rule in Tripolitania was short-lived though, and already in 1001 the Berbers of the [Banu Khazrun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banu_Khazrun "Banu Khazrun") broke away. Tripolitania remained under their control until 1146, when the region was overtaken by the [Normans of Sicily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normans_of_Sicily "Normans of Sicily").[^42] For the next 50 years, Tripolitania was the scene of numerous battles among [Ayyubids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayyubid_dynasty "Ayyubid dynasty"), the [Almohad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almohad_Caliphate "Almohad Caliphate") rulers and insurgents of the [Banu Ghaniya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banu_Ghaniya "Banu Ghaniya"). Later, a general of the Almohads, Muhammad ibn Abu Hafs, ruled Libya from 1207 to 1221 before the later establishment of the Tunisian [Hafsid Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hafsid_dynasty "Hafsid dynasty") [^42] independent from the Almohads. In the 14th century, the [Banu Thabit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banu_Thabit "Banu Thabit") dynasty ruled Tripolitania before reverting to direct Hafsid control. By the 16th century, the Hafsids became increasingly caught up in the power struggle between Spain and the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire").

After Abbasid control was weakened, Cyrenaica was under Egypt-based states such as the [Tulunids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulunids "Tulunids"), [Ikhshidids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ikhshidid_dynasty "Ikhshidid dynasty"), Ayyubids, and [Mamluks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mamluk_Sultanate "Mamluk Sultanate") before Ottoman conquest in 1517. [Fezzan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fezzan "Fezzan") acquired independence under Awlad Muhammad dynasty after [Kanem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanem%E2%80%93Bornu_Empire "Kanem–Bornu Empire") rule. Ottomans finally conquered Fezzan between 1556 and 1577.

### Ottoman Tripolitania: 1556–1911

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Capture_of_Tripoli_by_the_Ottomans_1551.jpg/250px-Capture_of_Tripoli_by_the_Ottomans_1551.jpg)

The siege of Tripoli in 1551 allowed the Ottomans to capture the city from the Knights of St. John.

After a successful invasion of Tripoli by [Habsburg Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habsburg_Spain "Habsburg Spain") in 1510,[^42] and its handover to the [Knights Hospitaller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller "Knights Hospitaller"), the [Ottoman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") admiral [Sinan Pasha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinan_Pasha_\(Ottoman_admiral\) "Sinan Pasha (Ottoman admiral)") took control of Libya in 1551.[^42] His successor [Turgut Reis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turgut_Reis "Turgut Reis") was named the [Bey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bey "Bey") of Tripoli and later [Pasha of Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasha_of_Tripoli "Pasha of Tripoli") in 1556. By 1565, administrative authority as regent in Tripoli was vested in a *[pasha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasha "Pasha")* appointed directly by the *[sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan "Sultan")* in [Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantinople "Constantinople") / [Istanbul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Istanbul "Istanbul"). In the 1580s, the rulers of [Fezzan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fezzan "Fezzan") gave their allegiance to the sultan, and although Ottoman authority was absent in [Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrenaica "Cyrenaica"), a *bey* was stationed in Benghazi late in the next century to act as agent of the government in Tripoli.[^37] European [slaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_slave_trade "Barbary slave trade") and large numbers of enslaved Blacks transported from Sudan were also a feature of everyday life in Tripoli. In 1551, [Turgut Reis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turgut_Reis "Turgut Reis") enslaved almost the entire population of the Maltese island of [Gozo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gozo "Gozo"), some 5,000 people, sending them to Libya.[^43] [^44]

In time, real power came to rest with the pasha's corps of [janissaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janissaries "Janissaries").[^42] In 1611 the *[deys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dey "Dey")* staged a coup against the pasha, and Dey Sulayman Safar was appointed as head of government. For the next hundred years, a series of *deys* effectively ruled Tripolitania. The two most important Deys were [Mehmed Saqizli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mehmed_Saqizli "Mehmed Saqizli") (r. 1631–49) and [Osman Saqizli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osman_Saqizli "Osman Saqizli") (r. 1649–72), both also Pasha, who ruled effectively the region.[^45] The latter conquered also Cyrenaica.[^45]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/La_citt%C3%A0_di_Tripoli%2C_capitale_del_regno_dello_stesso_nome_sulla_costa_di_Barbaria%2C_nell%27Africa%2C_1766_%E2%80%93_BEIC_IE8943210.jpg/250px-La_citt%C3%A0_di_Tripoli%2C_capitale_del_regno_dello_stesso_nome_sulla_costa_di_Barbaria%2C_nell%27Africa%2C_1766_%E2%80%93_BEIC_IE8943210.jpg)

A view of Tripoli, c. 1766

Lacking direction from the Ottoman government, Tripoli lapsed into a period of military anarchy during which coup followed coup and few deys survived in office more than a year. One such coup was led by Turkish officer [Ahmed Karamanli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmed_Karamanli "Ahmed Karamanli").[^45] The [Karamanlis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karamanli_dynasty "Karamanli dynasty") ruled from 1711 until 1835 mainly in Tripolitania, and had influence in Cyrenaica and Fezzan as well by the mid-18th century. Ahmed's successors proved to be less capable than himself, however, the region's delicate balance of power allowed the Karamanli. The [1793–95 Tripolitanian civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1793%E2%80%9395_Tripolitanian_civil_war "1793–95 Tripolitanian civil war") occurred in those years. In 1793, Turkish officer [Ali Pasha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trabluslu_Ali_Pasha "Trabluslu Ali Pasha") deposed Hamet Karamanli and briefly restored Tripolitania to Ottoman rule. Hamet's brother [Yusuf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yusuf_Karamanli "Yusuf Karamanli") (r. 1795–1832) re-established Tripolitania's independence. In 1816, Yusuf ordered the [massacre of the al-Jawazi tribe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Jawazi_massacre "Al-Jawazi massacre").[^46]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Decatur_Boarding_the_Tripolitan_Gunboat.jpg/250px-Decatur_Boarding_the_Tripolitan_Gunboat.jpg)

U.S. naval officer Stephen Decatur boarding a Tripolitan gunboat during the First Barbary War, 1804

In the early 19th century war broke out between the United States and Tripolitania, and a series of battles ensued in what came to be known as the [First Barbary War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Barbary_War "First Barbary War") and the [Second Barbary War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Barbary_War "Second Barbary War"). By 1819, the various treaties of the [Napoleonic Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars "Napoleonic Wars") had forced the Barbary states to give up piracy almost entirely, and Tripolitania's economy began to crumble. As Yusuf weakened, factions sprung up around his three sons. Civil war soon resulted.[^47]

Ottoman Sultan [Mahmud II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahmud_II "Mahmud II") sent in troops ostensibly to restore order, marking the end of both the Karamanli dynasty and an independent Tripolitania.[^47] Order was not recovered easily, and the revolt of the Libyan under Abd-El-Gelil and Gûma ben Khalifa lasted until the death of the latter in 1858.[^47] The second period of direct Ottoman rule saw administrative changes, and greater order in the governance of the three provinces of Libya. Ottoman rule finally reasserted to Fezzan between 1850 and 1875 for earning income from Saharan commerce.

### Italian colonisation and Allied occupation (1911–1951)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Omar_Mukhtar_13.jpg/250px-Omar_Mukhtar_13.jpg)

Omar Mukhtar was a prominent leader of Libyan resistance in Cyrenaica against Italian colonisation.

After the [Italo-Turkish War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italo-Turkish_War "Italo-Turkish War") (1911–1912), Italy simultaneously turned the three regions into colonies.[^48] From 1912 to 1927, the territory of Libya was known as [Italian North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Libya "Italian Libya"). From 1927 to 1934, the territory was split into two colonies, [Italian Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Cyrenaica "Italian Cyrenaica") and [Italian Tripolitania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Tripolitania "Italian Tripolitania"), run by Italian governors. Some 150,000 Italians settled in Libya, constituting roughly 20% of the total population.[^49]

[Omar Mukhtar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omar_Mukhtar "Omar Mukhtar") rose to prominence as a [resistance leader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libyan_resistance_movement "Libyan resistance movement") against Italian colonisation and became a national hero despite his capture and execution on 16 September 1931.[^50] His face is currently printed on the Libyan ten dinar note in memory and recognition of his patriotism. Another prominent resistance leader, Idris al-Mahdi as-Senussi (later [King Idris I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idris_of_Libya "Idris of Libya")), Emir of Cyrenaica, continued to lead the Libyan resistance until the outbreak of the [Second World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II").

The so-called " [pacification of Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacification_of_Libya "Pacification of Libya") " by the Italians resulted in mass deaths of the indigenous people in Cyrenaica, killing approximately one quarter of Cyrenaica's population of 225,000.[^51] [Ilan Pappé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ilan_Papp%C3%A9 "Ilan Pappé") estimates that between 1928 and 1932 the Italian military "killed half the Bedouin population (directly or through disease and starvation in [Italian concentration camps in Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_concentration_camps_in_Libya "Italian concentration camps in Libya"))." [^52]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Cartolina_Giovanni_Pastorelli.jpg/250px-Cartolina_Giovanni_Pastorelli.jpg)

Italian propaganda postcard depicting the Italian invasion of Libya in 1911

In 1934, Italy combined [Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrenaica "Cyrenaica"), [Tripolitania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripolitania "Tripolitania") and [Fezzan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fezzan "Fezzan") and adopted the name "Libya" (used by the [Ancient Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") for all of [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa") except [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt")) for the unified colony, with [Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripoli,_Libya "Tripoli, Libya") as its [capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_city "Capital city").[^53] The Italians emphasised infrastructure improvements and public works. In particular, they greatly expanded Libyan railway and road networks from 1934 to 1940, building hundreds of kilometres of new roads and railways and encouraging the establishment of new industries and dozens of new agricultural villages. In 1939, the coastal areas of Libya began to be administered as an integral part of Italy known as the [Fourth Shore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_Shore "Fourth Shore").

In June 1940, [Italy entered World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_history_of_Italy_during_World_War_II "Military history of Italy during World War II"). Libya became the setting for the hard-fought [North African Campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_African_Campaign "North African Campaign") that ultimately ended in defeat for Italy and its [German ally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afrika_Korps "Afrika Korps") in 1943. From 1943 to 1951, Libya was under [Allied occupation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allied_occupation_of_Libya "Allied occupation of Libya"). The British military [administered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Military_Administration_\(Libya\) "British Military Administration (Libya)") the two former Italian Libyan provinces of Tripolitana and Cyrenaïca, while the French [administered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Fezzan "French Fezzan") the province of Fezzan. In 1944, Idris returned from exile in [Cairo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cairo "Cairo") but declined to resume permanent residence in Cyrenaica until the removal of some aspects of foreign control in 1947. Under the terms of the [1947 peace treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Peace_with_Italy,_1947 "Treaty of Peace with Italy, 1947") with the [Allies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II "Allies of World War II"), Italy relinquished all claims to Libya.[^54]

### Independent Kingdom (1951–1969)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/IdrisI3.jpg/250px-IdrisI3.jpg)

King Idris of Libya

A national assembly crafted a constitution that established a [monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy "Monarchy") and extended an offer for the throne to [Sayyid Idris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idris_of_Libya "Idris of Libya"), the [Emir of Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emir_of_Cyrenaica "Emir of Cyrenaica").[^55] Sayyid Idris held the esteemed position as the leader of the influential [Senussi religious brotherhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senusiyya "Senusiyya"), which was founded by his grandfather in the preceding century as a response to Western influence in the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world").[^55] This devout Islamic movement garnered significant support from the [desert Bedouin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bedouin "Bedouin") and became a major political force in Libya. During the declining years of the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"), it effectively governed the Libyan interior.[^55] Born in an oasis in [Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrenaica "Cyrenaica") in 1890, Sayyid Idris assumed leadership of the Senussi at a young age. He spent a considerable period of exile in [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") under Italian rule and returned to Libya after the [Axis powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_powers "Axis powers") were ousted in 1943.[^55] On 24 December 1951, as King Idris I, he addressed the nation via radio from [Benghazi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benghazi "Benghazi").[^55] On 21 November 1949, the [UN General Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly "United Nations General Assembly") passed a resolution stating that Libya should become independent before 1 January 1952.[^56] Idris represented Libya in the subsequent UN negotiations. By 24 December 1951, Libya declared its independence as the [United Kingdom of Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Libya "Kingdom of Libya"), a constitutional and hereditary [monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy "Monarchy") under King [Idris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idris_I_of_Libya "Idris I of Libya").[^57]

However, the new kingdom faced challenging prospects.[^55] It lacked significant industry and agricultural resources.[^55] The kingdom's primary exports consisted of hides, wool, horses, and ostrich feathers.[^55] It had one of the lowest per capita incomes in the world and also one of the highest illiteracy rates.[^55] King Idris I, already in his sixties, had no direct heir to succeed him.[^55] His cousin, whom he had married in 1932, reportedly experienced numerous miscarriages, and their son, born in 1953, tragically died shortly after birth.[^55] Crown Prince Rida, Idris's brother, was the designated heir, but the royal family was riddled with incessant disputes.[^55] King Idris's devout Muslim piety, which solidified his support among the [Bedouin population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bedouin "Bedouin"), clashed with the modernising and urban intellectual currents in Libya.[^55] To address the rivalry between [Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrenaica "Cyrenaica") and [Tripolitania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripolitania "Tripolitania"), [Benghazi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benghazi "Benghazi") and [Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripoli,_Libya "Tripoli, Libya") alternated as the capital every two years.

The swift emergence of a large number of bureaucrats resulted in a costly royal government.[^55] The discovery of significant [oil reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_reserves "Oil reserves") in 1959 and the subsequent income from [petroleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum") sales enabled one of the world's poorest nations to establish an extremely wealthy state.[^57] Although oil drastically improved the Libyan government's finances, popular resentment began to build over the increased concentration of the nation's wealth in the hands of King Idris and the national elite.[^56] [^57] This discontent continued to mount with the rise of [Nasserism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasserism "Nasserism") and [Arab nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_nationalism "Arab nationalism") throughout [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa") and the [Middle East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_East "Middle East"), that inspire many anti-royal coups across the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world").[^57]

### Libya under the Gaddafi regime (1969–2011)

On 1 September 1969, a group of rebel military officers led by [Muammar Gaddafi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi "Muammar Gaddafi") launched a [coup d'état against King Idris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1969_Libyan_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1969 Libyan coup d'état"), which became known as the Al Fateh Revolution.[^58] [^59] Gaddafi was referred to as the " [Brother Leader and Guide of the Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brotherly_Leader_and_Guide_of_the_Revolution "Brotherly Leader and Guide of the Revolution") " in government statements and the official Libyan press.[^60] [^59] He began dominating history and politics of Libya for the next four decades.[^59] Moving to reduce Italian influence, in October 1970 all Italian-owned assets were expropriated and the 12,000-strong [Italian community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_settlers_in_Libya "Italian settlers in Libya") was [expelled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1970_expulsion_of_Italians_from_Libya "1970 expulsion of Italians from Libya") from Libya alongside the smaller community of [Italian Libyan Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Libya "History of the Jews in Libya").[^59] The day became a [national holiday](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_holiday "Public holiday") known as " [Day of Revenge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Day_of_Revenge "Day of Revenge") ";[^59] it was later renamed the "Day of Friendship" because of an improvement in [Italy–Libya relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy%E2%80%93Libya_relations "Italy–Libya relations").[^61]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Gaddafi_1972.jpg/250px-Gaddafi_1972.jpg)

Muammar Gaddafi, leader of Libya ( r. 1969–2011 )

Libya's increase in prosperity was accompanied by increased internal political repression, and political dissent was made illegal under Law 75 of 1973.[^62] Widespread surveillance of the population was carried out through Gaddafi's Revolutionary Committees.[^63] [^62] [^64] Gaddafi also wanted to ease the strict social restrictions imposed on women by the previous regime, establishing the [Revolutionary Women's Formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutionary_Women%27s_Formation "Revolutionary Women's Formation") to encourage reform.[^62] In 1970, a law was introduced affirming equality of the sexes and wage parity.[^62] In 1971, Gaddafi sponsored the creation of a Libyan General Women's Federation.[^62] In 1972, a law was passed criminalising the marriage of girls under the age of sixteen and making the woman's consent a necessary prerequisite for a marriage.[^65]

On 25 October 1975, a coup attempt was launched by a group of 20 military officers, mostly from the city of [Misrata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Misrata "Misrata").[^66] This resulted in the arrest and executions of the coup plotters.[^67] In March 1977, Libya officially became the "Socialist People's Libyan Arab Jamahiriya".[^62] Gaddafi officially passed power to the [General People's Committees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_People%27s_Committee "General People's Committee") and henceforth claimed to be no more than a symbolic figurehead.[^68] [^62] The new *jamahiriya* (Arab for "republic") governance structure he established was officially referred to as " [direct democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracy "Direct democracy") ".[^69] Gaddafi, in his vision of democratic government and [political philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_philosophy "Political philosophy"), published *[The Green Book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Green_Book_\(Gaddafi\) "The Green Book (Gaddafi)")* in 1975. His short book inscribed a representative mix of utopian socialism and Arab nationalism with a streak of [Bedouin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bedouin "Bedouin") supremacy.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/F-4J_of_VF-74_with_Libyan_MiG-23_over_Gulf_of_Sidra_1981.jpg/250px-F-4J_of_VF-74_with_Libyan_MiG-23_over_Gulf_of_Sidra_1981.jpg)

F-4J of VF-74 with Libyan MiG-23 over Gulf of Sidra in 1981

In February 1977, Libya started delivering military supplies to [Goukouni Oueddei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goukouni_Oueddei "Goukouni Oueddei") and the [People's Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Armed_Forces "People's Armed Forces") in Chad.[^62] The [Chadian–Libyan War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chadian%E2%80%93Libyan_War "Chadian–Libyan War") began in earnest when Libya's support of rebel forces in northern Chad escalated into an [invasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toyota_war "Toyota war").[^62] Later that same year, Libya and [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") fought a four-day border war that came to be known as the [Egyptian–Libyan War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian%E2%80%93Libyan_War "Egyptian–Libyan War").[^62] Both nations agreed to a [ceasefire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceasefire "Ceasefire") under the mediation of the Algerian president [Houari Boumédiène](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houari_Boum%C3%A9di%C3%A8ne "Houari Boumédiène").[^70] Hundreds of Libyans lost their lives in the country's support for [Idi Amin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idi_Amin "Idi Amin") 's [Uganda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uganda "Uganda") in [its war against Tanzania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uganda%E2%80%93Tanzania_War "Uganda–Tanzania War").[^62] Gaddafi financed various other groups from anti-nuclear movements to [Australian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia") trade unions.[^71] On 2 March 1977, Libya officially became the [Great Socialist People's Libyan Arab Jamahiriya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Libya_under_Muammar_Gaddafi#Great_Socialist_People's_Libyan_Arab_Jamahiriya_\(1977%E2%80%932011\) "History of Libya under Muammar Gaddafi").[^72]

Libya adopted its plain green national flag on 19 November 1977.[^62] The country had the only plain-coloured flag in the world until 2011, when Libya adopted [its current flag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_Libya "Flag of Libya"). From 1977 onward, per capita income in the country rose to more than US$11,000, the fifth-highest in Africa,[^73] while the [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index") became the highest in Africa and greater than that of [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia").[^74] This was achieved without borrowing any foreign loans, keeping Libya [debt-free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states_by_public_debt "List of sovereign states by public debt").[^75] The [Great Manmade River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Manmade_River "Great Manmade River") was also built to allow free access to fresh water across large parts of the country.[^74] In addition, financial support was provided for university scholarships and employment programs.[^76] Much of Libya's income from oil, which soared in the 1970s, was spent on arms purchases and on sponsoring dozens of paramilitaries and terrorist groups around the world.[^77] [^78] [^79]

[An American airstrike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1986_United_States_bombing_of_Libya "1986 United States bombing of Libya") led by then U.S. president [Ronald Reagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Reagan "Ronald Reagan") intended to kill Gaddafi failed in 1986.[^62] Libya was finally put under sanctions by the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") after [the bombing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockerbie_bombing "Lockerbie bombing") of a commercial flight at [Lockerbie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockerbie "Lockerbie") in 1988 killed 270 people.[^80] In the 1990s, the government's rule was threatened by militant Islamism and an unsuccessful attempt to assassinate Gaddafi.[^81] The government responded with repressive measures.[^81] Riots and Islamic activisim were crushed by [Revolutionary Guard Corps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutionary_Guard_Corps_\(Libyan_paramilitary_unit\) "Revolutionary Guard Corps (Libyan paramilitary unit)").[^81] Nevertheless, [Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrenaica "Cyrenaica") between 1995 and 1998 was politically unstable, due to the tribal allegiances of the local troops.[^81] In 2003, Gaddafi announced that all of his regime's [weapons of mass destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction "Libya and weapons of mass destruction") were disassembled, and that Libya was transitioning toward [nuclear power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power "Nuclear power").[^62]

### First Libyan Civil War and the fall of Gaddafi (2011)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Ambassador_Cretz_Stands_by_Fist_Crushing_a_U.S._Fighter_Plane_Sculpture.jpg/250px-Ambassador_Cretz_Stands_by_Fist_Crushing_a_U.S._Fighter_Plane_Sculpture.jpg)

U.S. Ambassador Cretz Stands by Fist Crushing a US Fighter Plane Sculpture which was captured after the fall of Tripoli

The first civil war came during the [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring") movements which overturned the rulers of [Tunisia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunisia "Tunisia") and [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt"). Libya first experienced protests against Gaddafi's regime on 15 February 2011, with a full-scale revolt beginning on [17 February](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolution_Day "Revolution Day").[^82] Libya's authoritarian regime led by Muammar Gaddafi put up much more of a resistance compared to the regimes in Egypt and Tunisia. While overthrowing the regimes in Egypt and Tunisia was a relatively quick process, Gaddafi's campaign posed significant stalls on the uprising in Libya.[^83] The first announcement of a competing political authority appeared online and declared the [Interim Transitional National Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interim_Transitional_National_Council "Interim Transitional National Council") as an alternative government. One of Gaddafi's senior advisors responded by posting a tweet, wherein he resigned, defected, and advised Gaddafi to flee.[^84] By 20 February, the unrest had spread to [Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripoli,_Libya "Tripoli, Libya"). On 27 February 2011, the [National Transitional Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Transitional_Council "National Transitional Council") was established to administer the areas of Libya under rebel control. On 10 March 2011, the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and many other nations recognised the council headed by [Mahmoud Jibril](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahmoud_Jibril "Mahmoud Jibril") as acting prime minister and as the legitimate representative of the Libyan people and withdrawing the recognition of Gaddafi's regime.[^85] [^86]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Green_square_protests_in_Tripoli%2C_Libya.jpg/250px-Green_square_protests_in_Tripoli%2C_Libya.jpg)

A protest against the anti-Gaddafi supporters in Tripoli

Pro-Gaddafi forces were able to respond militarily to rebel pushes in [Western Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripolitania "Tripolitania") and launched a counterattack along the coast toward Benghazi, the *de facto* centre of the uprising.[^87] The town of [Zawiya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zawiya,_Libya "Zawiya, Libya"), 48 kilometres (30 mi) from Tripoli, was bombarded by [air force planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libyan_Air_Force_\(1951%E2%80%932011\) "Libyan Air Force (1951–2011)") and army tanks and seized by [Jamahiriya troops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_Libyan_Arab_Jamahiriya "Armed Forces of the Libyan Arab Jamahiriya"), "exercising a level of brutality not yet seen in the conflict." [^88] Organizations of the United Nations, including [United Nations Secretary General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Secretary_General "United Nations Secretary General") [Ban Ki-moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon "Ban Ki-moon") [^89] and the [United Nations Human Rights Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council "United Nations Human Rights Council"), condemned the crackdown as violating international law, with the latter body expelling Libya outright in an unprecedented action.[^90] [^91] On 17 March 2011, the UN Security Council passed [Resolution 1973](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_1973 "United Nations Security Council Resolution 1973"),[^92] with a 10–0 vote and five abstentions including [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia"), [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China"), [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"), [Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil "Brazil") and [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany"). The resolution sanctioned the establishment of a [no-fly zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No-fly_zone "No-fly zone") and the use of "all means necessary" to protect civilians within Libya.[^93] On 19 March, the first act of [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") allies to secure the no-fly zone began by destroying Libyan air defences when French military jets entered Libyan airspace on a [reconnaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reconnaissance "Reconnaissance") mission heralding attacks on enemy targets.[^94]

In the weeks that followed, U.S. American forces were in the forefront of NATO operations against Libya. More than 8,000 US personnel in warships and aircraft were deployed in the area. At least 3,000 targets were struck in 14,202 strike sorties, 716 of them in Tripoli and 492 in [Brega](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brega "Brega").[^95] The U.S. air offensive included flights of [B-2 stealth bombers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northrop_B-2_Spirit "Northrop B-2 Spirit"), each bomber armed with sixteen 2000-pound bombs, flying out of and returning to their base in [Missouri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Missouri "Missouri").[^96] The support provided by the NATO air forces contributed to the ultimate success of the revolution.[^97] By 22 August 2011, [rebel fighters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Liberation_Army_\(Libya\) "National Liberation Army (Libya)") had entered Tripoli and occupied [Green Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martyrs%27_Square,_Tripoli "Martyrs' Square, Tripoli"),[^98] which they renamed Martyrs' Square in honour of those killed since 17 February 2011. On 20 October 2011, the last heavy fighting of the uprising came to an end in the city of [Sirte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirte "Sirte"). The [Battle of Sirte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Sirte_\(2011\) "Battle of Sirte (2011)") was both the last decisive battle and the last one in general of the [First Libyan Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Libyan_Civil_War "First Libyan Civil War") where Gaddafi was captured and killed by NATO-backed forces on 20 October 2011. Sirte was the last Gaddafi loyalist stronghold and his place of birth. The defeat of [loyalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loyalist "Loyalist") forces was celebrated on 23 October 2011, three days after the fall of Sirte. At least 30,000 Libyans died in the civil war.[^99] In addition, the [National Transitional Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Transitional_Council "National Transitional Council") estimated 50,000 wounded.[^100]

### Interwar period and the Second Libyan Civil War (2011–2020)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Libyan_Civil_War.svg/250px-Libyan_Civil_War.svg.png)

Areas of control in the Civil War, updated 11 June 2020: Tobruk-led Government Government of National Accord Petroleum Facilities Guard Tuareg tribes Local forces

Following the defeat of loyalist forces, Libya was torn among numerous rival, armed militias affiliated with distinct regions, cities and tribes, while the central government had been weak and unable to effectively exert its authority over the country. Competing militias pitted themselves against each other in a political struggle between [Islamist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamism "Islamism") politicians and their opponents.[^101] On 7 July 2012, Libyans held their first parliamentary elections since the end of the former regime. On 8 August, the [National Transitional Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Transitional_Council "National Transitional Council") officially handed power over to the wholly elected [General National Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_National_Congress "General National Congress"), which was then tasked with the formation of an interim government and the drafting of a new [Libyan Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libyan_interim_Constitutional_Declaration "Libyan interim Constitutional Declaration") to be approved in a general [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Next_Libyan_referendum "Next Libyan referendum").[^102] On 25 August 2012, in what Reuters reported as "the most blatant sectarian attack" since the end of the civil war, unnamed organised assailants bulldozed a [Sufi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sufism "Sufism") mosque with graves in the centre of the Libyan capital [Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripoli,_Libya "Tripoli, Libya"). It was the second such razing of a Sufi site in two days.[^103] Numerous acts of vandalism and destruction of heritage were carried out by suspected Islamist militias, including the removal of the Nude Gazelle Statue and the destruction and desecration of [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") –era British grave sites near [Benghazi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benghazi "Benghazi").[^104] [^105] Many other cases of heritage vandalism were reported to be carried out by Islamist-related radical militias and mobs that either destroyed, robbed, or looted a number of historic sites.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/LE_Eithne_Operation_Triton.jpg/250px-LE_Eithne_Operation_Triton.jpg)

Libya has emerged as a major transit point for people trying to reach Europe.

On 11 September 2012, Islamist militants mounted an [attack on the American diplomatic compound in Benghazi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Benghazi_attack "2012 Benghazi attack"),[^106] killing the U.S. ambassador to Libya, [J. Christopher Stevens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._Christopher_Stevens "J. Christopher Stevens"), and three others. The incident generated outrage in the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and Libya.[^107] On 7 October 2012, Libya's Prime Minister-elect [Mustafa A.G. Abushagur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mustafa_A._G._Abushagur "Mustafa A. G. Abushagur") was ousted after failing a second time to win parliamentary approval for a new cabinet.[^108] [^109] [^110] On 14 October 2012, the General National Congress elected former GNC member and human rights lawyer [Ali Zeidan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ali_Zeidan "Ali Zeidan") as prime minister-designate.[^111] Zeidan was sworn in after his cabinet was approved by the GNC.[^112] [^113] On 11 March 2014, after having been ousted by the GNC for his inability to halt a rogue oil shipment,[^114] Prime Minister Zeidan stepped down, and was replaced by Prime Minister [Abdullah al-Thani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdullah_al-Thani "Abdullah al-Thani").[^115]

The [Second Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Libyan_Civil_War "Second Libyan Civil War") began in May 2014 following fighting between rival parliaments with tribal militias and [jihadist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jihad "Jihad") groups soon taking advantage of the power vacuum. Most notably, radical Islamist fighters seized [Derna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derna,_Libya "Derna, Libya") in 2014 and [Sirte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirte "Sirte") in 2015 in the name of the [Islamic State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_State "Islamic State"). In February 2015, neighbouring [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") [launched airstrikes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/February_2015_Egyptian_airstrikes_in_Libya "February 2015 Egyptian airstrikes in Libya") against IS in support of the Tobruk government.[^116] [^117] [^118] In June 2014, [elections were held](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Libyan_parliamentary_election "2014 Libyan parliamentary election") to the [House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Representatives_\(Libya\) "House of Representatives (Libya)"), a new legislative body intended to take over from the [General National Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_National_Congress "General National Congress"). The elections were marred by violence and low turnout, with voting stations closed in some areas.[^119] Secularists and liberals did well in the elections, to the consternation of Islamist lawmakers in the GNC, who reconvened and declared a continuing mandate for the GNC, refusing to recognise the new House of Representatives.[^120] Armed supporters of the General National Congress occupied Tripoli, forcing the newly elected parliament to flee to [Tobruk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobruk "Tobruk").[^121] [^122]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Libyan_General_Khalifa_Haftar_in_2023.jpg/250px-Libyan_General_Khalifa_Haftar_in_2023.jpg)

Khalifa Haftar, the head of the Libyan National Army, one of the main factions in the 2014 civil war

In January 2015, meetings were held with the aim to find a peaceful agreement between the rival parties in Libya. The so-called Geneva-Ghadames talks were supposed to bring the GNC and the Tobruk government together at one table to find a solution of the internal conflict. However, the GNC actually never participated, a sign that internal division not only affected the "Tobruk Camp", but also the "Tripoli Camp". Meanwhile, terrorism within Libya steadily increased, also affecting neighbouring countries. The [terrorist attack against the Bardo Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bardo_National_Museum_attack "Bardo National Museum attack") in [Tunisia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunisia "Tunisia") on 18 March 2015 was reportedly carried out by two Libyan-trained militants.[^123] During 2015 an extended series of diplomatic meetings and peace negotiations were supported by the United Nations, as conducted by the Special Representative of the Secretary-General (SRSG), Spanish diplomat [Bernardino León](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernardino_Le%C3%B3n "Bernardino León").[^124] [^125] UN support for the SRSG-led process of dialogue carried on in addition to the usual work of the United Nations Support Mission in Libya (UNSMIL).[^126] In July 2015 SRSG Leon reported to the UN Security Council on the progress of the negotiations, which at that point had just achieved a political agreement on 11 July setting out "a comprehensive framework... includ\[ing\] guiding principles... institutions and decision-making mechanisms to guide the transition until the adoption of a permanent constitution." Talks, negotiations and dialogue continued on during mid-2015 at various international locations, culminating at Skhirat in Morocco in early September.[^127] [^128]

Also in 2015, as part of the ongoing support from the international community, the UN Human Rights Council requested a report about the Libyan situation [^129] [^130] and the High Commissioner for Human Rights, [Zeid Ra'ad Al Hussein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeid_bin_Ra%27ad "Zeid bin Ra'ad"), established an investigative body (OIOL) to report on human rights and rebuilding the Libyan justice system.[^131] Chaos-ridden Libya emerged as a major transit point for [people trying to reach Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015_European_migrant_crisis "2015 European migrant crisis"). Between 2013 and 2018, nearly 700,000 migrants reached Italy by boat, many of them from Libya.[^132] [^133] In May 2018 Libya's rival leaders agreed to hold parliamentary and presidential elections following a meeting in [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris "Paris").[^134] In April 2019, [Khalifa Haftar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalifa_Haftar "Khalifa Haftar") launched [Operation Flood of Dignity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Libya_campaign "Western Libya campaign"), in an offensive by the [Libyan National Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libyan_National_Army "Libyan National Army") aimed to seize Western territories from the [Government of National Accord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_National_Accord "Government of National Accord") (GNA).[^135] In June 2019, forces allied to Libya's UN-recognised Government of National Accord successfully captured [Gharyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gharyan "Gharyan"), a strategic town where military commander Khalifa Haftar and his fighters were based. According to a spokesman for GNA forces, Mustafa al-Mejii, dozens of LNA fighters under Haftar were killed, while at least 18 were taken prisoner.[^136]

In March 2020, Prime Minister of the GNA [Fayez Al-Sarraj](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fayez_al-Sarraj "Fayez al-Sarraj") commenced Operation Peace Storm. The government initiated the bid in response to the state of assaults carried by [Field Marshal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_Marshal "Field Marshal") [Haftar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalifa_Haftar "Khalifa Haftar") 's [LNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libyan_National_Army "Libyan National Army"). "We are a legitimate, civilian government that respects its obligations to the international community, but is committed primarily to its people and has an obligation to protect its citizens," Sarraj said in line with his decision.[^137] On 28 August 2020, the *[BBC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC "BBC") Africa Eye* and *BBC Arabic Documentaries* revealed that a drone operated by the [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates") (UAE) killed 26 young cadets at a military academy in Tripoli, on 4 January. Most of the cadets were teenagers and none of them were armed. The Chinese-made drone [Wing Loong II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAIG_Wing_Loong_II "CAIG Wing Loong II") fired Blue Arrow 7 missile, which was operated from UAE-run Al-Khadim Libyan air base. In February, these drones stationed in Libya were moved to an air base near [Siwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siwa_Oasis "Siwa Oasis") in the western Egyptian desert.[^138] *[The Guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Guardian "The Guardian")* probed and discovered the blatant violation of [UN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") arms embargo by the UAE and [Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey") on 7 October 2020. As per the reporting, both the nations sent large-scale military cargo planes to Libya in support of their respective parties.[^139] On 23 October 2020, [a permanent ceasefire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libyan_peace_process "Libyan peace process") was signed to end the war.[^140]

### Post-civil war years (2020–present)

In December 2021, the [country's first presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Libyan_presidential_election "2022 Libyan presidential election") was scheduled, but was delayed to June 2022 [^141] and later postponed further.

[Fathi Bashagha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fathi_Bashagha "Fathi Bashagha") was appointed prime minister by the parliament in February 2022 to lead a transitional administration, but standing prime minister [Abdul Hamid Dbeibeh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdul_Hamid_Dbeibeh "Abdul Hamid Dbeibeh") refused to hand over power as of April 2022. In protest against the Dbeibah government, tribal leaders from the desert town of [Ubari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubari "Ubari") shut down the [El Sharara oil field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Sharara_oil_field "El Sharara oil field"), Libya's largest oil field, on 18 April 2022. The shut down threatened to cause oil shortages domestically in Libya, and preclude the state-run National Oil Corp. from exploiting the increase in oil prices resulting from the [2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine").[^142] On 2 July, protesters stormed the parliament building in Tobruk and lit fires.[^143] [^144]

On 10 September 2023, catastrophic floods due to [dam failures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derna_dam_collapses "Derna dam collapses") generated by [Storm Daniel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Storm_Daniel "Storm Daniel") devastated the port city of [Derna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derna,_Libya "Derna, Libya"), killing more than 5,900 and possibly as many as 24,000.[^145] The floods were the worst natural disaster in Libya's modern history.[^146]

In November 2024, the [Government of National Unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_National_Unity_\(Libya\) "Government of National Unity (Libya)") announced it would instate a morality police to crack-down on "weird haircuts", enforce "modest" clothing, and require male guardians for women.[^147]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Koppen-Geiger_Map_LBY_present.svg/250px-Koppen-Geiger_Map_LBY_present.svg.png)

Libya map of Köppen climate classification

Libya extends over 1,759,540 square kilometres (679,362 sq mi), making it the [16th-largest nation in the world by size](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_area "List of countries and dependencies by area"). Libya is bounded to the north by the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea"), [the northwest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya%E2%80%93Tunisia_border "Libya–Tunisia border") by [Tunisia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunisia "Tunisia"), [the west](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria%E2%80%93Libya_border "Algeria–Libya border") by [Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria "Algeria"), [the southwest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya%E2%80%93Niger_border "Libya–Niger border") by [Niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger "Niger"), [the south](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chad%E2%80%93Libya_border "Chad–Libya border") by [Chad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chad "Chad"), [the southeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya%E2%80%93Sudan_border "Libya–Sudan border") by [Sudan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudan "Sudan"), and [the east](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt%E2%80%93Libya_border "Egypt–Libya border") by [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt"), as well as [maritime borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maritime_boundary "Maritime boundary") with [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece"), [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy") and [Malta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malta "Malta") to the north. Libya lies between latitudes [19°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/19th_parallel_north "19th parallel north") and [34°N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/34th_parallel_north "34th parallel north"), and longitudes [9°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/9th_meridian_east "9th meridian east") and [26°E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/26th_meridian_east "26th meridian east"). Libya comprises three historical regions: [Tripolitania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripolitania "Tripolitania"), [Fezzan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fezzan "Fezzan"), and [Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrenaica "Cyrenaica").

At 1,770 kilometres (1,100 mi), Libya's coastline is the longest of any [African country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states_and_dependent_territories_in_Africa "List of sovereign states and dependent territories in Africa") bordering the Mediterranean.[^148] [^149] The portion of the Mediterranean Sea north of Libya is often called the [Libyan Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libyan_Sea "Libyan Sea"). The climate is mostly extremely dry and desertlike in nature. However, the northern regions enjoy a milder [Mediterranean climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_climate "Mediterranean climate").[^150]

Six ecoregions lie within Libya's borders: [Saharan halophytics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saharan_halophytics "Saharan halophytics"), [Mediterranean dry woodlands and steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_dry_woodlands_and_steppe "Mediterranean dry woodlands and steppe"), [Mediterranean woodlands and forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_woodlands_and_forests "Mediterranean woodlands and forests"), [North Saharan steppe and woodlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Saharan_steppe_and_woodlands "North Saharan steppe and woodlands"), [Tibesti-Jebel Uweinat montane xeric woodlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibesti-Jebel_Uweinat_montane_xeric_woodlands "Tibesti-Jebel Uweinat montane xeric woodlands"), and [West Saharan montane xeric woodlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Saharan_montane_xeric_woodlands "West Saharan montane xeric woodlands").[^151]

Natural hazards come in the form of hot, dry, dust-laden [sirocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirocco "Sirocco") (known in Libya as the *gibli*). This is a southern wind blowing from one to four days in [spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_\(season\) "Spring (season)") and [autumn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autumn "Autumn"). There are also [dust storms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dust_storm "Dust storm") and sandstorms. [Oases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oasis "Oasis") can also be found scattered throughout Libya, the most important of which are [Ghadames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghadames "Ghadames") and [Kufra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kufra "Kufra").[^152]

Libya is highly vulnerable to the effects of [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") and underprepared to deal with them.[^153] [^154] The effects of [climate change in Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Libya "Climate change in Libya"), such as [desertification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desertification "Desertification"), sea level rise, flooding, and irregular weather patterns are already noticeable and are expected to increase.[^154] These pose significant threats to Libya's agriculture, food and water security and economic development and sustainability.[^155] [^156] For example [Storm Daniel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Storm_Daniel "Storm Daniel") in 2023 caused catastrophic flooding that not only resulted in thousands of deaths in Libya but also destroyed agricultural infrastructure and contaminated productive lands with debris and pollutants that will affect farming for years.[^157]

Libya was a pioneer state in North Africa in species protection, with the creation in 1975 of the El Kouf protected area. The fall of Muammar Gaddafi's regime favoured intense [poaching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poaching "Poaching"): "Before the fall of Gaddafi even hunting rifles were forbidden. But since 2011, poaching has been carried out with weapons of war and sophisticated vehicles in which one can find up to 200 gazelle heads killed by militiamen who hunt to pass the time. We are also witnessing the emergence of hunters with no connection to the tribes that traditionally practice hunting. They shoot everything they find, even during the breeding season. More than 500,000 birds are killed in this way each year, when protected areas have been seized by tribal chiefs who have appropriated them. The animals that used to live there have all disappeared, hunted when they are edible or released when they are not," explains zoologist Khaled Ettaieb.[^158]

### Libyan Desert

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Libyan_Desert_-_2006.jpg/250px-Libyan_Desert_-_2006.jpg)

Libya is a predominantly desert country. Over 95% of the land area is covered in desert. 159

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Water_stress%2C_top_countries_%282022%29.svg/250px-Water_stress%2C_top_countries_%282022%29.svg.png)

Libya was the fourth-most water-stressed country in the world in 2022.

The Libyan Desert, which covers most of Libya, is one of the most arid and sun-baked places on Earth.[^58] In places, decades may pass without seeing any rainfall at all, and even in the [highlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highland_\(geography\) "Highland (geography)") rainfall seldom happens, once every 5–10 years. At [Uweinat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabal_El_Uweinat "Gabal El Uweinat"), as of 2006 the last recorded rainfall was in September 1998.[^160]

Likewise, the temperature in the Libyan Desert can be extreme; on 13 September 1922, the town of ['Aziziya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%27Aziziya "'Aziziya"), which is located southwest of [Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripoli,_Libya "Tripoli, Libya"), recorded an air temperature of 58 °C (136.4 °F), considered to be a world record.[^161] [^162] [^163] In September 2012, however, the world record figure of 58 °C was determined to be invalid by the [World Meteorological Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization "World Meteorological Organization").[^162] [^163] [^164]

There are a few scattered uninhabited small oases, usually linked to the major depressions, where water can be found by digging to a few feet in depth. In the west there is a widely dispersed group of oases in unconnected shallow depressions, the Kufra group, consisting of Tazerbo, Rebianae and [Kufra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kufra "Kufra").[^160] Aside from the scarps, the general flatness is only interrupted by a series of [plateaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plateau "Plateau") and massifs near the centre of the Libyan Desert, around the convergence of the Egyptian-Sudanese-Libyan borders.

Slightly further to the south are the [massifs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massif "Massif") of Arkenu, Uweinat, and Kissu. These [granite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Granite "Granite") mountains are ancient, having formed long before the sandstones surrounding them. Arkenu and Western Uweinat are ring complexes very similar to those in the [Aïr Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A%C3%AFr_Mountains "Aïr Mountains"). Eastern Uweinat (the highest point in the Libyan Desert) is a raised sandstone plateau adjacent to the granite part further west.[^160]

The plain to the north of Uweinat is dotted with eroded volcanic features. With the discovery of oil in the 1950s also came the discovery of a massive [aquifer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquifer "Aquifer") underneath much of Libya. The water in the [Nubian Sandstone Aquifer System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nubian_Sandstone_Aquifer_System "Nubian Sandstone Aquifer System") pre-dates the last [ice ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_age "Ice age") and the [Sahara Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahara "Sahara") itself.[^165] This area also contains the [Arkenu structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arkenu_structures "Arkenu structures"), which were once thought to be two impact craters.[^166]
