**Morocco**,[^3] officially the **Kingdom of Morocco**,[^4] is a country in the [Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maghreb) region of [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa"). It has coastlines on the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") to the north and the [Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") to the west, and has land borders with [Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria "Algeria") to [the east](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria%E2%80%93Morocco_border "Algeria–Morocco border"); the [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain") [exclaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enclave_and_exclave "Enclave and exclave") of [Ceuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceuta "Ceuta"), [Melilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melilla "Melilla") and [Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n_de_V%C3%A9lez_de_la_Gomera "Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera") along [the north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco%E2%80%93Spain_border "Morocco–Spain border"), which it claims together with several small [Spanish-controlled islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plazas_de_soberan%C3%ADa "Plazas de soberanía");[^22] and the disputed territory of [Western Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Sahara "Western Sahara") to [the south](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco%E2%80%93Western_Sahara_border "Morocco–Western Sahara border"), partly occupied by Morocco since 1975. Morocco also claims to share a border with [Mauritania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauritania "Mauritania") through the disputed territory of Western Sahara. It has a population of approximately 37 million. [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") is both the official and predominant religion, while [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language") and [Berber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_language "Berber language") are the official languages. Additionally, [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language") and the Moroccan [dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect "Dialect") of Arabic are widely spoken. The [culture of Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Morocco "Culture of Morocco") is a mix of [Arab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_culture "Arab culture"), [Berber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berbers "Berbers"), [European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe") (specifically [Andalusian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Andalus "Al-Andalus") [^23]), and [African](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Africa "Culture of Africa") cultures. Its capital is [Rabat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rabat "Rabat"), while its largest city is [Casablanca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casablanca "Casablanca").[^24]

The region constituting Morocco has been inhabited since the [Paleolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleolithic "Paleolithic") era, more than 300,000 years ago. The [Idrisid dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idrisid_dynasty "Idrisid dynasty") was established by [Idris I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idris_I_of_Morocco "Idris I of Morocco") in 788, and Morocco was subsequently ruled by a series of other independent dynasties, reaching its zenith as a [regional power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power") in the 11th and 12th centuries, under the [Almoravid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almoravid_dynasty "Almoravid dynasty") and [Almohad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almohad_Caliphate "Almohad Caliphate") dynasties, when it controlled most of the [Iberian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula") and the Maghreb.[^25] Centuries of [Arab migration to the Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_migration_to_the_Maghreb "Arab migration to the Maghreb") since the 7th century shifted the demographic scope of the region. In the 15th and 16th centuries, Morocco faced external threats to its sovereignty, with [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire") seizing some territory and the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") encroaching from the east. The [Marinid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marinid_Sultanate "Marinid Sultanate") and [Saadi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saadi_Sultanate "Saadi Sultanate") dynasties otherwise resisted foreign domination, and Morocco was the only North African nation to escape Ottoman dominion. The Saadi dynasty expanded its territory through the [conquest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan_invasion_of_the_Songhai_Empire "Moroccan invasion of the Songhai Empire") of the [Songhai Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Songhai_Empire "Songhai Empire") in the late 16th century. The [Alawi dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alawi_dynasty "Alawi dynasty"), which rules the country to this day, seized power in 1631, and over the next two centuries expanded [diplomatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic "Diplomatic") and commercial relations with the [Western world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_world "Western world"). Morocco's strategic location near the mouth of the Mediterranean drew renewed European interest. In 1912, France and Spain established [protectorates](#French_and_Spanish_protectorates) over the country and designated [Tangier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangier "Tangier") as an [international zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangier_International_Zone "Tangier International Zone"), while the Sultan remained the formal sovereign with limited authority under colonial control.[^26] Following intermittent riots and [revolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolution_of_the_King_and_the_People "Revolution of the King and the People") against colonial rule, Morocco regained its independence and reunified in 1956 under the leadership of Sultan [Mohammed V](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_V_of_Morocco "Mohammed V of Morocco").[^27]

Since independence, Morocco has remained relatively stable. It has the [fifth-largest economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_African_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of African countries by GDP (nominal)") in Africa and wields significant influence in both Africa and the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world"); it is considered a [middle power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_power "Middle power") in global affairs and holds membership in the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League"), the [Arab Maghreb Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Maghreb_Union "Arab Maghreb Union"), the [Union for the Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_for_the_Mediterranean "Union for the Mediterranean"), and the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union").[^28] It is one of the few countries in Africa ranking high on the [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index") and is Africa’s leading industrial economy.[^29] Morocco is a [unitary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_state "Unitary state") semi- [constitutional monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy") with an elected parliament. The [executive branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_\(government\) "Executive (government)") is led by the [King of Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_of_Morocco "King of Morocco") and the [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Morocco "Prime Minister of Morocco"), while [legislative power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature "Legislature") is vested in the two chambers of parliament: the [House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Representatives_\(Morocco\) "House of Representatives (Morocco)") and the [House of Councillors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Councillors_\(Morocco\) "House of Councillors (Morocco)"). Judicial power rests with the Constitutional Court, which may review the validity of laws, elections, and referendums.[^30] The king holds vast executive and legislative powers, especially over the [military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Moroccan_Armed_Forces "Royal Moroccan Armed Forces"), foreign policy and religious affairs; he can issue [*dahirs*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan_Dahir "Moroccan Dahir"), decrees which have the force of law, and he can also dissolve the parliament after consulting the [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_heads_of_government_of_Morocco "List of heads of government of Morocco") and the president of the constitutional court. Morocco is classified as a [hybrid regime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybrid_regime "Hybrid regime") and, as of 2025, ranks as the leading such system in the Arab world, according to *[The Economist Democracy Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economist_Democracy_Index "The Economist Democracy Index")*.[^31]

Morocco claims ownership of the [non-self-governing territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_list_of_non-self-governing_territories "United Nations list of non-self-governing territories") of [Western Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Sahara "Western Sahara"), which it has designated its [Southern Provinces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan-occupied_Western_Sahara "Moroccan-occupied Western Sahara"). In 1975, after Spain [agreed to decolonise the territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madrid_Accords "Madrid Accords") and cede its control to Morocco and [Mauritania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauritania "Mauritania"), a [guerrilla war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Sahara_War "Western Sahara War") broke out between those powers and some of the [local inhabitants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahrawi_people "Sahrawi people"). In 1979, Mauritania relinquished [its claim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiris_al-Gharbiyya "Tiris al-Gharbiyya") to the area, but the war continued to rage. In 1991, a ceasefire agreement was reached, but the issue of sovereignty remained unresolved. Today, Morocco occupies about two-thirds of the territory,[^32] and [efforts to resolve the dispute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Sahara_peace_process "Western Sahara peace process") have thus far failed to break the political deadlock.

## Etymology and name

The [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language") *Morocco* is an [anglicisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglicisation "Anglicisation") of the [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language "Spanish language") name for the country, *Marruecos*, derived from the name of the city of [Marrakesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marrakesh "Marrakesh"), which was the capital of the [Almoravid dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almoravid_dynasty "Almoravid dynasty"), the [Almohad Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almohad_Caliphate "Almohad Caliphate"), and the [Saadian dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saadian_dynasty "Saadian dynasty").[^33] During the Almoravid dynasty, the city of Marrakesh was established under the name of *Tāmurākušt*, derived from the city's ancient [Berber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_languages "Berber languages") name of *amūr n Yakuš* (lit. 'land/country of God').[^34] In English, the first vowel has been changed, likely influenced by the word "Moor." [^35]

Morocco's modern [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language") name is *al-Maghrib* (المغرب, transl. the land of the sunset; the west), with the Kingdom's official Arabic name being *al-Mamlakah al-Maghribīyah* (المملكة المغربية; transl. the kingdom of sunset; the western kingdom).[^36] [^37] [^38] [^39]

Historically, Morocco has been part of what Muslim geographers referred to in Arabic as *[al-Maghrib al-Aqṣā](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Al-Maghrib_al-Aq%E1%B9%A3%C4%81&action=edit&redlink=1 "Al-Maghrib al-Aqṣā (page does not exist)")* (المغرب الأقصى, 'the Farthest West \[of the Islamic world\]' designating roughly the area from [Tiaret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiaret "Tiaret") to the [Atlantic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean")) in contrast with neighbouring regions of *[al-Maghrib al-Awsaṭ](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Al-Maghrib_al-Awsa%E1%B9%AD&action=edit&redlink=1 "Al-Maghrib al-Awsaṭ (page does not exist)")* (المغرب الأوسط, 'the Middle West': [Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripoli,_Libya "Tripoli, Libya") to [Béjaïa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B%C3%A9ja%C3%AFa "Béjaïa")) and *[al-Maghrib al-Adnā](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Al-Maghrib_al-Adn%C4%81&action=edit&redlink=1 "Al-Maghrib al-Adnā (page does not exist)")* (المغرب الأدنى, 'the Nearest West': [Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandria "Alexandria") to Tripoli).

In [Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_language "Turkish language"), Morocco is known as *Fas*, a name derived from its medieval capital of [Fes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fes "Fes") (فاس).[^40] [^41] In other parts of the Islamic world, for example in Egyptian and Middle Eastern Arabic literature before the mid-20th century, Morocco was commonly referred to as *Murrakush* (مراكش).[^42] The term is still used to refer to Morocco today in several [Indo-Iranian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Iranian_languages "Indo-Iranian languages") languages, including [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_language "Persian language"), [Urdu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urdu "Urdu"), and [Punjabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punjabi_language "Punjabi language").[^43]

Morocco has also been referred to politically by a variety of terms denoting the [Sharifi heritage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharifism "Sharifism") of the [Alawi dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alawi_dynasty "Alawi dynasty"), such as *al-Mamlakah ash-Sharīfah* (المملكة الشريفة), *al-Iyālah ash-Sharīfah* (الإيالة الشريفة) and *al-Imbarāṭūriyyah ash-Sharīfah* (الإمبراطورية الشريفة), rendered in French as *l'Empire chérifien* and in English as the 'Sharifian Empire'.[^44] [^45]

## History

### Prehistory and antiquity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Ida_3_petroglyph.png/250px-Ida_3_petroglyph.png)

Iron Age petroglyph with Libyco-Berber inscriptions, likely depicting a meteorite fall, found near the High Atlas village of Ida Ou Kazzou

The area of present-day Morocco has been inhabited since at least [Paleolithic times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleolithic "Paleolithic"), beginning sometime between 190,000 and 90,000 BC.[^46] A recent publication has suggested that there is evidence for even earlier human habitation of the area: *[Homo sapiens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_sapiens "Homo sapiens")* fossils that had been discovered in the late 2000s near the [Atlantic coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") in [Jebel Irhoud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jebel_Irhoud "Jebel Irhoud") were recently dated to roughly 315,000 years ago.[^47] [Hominin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hominin "Hominin") fossils from Thomas Quarry I in Casablanca, published in 2026, push evidence of hominin presence in the region back to approximately 773,000 years ago, near the root of the *Homo sapiens* lineage.[^48] During the [Upper Paleolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic "Upper Paleolithic"), the [Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maghreb "Maghreb") was more fertile than it is today, resembling a [savanna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savanna "Savanna"), in contrast to its modern arid landscape.[^49]

DNA studies of [Iberomaurusian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberomaurusian "Iberomaurusian") peoples at [Taforalt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taforalt "Taforalt"), Morocco dating to around 15,000 years ago have found them to have a distinctive Maghrebi ancestry formed from a mixture of [Near Eastern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_East "Near East") and African ancestry, which is still found as a part of the genome of modern Northwest Africans.[^50] Later during the [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic"), from around 7,500 years ago onwards, there was a migration into Northwest Africa of [European Neolithic Farmers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_European_Farmers "Early European Farmers") from the Iberian Peninsula (who had originated in [Anatolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolia "Anatolia") several thousand years prior), as well as pastoralists from the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant"), both of whom also significantly contributed to the ancestry of modern Northwest Africans.[^51] The [proto-Berber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Berber_language "Proto-Berber language") tribes evolved from these prehistoric communities during the late [Bronze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") - and early [Iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age") ages.[^52]

Excavations at [Oued Beht](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baht_River "Baht River") uncovered a Neolithic farming society dated to 3400–2900 BCE, the largest known agricultural complex in Africa outside the [Nile Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile "Nile") at that time.[^53] At Kach Kouch near the Strait of Gibraltar, [Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") occupation from 2200 BCE onward demonstrates settled agricultural life in the Maghreb centuries before Phoenician contact, by the 8th century BCE its inhabitants were selectively adopting Phoenician building techniques and crops while maintaining their own material culture.[^54] [^55]

In the early part of [Classical Antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Antiquity "Classical Antiquity"), indigenous communities in the region were already integrated into Mediterranean exchange networks when the [Phoenicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia") established trading colonies and settlements, the most substantial of which were [Chellah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chellah "Chellah"), [Lixus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lixus_\(ancient_city\) "Lixus (ancient city)"), and [Mogador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mogador "Mogador").[^56] [^55] [^54] Mogador was established as a Phoenician colony as early as the 6th century BC.[^57]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Volubilis_Longshot_II.jpg/250px-Volubilis_Longshot_II.jpg)

Roman ruins of the city of Volubilis, which once served as the capital of Mauretania and the Idrisids

Morocco later became a realm of the Northwest African civilisation of [ancient Carthage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Carthage "Ancient Carthage"), and part of the Carthaginian empire. The earliest known independent Moroccan state was the [Berber kingdom of Mauretania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauretania "Mauretania"), under King [Baga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baga_\(king\) "Baga (king)").[^58] This ancient kingdom (not to be confused with the modern state of [Mauritania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauritania "Mauritania")) flourished around 225 BC or earlier. Mauretania became a [client](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Client_state "Client state") kingdom of the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") in 33 BC. Emperor [Claudius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudius "Claudius") annexed Mauretania directly in 44 AD, making it a [Roman province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_province "Roman province") ruled by an imperial [governor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governor "Governor") (either a *procurator Augusti*, or a *legatus Augusti pro praetore*).

[Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity") in Morocco appeared during the [Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") times, when it was practiced by [Berber Christians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_Christians "Berber Christians") in Roman [Mauretania Tingitana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauretania_Tingitana "Mauretania Tingitana").[^59] During the [Crisis of the Third Century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crisis_of_the_Third_Century "Crisis of the Third Century"), parts of Mauretania were reconquered by Berbers. By the late 3rd century, direct Roman rule had become confined to a few coastal cities, such as Septum ([Ceuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceuta "Ceuta")) in Mauretania Tingitana and [Cherchell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cherchell "Cherchell") in [Mauretania Caesariensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauretania_Caesariensis "Mauretania Caesariensis"). When, in 429 AD, the area was devastated by the [Vandals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vandals "Vandals"), the Roman Empire lost its remaining possessions in Mauretania, and local [Mauro-Roman kings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauro-Roman_kingdom "Mauro-Roman kingdom") assumed control of them. In the 530s, the [Eastern Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire "Eastern Roman Empire"), under Byzantine control, re-established direct imperial rule of Septum and [Tingi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tingi "Tingi"), fortified Tingis and erected a church.

### Foundation and dynasties

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Idrisids_coin_minted_at_Al_Aliyah_Morocco_840_CE.jpg/250px-Idrisids_coin_minted_at_Al_Aliyah_Morocco_840_CE.jpg)

Idrisid coin in Fes, 840

The [Muslim conquest of the Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_the_Maghreb "Muslim conquest of the Maghreb") that had begun during the mid-7th century was completed under the [Umayyad Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") by 709. The caliphate introduced both [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") and the Arabic language to the area; this period also saw the beginning of a trend of [Arab migration to the Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_migration_to_the_Maghreb "Arab migration to the Maghreb") which would last for centuries and effect a demographic shift in the region. While constituting part of the larger empire, Morocco was initially organised as a subsidiary province of [Ifriqiya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ifriqiya "Ifriqiya"), with the local governors appointed by the Muslim governor in [Kairouan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kairouan "Kairouan").[^60]

The indigenous Berber peoples adopted Islam, but retained their [customary laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customary_law "Customary law"). They also paid taxes and tribute to the new Muslim administration.[^61] The first independent Muslim state in the area of modern Morocco was the [Kingdom of Nekor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Nekor "Kingdom of Nekor"), an emirate in the [Rif Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rif_Mountains "Rif Mountains"). It was founded by [Salih I ibn Mansur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salih_I_ibn_Mansur "Salih I ibn Mansur") in 710, as a client state to the Umayyad Caliphate. After the outbreak of the [Berber Revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_Revolt "Berber Revolt") in 739, the Berbers formed other independent states such as the [Miknasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miknasa "Miknasa") of [Sijilmasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sijilmasa "Sijilmasa") and the [Barghawata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barghawata "Barghawata").

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/29610-Fez_%2828134041211%29_%28qarawiyyin_crop%29.jpg/250px-29610-Fez_%2828134041211%29_%28qarawiyyin_crop%29.jpg)

al-Qarawiyyin, founded in Fes in the 9th century, was a major spiritual, literary, and intellectual centre.

The founder of the [Idrisid dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idrisid_dynasty "Idrisid dynasty") and the great-grandson of [Hasan ibn Ali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasan_ibn_Ali "Hasan ibn Ali"), [Idris ibn Abdallah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idris_ibn_Abdallah "Idris ibn Abdallah"), had fled to Morocco after the massacre of his family by the [Abbasids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate") in the [Hejaz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hejaz "Hejaz"). He convinced the Awraba Berber tribes to break their allegiance to the distant Abbasid caliphs and he founded the Idrisid dynasty in 788. The Idrisids established [Fes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fes,_Morocco "Fes, Morocco") as their capital and Morocco became a centre of Muslim learning and a major [regional power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power"). The Idrisids were ousted in 927 by the [Fatimid Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimid_Caliphate "Fatimid Caliphate") and their Miknasa allies. After Miknasa broke off relations with the Fatimids in 932, they were removed from power by the [Maghrawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maghrawa "Maghrawa") of Sijilmasa in 980.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Almohad_dynasty_en.svg/250px-Almohad_dynasty_en.svg.png)

The empire of the Almohad dynasty at its greatest extent, c. 1212

From the 11th century onward, a series of Berber dynasties arose.[^62] [^63] [^64] Under the [Sanhaja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanhaja "Sanhaja") [Almoravid dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almoravid_dynasty "Almoravid dynasty") and the [Masmuda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masmuda "Masmuda") [Almohad dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almohad_dynasty "Almohad dynasty"),[^65] Morocco dominated the Maghreb, [al-Andalus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Andalus "Al-Andalus") in [Iberia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberia "Iberia"), and the western Mediterranean region. From the 13th century onward the country saw a [massive migration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_migration_to_the_Maghreb "Arab migration to the Maghreb") of the [Banu Hilal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banu_Hilal "Banu Hilal") Arab tribes. In the 13th and 14th centuries the [Zenata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenata "Zenata") Berber [Marinids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marinids "Marinids") held power in Morocco and strove to replicate the successes of the Almohads through military campaigns in Algeria and Spain. They were followed by the [Wattasids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wattasids "Wattasids"). In the 15th century, the [Reconquista](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reconquista "Reconquista") ended Muslim rule in Iberia, with [Muslims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morisco "Morisco") and [Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Morocco "History of the Jews in Morocco") fleeing to find refuge in Morocco.[^66] [Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portugal "Portugal") efforts to control the Atlantic sea trade in the 15th century did not greatly affect the interior of Morocco, despite that the Portuguese managed to control some possessions on the Moroccan coast, while not venturing further afield inland.

In 1549, the region fell to successive Arab dynasties claiming descent from the Islamic prophet [Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad "Muhammad"): first the [Saadi dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saadi_dynasty "Saadi dynasty") who ruled from 1549 to 1659, and then the [Alawi dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alawi_dynasty "Alawi dynasty"), who have remained in power since the 17th century. Morocco faced aggression from [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain") in the north, and the Ottoman Empire's allies pressing westward.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Ahmad_al-Mansur_recognizing_King_Sebastiao%27s_dead_body.jpg/330px-Ahmad_al-Mansur_recognizing_King_Sebastiao%27s_dead_body.jpg)

The corpse of King Sebastian of Portugal being presented before Sultan Ahmad al-Mansur after the Battle of Alcácer Quibir, which marked the end of Portugal's ruling Aviz dynasty

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Palais_El_Badii_-_panoramio.jpg/250px-Palais_El_Badii_-_panoramio.jpg)

The remains of the Saadi sultan Ahmad al-Mansur 's 16th-century Badi' Palace

Under the Saadis, the sultanate [ended the Portuguese Aviz dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_succession_crisis_of_1580 "Portuguese succession crisis of 1580") in 1578 at the [Battle of Alcácer Quibir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Alc%C3%A1cer_Quibir "Battle of Alcácer Quibir"). The reign of [Ahmad al-Mansur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmad_al-Mansur "Ahmad al-Mansur") brought new wealth and prestige to the Sultanate, and a [large expedition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saadian_invasion_of_the_Songhai_Empire "Saadian invasion of the Songhai Empire") to West Africa inflicted a crushing defeat on the [Songhay Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Songhay_Empire "Songhay Empire") in 1591. However, managing the territories across the [Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahara "Sahara") proved too difficult.[^67] Upon the death of al-Mansur, the country was divided among his sons.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Mausoleum_of_moulay_ismail_DSCF5915.jpg/250px-Mausoleum_of_moulay_ismail_DSCF5915.jpg)

The Mausoleum of Sultan Moulay Ismail in Meknès, near his tomb are two grandfather clocks which were gifted by King Louis XIV of France

After a period of political fragmentation and conflict during the decline of the Saadi dynasty, Morocco was finally reunited by the Alawi sultan [al-Rashid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Rashid_of_Morocco "Al-Rashid of Morocco") in the late 1660s, who took Fez in 1666 and Marrakesh in 1668.[^24]<sup><span title="Page / location: 230">: 230</span> </sup> [^68]<sup><span title="Page / location: 225">: 225</span> </sup> The 'Alawis succeeded in stabilising their position, and while the kingdom was smaller than previous ones in the region, it remained quite wealthy. Against the opposition of local tribes [Ismail Ibn Sharif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ismail_Ibn_Sharif "Ismail Ibn Sharif") (1672–1727) began to create a unified state.[^69] With his [Riffian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rif "Rif") army, he re-occupied [Tangier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Tangier "English Tangier") from the English who had abandoned it in 1684 and drove the Spanish from [Larache](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larache "Larache") in 1689. The Portuguese abandoned [Mazagão](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mazagan "Mazagan"), their last territory in Morocco, in 1769. However, the [siege of Melilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Melilla_\(1774\) "Siege of Melilla (1774)") against the Spanish ended in defeat in 1775.

Morocco was the [first nation to recognise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_date_of_recognition_of_the_United_States "List of countries by date of recognition of the United States") the fledgling [United States of America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") as an independent nation in 1777, only a year after the country's founding.[^70] [^71] [^72] In the beginning of the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution "American Revolution"), American merchant ships in the Atlantic Ocean were subject to attacks by other fleets from the [Barbary corsairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_corsairs "Barbary corsairs"). On 20 December 1777, Morocco's Sultan [Mohammed III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_ben_Abdallah "Mohammed ben Abdallah") declared that American merchant ships would be under the protection of the sultanate and could thus enjoy safe passage. The 1786 [Moroccan–American Treaty of Friendship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan%E2%80%93American_Treaty_of_Friendship "Moroccan–American Treaty of Friendship") stands as the United States' oldest unbroken friendship treaty.[^73] [^74]

### French and Spanish protectorates

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/La_Paz_de_Wad-Ras.jpg/250px-La_Paz_de_Wad-Ras.jpg)

The Treaty of Wad Ras after the Hispano-Moroccan War (1859–1860) bankrupted Morocco's national treasury, forcing the Makhzen to take on a British loan. 75

As Europe industrialised, Northwest Africa was increasingly prized for its potential for colonisation. France showed a strong interest in Morocco as early as 1830, not only to protect the border of its Algerian territory, but also because of the strategic position of Morocco with coasts on the Mediterranean and the open Atlantic.[^76] In 1860, a dispute over Spain's [Ceuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceuta "Ceuta") enclave led Spain to declare war. Victorious Spain won a further enclave and an enlarged Ceuta in the settlement. In 1884, Spain created a protectorate in the coastal areas of Morocco.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Louis_Comfort_Tiffany_-_Market_day_outside_the_walls_of_Tangiers%2C_Morocco.jpg/250px-Louis_Comfort_Tiffany_-_Market_day_outside_the_walls_of_Tangiers%2C_Morocco.jpg)

Tangier 's population in 1956 included 40,000 Muslims, 31,000 Europeans and 15,000 Jews. 77

In 1904, France and Spain carved out zones of influence in Morocco. Recognition by the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") of France's [sphere of influence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence "Sphere of influence") provoked a strong reaction from the [German Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Empire "German Empire"); and a crisis loomed in 1905. The matter was resolved at the [Algeciras Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeciras_Conference "Algeciras Conference") in 1906. The [Agadir Crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agadir_Crisis "Agadir Crisis") of 1911 increased tensions between European powers. The 1912 [Treaty of Fez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Fez "Treaty of Fez") made Morocco a [protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protectorate "Protectorate") of France, and triggered the [1912 Fez riots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1912_Fez_riots "1912 Fez riots").[^78] Spain continued to operate its coastal protectorate. By the same treaty, Spain assumed the role of [protecting power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protecting_power "Protecting power") over the northern coastal and southern [Saharan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahara "Sahara") zones.[^79]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Pacification.jpg/250px-Pacification.jpg)

Map depicting the French conquest of Morocco from 1907 to 1934

Tens of thousands of colonists entered Morocco. Some bought up large amounts of rich agricultural land, while others organised the exploitation and modernisation of mines and harbours. Interest groups that formed among these elements continually pressured France to increase its control over Morocco – with some Moroccan tribes allying with the French against other competing tribes from early on in its conquest. The French colonial administrator, general Marshal [Hubert Lyautey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubert_Lyautey "Hubert Lyautey"), sincerely admired Moroccan culture and succeeded in imposing a joint Moroccan-French administration, while creating a modern school system. Several divisions of Moroccan soldiers ([Goumiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goumier "Goumier") or regular troops and officers) served in the [French army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_army "French army") in both [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") and [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), and in the Spanish [Nationalist Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francoism "Francoism") in the [Spanish Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War "Spanish Civil War") and after (*[Regulares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulares "Regulares")*).[^80] The institution of [slavery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery_in_Morocco "Slavery in Morocco") was abolished in 1925.[^81]

Between 1921 and 1926, an [uprising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rif_War "Rif War") in the [Rif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rif "Rif") Mountains, led by [Abd el-Krim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abd_el-Krim "Abd el-Krim"), led to the establishment of the [Republic of the Rif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_the_Rif "Republic of the Rif"). The Spanish used anti-civilian bombing raids and mustard gas to prevent the Rif republic from gaining independence.[^82] They lost more than 13,000 soldiers at [Annual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Annual "Battle of Annual") in July–August 1921 alone.[^83] The Riffi were eventually suppressed by 1927 by the Franco-Spanish military. The casualties on the Spanish-French side were 52,000, while approximately 10,000 [Riffians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riffians "Riffians") died.[^84]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/%D9%86%D8%B5%D8%A8_11_%D9%8A%D9%86%D8%A7%D9%8A%D8%B1_1944_-_%D8%B3%D9%84%D8%A7.jpg/330px-%D9%86%D8%B5%D8%A8_11_%D9%8A%D9%86%D8%A7%D9%8A%D8%B1_1944_-_%D8%B3%D9%84%D8%A7.jpg)

Monument in memory of the Proclamation of Independence of 1944 in Salé

In 1943, the [Istiqlal Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Istiqlal_Party "Istiqlal Party") (Independence Party) was founded to press for independence, with discreet US support. Moroccan nationalists drew heavily on [transnational activist networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolution_of_the_King_and_the_People "Revolution of the King and the People") for lobbying to end colonial rule, primarily at the United Nations.[^85] The Istiqlal Party subsequently provided most of the leadership for the nationalist movement.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Mohammed_V_Morocco_1957.lowres.jpeg/250px-Mohammed_V_Morocco_1957.lowres.jpeg)

King Mohammed V during a visit to the United States in 1957

France's exile of Sultan [Mohammed V](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_V_of_Morocco "Mohammed V of Morocco") in 1953 to [Madagascar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madagascar "Madagascar") and his replacement by the unpopular [Mohammed Ben Aarafa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_Ben_Aarafa "Mohammed Ben Aarafa") sparked active opposition to the French and Spanish protectorates.[^86] The most notable violence occurred in [Oujda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oujda "Oujda") where Moroccans attacked French and other European residents in the streets.[^87] France allowed Mohammed V to return in 1955, and the negotiations that led to Moroccan independence began the following year.[^88] In March 1956 Morocco regained its independence from France as the Kingdom of Morocco. A month later, Spain forsook its protectorate in northern Morocco to the new state but kept its two coastal-Mediterranean enclaves ([Ceuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceuta "Ceuta") and [Melilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melilla "Melilla"), which dated from earlier conquests), over which Morocco still claims [sovereignty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereignty "Sovereignty") to this day.

### Post-independence

Sultan Mohammed became King in 1957. Upon the death of Mohammed V, [Hassan II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassan_II_of_Morocco "Hassan II of Morocco") became King of Morocco on 3 March 1961. Morocco held its first [general elections in 1963](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan_parliamentary_election,_1963 "Moroccan parliamentary election, 1963"). However, Hassan declared a state of emergency and suspended parliament in 1965. In 1971 and 1972, there were two failed attempts to depose the king and establish a republic. A truth commission set up in 2005 to investigate human rights abuses during his reign confirmed nearly 10,000 cases, ranging from death in detention to forced exile. Some 592 people were recorded killed during Hassan's rule according to the truth commission.

In 1963, the [Sand War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand_War "Sand War") was fought between Algerian and Moroccan troops over Moroccan claims to parts of Algerian territory. A formal peace agreement was signed in February 1964; however, relations remained strained between the two countries following the conflict.[^89] The Spanish [enclave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enclave "Enclave") of [Ifni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ifni "Ifni") in the south was returned to Morocco in 1969.[^90]

The [Polisario movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polisario_movement "Polisario movement") was formed in 1973, with the aim of establishing an independent state in the Spanish Sahara. On 6 November 1975, King Hassan asked for volunteers to cross into the Spanish Sahara. Some 350,000 civilians were reported as being involved in the " [Green March](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_March "Green March") ".[^91] A month later, Spain agreed to leave the Spanish Sahara, soon to become Western Sahara, and to transfer it to joint Moroccan-Mauritanian control, despite the objections and threats of military intervention by Algeria. Moroccan forces occupied the territory.[^66]

Moroccan and Algerian troops [soon clashed in Western Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Sahara_War "Western Sahara War"). Morocco and Mauritania divided up Western Sahara. Fighting between the Moroccan military and Polisario forces continued for many years. The prolonged war was a considerable financial drain on Morocco. In 1983, Hassan cancelled planned elections amid political unrest and economic crisis. In 1984, Morocco left the Organisation of African Unity in protest at the [SADR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic "Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic") 's admission to the body. Polisario claimed to have killed more than 5,000 Moroccan soldiers between 1982 and 1985.[^92] Algerian authorities have estimated the number of [Sahrawi refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahrawi_refugees "Sahrawi refugees") in Algeria to be 165,000.[^93] Diplomatic relations with Algeria were restored in 1988. In 1991, a UN-monitored ceasefire began in Western Sahara, but the territory's status remains undecided and ceasefire violations are reported. The following decade saw much wrangling over a [proposed referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houston_Agreement "Houston Agreement") on the future of the territory but the deadlock was not broken.[^94]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Redeployment_of_Polisario_Forces_9-83.png/250px-Redeployment_of_Polisario_Forces_9-83.png)

Map of the Western Sahara War (1975–1991)

Political reforms in the 1990s resulted in the establishment of a bicameral legislature with Morocco's first opposition-led government coming to power. King [Hassan II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassan_II_of_Morocco "Hassan II of Morocco") died in 1999 and was succeeded by his son, [Mohammed VI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_VI_of_Morocco "Mohammed VI of Morocco").[^95] He is a cautious moderniser who has introduced economic and social liberalisation.[^96] Mohammed VI paid a controversial visit to the Western Sahara in 2002.[^97] Morocco unveiled an autonomy blueprint for Western Sahara to the United Nations in 2007.[^98] The Polisario rejected the plan and put forward its own proposal.[^99] Morocco and the Polisario Front held UN-sponsored talks in New York City but failed to come to any agreement.[^100] In 2010, security forces stormed a protest camp in the Western Sahara, triggering violent demonstrations in the regional capital [El Aaiún](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Aai%C3%BAn "El Aaiún").[^101]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/President_Donald_J._Trump_and_First_Lady_Melania_Trump_Visit_France_%2844046554080%29.jpg/250px-President_Donald_J._Trump_and_First_Lady_Melania_Trump_Visit_France_%2844046554080%29.jpg)

Mohammed VI and other world leaders and representatives attend the Armistice Day centenary in November 2018.

In 2002, Morocco and Spain agreed to a US-brokered resolution over the disputed island of [Perejil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perejil_Island "Perejil Island"). Spanish troops had taken the normally uninhabited island after Moroccan soldiers landed on it and set up tents and a flag.[^102] There were renewed tensions in 2005, as dozens of African migrants stormed the borders of the Spanish enclaves of Melilla and Ceuta. In response, Spain deported dozens of the illegal migrants to Morocco from Melilla.[^103] In 2006, the Spanish Premier [Zapatero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Luis_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Zapatero "José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero") visited Spanish enclaves. He was the first Spanish leader in 25 years to make an official visit to the territories.[^104] The following year, Spanish King [Juan Carlos I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I "Juan Carlos I") visited Ceuta and Melilla, further angering Morocco which demanded control of the enclaves.[^105]

During the [2011–2012 Moroccan protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011%E2%80%932012_Moroccan_protests "2011–2012 Moroccan protests"), thousands of people rallied in Rabat and other cities calling for political reform and a new constitution curbing the powers of the king. In July 2011, the King won a landslide victory in a [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_Moroccan_constitutional_referendum "2011 Moroccan constitutional referendum") on a reformed constitution he had proposed to placate the [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring") protests.[^106] In the first [general elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_Moroccan_general_election "2011 Moroccan general election") that followed, the moderate Islamist [Justice and Development Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justice_and_Development_Party_\(Morocco\) "Justice and Development Party (Morocco)") won a plurality of seats, with [Abdelilah Benkirane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdelilah_Benkirane "Abdelilah Benkirane") being designated as head of government per the new constitution.[^107] Despite the reforms made by Mohammed VI, demonstrators continued to call for deeper reforms. Hundreds took part in a trade union rally in Casablanca in May 2012. Participants accused the government of failing to deliver on reforms.[^108]

On 24 August 2021, neighbouring Algeria cut diplomatic relations with Morocco, accusing Morocco of supporting a separatist group and hostile actions against Algeria. Morocco called the decision unjustified. Foreign Minister [Ramtane Lamamra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramtane_Lamamra "Ramtane Lamamra") accused Morocco of using [Pegasus spyware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pegasus_\(spyware\) "Pegasus (spyware)") against its officials.[^109] [Amnesty International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amnesty_International "Amnesty International") found that two phones of Sahraoui human rights defender [Aminatou Haidar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aminatou_Haidar "Aminatou Haidar") were infected in November 2021.[^110]

On 10 December 2020, the [Israel–Morocco normalisation agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel%E2%80%93Morocco_normalization_agreement "Israel–Morocco normalization agreement") was announced, and Morocco announced its intention to resume diplomatic relations with Israel.[^111] Joint Declaration of the Kingdom of Morocco, the United States of America and the State of Israel was signed on 22 December 2020.[^112] The agreement was met with criticism due to the October 2023 [Gaza war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_war "Gaza war"). Moroccan Foreign Minister [Nasser Bourita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasser_Bourita "Nasser Bourita") argues that maintaining relations does not signify endorsement of the State of Israel’s actions.[^113]

On 8 September 2023, a 6.8 magnitude [earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Al_Haouz_earthquake "2023 Al Haouz earthquake") hit Morocco killing more than 2,800 people and injuring thousands. The [epicentre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicenter "Epicenter") of the quake was around 70 km southwest of city of Marrakesh.[^114]

The [2025 Moroccan protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Moroccan_Gen_Z_protests "2025 Moroccan Gen Z protests") emerged against a backdrop of high youth unemployment, which official statistics placed at 35.8%, in addition to allegations of corruption and popular discontent over [social inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_inequality "Social inequality") in Morocco.[^115]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/My_Toubkal.jpg/250px-My_Toubkal.jpg)

Toubkal, the highest peak in Northern Africa, at 4,167 m (13,671 ft)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Panorama_Djebel_el_Kest.jpg/250px-Panorama_Djebel_el_Kest.jpg)

A section of the Anti-Atlas near Tafraout

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Moroccan_Atlantic_cedar.jpg/250px-Moroccan_Atlantic_cedar.jpg)

An old Atlas cedar tree in the Atlas range

Morocco has a coast on the [Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") that reaches past the [Strait of Gibraltar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar "Strait of Gibraltar") into the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea"). It is bordered by [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain") to the north (a water border through the Strait and [land borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco%E2%80%93Spain_border "Morocco–Spain border") with three small Spanish-controlled [exclaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclave "Exclave"), [Ceuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceuta "Ceuta"), [Melilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melilla "Melilla"), and [Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n_de_V%C3%A9lez_de_la_Gomera "Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera")), [Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria "Algeria") to the east, and [Western Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Sahara "Western Sahara") to the south. Since Morocco controls most of Western Sahara, its *de facto* southern border is with [Mauritania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauritania "Mauritania").[^116] The geography of Morocco spans from the Atlantic Ocean, to mountainous areas, to the Sahara desert. Morocco is a [Northern African](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Africa "Northern Africa") country, bordering the [North Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean "North Atlantic Ocean") and the Mediterranean Sea, between Algeria and the annexed Western Sahara. Morocco is one of only three countries (along with Spain and [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France")) to have both Atlantic and Mediterranean coastlines.[^117]

A large part of Morocco is mountainous. The [Atlas Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_Mountains "Atlas Mountains") are located mainly in the centre and the south of the country. The [Rif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rif "Rif") Mountains are located in the north of the country. Both ranges are mainly inhabited by the [Berber people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_people "Berber people").[^118] Its total area is about 446,300 km <sup>2</sup> (172,317 sq mi).[^119] Algeria borders Morocco to the east and southeast, though the border between the two countries has been closed since 1994.[^120] The internationally recognised borders of the country lie between latitudes [27°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/27th_parallel_north "27th parallel north") and 36°N, and longitudes 1° and [14°W](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/14th_meridian_west "14th meridian west"). Morocco's capital city is [Rabat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rabat "Rabat"), a city that is beside the [Oued Bou Regreg River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bou_Regreg "Bou Regreg"); its largest city is its main port, [Casablanca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casablanca "Casablanca"). Other cities recording a population over 500,000 in the [2014 Moroccan census](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Moroccan_census "2014 Moroccan census") are [Fes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fes "Fes"), [Marrakesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marrakesh "Marrakesh"), [Meknes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meknes "Meknes"), [Salé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sal%C3%A9 "Salé") and [Tangier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangier "Tangier").[^121]

The Rif Mountains stretch over the region bordering the Mediterranean from the northwest to the northeast. The Atlas Mountains run down the backbone of the country,[^122] from the northeast to the southwest. Most of the southeast portion of the country is in the [Sahara Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahara_Desert "Sahara Desert") and as such is generally sparsely populated and unproductive economically. Most of the population lives to the north of these mountains, with Morocco's largest cities encapsulated by the [Middle Atlas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Atlas "Middle Atlas") and the [High Atlas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Atlas "High Atlas") mountain range, while to the south lies the Western Sahara, a former Spanish colony that was annexed by Morocco in 1975 during the [Green March](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_March "Green March").[^5] Morocco claims that the Western Sahara is part of its territory and refers to that as its [Southern Provinces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan-occupied_Western_Sahara "Moroccan-occupied Western Sahara").[^116]

Spanish territory in Northwest Africa neighbouring Morocco comprises five enclaves on the Mediterranean coast: [Ceuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceuta "Ceuta"), [Melilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melilla "Melilla"), [Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n_de_V%C3%A9lez_de_la_Gomera "Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera"), [Peñón de Alhucemas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n_de_Alhucemas "Peñón de Alhucemas"), the [Chafarinas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islas_Chafarinas "Islas Chafarinas") islands, and the disputed islet [Perejil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isla_Perejil "Isla Perejil"). Off the Atlantic coast the [Canary Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canary_Islands "Canary Islands") belong to Spain, whereas [Madeira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madeira "Madeira") to the north is [Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portugal "Portugal"). To the north, Morocco is bordered by the [Strait of Gibraltar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar "Strait of Gibraltar"), where international shipping has unimpeded [transit passage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_passage "Transit passage") between the Atlantic and Mediterranean.

Morocco is represented in the [ISO 3166-1 alpha-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_3166-1_alpha-2 "ISO 3166-1 alpha-2") geographical encoding standard by the symbol *MA*.[^123] This code was used as the basis for Morocco's internet domain, [.ma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/.ma ".ma").[^123]

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Morocco_K%C3%B6ppen.svg/250px-Morocco_K%C3%B6ppen.svg.png)

Köppen climate types in Morocco

In area, Morocco's climate is mainly " [hot summer Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_climate#Hot-summer_Mediterranean_climate "Mediterranean climate") " (*Csa*) and " [hot desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_desert_climate "Hot desert climate") " (*BWh*) zones.[^124]

Central mountain ranges and the effects of the cold [Canary Current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canary_Current "Canary Current"), off the [Atlantic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic "Atlantic") coast, are significant factors in Morocco's relatively large variety of vegetation zones, ranging from lush forests in the northern and central mountains, giving way to steppe, semi-arid and desert areas in the eastern and southern regions. The Moroccan coastal plains experience moderate temperatures even in summer.[^125]

In the Rif, Middle and High Atlas Mountains, there exist several different types of climates: Mediterranean along the coastal lowlands, giving way to a humid temperate climate at higher elevations with sufficient moisture to allow for the growth of different species of oaks, moss carpets, junipers, and Atlantic fir which is a royal conifer tree [endemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endemism "Endemism") to Morocco.[^126] In the valleys, fertile soils and high precipitation allow for the growth of thick and lush forests. Cloud forests can be found in the west of the Rif Mountains and Middle Atlas Mountains. At higher elevations, the climate becomes alpine in character, and can sustain ski resorts.[^125]

Southeast of the Atlas mountains, near the Algerian borders, the climate becomes very dry, with long and hot summers. Extreme heat and low moisture levels are especially pronounced in the lowland regions east of the Atlas range due to the rain shadow effect of the mountain system. The southeasternmost portions of Morocco are very hot, and include portions of the [Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahara "Sahara") desert, where vast swathes of sand dunes and rocky plains are dotted with lush [oases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oasis "Oasis").[^127]

In contrast to the Sahara region in the south, coastal [plains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plain "Plain") are fertile in the central and northern regions of the country, and comprise the backbone of the country's agriculture, in which 95% of the population live. The direct exposure to the North Atlantic Ocean, the proximity to mainland Europe and the long stretched Rif and Atlas mountains are the factors of the rather European-like climate in the northern half of the country. That makes Morocco a country of contrasts. [Forested areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest "Forest") cover about 12% of the country while arable land accounts for 18%. Approximately 5% of Moroccan land is irrigated for agricultural use.[^128]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Merzouga_sahara.jpg/250px-Merzouga_sahara.jpg)

Landscape of the Erg Chebbi

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Djebel_Toubkal_01.jpg/250px-Djebel_Toubkal_01.jpg)

Atlas Mountains

In general, apart from the southeast regions (pre-Saharan and desert areas), Morocco's climate and geography are very similar to the Iberian peninsula. Thus Morocco has the following climate zones:

- **Mediterranean:** Dominates the coastal Mediterranean regions of the country, along the (500 km strip), and some parts of the Atlantic coast. Summers are hot to moderately hot and dry, average highs are between 29 °C (84.2 °F) and 32 °C (89.6 °F). Winters are generally mild and wet, daily average temperatures hover around 9 °C (48.2 °F) to 11 °C (51.8 °F), and average low are around 5 °C (41.0 °F) to 8 °C (46.4 °F), typical to the coastal areas of the west Mediterranean. Annual Precipitation in this area varies from 600 to 800 mm in the west to 350–500 mm in the east. Notable cities that fall into this zone are [Tangier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangier "Tangier"), [Tétouan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C3%A9touan "Tétouan"), [Al Hoceima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Hoceima "Al Hoceima"), [Nador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nador "Nador") and [Safi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safi,_Morocco "Safi, Morocco").
- **Sub-Mediterranean:** It influences cities that show Mediterranean characteristics, but remain fairly influenced by other climates owing to their either relative elevation, or direct exposure to the North Atlantic Ocean. There are thus two main influencing climates:

- *Oceanic:* Determined by the cooler summers, where highs are around 27 °C (80.6 °F) and in terms of the Essaouira region, are almost always around 21 °C (69.8 °F). The medium daily temperatures can get as low as 19 °C (66.2 °F), while winters are chilly to mild and wet. Annual precipitation varies from 400 to 700 mm. Notable cities that fall into this zone are [Rabat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rabat "Rabat"), [Casablanca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casablanca "Casablanca"), [Kénitra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A9nitra "Kénitra"), [Salé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sal%C3%A9 "Salé") and [Essaouira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essaouira "Essaouira").[^127]

- *Continental:* Determined by the bigger gap between highs and lows, that results in hotter summers and colder winters, than found in typical Mediterranean zones. In summer, daily highs can get as high as 40 °C (104.0 °F) during heat waves, but usually are between 32 °C (89.6 °F) and 36 °C (96.8 °F). However, temperatures drop as the sun sets. Night temperatures usually fall below 20 °C (68.0 °F), and sometimes as low as 10 °C (50.0 °F) in mid-summer. Winters are cooler, and can get below the freezing point multiple times between December and February. Also, snow can fall occasionally. Fès for example registered −8 °C (17.6 °F) in winter 2005. Annual precipitation varies between 500 and 900 mm. Notable cities are [Fès](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F%C3%A8s "Fès"), [Meknès](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mekn%C3%A8s "Meknès"), [Chefchaouen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chefchaouen "Chefchaouen"), [Beni-Mellal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beni-Mellal "Beni-Mellal") and [Taza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taza "Taza").[^127]

- **Continental:** Dominates the mountainous regions of the north and central parts of the country, where summers are hot to very hot, with highs between 32 °C (89.6 °F) and 36 °C (96.8 °F). Winters on the other hand are cold, and lows usually go beyond the freezing point. And when cold damp air comes to Morocco from the northwest, for a few days, temperatures sometimes get below −5 °C (23.0 °F). It often snows abundantly in this part of the country. Precipitation varies between 400 and 800 mm. Notable cities are [Khenifra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khenifra "Khenifra"), [Imilchil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imilchil "Imilchil"), [Midelt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midelt "Midelt") and [Azilal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azilal "Azilal").
- **Alpine:** Found in some parts of the Middle Atlas Mountain range and the eastern part of the High Atlas Mountain range. Summers are very warm to moderately hot, and winters are longer, cold and snowy. Precipitation varies between 400 and 1200 mm. In summer highs barely go above 30 °C (86.0 °F), and lows are cool and average below 15 °C (59.0 °F). In winters, highs average around 8 °C (46.4 °F), and lows go well below the freezing point. In this part of the country, there are many ski resorts, such as [Oukaimeden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ouka%C3%AFmeden "Oukaïmeden") and Mischliefen. Notable cities are [Ifrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ifrane "Ifrane"), [Azrou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azrou "Azrou") and [Boulmane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boulmane "Boulmane").
- **Semi-arid:** This type of climate is found in the south of the country and some parts of the east of the country, where rainfall is lower and annual precipitations are between 200 and 350 mm. However, one usually finds Mediterranean characteristics in those regions, such as the precipitation pattern and thermal attributes. Notable cities are [Agadir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agadir "Agadir"), [Marrakesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marrakesh "Marrakesh") and [Oujda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oujda "Oujda").[^127]

South of Agadir and east of Jerada near the Algerian borders, arid and desert climate starts to prevail.

Due to Morocco's proximity to the Sahara desert and the North Sea of the Atlantic Ocean, two phenomena occur to influence the regional seasonal temperatures, either by raising temperatures by 7–8 degrees Celsius when [sirocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirocco "Sirocco") blows from the east creating heatwaves, or by lowering temperatures by 7–8 degrees Celsius when cold damp air blows from the northwest, creating a coldwave or cold spell. However, these phenomena do not last for more than two to five days on average.[^129]

[Climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Morocco "Climate change in Morocco") is expected to significantly impact Morocco on multiple dimensions. As a coastal country with hot and arid climates, environmental impacts are likely to be wide and varied. As of the 2019 [Climate Change Performance Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_Change_Performance_Index "Climate Change Performance Index"), Morocco was ranked second in [preparedness behind Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Sweden "Climate change in Sweden").[^130]

### Biodiversity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Portrait_of_a_father.jpg/250px-Portrait_of_a_father.jpg)

An adult male Barbary macaque carrying his offspring, a behaviour rarely found in other primates

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Caracal_-_Flickr_-_Ragnhild_%26_Neil_Crawford_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Caracal_-_Flickr_-_Ragnhild_%26_Neil_Crawford_%28cropped%29.jpg)

The Caracal

Morocco has a wide range of [biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity "Biodiversity"). It is part of the [Mediterranean basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin "Mediterranean Basin"), an area with exceptional concentrations of endemic species undergoing rapid rates of habitat loss, and is therefore considered to be a hotspot for conservation priority.[^131] [Avifauna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bird "Bird") are notably variant.[^132] The avifauna of Morocco includes a total of 454 species, five of which have been [introduced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduced_species "Introduced species") by humans, and 156 are rarely or accidentally seen.[^133] Morocco is home to six terrestrial ecoregions: [Mediterranean conifer and mixed forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_conifer_and_mixed_forests "Mediterranean conifer and mixed forests"), [Mediterranean High Atlas juniper steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_High_Atlas_juniper_steppe "Mediterranean High Atlas juniper steppe"), [Mediterranean acacia-argania dry woodlands and succulent thickets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_acacia-argania_dry_woodlands_and_succulent_thickets "Mediterranean acacia-argania dry woodlands and succulent thickets"), [Mediterranean dry woodlands and steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_dry_woodlands_and_steppe "Mediterranean dry woodlands and steppe"), [Mediterranean woodlands and forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_woodlands_and_forests "Mediterranean woodlands and forests") and [North Saharan steppe and woodlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Saharan_steppe_and_woodlands "North Saharan steppe and woodlands").[^134]

The [Barbary lion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_lion "Barbary lion"), hunted to extinction in the wild, was a subspecies native to Morocco and is a national emblem.[^8] The last Barbary lion in the wild was shot in the Atlas Mountains in 1922.[^135] The other two primary predators of northern Africa, the [Atlas bear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_bear "Atlas bear") and [Barbary leopard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_leopard "Barbary leopard"), are now extinct and critically endangered, respectively. Relic populations of the [West African crocodile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_African_crocodile "West African crocodile") persisted in the [Draa river](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Draa_river "Draa river") until the 20th century.[^136] The Barbary macaque, a primate endemic to Morocco and Algeria, is also facing extinction due to offtake for trade [^137] human interruption, urbanisation, wood and real estate expansion that diminish forested area—the macaque's habitat.

Trade of animals and plants for food, pets, medicinal purposes, souvenirs and photo props is common across Morocco, despite laws making much of it illegal.[^138] [^139] This trade is unregulated and causing unknown reductions of wild populations of native Moroccan wildlife. Because of the proximity of northern Morocco to Europe, species such as cacti, tortoises, mammal skins, and high-value birds (falcons and bustards) are harvested in various parts of the country and exported in appreciable quantities, with especially large volumes of eel harvested – 60 tons exported to the Far East in the period 2009‒2011.[^140]

## Government and politics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/King_Mohammed_VI_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-King_Mohammed_VI_%28cropped%29.jpg)

The King of Morocco, Mohammed VI

As reported in 2022 by *[The Economist Democracy Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economist_Democracy_Index "The Economist Democracy Index")*, Morocco is classified as a [hybrid regime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybrid_regime "Hybrid regime"), ranking third in the [MENA region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MENA_region "MENA region") and 95th globally in democracy quality.[^141] Morocco was given a "difficult" ranking in the 2023 *[World Press Freedom Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Press_Freedom_Index "World Press Freedom Index")*.[^142]

Following the March 1998 elections, a coalition government headed by the [parliamentary opposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_opposition "Parliamentary opposition") and [socialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist "Socialist") leader [Abderrahmane Youssoufi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abderrahmane_Youssoufi "Abderrahmane Youssoufi") was formed, composed largely of ministers drawn from opposition parties. Prime Minister Youssoufi's government was the country's first-ever government drawn primarily from opposition parties. Also, it represented the first opportunity for a coalition of socialists, [left-of-centre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre-left_politics "Centre-left politics"), and nationalist parties to be included in the government until October 2002. It was also the first time in modern Arab political history that a parliamentary opposition coalition assumed power following an election. As of November 2025, the current government is headed by [Aziz Akhannouch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aziz_Akhannouch "Aziz Akhannouch").

The [Constitution of Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Morocco "Constitution of Morocco") provides for a monarchy with a [Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Morocco "Parliament of Morocco") and an independent [judiciary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judiciary "Judiciary"). With the [2011 constitutional reforms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan_constitutional_referendum,_2011 "Moroccan constitutional referendum, 2011"), the King of Morocco retains fewer executive powers, whereas the prime minister's powers have been enlarged.[^143] [^144] The constitution endows the king with honorific powers, among others; he serves as both the country's secular political leader and [Amir al-Mu'minin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amir_al-Mu%27minin "Amir al-Mu'minin") (Commander of the Faithful), directly descended from the prophet [Mohammed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed "Mohammed"). He chairs the Council of Ministers, selects the prime minister from the party with the most parliamentary seats, and, based on the latter's recommendations, appoints government members.

The constitution of 1996 theoretically allowed the king to terminate the tenure of any minister, and after consultation with the heads of the higher and lower Assemblies, to dissolve the parliament, suspend the constitution, call for new elections, or rule by decree. The only time this happened was in 1965. The King is formally the commander-in-chief of the armed forces.

### Legislative branch

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Parliament_of_morocco.jpg/250px-Parliament_of_morocco.jpg)

The legislature's building in Rabat

Since the 1996 constitutional reform, the bicameral legislature consists of two chambers. The [House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Representatives_\(Morocco\) "House of Representatives (Morocco)") (*Majlis an-Nuwwâb/Assemblée des Répresentants*) has 395 members elected for a five-year term, 305 elected in multi-seat [constituencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constituency "Constituency") and 90 in national lists consisting of women and youth.

The [House of Councillors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Councillors_\(Morocco\) "House of Councillors (Morocco)") (*Majlis al-Mustasharin*) has 120 members, elected for a six-year term. Seventy-two members are elected at the regional level, 20 members are elected from trade unions, eight seats from professional organisations, and 20 from wage-earners.[^145]

Although relatively limited, the Parliament's powers were expanded under a series of constitutional revisions (1992, 1996, and 2011), to include [budgetary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budget "Budget") matters, approvals of [bills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_\(proposed_law\) "Bill (proposed law)"), questioning ministers, and establishing ad hoc commissions of inquiry to investigate the state's actions. The Lower Chamber of Parliament may dissolve the government through a [vote of no confidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vote_of_no_confidence "Vote of no confidence").[^146] [^147] [^148]

The latest parliamentary elections were held on [8 September 2021](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Moroccan_general_election "2021 Moroccan general election"). During the elections, voter turnout was estimated at 50.35% (among registered voters).[^149] [^150]

### Foreign relations

Morocco is a member of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") and belongs to the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union") (AU), [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League"), [Arab Maghreb Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Maghreb_Union "Arab Maghreb Union") (UMA), [Organisation of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation") (OIC), the [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement") and the [Community of Sahel–Saharan States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_of_Sahel%E2%80%93Saharan_States "Community of Sahel–Saharan States") (CEN–SAD). Morocco's relationships vary greatly between African, Arab, and Western states. Morocco has had strong ties to the West in order to gain economic and political benefits.[^153] France and Spain remain the primary trade partners, as well as the primary creditors and foreign investors in Morocco. Among total foreign investments in Morocco, the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") accounts for approximately 73.5%, whereas the Arab world accounts for 19.3%. [Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_states_of_the_Persian_Gulf "Arab states of the Persian Gulf") and neighbouring [Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maghreb "Maghreb") countries are becoming more involved in large-scale projects in Morocco.[^154]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Ceuta-melilla.png/330px-Ceuta-melilla.png)

Morocco claims sovereignty over Spanish enclaves of Ceuta and Melilla.

In 2002, a [dispute with Spain over the small island of Perejil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perejil_Island_crisis "Perejil Island crisis") arose, which brought attention to the issue of the sovereignty of [Melilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melilla "Melilla") and [Ceuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceuta "Ceuta").[^155] These small enclaves on the Mediterranean coast are surrounded by Morocco and have been under Spanish administration for centuries.

In 2004, the [George W. Bush administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_W._Bush_administration "George W. Bush administration") granted Morocco the status of [major non-NATO ally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_non-NATO_ally "Major non-NATO ally").[^156] Morocco was the first country in the world to recognise US sovereignty, in 1777. After gaining independence, [Morocco established strong ties with the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco%E2%80%93United_States_relations "Morocco–United States relations"), receiving significant economic and military aid.[^85] This partnership flourished during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), with Morocco becoming a key ally against communist expansion in [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa"). In return, the US supported Morocco's territorial ambitions and efforts to modernise its economy. Morocco received more than $400 million in American aid between 1957 and 1963, which elevated it to the fifth-largest recipient of US agricultural assistance by 1966. The long-lasting relationship between the two nations has endured, with the US remaining one of Morocco's top allies. Additionally, Morocco is included in the European Union's [European Neighbourhood Policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Neighbourhood_Policy "European Neighbourhood Policy") (ENP), which aims at bringing the [EU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") and its neighbours closer.[^157]

Morocco's membership in the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union") has been marked by significant events. In 1984, Morocco withdrew from the organisation after it admitted the [Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic "Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic") in 1982 without conducting a referendum of self-determination in the disputed territory of Western Sahara.[^158] [^159] This decision was made unilaterally by Morocco. However, in 2017, Morocco rejoined the AU, signalling a shift in its diplomatic stance. In November 2020, [Brahim Ghali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahim_Ghali "Brahim Ghali"), leader of the Polisario Front and the Sahrawi president, unilaterally ended a 29-year ceasefire agreement with Morocco, which the United Nations had overseen.[^160] In December 2020, Morocco had started to pursue military cooperation with Israel from a normalisation agreement. Algeria backs the Polisario Front of Morocco's breakaway state, the [Western Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Sahara "Western Sahara").[^161] In August 2021, Algeria severed diplomatic relations with Morocco.[^162] Algerian authorities have accused Rabat of supporting the [Movement for the Self-Determination of Kabylie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Movement_for_the_Self-Determination_of_Kabylie "Movement for the Self-Determination of Kabylie") (MAK), which it classifies as a terrorist organisation.[^162]

### Western Sahara status

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Westernsaharamap.png/250px-Westernsaharamap.png)

Morocco annexed Western Sahara in 1975. The Polisario Front control the territory east of the Moroccan berm (wall).

The status of the [Saguia el-Hamra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saguia_el-Hamra "Saguia el-Hamra") and [Río de Oro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_Oro "Río de Oro") regions is disputed. The [Western Sahara War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Sahara_War "Western Sahara War") saw the [Polisario Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polisario_Front "Polisario Front"), the Sahrawi rebel national liberation movement, battling both Morocco and Mauritania between 1976 and a ceasefire in 1991. The Moroccan government has stated that their claimed area of Western Sahara is referred to as the "Southern Provinces".[^163] A United Nations mission, [MINURSO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Mission_for_the_Referendum_in_Western_Sahara "United Nations Mission for the Referendum in Western Sahara"), is tasked with organising a referendum on whether the territory should become independent or recognised as a part of Morocco.

Part of the territory, the [Free Zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_Zone_\(Western_Sahara_region\) "Free Zone (Western Sahara region)"), is a mostly uninhabited area that the Polisario Front controls as the [Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic "Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic"). Its administrative headquarters are located in [Tindouf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tindouf "Tindouf"), Algeria. As of 2006, no [UN member state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_state_of_the_United_Nations "Member state of the United Nations") had recognised Moroccan sovereignty over Western Sahara.[^164] In 2006, the government of Morocco suggested [autonomous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomy_for_Western_Sahara "Autonomy for Western Sahara") status for the region through the Moroccan [Royal Advisory Council for Saharan Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Advisory_Council_for_Saharan_Affairs "Royal Advisory Council for Saharan Affairs") (CORCAS). The project was presented to the [United Nations Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council") in mid-April 2007. Moroccan allies, including the United States, France, and Spain, supported the proposal.[^165] The Security Council has called upon the parties to enter into direct and unconditional negotiations to reach a mutually accepted political solution.[^166]

In 2020, the United States became the first Western country to back Morocco's contested sovereignty over the disputed Western Sahara region, on the agreement that Morocco would simultaneously normalise [relations with Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel%E2%80%93Morocco_relations "Israel–Morocco relations").[^167]

### Military

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/FREMM_Mohammed_VI_-_Lorient_2013-05.JPG/250px-FREMM_Mohammed_VI_-_Lorient_2013-05.JPG)

Mohammed VI, a FREMM multipurpose frigate of the Royal Moroccan Navy

Morocco's military consists of the Royal Armed Forces—this includes the [Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Moroccan_Army "Royal Moroccan Army") (the largest branch), the [Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Moroccan_Navy "Royal Moroccan Navy"), the [Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Moroccan_Air_Force "Royal Moroccan Air Force"), the [Royal Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan_Royal_Guard "Moroccan Royal Guard"), the [Royal Gendarmerie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Moroccan_Gendarmerie "Royal Moroccan Gendarmerie") and the [Auxiliary Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auxiliary_Forces "Auxiliary Forces"). Internal security is generally effective, and acts of political violence are rare (with one exception, the [2003 Casablanca bombings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2003_Casablanca_bombings "2003 Casablanca bombings") which killed 45 people [^168]).

The UN maintains a small observer force in Western Sahara, where a large number of Moroccan troops are stationed. The Sahrawi [Polisario Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polisario_Front "Polisario Front") maintains an active militia of an estimated 5,000 fighters in Western Sahara and has engaged in intermittent warfare with Moroccan forces since the 1970s.

### Human rights

During the early 1960s to the late 1980s, under the leadership of [Hassan II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassan_II "Hassan II"), Morocco had one of the worst human rights records in both Africa and the world. Government repression of political dissent was widespread during Hassan II's leadership, until it dropped sharply in the mid-1990s. The decades during which abuses were committed are referred to as the [Years of Lead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Years_of_Lead_\(Morocco\) "Years of Lead (Morocco)") (*les années de plomb*), and included [forced disappearances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forced_disappearance "Forced disappearance"), assassinations of government opponents and protesters, and secret internment camps such as [Tazmamart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tazmamart "Tazmamart"). To examine abuses committed during the reign of [King Hassan II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassan_II_of_Morocco "Hassan II of Morocco") (1961–1999), the government under King Mohammed VI established the [Equity and Reconciliation Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equity_and_Reconciliation_Commission "Equity and Reconciliation Commission") (IER).[^169] [^170]

According to a [Human Rights Watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch "Human Rights Watch") annual report in 2016, Moroccan authorities restricted the rights to peaceful expression, association, and assembly through several laws. The authorities continue to prosecute both printed and online media that criticise the government or the [king](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_VI_of_Morocco "Mohammed VI of Morocco") (or the royal family).[^171] There are also persistent allegations of violence against both [Sahrawi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahrawi_people "Sahrawi people") pro- [independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic "Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic") and pro- [Polisario](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polisario "Polisario") [demonstrators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_Intifada_\(Western_Sahara\) "Independence Intifada (Western Sahara)") [^172] in Western Sahara; a disputed territory which is occupied by and considered by Morocco as part of its [Southern Provinces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan-occupied_Western_Sahara "Moroccan-occupied Western Sahara"). Morocco has been accused of detaining Sahrawi pro-independence activists as prisoners of conscience.[^173]

[Homosexual acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LGBT_rights_by_country_or_territory "LGBT rights by country or territory"), as well as extra-marital sex, are unlawful in Morocco, and can be punishable by six months to three years of imprisonment.[^174] [^175] It is illegal to [proselytise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proselytism "Proselytism") for any religion other than [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Morocco "Islam in Morocco") (Article 220 of the Moroccan Penal Code), and that crime is punishable by a maximum of 15 years of imprisonment.[^176] [^177] Violence against women and sexual harassment have been criminalised. The penalty can be from one month to five years, with fines ranging from $200 to $1,000.[^178] It is also a criminal offence in Morocco to undermine the monarchy; in August 2023, a Moroccan resident of Qatar was sentenced to five years' imprisonment for criticising the King's policy decisions on Facebook.[^179]

## Economy

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Casablanca_Finance_City_37.jpg/250px-Casablanca_Finance_City_37.jpg)

Casablanca Finance City

Morocco's economy is considered a [liberal economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_economy "Liberal economy"), governed by the [law of supply and demand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supply_and_demand "Supply and demand"). Since 1993, the country has followed a policy of [privatisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privatization "Privatization") of certain economic sectors which used to be in the hands of the [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Morocco "Politics of Morocco").[^180] Morocco has become a major player in African economic affairs,[^181] and is the [sixth largest economy in Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_African_countries_by_GDP_\(PPP\) "List of African countries by GDP (PPP)") by GDP (PPP). Morocco was ranked as the first African country by the [Economist Intelligence Unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economist_Intelligence_Unit "Economist Intelligence Unit") 's [quality-of-life index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quality-of-life_index "Quality-of-life index"), ahead of [South Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Africa "South Africa").[^182] However, in the years since that first-place ranking was given, Morocco has slipped into fourth place behind [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt").

In 2025, Morocco became Africa's leading industrial economy according to the [African Development Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Development_Bank "African Development Bank"), overtaking South Africa for the first time since the ranking began.[^29]

Government reforms and steady yearly growth in the region of 4–5% from 2000 to 2007, including 4.9% year-on-year growth in 2003–2007 helped the Moroccan economy to become much more robust compared to a few years earlier. For 2012, the World Bank forecast a rate of 4% growth for Morocco and 4.2% for following year, 2013.[^183] Between 2000 and 2019, the share of Moroccan workers in agriculture declined, while those that are in industry increased.[^184]

### Tourism

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Jamaa_el_Fna_%287346063132%29.jpg/250px-Jamaa_el_Fna_%287346063132%29.jpg)

The Jemaa el-Fnaa in Marrakesh

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Artisanat_marocain_24-10-1971_JPG_Jean-Pol_Grandmont.JPG/250px-Artisanat_marocain_24-10-1971_JPG_Jean-Pol_Grandmont.JPG)

Amazigh fibula, ceramic vases, Koummya (dagger) and typical lantern

Tourism is one of the most important sectors in the Moroccan economy.[^185] It is a strong tourist industry focused on the country's coast, culture, and history. In 2022, tourism in Morocco had surpassed the average number of visitors in the 2010s, while setting an all-time high in 2023 with 14.5 million international tourist arrivals and MAD 104.7 billion in receipts.[^184] In 2010, the government launched its Vision 2020, which plans to make Morocco one of the top 20 tourist destinations in the world and to double the annual number of international arrivals to 20 million by 2020.[^186] In November 2024, Morocco had nearly 16 million tourists visiting that contributed to 7% of its GDP.[^187] [^184]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Bab_Bou_Jeloud_%28Fes%2C_Marocco%29.jpg/250px-Bab_Bou_Jeloud_%28Fes%2C_Marocco%29.jpg)

Bab Bou Jeloud Gates, Fes.

Tourism is increasingly focused on Morocco's culture, such as its ancient cities. The modern tourist industry capitalises on Morocco's ancient and Islamic sites and on its landscape and cultural history. 60% of Morocco's tourists visit for its culture and heritage. Agadir is a major coastal resort and has a third of all Moroccan bed nights.[^188] It is a base for tours to the Atlas Mountains.[^188] Other resorts in northern Morocco are also very popular.[^189] [^190]

Large government-sponsored marketing campaigns to attract tourists advertised Morocco as an inexpensive and exotic, yet safe, place for tourists. Most of the visitors to Morocco continue to be European, with French nationals making up almost 20% of all visitors. Most Europeans visit between April and August.[^191] Casablanca is the major cruise port in Morocco, and has a developed market for tourists in Morocco. The Majorelle botanical garden in Marrakesh is a popular tourist attraction. It was bought by the fashion designer Yves Saint-Laurent and Pierre Bergé in 1980.[^192] As of 2006, activity and adventure tourism in the Atlas and Rif Mountains are the fastest growth area in Moroccan tourism. These locations have walking and trekking opportunities from late March to mid-November. The Moroccan government is investing in trekking circuits. It is also developing desert tourism, in competition with [Tunisia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunisia "Tunisia").[^193]

### Agriculture

[Agriculture in Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture_in_Morocco "Agriculture in Morocco") employs 23.4% of the nation's workforce, making it one of the largest employers in the country.[^194] In the rainy sections of the northwest, [barley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barley "Barley"), [wheat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wheat "Wheat"), and other cereals can be raised without irrigation. On the Atlantic coast, where there are extensive plains, [olives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olive "Olive"), [citrus fruits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citrus_fruits "Citrus fruits"), and [wine grapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wine_grapes "Wine grapes") are grown, largely with water supplied by artesian wells. [Livestock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livestock "Livestock") are raised and forests yield cork, cabinet wood, and building materials. Part of the maritime population fishes for its livelihood. [Agadir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agadir "Agadir"), [Essaouira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essaouira "Essaouira"), [El Jadida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Jadida "El Jadida"), and [Larache](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larache "Larache") are among the important fishing harbors.[^195] Both the agriculture and fishing industries are [expected to be severely impacted by climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Morocco "Climate change in Morocco").[^196] [^197]

Moroccan agricultural production also consists of [oranges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orange_\(fruit\) "Orange (fruit)"), [tomatoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tomato "Tomato"), [potatoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potato "Potato"), olives, and [olive oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olive_oil "Olive oil"). High quality agricultural products are usually exported to Europe. Morocco produces enough food for domestic consumption except for grains, [sugar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar "Sugar"), [coffee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee "Coffee") and [tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea "Tea"). More than 40% of Morocco's consumption of grains and flour is imported from the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France").

The agriculture industry in Morocco enjoyed a complete tax exemption until 2013. Many Moroccan critics said that rich farmers and large agricultural companies were taking too much benefit of not paying the taxes and that poor farmers were struggling with high costs and getting little support from the state. In 2014, as part of the Finance Law, it was decided that agricultural companies with a turnover greater than MAD 5 million would pay progressive corporate income taxes.[^198]

### Infrastructure

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Al_Boraq_RGV2N2_at_Tanger_Ville.jpg/250px-Al_Boraq_RGV2N2_at_Tanger_Ville.jpg)

Al Boraq RGV2N2 high-speed trainset at Tanger-Ville railway station in November 2018

According to the [Global Competitiveness Report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Competitiveness_Report "Global Competitiveness Report") of 2019, Morocco Ranked 32nd in the world in terms of Roads, 16th in Sea, 45th in Air and 64th in Railways. This gives Morocco the best infrastructure rankings in the African continent.[^199] To meet the growing domestic demand, the Moroccan government invested more than $15 billion from 2010 to 2015 in upgrading its basic infrastructure.[^200] Morocco also has the largest port in Africa and the Mediterranean, [Tanger-Med](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanger-Med "Tanger-Med"), which is ranked the 18th in the world with a handling capacity of over 9 million containers. It is situated in the Tangier free economic zone and serves as a logistics hub for Africa and the world.[^201]

In 2014, Morocco began the construction of the first [^202] high-speed railway system in Africa linking the cities of Tangier and Casablanca. It was inaugurated in 2018 by the King following over a decade of planning and construction by Moroccan national railway company [Office National des Chemins de Fer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ONCF "ONCF") (ONCF).[^203] It is the first phase of what is planned to eventually be a 1,500 kilometres (930 mi) high-speed rail network in Morocco. An extension of the line to Marrakesh is already being planned. The [Moroccan Ministry of Equipment, Transport and Logistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Equipment,_Transport_and_Logistics_\(Morocco\)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Ministry of Equipment, Transport and Logistics (Morocco) (page does not exist)") aims to build an additional 3,380 kilometres of expressway and 2,100 kilometres of highway by 2030 with [SNCF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNCF "SNCF"); the project came at an expected cost of US$9.6 billion. The project also involved purchasing 18 high-speed trains and 150 multi-service trains.[^204]

### Energy

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Isofoton_Marruecos.JPG/250px-Isofoton_Marruecos.JPG)

Solar cell panels in eastern Morocco

In 2008, about 56% of Morocco's electricity supply was provided by coal.[^205] However, as forecasts indicate that energy requirements in Morocco will rise 6% per year between 2012 and 2050,[^206] a new law passed encouraging Moroccans to look for ways to diversify the energy supply, including more [renewable resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renewable_resources "Renewable resources"). The Moroccan government has launched a project to build a [solar thermal energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy "Solar thermal energy") [power plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_plant "Power plant") [^207] and is also looking into the use of natural gas as a potential source of revenue for Morocco's government.[^206]

Morocco has embarked upon the construction of large [solar energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_energy "Solar energy") farms to lessen dependence on fossil fuels, and to eventually export electricity to [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe").[^208] On 17 April 2022, Rabat-Moroccan agency for solar energy (Masen) and the ministry of energy transition and sustainable development announced the launch of phase one of the mega project Nor II solar energy plant which is a multi-site solar energy project with a total capacity set at 400 [megawatts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt "Watt") (MN).[^209]

### Narcotics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Cannabis_Fields_in_Ketama_Tidighine_mountain%2C_Morocco.jpg/250px-Cannabis_Fields_in_Ketama_Tidighine_mountain%2C_Morocco.jpg)

Cannabis field at Ketama Tidighine mountain, Morocco

Since the 7th century, [cannabis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cannabis_\(drug\) "Cannabis (drug)") has been cultivated in the [Rif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rif "Rif") region.[^210] In 2004, according to the UN World Drugs Report, cultivation and transformation of cannabis represents 0.57% of the national GDP of Morocco in 2002.[^211] According to a French Ministry of the Interior 2006 report, 80% of the cannabis resin ([hashish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hashish "Hashish")) consumed in Europe comes from the Rif region in Morocco, which is mostly mountainous terrain in the north of Morocco, also hosting plains that are very fertile and expanding from Melwiyya River and [Ras Kebdana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ras_Kebdana "Ras Kebdana") in the East to Tangier and [Cape Spartel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_Spartel "Cape Spartel") in the West. Also, the region extends from the Mediterranean in the south, home of the Wergha River, to the north.[^212] In addition, Morocco is a transit point for cocaine from South America destined for Western Europe.[^213]

### Water supply and sanitation

Water supply and sanitation in Morocco are provided by a wide array of utilities. They range from private companies in the largest city— [Casablanca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casablanca "Casablanca"), Rabat, and two other cities—to public municipal utilities in 13 other cities, as well as a national electricity and water company.[^214] Morocco's Office National de l'Eau Potable (ONEP) is in charge of bulk water supply in about 500 towns.[^215] According to a study by the National Liquid Sanitation Master Plan (SNDAL) that started in 1994, only 15 of their 63 treatment plants are operational, and out of approximately 500 million cubic meters of wastewater generated annually, 95% is discharged untreated into natural water bodies.[^214]

There have been substantial improvements in access to water supply, and to a lesser extent to sanitation, over the past fifteen years. Remaining challenges include a low level of wastewater treatment (only 13% of collected wastewater is being treated), lack of house connections in the poorest urban neighbourhoods and limited sustainability of rural systems (20% of rural systems are estimated not to function). In 2005, a National Sanitation Programme was approved that aims at treating 60% of collected wastewater and connecting 80% of urban households to sewers by 2020.[^216] The issue of lack of water connections for some of the urban poor is being addressed as part of the [National Human Development Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Human_Development_Initiative "National Human Development Initiative"), under which residents of informal settlements have received land titles and have fees waived that are normally paid to utilities in order to connect to the water and sewer network. An investment programme of about MAD 15 billion was made to cover the centres managed by ONEP from 2003–2017.[^214] Japanese and Canadian cooperators and [AFESD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Fund_for_Economic_and_Social_Development "Arab Fund for Economic and Social Development") have also financed several projects for ONEP.[^214]

Though, between 1960 and 2020, the per capita availability of [renewable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renewable_resource "Renewable resource") water resources went from 2,560 m <sup>3</sup> to about 620 m <sup>3</sup> per person annually. The World Bank has reported that they have supported the [Noor Solar Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ouarzazate_Solar_Power_Station "Ouarzazate Solar Power Station") project in Morocco with US$700 million in financing the project "To address water scarcity and its impacts on agriculture." [^217]

### Science and technology

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Logo_UM6P.jpg/330px-Logo_UM6P.jpg)

Campus of the Mohammed VI Polytechnic University in Benguerir

The [Moroccan government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan_government "Moroccan government") has been implementing reforms to improve the quality of education and make research more responsive to socio-economic needs. In May 2009, Morocco's prime minister, [Abbas El Fassi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbas_El_Fassi "Abbas El Fassi"), announced that investment in science and technology would rise from US$620,000 in 2008 to US$8.5 million (69 million Moroccan dirhams) in 2009 to finance the laboratories construction, training courses for researchers and a scholarship programme for science during a meeting at the National Centre for Scientific and Technical Research.[^218] Morocco was ranked 67th in the [Global Innovation Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Innovation_Index "Global Innovation Index") in 2025,[^219] [^220] an increase in ranking from 2020 at 75th.[^221]

The *Moroccan Innovation Strategy* was launched at the country's first National Innovation Summit in June 2009 by the Ministry of Industry, Commerce, Investment and the Digital Economy. The *Moroccan Innovation Strategy* fixed the target of producing 1,000 Moroccan patents and creating 200 innovative start-ups by 2014. In 2012, Moroccan inventors applied for 197 patents, up from 152 two years earlier. In 2011, the Ministry of Industry, Commerce and New Technologies created a Moroccan Club of Innovation, in partnership with the Moroccan Office of Industrial and Commercial Property. The idea is to create a network of players in innovation to help them develop innovative projects.[^222]

The Ministry of Higher Education and Scientific Research is supporting research in advanced technologies. The [Moroccan Phosphate Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OCP_Group "OCP Group") (*Office chérifien des phosphates*) has invested in a project to develop a [smart city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smart_city "Smart city"), King Mohammed VI Green City, around Mohammed VI University located between Casablanca and Marrakesh,[^223] at a cost of DH 4.7 billion (circa US$479 million).[^222] [^224] In 2012, the [Hassan II Academy of Sciences and Technologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassan_II_Academy_of_Sciences_and_Technologies "Hassan II Academy of Sciences and Technologies") identified a number of sectors where Morocco has a comparative advantage and skilled human capital, including mining, fisheries, food chemistry and new technologies. It also identified a number of strategic sectors, such as renewable energies, health sectors, the environment and geosciences.[^222] [^225]

On 20 May 2015 – less than a year post its inception – the Higher Council for Education, Training and Scientific Research presented a report to the King offering a *Vision for Education in Morocco 2015–2030*. The report advocated making education egalitarian and, thus, accessible to the greatest number. The report also recommended developing an integrated national innovation system which would be financed by gradually increasing the share of GDP devoted to research and development (R&D) from 0.73% of GDP in 2010 to '1% in the short term, 1.5% by 2025 and 2% by 2030'.[^222] As of 2015, Morocco had three technoparks. Since the first technopark was established in Rabat in 2005, a second has been set up in Casablanca, followed, in 2015, by a third in Tangier. The technoparks host start-ups and small and medium-sized enterprises specialising in [information and communication technologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_and_communications_technology "Information and communications technology") (ICTs), 'green' technologies (namely, [environmentally friendly technologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_technology "Environmental technology")) and cultural industries.[^222] According to the *Office Marocain de la Propriété Industrielle et Commerciale*, [patent filing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patent_application "Patent application") in Morocco grew by 167% during the period 2015–2019.[^226]

In 2024, Morocco's population is among the top four most-connected to the Internet in continental Africa, by number of population.[^227] In 2022, the number of Internet users in Morocco reached around 31.6 million.[^228] Later, as of January 2024, Morocco had approximately 34.5 million internet users; it has a [penetration rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Internet_usage "Global Internet usage") of about 90.7%.[^229] Morocco has several Internet-related projects; an example of such is the National Digital Development Strategy 2030.[^230] In 2024, as part of another program called the Connected Campus, the American wireless network provider [Cambium Networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambium_Networks "Cambium Networks") deployed 18,000 [Wi-Fi access points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wireless_access_point "Wireless access point") for public universities in Morocco.[^231]

## Demographics

### Population

Morocco has a population of around 37,076,584 inhabitants (2021 estimate).[^232] [^233] Morocco's population was 11.6 million in 1960.[^234] In 2024, 49.7% of the population is female, while 50.3% of it is male.[^229] According to the 2014 Morocco population census, there were around 84,000 immigrants in the country. Of these foreign-born residents, most were of [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_people "French people") origin, followed by individuals mainly from various nations in West Africa and Algeria.[^235] There are also a number of foreign residents of [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_people "Spanish people") origin. Some of them are descendants of colonial settlers, who primarily work for European multinational companies, while others are married to Moroccans or are retirees. Prior to independence, Morocco was home to half a million [Europeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Moroccans "European Moroccans"), most of whom were [Christians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian "Christian").[^236] Also, prior to independence, Morocco was home to 250,000 Spaniards.[^237] Morocco's once prominent [Jewish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jews "Jews") minority has decreased significantly since its peak of 265,000 in 1948, declining to around 3,500 in 2022.[^238]

Morocco has a large [diaspora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan_diaspora "Moroccan diaspora"), most of which is located in France, which has reportedly more than one million Moroccans (up to the third generation). There are also large Moroccan communities in Spain (about 700,000 Moroccans),[^239] the Netherlands (360,000), and Belgium (300,000).[^240] Other large communities can be found in Italy, Canada, the United States, and Israel, where [Moroccan Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moroccan_Jews "Moroccan Jews") are thought to constitute the second-largest Jewish ethnic subgroup.[^241]
