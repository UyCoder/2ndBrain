**Algeria**,[^4] officially the **People's Democratic Republic of Algeria**,[^5] is a country in the [Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maghreb "Maghreb") region of [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa"). Spanning over 2,381,741 square kilometres (919,595 sq mi), it is the [largest country in Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_African_countries_by_area "List of African countries by area") and the [tenth largest in the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_area "List of countries and dependencies by area"). It is bordered to [the northeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria%E2%80%93Tunisia_border "Algeria–Tunisia border") by [Tunisia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunisia "Tunisia"); to [the east](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria%E2%80%93Libya_border "Algeria–Libya border") by [Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya "Libya"); to [the southeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria%E2%80%93Niger_border "Algeria–Niger border") by [Niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger "Niger"); to [the southwest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria%E2%80%93Western_Sahara_border "Algeria–Western Sahara border") by [Mali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mali "Mali"), [Mauritania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauritania "Mauritania"), and [Western Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Sahara "Western Sahara"); to [the west](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria%E2%80%93Morocco_border "Algeria–Morocco border") by [Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco "Morocco"); and to the north by the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea"). With a population of over 47 million, Algeria is the [tenth-most populous country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_African_countries_by_population "List of African countries by population") in Africa. Its capital and [largest city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Algeria "List of cities in Algeria") is [Algiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algiers "Algiers").

Algeria's territory has been a crossroads of cultures and civilizations for millennia, shaped first by indigenous [Amazigh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berbers "Berbers") societies, including the [Numidians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numidians "Numidians"), whose [kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numidia "Numidia") became one of the earliest major states in the region, as well as by [Phoenician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia"), [Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauretania_Caesariensis "Mauretania Caesariensis"), [Vandal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vandals "Vandals"), and [Byzantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire") influences. From the 7th century onward, the [spread of Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Muslim_conquests "Early Muslim conquests") and [the arrival of Arab tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_migrations_to_the_Maghreb "Arab migrations to the Maghreb") gradually transformed the region's linguistic, religious, and cultural landscape, contributing to the development of a distinct Algerian identity rooted in both Arab-Islamic and Amazigh heritage. Between the 8th and 15th centuries, Algeria was ruled by a succession of local Islamic dynasties including [Rustamids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rustamid_dynasty "Rustamid dynasty"), [Fatimids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimid_Caliphate "Fatimid Caliphate"), [Zirids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zirid_dynasty "Zirid dynasty"), [Hammadids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hammadid_dynasty "Hammadid dynasty"), [Almohads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almohad_Caliphate "Almohad Caliphate") and [Zayyanids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zayyanid_dynasty "Zayyanid dynasty"). In 1516, the [Regency of Algiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regency_of_Algiers "Regency of Algiers") was established, gradually emerging as a largely autonomous state owing nominal allegiance to the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"). After nearly three centuries as a major power in the Mediterranean, the country was [invaded by France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasion_of_Algiers_in_1830 "Invasion of Algiers in 1830") in 1830 and [formally annexed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Algeria "French Algeria") in 1848, though it was not fully [conquered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_conquest_of_Algeria "French conquest of Algeria") and [pacified](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacification_of_Algeria "Pacification of Algeria") until 1903. French rule brought [mass European settlement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pied-Noir "Pied-Noir") that displaced the local population; by mid-1870, indigenous Algerians declined by up to a third due to warfare, disease, and starvation.[^17] French colonization faced continuous Algerian resistance, from [Emir Abdelkader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emir_Abdelkader "Emir Abdelkader") 's campaigns and the [Mokrani Revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mokrani_Revolt "Mokrani Revolt") to the rise of nationalist movements in the 20th century. The [Sétif and Guelma massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A9tif_and_Guelma_massacre "Sétif and Guelma massacre") in 1945 catalysed local resistance that culminated in the outbreak of the [Algerian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_War "Algerian War") in 1954. Algeria [gained independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_Day_\(Algeria\) "Independence Day (Algeria)") in 1962. It descended into a [bloody civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_Civil_War "Algerian Civil War") from 1992 to 2002, remaining in an official state of emergency until the [2010–2012 Algerian protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010%E2%80%932012_Algerian_protests "2010–2012 Algerian protests") during the [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring").

Algeria has a semi-arid climate, with the [Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahara "Sahara") desert dominating most of the territory except for its [fertile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_fertility "Soil fertility") and mountainous north, where most of the population is concentrated. Its official languages are [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic "Modern Standard Arabic") and [Tamazight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Algerian_Berber "Standard Algerian Berber"), while the vast majority of the population speak the [Algerian dialect of Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_Arabic "Algerian Arabic"). The [usage of French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language_in_Algeria "French language in Algeria") persists, especially in media, education, and certain administrative matters, but has no official status. Most Algerians identify as [Arabs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabs "Arabs"), while [Berbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berbers "Berbers") form a sizeable minority. [Sunni Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") is the official religion and practised by 99 percent of the population.[^18]

Algeria is a [semi-presidential republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-presidential_system "Semi-presidential system") composed of [69 provinces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provinces_of_Algeria "Provinces of Algeria") ([*wilayas*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilaya "Wilaya")) and [1,541 communes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communes_of_Algeria "Communes of Algeria"). It is a [regional power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power") in North Africa and a [middle power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_power "Middle power") in global affairs, with the [largest military budget](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_with_highest_military_expenditures "List of countries with highest military expenditures") in Africa. As of 2025, Algeria has the highest [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index") in continental Africa, and the [third largest economy in Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_African_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of African countries by GDP (nominal)"), due mostly to its large petroleum and natural gas reserves, which are the [sixteenth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_proven_oil_reserves "List of countries by proven oil reserves") and [ninth largest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_natural_gas_proven_reserves "List of countries by natural gas proven reserves") in the world, respectively. [Sonatrach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonatrach "Sonatrach"), the national oil company, is the largest company in Africa and a major supplier of natural gas to Europe. Algeria is a member of the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union"), the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League"), the [OIC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation"), [OPEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OPEC "OPEC"), the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), and the [Arab Maghreb Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Maghreb_Union "Arab Maghreb Union"), of which it is a founding member.

## Name

![Page of typeset book](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Civitates-Orbis-Terrarium-1575.jpg/250px-Civitates-Orbis-Terrarium-1575.jpg)

"Algeria" page in the Civitates Orbis Terrarium of 1575

Different forms of the name Algeria include: [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"): الجزائر, [romanised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic "Romanization of Arabic"): *al-Jazāʾir*, [Algerian Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_Arabic_language "Algerian Arabic language"): دزاير, romanised: *dzāyer*, [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language"): *l'Algérie*. The country's full name is officially the *People's Democratic Republic of Algeria* [^19] (Arabic: الجمهورية الجزائرية الديمقراطية الشعبية, romanised: *al-Jumhūriyah al-Jazāʾiriyah ad-Dīmuqrāṭiyah ash‑Shaʿbiyah*; French: *République algérienne démocratique et populaire*, abbr. RADP; Standard Algerian Berber: Tagduda tazzayrit tamagdayt taɣerfant, ⵜⴰⴳⴷⵓⴷⴰ ⵜⴰⵣⵣⴰⵢⵔⵉⵜ ⵜⴰⵎⴰⴳⴷⴰⵢⵜ ⵜⴰⵖⴻⵔⴼⴰⵏⵜ [^20] [^21] [^22] [^6]).

### Etymology

The name Algeria ultimately derives from the city of [Algiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algiers "Algiers"), whose Arabic name, *al-Jazāʾir* (الجزائر, meaning “the islands”), referred to the small islands once located off the city’s coast.[^24] The term itself was a shortened form of *Jazāʾir Banī Mazghanna* (جزائر بني مزغنة, “the islands of the Bani Mazghanna”), named after a local Berber tribe. The city was founded in 950 by the [Sanhaja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanhaja "Sanhaja") Berber Emir [Buluggin ibn Ziri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buluggin_ibn_Ziri "Buluggin ibn Ziri") on the site of the ancient city of [Icosium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosium "Icosium"), and its name later appeared in the writings of medieval Muslim geographers such as [Al-Bakri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Bakri "Al-Bakri").[^25]

The modern state took its name from the Regency of Algiers, the Ottoman polity established in the central Maghreb during the early 16th century. Under Ottoman rule, a political and administrative structure gradually emerged that helped define the territory and borders of what would become Algeria.[^26] Contemporary sources referred to the land as *Watan al-Jazāʾir* (وطن الجزائر, “the country of Algiers”), while the [Ottoman Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Turks "Ottoman Turks") [aristocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristocracy "Aristocracy") settled in the region identified both itself and the local population as “ [Algerians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerians "Algerians").” [^27] [^28] [^29]

## History

### Prehistory and ancient history

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/The_Tanzoumaitak_cave_painting_in_Tassili_n%27ajjer.jpg/250px-The_Tanzoumaitak_cave_painting_in_Tassili_n%27ajjer.jpg)

Rock art in the Tassili n’Ajjer plateau of Algeria has been dated to seven to 10 thousand years ago

Around ~1.8-million-year-old stone artifacts from Ain Hanech (Algeria) were considered to represent the oldest archaeological materials in [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa").[^30] Stone artifacts and cut-marked bones that were excavated from two nearby deposits at Ain Boucherit are estimated to be ~1.9 million years old, and even older stone artifacts to be as old as ~2.4 million years.[^30] Hence, the Ain Boucherit evidence shows that ancestral hominins inhabited the Mediterranean fringe in northern Africa much earlier than previously thought. The evidence strongly argues for early dispersal of stone tool manufacture and use from East Africa, or a possible multiple-origin scenario of stone technology in both East and North Africa.

Neanderthal tool makers produced hand axes in the [Levalloisian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levallois_technique "Levallois technique") and [Mousterian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mousterian "Mousterian") styles (43,000 BC) similar to those in the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant").[^31] [^32] Algeria was the site of the highest state of development of [Middle Paleolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Paleolithic "Middle Paleolithic") [Flake tool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flake_tool "Flake tool") techniques.[^33] Tools of this era, starting about 30,000 BC, are called [Aterian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aterian "Aterian") (after the archaeological site of [Bir el Ater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bir_el_Ater "Bir el Ater"), south of [Tebessa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C3%A9bessa "Tébessa")).

The earliest blade industries in North Africa are called [Iberomaurusian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberomaurusian "Iberomaurusian") (located mainly in the [Oran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oran "Oran") region). This industry appears to have spread throughout the coastal regions of the [Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maghreb "Maghreb") between 15,000 and 10,000 BC. [Neolithic civilisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") (animal domestication and agriculture) developed in the Saharan and Mediterranean Maghreb perhaps as early as 11,000 BC [^34] or as late as between 6000 and 2000 BC. This life, richly depicted in the [Tassili n'Ajjer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tassili_n%27Ajjer "Tassili n'Ajjer") paintings, predominated in Algeria until the classical period. The mixture of peoples of North Africa coalesced eventually into a distinct native population that came to be called [Berbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_people "Berber people"), who are the indigenous peoples of northern Africa.[^35]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Monnaie_-_Bronze%2C_Incertain%2C_Numidie%2C_Massinissa_-_btv1b8483901q_%281_of_2%29.jpg/250px-Monnaie_-_Bronze%2C_Incertain%2C_Numidie%2C_Massinissa_-_btv1b8483901q_%281_of_2%29.jpg)

Numidian coin of King Masinissa

From their principal center of power at [Carthage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carthage "Carthage"), the [Carthaginians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carthaginians "Carthaginians") expanded and established small settlements along the North African coast; by 600 BC, a [Phoenician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia") presence existed in the Algerian coast, such as [Icosium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosium "Icosium") in modern day Algiers and [Hippo Regius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippo_Regius "Hippo Regius") (modern [Annaba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annaba "Annaba")). These settlements served as market towns as well as anchorages.[^36] The Carthaginians avoided conquering the strong Berber tribes in the interior. Instead, they collected tribute, focused on trade (especially metals), and maintained friendly relations. The Berbers supplied them with goods, slaves, and skilled cavalry through trans-Saharan networks.[^37] Carthaginian culture strongly influenced nearby Berber tribes, who adopted many of its elements. From the sixth century BC, under the [Magonid dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magonid_dynasty "Magonid dynasty"), Carthage became more aggressive, partly due to rivalry with the [Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece"), leading to [conflict in Sicily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sicilian_Wars "Sicilian Wars") in 580 BC.[^37]

As Carthaginian power grew, its impact on the indigenous population increased dramatically. By the fifth century BC, Berbers in what is now Algeria had developed mixed farming and pastoral economies, advanced manufacturing, trade and political organisation supported several states.[^38] [^37] By the third century BC, two major Berber kingdoms existed: the [Masaesyli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masaesyli "Masaesyli") (from the [Moulouya River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moulouya_River "Moulouya River") in eastern Morocco to the [Rhummel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhumel_River "Rhumel River") in Algeria) and the [Massyli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massylii "Massylii") (in extreme eastern Algeria and western Tunisia).[^39] In 203–202 BC, [Masinissa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masinissa "Masinissa") of the Massyli, after being supported by Rome and playing a key role in the [Battle of Zama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Zama "Battle of Zama") against Carthaginian general [Hannibal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hannibal "Hannibal"), defeated his western rivals (including [Syphax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syphax "Syphax")) and unified the territories into the [Kingdom of Numidia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Numidia "Kingdom of Numidia").[^40] Stretching from [Tabarka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabarka "Tabarka") in Tunisia to the Moulouya River, with its capital at Cirta (Constantine), Numidia represented a high point of Berber civilization.[^41] Masinissa promoted agriculture, built a strong army and navy, and was praised by ancient writers such as Roman historian [Livy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livy "Livy") as an exceptional ruler.[^42] After Masinissa’s death in 148 BC, succession struggles arose. His grandson [Jugurtha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jugurtha "Jugurtha") (r. 118–106 BC) reunified Numidia through ruthless means but provoked Rome through conflict and the massacre of Roman citizens at Cirta. Despite brilliant resistance, Jugurtha was defeated and captured in 106 BC with the betrayal of his father-in-law [Bocchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bocchus_I "Bocchus I") of Mauretania.[^43]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/L%27arc_de_timgade_%282%29.jpg/250px-L%27arc_de_timgade_%282%29.jpg)

The Arch of Trajan in the Roman ruins of Timgad

[Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") gradually tightened control: first turning Numidia into a protectorate, then dividing and weakening it. Later Berber client kings, such as [Juba I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juba_I_of_Numidia "Juba I of Numidia") (who supported [Pompey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pompey "Pompey") and was defeated by [Caesar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Caesar "Julius Caesar") at [Thapsus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Thapsus "Battle of Thapsus") in 46 BC), continued to rule diminished territories. [Augustus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustus "Augustus") installed [Juba II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juba_II "Juba II") (r. 25 BC–c. 23 AD), a highly cultured scholar and husband of [Cleopatra Selene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleopatra_Selene_II "Cleopatra Selene II"), as client [king of Mauretania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauretania "Mauretania") (central Algeria and Morocco), bringing a period of prosperity.[^44] In 40 AD, Emperor [Caligula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caligula "Caligula") executed Juba II’s son [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy_of_Mauretania "Ptolemy of Mauretania"). Following a subsequent Berber revolt, Rome abolished the last client kingdoms and imposed direct rule, dividing the territory into provinces ([Mauretania Caesariensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauretania_Caesariensis "Mauretania Caesariensis"), [Mauretania Tingitana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauretania_Tingitana "Mauretania Tingitana"), and [Africa Proconsularis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa_Proconsularis "Africa Proconsularis")).[^45]

Roman rule had a largely negative long-term impact on Berber society. While coastal areas saw grand Roman construction, much of the interior was turned into grain-exporting [latifundia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latifundium "Latifundium"), pushing Berbers into the hinterland where they retribalized and resisted for generations. By the time the [Vandals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vandals "Vandals") ended Roman control in 429 AD, most of Algeria had returned to Berber tribal rule.[^41] [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity") spread in the 3rd–4th centuries. Its most prominent Berber figures were [Saint Augustine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo "Augustine of Hippo"), the bishop of Hippo Regius (modern-day Annaba, Algeria) and his mother, [Saint Monica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Monica "Saint Monica"). However, Christianity rapidly declined among Berbers after being associated with Roman exploitation, especially following the suppression of the [Donatist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donatism "Donatism") movement.[^41]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Vandals_and_North_Africa_533.png/250px-Vandals_and_North_Africa_533.png)

The Vandal and Moorish Kingdoms, early 6th century.

Under King [Genseric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaiseric "Gaiseric"), the [Vandal Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vandal_Kingdom "Vandal Kingdom") successfully defended Carthage against two [Byzantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire") expeditions from Constantinople in 460 and 468. A peace treaty in 474 brought several decades of stability. This period ended when [Gelimer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gelimer "Gelimer") deposed his cousin [Hilderic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilderic "Hilderic") and seized power.[^46]

In 533, Emperor [Justinian I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justinian_I "Justinian I"), seeking to restore the Roman Empire, launched a major [campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vandalic_War "Vandalic War") against the Vandals. A large fleet commanded by General [Belisarius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belisarius "Belisarius") sailed from [Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantinople "Constantinople"). After landing near [Carthage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Carthage "Roman Carthage"), Belisarius decisively defeated the Vandals in two battles, occupied Carthage, and captured King Gelimer, who was exiled to Anatolia. Many Vandals were enslaved or expelled from North Africa.[^47] [^48] Byzantine rule in Africa was limited mainly to the provinces of Byzacena, Proconsular Africa, and Numidia. While some urban life persisted, rural areas suffered from heavy taxation, frequent revolts, and insecurity. The devastating [Justinian Plague](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justinian_Plague "Justinian Plague") further weakened the region. The Byzantines then faced resistance from the Moorish tribes of the Aurès Mountains. Governor [Solomon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon_\(Byzantine_general\) "Solomon (Byzantine general)") defeated them and reinforced control through an extensive fortification program, but he was killed in battle near Tebessa in 543.[^45] General [John Troglita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Troglita "John Troglita") later restored a fragile peace, granting significant autonomy to the Moorish kingdoms.[^49] The most prominent of these were the [Mauro-Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauro-Roman_Kingdom "Mauro-Roman Kingdom") and [Altava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Altava "Kingdom of Altava") Kingdoms.[^50] [^51] A growing sense of local autonomy emerged, exemplified by the powerful governor [Gregory the Patrician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregory_the_Patrician "Gregory the Patrician") (from 646), who minted his own coins and moved the capital to Sufetula ([Sbeitla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sbeitla "Sbeitla")).[^49]

### Middle Ages

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Statue_of_Dyhia_in_Khenchela_%28Algeria%29.jpg/250px-Statue_of_Dyhia_in_Khenchela_%28Algeria%29.jpg)

Dihya memorial in Khenchela, Algeria

The [Muslim conquest of the Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_the_Maghreb "Muslim conquest of the Maghreb") took place between 647 and 709, marking the Arab-Islamic expansion into North Africa, which was then divided between Byzantine-controlled coastal territories and independent Berber kingdoms and tribes in the interior.[^41] The conquest unfolded in three main phases and ultimately brought most of the region under [Umayyad Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") control.[^52]

The first invasion (647–648), led by [Abdallah ibn Saad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abd_Allah_ibn_Sa%27d "Abd Allah ibn Sa'd") under [Caliph Uthman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uthman "Uthman"), targeted the [Exarchate of Carthage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exarchate_of_Africa "Exarchate of Africa"). The Arabs defeated the Byzantine patrician Gregory at the [Battle of Sufetula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Sufetula_\(647\) "Battle of Sufetula (647)"), weakening Byzantine authority in Ifriqiya. However, the campaign did not extend beyond raiding.[^53] After a period of [civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Fitna "First Fitna") in the Muslim world, a second wave began around 665–689. [Oqba ibn Nafi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oqba_ibn_Nafi "Oqba ibn Nafi") played a central role: in 670, he founded the city of [Kairouan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kairouan "Kairouan"), which became a major Islamic base and cultural center.[^54] He led ambitious campaigns deep into the interior, reportedly reaching the Atlantic coast, but faced strong resistance from Byzantine and Berber forces. A coalition led by the Berber leader [Kusayla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kusaila "Kusaila") ambushed and killed Oqba at the [Battle of Tahouda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Vescera "Battle of Vescera") in 683. Kusayla temporarily captured Kairouan before being defeated.[^55]

The third and decisive phase (697–709) was led by [Hassan ibn al-Nu'man](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassan_ibn_al-Nu%27man "Hassan ibn al-Nu'man") and later [Musa ibn Nusayr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musa_ibn_Nusayr "Musa ibn Nusayr"). Hassan recaptured and destroyed Carthage in 697 after fierce fighting against Byzantine reinforcements. A major Berber resistance then emerged under the legendary queen [Kahina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kahina "Kahina") (Dihya), who initially defeated the Arabs in the Aurès Mountains. However, Hassan returned with fresh troops, defeated and killed Kahina around 697.[^56] Musa ibn Nusayr completed the conquest of the western Maghreb, subduing Berber tribes, capturing Tangier, and integrating many converted Berbers into the Muslim army. By 709, the entire Maghreb was under Umayyad control.[^57]

Following the Berber general [Tariq ibn Ziyad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tariq_ibn_Ziyad "Tariq ibn Ziyad") ’s [conquest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_the_Iberian_Peninsula "Muslim conquest of the Iberian Peninsula") of [Visigothic Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visigothic_Spain "Visigothic Spain") in 711,[^58] Islam had already spread throughout North Africa, and Berbers actively participated in raids into Europe, reaching as far as [Lyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyon "Lyon") and [Autun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autun "Autun") in France.[^59] However, Berber converts were still treated as second-class non-Arabs;[^60] the difficult conquest of the Maghreb prompted the Umayyads to impose heavy taxation and oppressive governance.[^61] Attracted by ideals of equality and religious purity, many Berbers supported Kharijite movements, triggering frequent revolts in Ifriqiya.[^60] [^62]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Rustumid_Coin_-_Fals_Abd_al-Wahab_ibn_Rustam_%28c._788-824%29_Stephen_Album_Rare_Coins.png/250px-Rustumid_Coin_-_Fals_Abd_al-Wahab_ibn_Rustam_%28c._788-824%29_Stephen_Album_Rare_Coins.png)

Rustumid Coin - Fals Abd al-Wahab ibn Rustam (c. 788-824)

Following the [Berber revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_Revolt "Berber Revolt") numerous independent states emerged across the Maghreb. In 778, [Abdel Rahman Ibn Rustam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abd_al-Rahman_ibn_Rustam "Abd al-Rahman ibn Rustam") established a Kharijite emirate at [Tahert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tahert "Tahert") in central Algeria known as the [Rustamid imamate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rustamid_kingdom "Rustamid kingdom"), marking the first independent Muslim state in the Maghrib. At its peak, the kingdom extended from Tlemcen to Tripoli.[^63] It prospered through [trans-Saharan trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Saharan_trade "Trans-Saharan trade"). The Rustamids faced constant threats from the [Aghlabids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aghlabid_dynasty "Aghlabid dynasty"), backed by the [Abbasid Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate"), in the east, and the [Alid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alids "Alids") [Idrisids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idrisids "Idrisids") in the west. Due to the strict religious nature of [Ibadi society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibadism_in_the_Maghreb "Ibadism in the Maghreb"), the imams frequently dealt with internal rebellions, which weakened their authority unless the ruler was particularly charismatic.[^63] The dynasty was defeated by the [Fatimids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimids "Fatimids") in 909, prompting the Ibadis to settle in the [M'zab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%27zab "M'zab") region.[^64]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Fatimid.jpg/250px-Fatimid.jpg)

Fatimid Caliphate, a Shia Ismaili dynasty that ruled much of North Africa, c. 960–1100

The Fatimid da'wa entered the Maghreb through the skilled [Ismaili Shia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ismailism "Ismailism") missionary [Abu Abdullah al-San'ani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Abdallah_al-Shi%27i "Abu Abdallah al-Shi'i") (al-Shi'i), who arrived around 900 and settled among the [Kabyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabyle_people "Kabyle people") [Kutama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kutama "Kutama") Berbers near Sétif. He gradually gained their support through preaching the coming of the [Mahdi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahdi "Mahdi"), organized them militarily, and succeeded in overthrowing the Aghlabid dynasty. In 909, he captured Kairouan and prepared the ground for the Fatimid state.[^65] [^66] He then liberated [Ubayd Allah al-Mahdi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubayd_Allah_al-Mahdi "Ubayd Allah al-Mahdi") from prison in [Sijilmasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sijilmasa "Sijilmasa") and handed him power. Al-Mahdi declared himself Caliph in 910, becoming the founder of the [Fatimid Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimid_Caliphate "Fatimid Caliphate") in Ifriqiya (Tunisia). The Fatimids imposed Ismaili Shia doctrine, faced strong opposition from the [Sunni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunni_Islam "Sunni Islam") [Maliki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maliki_school "Maliki school") population,[^67] and dealt with several revolts, especially the major rebellion of [Abu Yazid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Yazid "Abu Yazid") (the Man of the Donkey).[^68]

The Fatimids built a powerful [army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimid_army "Fatimid army") and [navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimid_navy "Fatimid navy") and achieved military successes. They maintained control of Sicily, [raided northern Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimid_sack_of_Genoa "Fatimid sack of Genoa") (934–935 and 1004–1015), and developed a prosperous economy there, with many mosques, [sugar cane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugarcane "Sugarcane"), [papyrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papyrus "Papyrus"), and [cotton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton "Cotton") production that rivaled Spain. In this way, the Fatimids established a powerful North African empire that would not be rivaled until the rise of the Almoravids and Almohads.[^69] Their greatest achievement was the successful shift of the caliphate's center to Egypt; Under [al-Mu'izz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Mu%27izz_li-Din_Allah "Al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah"), the brilliant general [Jawhar al-Siqilli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jawhar_al-Siqilli "Jawhar al-Siqilli") [conquered Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimid_conquest_of_Egypt "Fatimid conquest of Egypt") in 969, founded [Cairo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cairo "Cairo") and [al-Azhar University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Azhar_University "Al-Azhar University"), and the Fatimids moved their capital to Egypt in 971.[^70]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Bologhine_Benziri_Benmenad.jpg/250px-Bologhine_Benziri_Benmenad.jpg)

Statut of Bologhine ibn Ziri founder of the Zirid Dynasty

After the Fatimids transferred their capital to Egypt in 971, they appointed Buluggin ibn Ziri, a Sanhaja Berber leader, as their governor over Ifriqiya and the [central Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Maghreb "Central Maghreb"). This marked the beginning of the [Zirid Dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zirid_dynasty "Zirid dynasty") (362–543 AH / 973–1152 CE), which ruled from [Achir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achir "Achir") then [al-Mansuriyya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mansouria,_Tunisia "Mansouria, Tunisia") near Kairouan.[^71] [^72] Buluggin founded the cities of Algiers, Médéa and Miliana, making central Algeria politically important.[^65] He then expanded his authority across Algeria and most of Morocco briefly, fighting [Zenata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenata "Zenata") tribes.[^71] Under [al-Mu'izz ibn Badis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Mu%27izz_ibn_Badis "Al-Mu'izz ibn Badis"), the Zirids cut ties with the Shia Fatimids in 1043, adopted Sunni Islam, and recognized the Abbasid Caliphate. In retaliation, the Fatimids sent the Arab tribes of [Banū Hilāl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banu_Hilal "Banu Hilal") and [Banū Sulaym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banu_Sulaym "Banu Sulaym"), whose invasion caused widespread destruction, especially in Kairouan.[^73] The Zirid state gradually weakened and fragmented.[^74]

A branch of the family, led by [Hammad ibn Buluggin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hammad_ibn_Buluggin "Hammad ibn Buluggin"), broke away and established the [Hammadid Dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hammadid_dynasty "Hammadid dynasty") (408–552 AH / 1018–1152 CE) in central Algeria. Their first capital was [Qal'at Bani Hammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qal%27at_Bani_Hammad "Qal'at Bani Hammad"), later moving to Béjaïa (Bougie), which became a major prosperous city.[^75] The Hammadids developed a strong navy, promoted agriculture, industry, trade, and culture. Cities like Al-Qal’a' and Béjaïa flourished with scholars, poets, and architects.[^76] However, the dynasty suffered from internal divisions, revolts, attacks by the [Almoravids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almoravid_dynasty "Almoravid dynasty"), and Genoese raids on the coast. The Hammadids were finally defeated by [Abd al-Mu'min](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abd_al-Mu%27min "Abd al-Mu'min"), the founder of the [Almohad Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almohad_Caliphate "Almohad Caliphate"), in 1152, ending both Sanhaja dynasties in the region.[^75]

The Almoravids (Al-Murabitun), a puritanical religious-military movement of Sanhaja Berbers (mainly the [Lamtuna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamtuna "Lamtuna") tribe) from the Sahara in [Mauritania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauritania "Mauritania"), was founded in the mid-11th century by the Maliki scholar [Abd Allah ibn Yasin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdallah_ibn_Yasin "Abdallah ibn Yasin"). The movement emphasized strict adherence to Maliki Sunni Islam.[^77] Under leaders like [Yusuf ibn Tashfin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yusuf_ibn_Tashfin "Yusuf ibn Tashfin"), they built a powerful empire, establishing [Marrakesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marrakesh "Marrakesh") as their capital in 1062.[^78] They conquered Sijilmasa, Fez, much of western and central Algeria (including Tlemcen, Oran, and Algiers in 1082), and defeated the [Ghana Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghana_Empire "Ghana Empire") in the south. They also intervened in [al-Andalus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Andalus "Al-Andalus") (Spain), defeating Christian forces and extending their rule there.[^78] The Almoravids strengthened Maliki Islam and brought Andalusian cultural influence to the Maghreb, but their rigid legalism later contributed to their downfall.[^79] They were overthrown by the Almohads, who captured Marrakesh in 1147.[^80]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Cantiga_181_miniature.jpg/250px-Cantiga_181_miniature.jpg)

Almohad troops depicted in a 13th century miniature of the Cantigas de Santa Maria.

The Almoravids' successors were the Almohads (Al-Muwahhidun), meaning "Unitarians"; a reformist movement founded by [Muhammad ibn Tumart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Tumart "Ibn Tumart"), a [Masmuda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masmuda "Masmuda") Berber who proclaimed himself the Mahdi.[^81] They emphasized the absolute unity of God ([Tawhid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tawhid "Tawhid")) and opposed what they viewed as Almoravid religious errors. Abd al-Mu'min became the first Almohad Caliph and completed the conquest of the Maghreb by 1147, unifying the region for the first time.[^82] In Algeria, they ended the Hammadid dynasty by capturing Béjaïa, Algiers, and Qal'at Bani Hammad.[^83] The empire reached its peak under [Abu Yaqub Yusuf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Yaqub_Yusuf "Abu Yaqub Yusuf") and [Yaqub al-Mansur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yaqub_al-Mansur "Yaqub al-Mansur"), who patronized [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy") (including Ibn Rushd/ [Averroes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Averroes "Averroes") and [Ibn Tufayl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Tufayl "Ibn Tufayl")), arts, and architecture, while maintaining control in al-Andalus.[^84] However, major defeats against Christian forces, especially at [Las Navas de Tolosa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Las_Navas_de_Tolosa "Las Navas de Tolosa") in 1212, and internal divisions led to the empire's collapse.[^84] By the mid-13th century, the Almohad state fragmented, giving rise to the [Hafsids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hafsid_dynasty "Hafsid dynasty") in Ifriqiya, the [Zayyanids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Tlemcen "Kingdom of Tlemcen") in Tlemcen, and the [Marinids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marinid_dynasty "Marinid dynasty") in Fez.[^85]

Following the collapse of the Almohad Empire, [Yaghmurasen ibn Zayyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yaghmurasen_ibn_Zyan "Yaghmurasen ibn Zyan") founded a Zanata Berber dynasty known as the [Zayyanid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zayyanid_dynasty "Zayyanid dynasty") or Abd al-Wadid dynasty, which ruled the central Maghrib from 1236 to 1550.[^86] The dynasty produced 27 rulers and made [Tlemcen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tlemcen "Tlemcen") its capital.[^87] The Zayyanids generally used the title of emir, though some adopted shaykh or khalifa. Their administration included qadis and wazirs.[^87] Thanks to its strategic location on the trans-Saharan trade routes, Tlemcen flourished as a major political, economic, and cultural center, famous for trade in leather goods, precious metals, and [slaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery "Slavery"). Zayyanid rulers actively supported learning by constructing mosques and madrasas, turning their capital into an important theological and intellectual hub.[^87] Their history is relatively well documented, particularly through the works of the famous historian [Ibn Khaldun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun "Ibn Khaldun").[^88]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Maghreb_15th_Century.svg/330px-Maghreb_15th_Century.svg.png)

The Zayyanid kingdom of Tlemcen in the fifteenth century and its neighbours

Geopolitically, the kingdom was constantly threatened by its powerful neighbors — the Marinids of Morocco to the west and the Hafsids of Ifriqiya to the east [^88] — while also facing internal family intrigues and hostility from rival Zanata tribes.[^89] Several rulers stood out in the 14th century, notably [Abu Hammu I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Hammu_I "Abu Hammu I") (1308–1318), who restored Tlemcen and expanded influence eastward; [Abu Tashfin I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Tashfin_Abd_al-Rahman_I "Abu Tashfin Abd al-Rahman I") (1318–1337), a patron of education and literature who died defending the city against the Marinids; [Abu Hammu II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Hammu_Musa_II "Abu Hammu Musa II") (1359–1389), known for his resilience in recovering Tlemcen multiple times; and his son Abu Tashfin II, who conspired against him, making Tlemcen a Marinid vassal.[^90] Over time, the dynasty weakened due to endless external conflicts and internal betrayals, with the last Zayyanid rulers becoming Spanish then Ottoman vassals.[^91]

### Early modern era

![Monument of a soldiers holding a sword](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/MONUMENT_BABA_AROUDJ_AINTEMOUCHENT.jpg/250px-MONUMENT_BABA_AROUDJ_AINTEMOUCHENT.jpg)

Aruj Reis' monument in Aïn Témouchent.

After the [fall of Granada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_Granada "Fall of Granada") in 1492, the Ottoman Regency of Algiers on the North African coast emerged during the [Ottoman–Habsburg struggles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman%E2%80%93Habsburg_wars "Ottoman–Habsburg wars") of the 16th century as a response to increasing Spanish encroachement on Algerian coastal cities.[^92] The regency was founded in 1516 by the corsair brothers [Aruj Barbarossa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aruj_Barbarossa "Aruj Barbarossa") and [Hayreddin Barbarossa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hayreddin_Barbarossa "Hayreddin Barbarossa"), who seized Algiers with local support against Spanish expansion and placed the territory under the protection of the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire").[^93] Although nominally loyal to the Ottoman sultan, the Regency gradually evolved into a highly autonomous “imperial state” dominated by [janissaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janissaries "Janissaries"), [corsair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_corsairs "Barbary corsairs") captains, and the [divan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divan "Divan") council rather than direct Ottoman administration.[^94]

Over the centuries, power shifted through several ruling systems: [beylerbeys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beylerbey "Beylerbey") appointed by Istanbul, then [pashas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasha "Pasha") and [aghas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aghas "Aghas"), before the era of the deys began in 1671. In practice, authority often rested with the [Odjak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odjak_of_Algiers "Odjak of Algiers") (the janissary corps) and the [ta'ifa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corsairs_of_Algiers "Corsairs of Algiers") of corsair captains, while the dey ruled through negotiation with military elites.[^95] Historians have described the Regency as a “ [military republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratocracy "Stratocracy") ”,[^96] combining Ottoman administrative institutions with local political traditions inherited from medieval North African states.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Alawids_and_Ottoman_regencies_in_17th-19th_centuries.png/250px-Alawids_and_Ottoman_regencies_in_17th-19th_centuries.png)

Ottoman Algeria and its neighboring states in 17th-19th centuries.

The Regency became one of the major naval powers of the Mediterranean during the 16th and 17th centuries.[^97] Its economy relied heavily on [privateering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privateering "Privateering"), [slave-taking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_slave_trade "Barbary slave trade"), [ransom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ransom "Ransom"), [tribute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribute "Tribute") payments, agriculture, artisanal production, and [trans-Saharan trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Saharan_trade "Trans-Saharan trade").[^98] Corsair fleets operating from Algiers attacked European shipping across the Mediterranean and even into the Atlantic, forcing states such as Britain, France, Spain, the Netherlands, Denmark, and later the young United States to pay tribute or negotiate treaties directly with Algiers.[^99] The city itself became wealthy and heavily fortified, especially after failed European expeditions such as the [attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algiers_expedition_\(1541\) "Algiers expedition (1541)") of Emperor [Charles V](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor "Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor") in 1541.[^100] The [Casbah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casbah_of_Algiers "Casbah of Algiers"), one of the most fascinating urban neighborhoods in the world, was built during this period.[^98]

Internally, the Regency expanded its authority over much of present-day Algeria while allowing considerable autonomy to tribal and regional leaders. Political and military power in Algeria was largely controlled by the Turks, while Algerian locals mainly served as [taxpayers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxpayer "Taxpayer"), soldiers, merchants and religious officials.[^101] Socially, Algeria resembled pre-Revolutionary southern European societies, with a powerful aristocracy, a poor [peasantry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peasant "Peasant"), a weak middle class, and little influence from [Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment") ideas.[^102] Tensions sometimes emerged between the Turkish janissary elite, local populations, and the [Kouloughlis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kouloughlis "Kouloughlis") (people of mixed Turkish and Algerian ancestry), leading to revolts and political instability. Although dissatisfaction with Ottoman injustice and corruption was widespread, it was counterbalanced by religious loyalty to the [Ottoman Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Caliphate "Ottoman Caliphate"), gratitude for Ottoman protection against the Spanish, fear of European ambitions, strong cultural links,[^103] and the absence of a strong [middle class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_class "Middle class") or [national unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_unity "National unity").[^101] Algerian society was deeply influenced by religion and [absolute rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_rule "Absolute rule"), with education centered on religious studies under clerical authority.[^104]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/De_Engels-Nederlandse_vloot_in_de_Baai_van_Algiers_ter_ondersteuning_van_het_ultimatum_tot_vrijlating_van_blanke_slaven%2C_26_augustus_1816._Rijksmuseum_SK-A-1377.jpeg/250px-thumbnail.jpeg)

Bombardment of Algiers by the Anglo-Dutch fleet, August 1816

Algiers maintained diplomatic relations with European powers and acted increasingly independently from Istanbul,[^105] especially after the [janissary coup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odjak_of_Algiers_Revolution "Odjak of Algiers Revolution") of 1659 and the consolidation of dey rule in the 18th century.[^106] By the early 19th century, the Regency began to decline. European naval superiority, the weakening of corsair warfare after the [Napoleonic era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_era "Napoleonic era"), internal unrest, economic difficulties, and repeated bombardments reduced Algerian power.[^107] The [Barbary Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_Wars "Barbary Wars") and the [Anglo-Dutch bombardment of Algiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bombardment_of_Algiers_\(1816\) "Bombardment of Algiers (1816)") further weakened the state and forced concessions to the United States and European powers.[^108] Relations with France deteriorated after the 1827 [Fly-Whisk Incident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fly-whisk_incident "Fly-whisk incident"), when [Dey Hussein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hussein_Dey "Hussein Dey") struck the French consul during a dispute over debts and grain payments.[^109] France used the episode as a pretext to [invade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasion_of_Algiers_\(1830\) "Invasion of Algiers (1830)") in 1830, capturing Algiers and ending the Regency after more than three centuries of existence.[^110] Resistance continued in Constantine under [Ahmed Bey ben Mohamed Chérif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmed_Bey_ben_Mohamed_Ch%C3%A9rif "Ahmed Bey ben Mohamed Chérif") until 1837.[^111]

Today, many Algerian historians regard the Regency period as a foundational stage of modern Algerian [statehood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statehood "Statehood") and [national consciousness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_consciousness "National consciousness"), since it unified much of the territory, developed enduring political institutions, and fostered an early sense of Algerian identity under the name “Al-Jaza’ir.” [^112] Prominent historian [Abou el-Kacem Saadallah](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Abou_el-Kacem_Saadallah&action=edit&redlink=1 "Abou el-Kacem Saadallah (page does not exist)") refers to the Regency as the "Algerian *[Ancien régime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancien_r%C3%A9gime "Ancien régime")* ".[^101] Historian [Ahmad Tawfiq al-Madani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmed_Tewfik_El_Madani "Ahmed Tewfik El Madani") regards it as the "first Algerian state" and the "Algerian Ottoman republic".[^112] [^113] [^114]

### French colonisation (1830–1962)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Abdelkader_Ibn_Muhieddine_%281808-1883%29.jpg/250px-Abdelkader_Ibn_Muhieddine_%281808-1883%29.jpg)

Emir Abdelkader, Algerian leader insurgent against French colonial rule, 1865

Although the French dismantled the Ottoman Regency of Algiers, an embryonic form of [Algerian nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_nationalism "Algerian nationalism") and state consciousness already existed. When the French invaded, local tribes rallied to support the Ottomans despite their resistance to the Regency.[^115] The French gradually expanded control, [capturing Constantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Constantine "Siege of Constantine") in 1837. In western Algeria, [Emir Abdelkader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emir_Abdelkader "Emir Abdelkader") organized a real [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Abdelkader "Emirate of Abdelkader") and led fierce resistance against the French, notably at the [Battle of Macta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Macta "Battle of Macta") (1835).[^116] After years of war, marked by ruthless French tactics including razzias, he surrendered in 1847.[^117] He remains Algeria’s greatest national hero, and his struggle deeply inspired future Algerian nationalism. Resistance continued through revolts by the Kabyles in the 1850s and [1871](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mokrani_Revolt "Mokrani Revolt"),[^118] and other uprisings, all brutally suppressed, followed by massive land expropriation.[^119] According to several historians, the methods used by the French to establish control over Algeria reached [genocidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_genocide "Algerian genocide") proportions.[^120] [^121] [^122] Historian [Ben Kiernan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Kiernan "Ben Kiernan") wrote on the French conquest of Algeria: "By 1875, the French conquest was complete. The war had killed approximately 825,000 indigenous Algerians since 1830".[^123] French losses from 1831 to 1851 were 92,329 dead in the hospital and only 3,336 killed in action.[^124] [^125]

Colonial Algeria was marked by tensions between military rule and European settlers. While formally assimilated as French departments in 1848,[^126] it operated under a [governor-general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governor_General_of_Algeria "Governor General of Algeria") and institutions that strongly favored settlers, who became known as *colons* and later, as *[Pied-Noirs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pied-Noir "Pied-Noir").*[^127] By 1848, European population stood at more than 100.000,[^128] and reached 244.600 by 1872.[^129] [Napoleon III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_III "Napoleon III") ’s more sympathetic policies toward Muslims alarmed the settlers.[^130] After the [Franco-Prussian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War "Franco-Prussian War"), the [Third Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Third_Republic "French Third Republic") firmly established settler dominance, reinforced by figures like [Eugène Étienne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_%C3%89tienne "Eugène Étienne"). A settler uprising in 1898 led to greater autonomy for the colonial administration in Algiers. The economy became heavily dependent on France, dominated by [vineyards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vineyard "Vineyard") at the expense of food production,[^131] leaving the Muslim population marginalized, discriminated against, and impoverished. By the early 20th century, the *colons* formed a majority of the population in both Algiers and Oran.[^132]

Algerian nationalism emerged before [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") through moderate, French-educated Jeunes Algériens, who demanded genuine assimilation and equal rights.[^133] World War I proved transformative: Algerians fought bravely (25,000 dead) and worked in French factories, yet promised reforms like the [Jonnart Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonnart_Law "Jonnart Law") (1919) were largely undermined by settler opposition.[^134] This radicalized the movement. [**Emir Khaled**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalid_ibn_Hashim "Khalid ibn Hashim") (grandson of Abdelkader) called for broader [democratic rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights "Civil and political rights") and was deported in 1923.[^135] **[Ferhat Abbas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferhat_Abbas "Ferhat Abbas")** shifted from assimilationism after the rejection of the [Blum-Viollette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blum%E2%80%93Viollette_proposal "Blum–Viollette proposal") bill (1936). **[Messali Hadj](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messali_Hadj "Messali Hadj")** consistently demanded full [independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence "Independence") through the [ENA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89toile_Nord-Africaine "Étoile Nord-Africaine") and [PPA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_People%27s_Party "Algerian People's Party"), while **[Shaykh Ben Badis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdel-Hamid_ibn_Badis "Abdel-Hamid ibn Badis")** fostered cultural and Islamic nationalism via the [AUMA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_of_Algerian_Muslim_Ulema "Association of Algerian Muslim Ulema") (1931), promoting Arabic and Algerian identity.[^136]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Six_chefs_FLN_-_1954.jpg/250px-Six_chefs_FLN_-_1954.jpg)

The six historical Leaders of the FLN: Rabah Bitat, Mostefa Ben Boulaïd Mourad Didouche Mohammed Boudiaf Krim Belkacem and Larbi Ben M'Hidi.

During the [Second World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_World_War "Second World War"), Algeria came under [Vichy control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vichy_France "Vichy France") before being liberated by the [Allies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II "Allies of World War II") in [Operation Torch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Torch "Operation Torch"), which saw the first large-scale deployment of [American troops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_history_of_the_United_States_during_World_War_II "Military history of the United States during World War II") in the [North African campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_African_campaign "North African campaign").[^137] In 1943, Abbas issued the Manifesto of the Algerian People demanding autonomy.[^138] Growing unity among nationalists through the [AML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friends_of_the_Manifesto_and_Liberty "Friends of the Manifesto and Liberty") movement,[^139] combined with French repression, led to the violent [massacre at Sétif and Guelma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A9tif_and_Guelma_massacre "Sétif and Guelma massacre") in May 1945,[^140] where brutal colonial reprisals convinced many that armed struggle was the only solution.[^141] The flawed Organic Statute of 1947 further alienated the younger generation.[^136] A group of militants, including [Ahmed Ben Bella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmed_Ben_Bella "Ahmed Ben Bella"), [Hocine Ait Ahmed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hocine_A%C3%AFt_Ahmed "Hocine Aït Ahmed"), [Rabah Bitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rabah_Bitat "Rabah Bitat"), [Larbi Ben M’hidi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larbi_Ben_M%27hidi "Larbi Ben M'hidi"), and others — later known as the historic leaders — broke away and formed the [CRUA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutionary_Committee_of_Unity_and_Action "Revolutionary Committee of Unity and Action") in 1953, which became the **[Front de Libération Nationale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Liberation_Front_\(Algeria\) "National Liberation Front (Algeria)") (FLN)** in 1954, preparing the country for the [Algerian War of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_War_of_Independence "Algerian War of Independence").[^142]

The Algerian War was one of the most important [Third World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_World "Third World") [anti-colonial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decolonization "Decolonization") struggles. It succeeded due to the FLN’s consistent pursuit of its core objectives (as outlined in the 1 November 1954 [Proclamation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_1_November_1954 "Declaration of 1 November 1954") and the 1956 [Soummam Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soummam_conference "Soummam conference"));[^142] attracting international attention to the struggle for "the restoration of the Algerian state." [^143] [^142] [^144] The FLN's military wing, known as the [ALN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Liberation_Army_\(Algeria\) "National Liberation Army (Algeria)"), managed to pose a serious security threat to France while surviving deep internal rivalries.[^142]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Map_of_Plan_Challe_%28Algerian_War%29.svg/250px-Map_of_Plan_Challe_%28Algerian_War%29.svg.png)

Map illustrating the division of Algerian territory into 6 wilayas drawn up during the Soummam Congress in 1956 and the various parts of the French Challe Plan (offensive operations) during the Algerian War

The war began with coordinated FLN attacks on 31 October–1 November 1954.[^145] France responded with military force combined with social and economic measures under leaders like [Mendès-France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Mend%C3%A8s_France "Pierre Mendès France"), [Soustelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Soustelle "Jacques Soustelle") (who promoted "integration"),[^146] and later [Guy Mollet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guy_Mollet "Guy Mollet").[^147] Violence escalated dramatically after the FLN’s brutal [attacks in the Nord Constantinois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Philippeville "Battle of Philippeville") in August 1955.[^148] The FLN used hit and run attacks in Algeria and France as part of its war, and the French conducted severe reprisals. In addition, the French destroyed over 8,000 villages [^149] and relocated over 2 million Algerians to [concentration camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internment_camps_in_France#Algerian_War "Internment camps in France").[^150] The [Battle of Algiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Algiers_\(1956%E2%80%931957\) "Battle of Algiers (1956–1957)") (1956–57) saw the FLN resort to [urban terrorism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urban_terrorism "Urban terrorism") and bombings, which the [French paratroopers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_French_paratrooper_units "List of French paratrooper units") crushed using [torture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torture_during_the_Algerian_War_of_Independence "Torture during the Algerian War of Independence") — gaining military victory but suffering major political damage.[^151] The conflict was marked by fierce internal divisions: FLN vs. Messali Hadj’s [MNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_National_Movement "Algerian National Movement") (resulting in bloody fratricidal massacres like Mélouza),[^152] and tensions between “internal” and “external” FLN forces.[^153] The [French army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Army "French Army"), drawing lessons from [Indochina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Indochina_War "First Indochina War"), achieved significant military successes, notably with the [Morice Line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morice_Line "Morice Line") and [General Challe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurice_Challe "Maurice Challe") ’s campaigns, but faced growing political frustration.[^154]

After [Charles de Gaulle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle "Charles de Gaulle") ’s return to power in 1958, the FLN created the [Provisional Government of the Algerian Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_the_Algerian_Republic "Provisional Government of the Algerian Republic") (GPRA) and appointed Ferhat Abbas as its president. The GPRA rejected de Gaulle's ceasefire proposal termed "Peace of the Brave",[^155] causing him to shift his position toward [self-determination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-determination "Self-determination") (announced in 1959).[^156] [^157] This triggered settler and military revolts, including the failed [putsch of April 1961](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algiers_putsch_of_1961 "Algiers putsch of 1961") by generals led by Challe and [Salan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raoul_Salan "Raoul Salan"), who later joined the terrorist [OAS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_arm%C3%A9e_secr%C3%A8te "Organisation armée secrète").[^156] After difficult negotiations, the [Évian Accords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89vian_Accords "Évian Accords") were signed in March 1962,[^158] leading to a ceasefire and a [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1962_Algerian_independence_referendum "1962 Algerian independence referendum") in which Algerians overwhelmingly voted for independence. Algeria became independent on 5 July 1962, ending 132 years of French colonial rule.[^159]

The human cost was immense, Historians, like [Alistair Horne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alistair_Horne "Alistair Horne") and [Raymond Aron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Aron "Raymond Aron"), state that the actual number of Algerian Muslim war dead was far greater than the original FLN and official French estimates but was less than the 1 million deaths claimed by the Algerian government after independence. Horne estimated Algerian casualties during the span of eight years to be around 700,000.[^160] The war uprooted more than 2 million Algerians.[^161]

The number of European *Pied-Noirs* who fled Algeria totalled more than 900,000 between 1962 and 1964.[^162] The exodus to mainland France accelerated after the [Oran massacre of 1962](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oran_massacre_of_1962 "Oran massacre of 1962"), in which many Europeans were murdered as reprisal for OAS terror attacks.[^163] It is estimated that between 30,000 and 150,000 [Harkis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harki "Harki") and their dependents were killed by the FLN or by [lynch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lynching "Lynching") mobs in Algeria.[^164] The country was left devastated: massive displacement, economic collapse due to the sudden departure of most European settlers, and deep social trauma.[^165]

### The first three decades of independence (1962–1991)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Houari_Boumediene%27s_Portrait.jpg/250px-Houari_Boumediene%27s_Portrait.jpg)

Houari Boumediene

The FLN’s fragile unity broke down immediately after independence, leading to power struggles between the GPRA's president [Ben Youcef Ben Khedda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benyoucef_Benkhedda "Benyoucef Benkhedda"), other GPRA members including Ait Ahmed, [Rabah Bitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rabah_Bitat "Rabah Bitat") and [Ahmed Ben Bella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmed_Ben_Bella "Ahmed Ben Bella"). The latter was supported by the external ALN under [Houari Boumedienne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houari_Boum%C3%A9di%C3%A8ne "Houari Boumédiène") who marched on Algiers. After mass [demonstrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demonstration "Demonstration") organised by the [UGTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Union_of_Algerian_Workers "General Union of Algerian Workers"), civil war was avoided and Ben Bella took power in September 1962.[^166] The new nation faced enormous challenges in rebuilding a dislocated economy and forging a coherent [national identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_identity "National identity").[^167]

Algeria's first president was the Front de Libération Nationale (FLN) leader Ahmed Ben Bella. Morocco's claim to [portions of western Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Morocco "Greater Morocco") led to the [Sand War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand_War "Sand War") in 1963. Ben Bella was [overthrown in 1965](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1965_Algerian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1965 Algerian coup d'état") by Houari Boumédiène, his former ally and defence minister. Under Ben Bella, the government had become increasingly [socialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_Socialism "Algerian Socialism") and authoritarian; Boumédienne continued this trend. However, he relied much more on the army for his support, and reduced the sole legal party to a symbolic role. He [collectivised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_farming "Collective farming") agriculture and launched a massive industrialisation drive. [Oil extraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraction_of_petroleum "Extraction of petroleum") facilities were nationalised. This was especially beneficial to the leadership after the international [1973 oil crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_oil_crisis "1973 oil crisis").

Boumédienne's successor, [Chadli Bendjedid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chadli_Bendjedid "Chadli Bendjedid"), introduced some liberal economic reforms. He promoted a policy of [Arabisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabisation "Arabisation") in Algerian society and public life. Teachers of Arabic, brought in from other Muslim countries, spread conventional Islamic thought in schools and sowed the seeds of a return to Orthodox Islam.[^168]

The Algerian economy became increasingly dependent on oil, leading to hardship when the price collapsed during the [1980s oil glut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1980s_oil_glut "1980s oil glut").[^169] Economic recession caused by the crash in world oil prices resulted in Algerian social unrest during the 1980s; by the end of the decade, Bendjedid introduced a multi-party system. Political parties developed, such as the [Islamic Salvation Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Salvation_Front "Islamic Salvation Front") (FIS), a broad coalition of Muslim groups.[^168]

### Civil War (1991–2002) and aftermath

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Algerian_massacres_1997-1998.png/250px-Algerian_massacres_1997-1998.png)

Massacres of over 50 people in 1997–1998. The Armed Islamic Group (GIA) claimed responsibility for many of them.

In December 1991 the [Islamic Salvation Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Salvation_Front "Islamic Salvation Front") dominated the first of two rounds of [legislative elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_legislative_election,_1991 "Algerian legislative election, 1991"). Fearing the election of an Islamist government, the authorities intervened on 11 January 1992, cancelling the elections. Bendjedid resigned and a [High Council of State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Council_of_State_\(Algeria\) "High Council of State (Algeria)") was installed to act as the Presidency. It banned the FIS, triggering a civil [insurgency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurgency "Insurgency") between the Front's armed wing, the [Armed Islamic Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armed_Islamic_Group_of_Algeria "Armed Islamic Group of Algeria"), and the national armed forces, in which more than 100,000 people are thought to have died. The Islamist militants conducted a violent campaign of [civilian massacres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_massacres_during_the_Algerian_Civil_War "List of massacres during the Algerian Civil War").[^170] At several points in the conflict, the situation in Algeria became a point of international concern, most notably during the crisis surrounding [Air France Flight 8969](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_France_Flight_8969 "Air France Flight 8969"), a hijacking perpetrated by the Armed Islamic Group. The Armed Islamic Group declared a ceasefire in October 1997.[^168]

Algeria held [elections in 1999](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_presidential_election,_1999 "Algerian presidential election, 1999"), considered biased by international observers and most opposition groups [^171] which were won by President [Abdelaziz Bouteflika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdelaziz_Bouteflika "Abdelaziz Bouteflika"). He worked to restore political stability to the country and announced a "Civil Concord" initiative, approved in a [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_Civil_Concord_referendum,_1999 "Algerian Civil Concord referendum, 1999"), under which many political prisoners were pardoned, and several thousand members of armed groups were granted exemption from prosecution under a limited amnesty, in force until 13 January 2000. The AIS disbanded and levels of insurgent violence fell rapidly. The [Groupe Salafiste pour la Prédication et le Combat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groupe_Salafiste_pour_la_Pr%C3%A9dication_et_le_Combat "Groupe Salafiste pour la Prédication et le Combat") (GSPC), a splinter group of the Armed Islamic Group, continued a terrorist campaign against the Government.[^168]

Bouteflika was re-elected in the [April 2004 presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_presidential_election,_2004 "Algerian presidential election, 2004") after campaigning on a programme of national reconciliation. The programme comprised economic, institutional, political and social reform to modernise the country, raise living standards, and tackle the causes of alienation. It also included a second amnesty initiative, the [Charter for Peace and National Reconciliation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charter_for_Peace_and_National_Reconciliation "Charter for Peace and National Reconciliation"), which was approved in a [referendum in September 2005](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_national_reconciliation_referendum,_2005 "Algerian national reconciliation referendum, 2005"). It offered amnesty to most guerrillas and Government security forces.[^168]

In November 2008, the [Algerian Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_Constitution "Algerian Constitution") was amended following a vote in Parliament, removing the two-term limit on Presidential incumbents. This change enabled Bouteflika to stand for re-election in the [2009 presidential elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_presidential_election,_2009 "Algerian presidential election, 2009"), and he was re-elected in April 2009. During his election campaign and following his re-election, Bouteflika promised to extend the programme of national reconciliation and a $150-billion spending programme to create three million new jobs, the construction of one million new housing units, and to continue public sector and infrastructure modernisation programmes.[^168]

A continuing series of protests throughout the country started on 28 December 2010, inspired by similar [protests across the Middle East and North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring"). On 24 February 2011, the government lifted Algeria's 19-year-old [state of emergency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_of_emergency "State of emergency").[^172] The government enacted legislation dealing with political parties, the electoral code, and the representation of women in elected bodies.[^173] In April 2011, Bouteflika promised further constitutional and political reform.[^168] However, elections are routinely criticised by opposition groups as unfair and international human rights groups say that media censorship and harassment of political opponents continue.

On 2 April 2019, Bouteflika resigned from the presidency after [mass protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019%E2%80%932020_Algerian_protests "2019–2020 Algerian protests") against his candidacy for a fifth term in office.[^174]

In December 2019, [Abdelmadjid Tebboune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdelmadjid_Tebboune "Abdelmadjid Tebboune") became Algeria's president, after winning the first round of the [presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_Algerian_presidential_election "2019 Algerian presidential election") with a record abstention rate – the highest of all presidential elections since Algeria's democracy in 1989. Tebboune is accused of being close to the military and being loyal to the deposed president. Tebboune rejects these accusations, claiming to be the victim of a witch hunt. He also reminds his detractors that he was expelled from the Government in August 2017 at the instigation of oligarchs languishing in prison.[^175] In [September 2024](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Algerian_presidential_election "2024 Algerian presidential election"), President Tebboune won a second term with a landslide 84.3 percent of the vote, although his opponents called the results fraud.[^176]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Algeria_relief.png/250px-Algeria_relief.png)

The Sahara, the Hoggar Mountains and the Atlas Mountains compose the Algerian relief.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/The_desert_in_Algeria.jpg/250px-The_desert_in_Algeria.jpg)

The Algerian Desert makes up more than 90% of the country's total area. 177

Since the 2011 breakup of Sudan, and the creation of South Sudan, Algeria has been the largest country in Africa. It is also the largest country of the [Mediterranean basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_basin "Mediterranean basin"). Its southern part includes a significant portion of the [Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahara "Sahara"). To the north, the [Tell Atlas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tell_Atlas "Tell Atlas") forms with the [Saharan Atlas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saharan_Atlas "Saharan Atlas"), further south, two parallel sets of reliefs in approaching eastbound, and between which are inserted vast plains and highlands. Both Atlas tend to merge in eastern Algeria. The vast mountain ranges of [Aures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aures "Aures") and [Nememcha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C3%A9bessa "Tébessa") occupy the entire northeastern Algeria and are delineated by the Tunisian border. The highest point is [Mount Tahat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Tahat "Mount Tahat") (3,003 metres or 9,852 feet).

Algeria lies mostly between latitudes [19°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/19th_parallel_north "19th parallel north") and [37°N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/37th_parallel_north "37th parallel north") (a small area is north of 37°N and south of 19°N), and longitudes [9°W](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/9th_meridian_west "9th meridian west") and [12°E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/12th_meridian_east "12th meridian east"). Most of the coastal area is hilly, sometimes even mountainous, and there are a few natural [harbours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harbour "Harbour"). The area from the coast to the Tell Atlas is fertile. South of the Tell Atlas is a [steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steppe "Steppe") landscape ending with the [Saharan Atlas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saharan_Atlas "Saharan Atlas"); farther south, there is the Sahara desert.[^178]

The [Hoggar Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hoggar_Mountains "Hoggar Mountains") ([Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"): جبال هقار), also known as the Hoggar, are a highland region in central Sahara, southern Algeria. They are located about 1,500 km (932 mi) south of the capital, Algiers, and just east of [Tamanghasset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamanghasset "Tamanghasset"). Algiers, [Oran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oran "Oran"), [Constantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantine,_Algeria "Constantine, Algeria"), and [Annaba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annaba "Annaba") are Algeria's main cities.[^178]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Djurdjura_mountains.jpg/250px-Djurdjura_mountains.jpg)

The Babor mountains and the Jijel Coast. The Erraguene lake can be seen on the right.

### Climate and hydrology

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Koppen-Geiger_Map_DZA_present.svg/250px-Koppen-Geiger_Map_DZA_present.svg.png)

Algeria map of Köppen climate classification

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Water_stress%2C_top_countries_%282022%29.svg/250px-Water_stress%2C_top_countries_%282022%29.svg.png)

Algeria was the seventh most water-stressed country in the world in 2022.

In this region, midday desert temperatures can be hot year round. After sunset, however, the clear, dry air permits rapid loss of heat, and the nights are cool to chilly. Enormous daily ranges in temperature are recorded.

Rainfall is fairly plentiful along the coastal part of the Tell Atlas, ranging from 400 to 670 mm (15.7 to 26.4 in) annually, the amount of precipitation increasing from west to east. [Precipitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation_\(meteorology\) "Precipitation (meteorology)") is heaviest in the northern part of eastern Algeria, where it reaches as much as 1,000 mm (39.4 in) in some years.

Farther inland, the rainfall is less plentiful. Algeria also has [ergs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erg_\(landform\) "Erg (landform)"), or sand dunes, between mountains. Among these, in the summer time when winds are heavy and gusty, temperatures can go up to 43.3 °C (110 °F).

[Climate change in Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Algeria "Climate change in Algeria") has wide-reaching effects on the country. Algeria was not a significant contributor to climate change,[^179] but, like other countries in the [Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_East_and_North_Africa "Middle East and North Africa"), is expected to be among the most affected by [climate change impacts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change "Effects of climate change").[^180] Because a large part of the country is in already hot and [arid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aridity "Aridity") geographies, including part of the [Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahara "Sahara"), already strong heat and [water resource](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_resource "Water resource") access challenges are expected to get worse.[^179] As early as 2014, scientists were attributing extreme [heat waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_wave "Heat wave") to climate change in Algeria.[^179] Algeria was ranked 46th of countries in the 2020 [Climate Change Performance Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_Change_Performance_Index "Climate Change Performance Index").[^181]

### Fauna and flora

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Artis_Desert_fox_-_eye_to_eye_%285528498997%29.jpg/250px-Artis_Desert_fox_-_eye_to_eye_%285528498997%29.jpg)

The fennec fox is the national animal of Algeria.

The varied vegetation of Algeria includes [coastal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastal "Coastal"), [mountainous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountainous "Mountainous") and grassy [desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert "Desert") -like regions which all support a wide range of wildlife.

In Algeria [forest cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_cover "Forest cover") is around 1% of the total land area, equivalent to 1,949,000 hectares (ha) of forest in 2020, up from 1,667,000 hectares (ha) in 1990. In 2020, naturally regenerating forest covered 1,439,000 hectares (ha) and planted forest covered 510,000 hectares (ha). Of the naturally regenerating forest 0% was reported to be [primary forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_forest "Primary forest") (consisting of native tree species with no clearly visible indications of human activity) and around 6% of the forest area was found within protected areas. For the year 2015, 80% of the forest area was reported to be under [public ownership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_ownership "State ownership"), 18% [private ownership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_property "Private property") and 2% with ownership listed as other or unknown.[^182] [^183]

Many of the creatures constituting the Algerian wildlife live in close proximity to civilisation. The most commonly seen animals include the wild [boars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boar "Boar"), [jackals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jackal "Jackal"), and [gazelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gazelle "Gazelle"), although it is not uncommon to spot [fennecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fennecs "Fennecs") (foxes), and [jerboas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerboas "Jerboas"). Algeria also has a small [African leopard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_leopard "African leopard") and [Saharan cheetah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saharan_cheetah "Saharan cheetah") population, but these are seldom seen. A species of deer, the [Barbary stag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_stag "Barbary stag"), inhabits the dense humid forests in the north-eastern areas. The [fennec fox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fennec_fox "Fennec fox") is the [national animal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_animal "National animal") of Algeria.[^184]

A variety of bird species makes the country an attraction for bird watchers. The forests are inhabited by boars and jackals. [Barbary macaques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_macaque "Barbary macaque") are the sole native monkey. Snakes, [monitor lizards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monitor_lizard "Monitor lizard"), and numerous other reptiles can be found living among an array of [rodents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodent "Rodent") throughout the [semi arid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi_arid "Semi arid") regions of Algeria. Many animals are now extinct, including the [Barbary lions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_lion "Barbary lion"), [Atlas bears](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_bear "Atlas bear") and [crocodiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_African_crocodile "West African crocodile").[^185]

In the north, some of the native flora includes [Macchia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macchia "Macchia") scrub, [olive trees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olive "Olive"), [oaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oak "Oak"), [cedars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cedrus "Cedrus") and other [conifers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinophyta "Pinophyta"). The mountain regions contain large forests of evergreens ([Aleppo pine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleppo_pine "Aleppo pine"), [juniper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juniper "Juniper"), and [evergreen oak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evergreen_oak "Evergreen oak")) and some deciduous trees. [Fig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ficus "Ficus"), [eucalyptus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eucalyptus "Eucalyptus"), [agave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agave "Agave"), and various [palm trees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arecaceae "Arecaceae") grow in the warmer areas. The [grape vine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grape_vine "Grape vine") is indigenous to the coast. In the Sahara region, some oases have palm trees. [Acacias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acacia "Acacia") with wild [olives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olive "Olive") are the predominant flora in the remainder of the [Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahara "Sahara"). Algeria had a 2018 [Forest Landscape Integrity Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_Landscape_Integrity_Index "Forest Landscape Integrity Index") mean score of 5.22/10, ranking it 106th globally out of 172 countries.[^186]

Camels are used extensively; the desert also abounds with venomous and nonvenomous snakes, [scorpions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scorpion "Scorpion"), and numerous insects.

## Government and politics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Abdelmadjid_Tebboune_%282023%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Abdelmadjid_Tebboune_%282023%29_%28cropped%29.jpg)

Abdelmadjid Tebboune, President of Algeria since 2019

Algeria's government has been described as authoritarian,[^187] [^188] [^189] [^190] and elected politicians have relatively little sway over affairs in the country. Instead, a group of unelected civilian and military "décideurs" ("deciders") known as "le pouvoir" ("the power") exercise *de facto* rule over the country, even deciding who should be president.[^191] [^192] [^193] The most powerful man might have been [Mohamed Mediène](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohamed_Medi%C3%A8ne "Mohamed Mediène"), the head of military intelligence, before he was brought down during the [2019 protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019%E2%80%932020_Algerian_protests "2019–2020 Algerian protests").[^194] In recent years, many of these generals have died, retired, or been imprisoned. After the death of General [Larbi Belkheir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larbi_Belkheir "Larbi Belkheir"), previous president [Bouteflika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdelaziz_Bouteflika "Abdelaziz Bouteflika") put loyalists in key posts, notably at [Sonatrach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonatrach "Sonatrach"), and secured constitutional amendments that made him re-electable indefinitely, until he was brought down in 2019 during [protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019%E2%80%932020_Algerian_protests "2019–2020 Algerian protests").[^195]

The head of state is the [President of Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Algeria "President of Algeria"), who is elected for a five-year term. The president is limited to two five-year terms. The [most recent presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_Algerian_presidential_election "2019 Algerian presidential election") was planned to be in April 2019, but [widespread protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_Algerian_protests "2019 Algerian protests") erupted on 22 February against the president's decision to participate in the election, which resulted in President Bouteflika announcing his resignation on 3 April.[^196] [Abdelmadjid Tebboune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdelmadjid_Tebboune "Abdelmadjid Tebboune"), an independent candidate, was elected as president after the election eventually took place on 12 December 2019. Protestors refused to recognise Tebboune as president, citing demands for comprehensive reform of the political system.[^197] Algeria has universal [suffrage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suffrage "Suffrage") at 18 years of age.[^10] The President is the head of the [army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_Army "Algerian Army"), the [Council of Ministers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_of_Algeria "Council of Ministers of Algeria") and the [High Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=High_Security_Council&action=edit&redlink=1 "High Security Council (page does not exist)"). He appoints the [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Algeria "Prime Minister of Algeria") who is also the head of government.[^198]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/H%C3%A9micycle_de_l%27assembl%C3%A9e_populaire_nationale_%28Alg%C3%A9rie%29.jpg/250px-H%C3%A9micycle_de_l%27assembl%C3%A9e_populaire_nationale_%28Alg%C3%A9rie%29.jpg)

The People's National Assembly

The Algerian parliament is [bicameral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicameral "Bicameral"); the lower house, the [People's National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_National_Assembly "People's National Assembly"), has 462 members who are directly elected for five-year terms, while the upper house, the [Council of the Nation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_the_Nation "Council of the Nation"), has 144 members serving six-year terms, of which 96 members are chosen by local assemblies and 48 are appointed by the president.[^199] According to the [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Algeria "Constitution of Algeria"), no political association may be formed if it is "based on differences in religion, language, race, gender, profession, or region". In addition, political campaigns must be exempt from the aforementioned subjects.[^200]

Parliamentary elections were last held in [June 2021](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Algerian_parliamentary_election "2021 Algerian parliamentary election"). In the elections, the [FLN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Liberation_Front_\(Algeria\) "National Liberation Front (Algeria)") lost 66 of its seats, but remained the largest party with 98 seats. Other parties included the [Movement of the Society for Peace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Movement_of_Society_for_Peace "Movement of Society for Peace") which won 65 seats, the [National Rally for Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Rally_for_Democracy_\(Algeria\) "National Rally for Democracy (Algeria)") which won 58 seats, the [Future Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Future_Front "Future Front") which won 48 seats, and the [National Construction Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Construction_Movement "National Construction Movement") which won 39 seats.

### Foreign relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Abdelaziz_Bouteflika_and_George_W_Bush_20080707.jpg/250px-Abdelaziz_Bouteflika_and_George_W_Bush_20080707.jpg)

President Abdelaziz Bouteflika and George W. Bush exchange handshakes at the Windsor Hotel Toya Resort and Spa in Tōyako Town, Abuta District, Hokkaidō in 2008. With them are Dmitriy Medvedev, left, and Yasuo Fukuda, right.

Algeria is included in the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") 's [European Neighbourhood Policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Neighbourhood_Policy "European Neighbourhood Policy") (ENP) which aims at bringing the EU and its neighbours closer. Giving incentives and rewarding best performers, as well as offering funds in a faster and more flexible manner, are the two main principles underlying the European Neighbourhood Instrument (ENI) that came into force in 2014. It has a budget of €15.4 billion and provides the bulk of funding through a number of programmes.

In 2009, the French government agreed to compensate victims of nuclear tests in Algeria. Defence Minister Hervé Morin stated that "It's time for our country to be at peace with itself, at peace thanks to a system of compensation and reparations", when presenting the draft law on the payouts. Algerian officials and activists believe that this is a good first step and hope that this move would encourage broader reparation.[^201]

[Tensions between Algeria and Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria%E2%80%93Morocco_relations "Algeria–Morocco relations") in relation to the [Western Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Sahara "Western Sahara") have been an obstacle to tightening the [Arab Maghreb Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Maghreb_Union "Arab Maghreb Union"), nominally established in 1989, but which has carried little practical weight.[^202] On 24 August 2021, Algeria announced the break of diplomatic relations with Morocco.[^203]

Algeria's current foreign minister is [Ahmed Attaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmed_Attaf "Ahmed Attaf"). Their representative to the United Nations is [Amar Bendjama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amar_Bendjama "Amar Bendjama").[^204]

### Military

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Al-chihab.jpg/250px-Al-chihab.jpg)

A Djebel Chenoua -class corvette, designed and built in Algeria

The military of Algeria consists of the [People's National Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_National_Army "People's National Army") (ANP), the [Algerian National Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_National_Navy "Algerian National Navy") (MRA), and the [Algerian Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_Air_Force "Algerian Air Force") (QJJ), plus the [Territorial Air Defence Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_Air_Defence_Forces "Territorial Air Defence Forces").[^18] It is the direct successor of the [National Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Liberation_Army_\(Algeria\) "National Liberation Army (Algeria)") (Armée de Libération Nationale or ALN), the armed wing of the nationalist National Liberation Front which fought French colonial [occupation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_occupation "Military occupation") during the Algerian War of Independence (1954–62).

Total military personnel include 147,000 active, 150,000 reserve, and 187,000 paramilitary staff (2008 estimate).[^205] Service in the military is compulsory for men aged 19–30, for a total of 12 months.[^206] The military expenditure was 4.3% of the gross domestic product (GDP) in 2012.[^18] Algeria has the [second-largest military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_number_of_military_and_paramilitary_personnel "List of countries by number of military and paramilitary personnel") in North Africa with the largest defence budget in Africa ($10 billion).[^207] Most of Algeria's weapons are imported from Russia, with whom they [are a close ally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria%E2%80%93Russia_relations "Algeria–Russia relations").[^207] [^208]

In 2007, the Algerian Air Force signed a deal with Russia to purchase 49 [MiG-29](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MiG-29 "MiG-29") SMT and 6 MiG-29UBT at an estimated cost of $1.9 billion. Russia is also building two [636-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilo_class_submarine "Kilo class submarine") diesel [submarines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submarines "Submarines") for Algeria.[^209]

### Human rights

Algeria has been categorised by the US government funded [Freedom House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_House "Freedom House") as "not free" since it began publishing such ratings in 1972, with the exception of 1989, 1990, and 1991, when the country was labelled "partly free".[^210] In December 2016, the *Euro-Mediterranean Human Rights Monitor* issued a report regarding violation of [media freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Media_freedom "Media freedom") in Algeria. It clarified that the Algerian government imposed restrictions on [freedom of the press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press "Freedom of the press"); expression; and right to peaceful demonstration, protest and assembly as well as intensified censorship of the media and websites. Due to the fact that the journalists and activists criticise the ruling government, some media organisations' licenses are cancelled.[^211]

Independent and autonomous trade unions face routine harassment from the government, with many leaders imprisoned and protests suppressed. In 2016, a number of unions, many of which were involved in the 2010–2012 Algerian Protests, have been deregistered by the government.[^212] [^213] [^214]

[Homosexuality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_Algeria "LGBT rights in Algeria") is illegal in Algeria.[^215] Public homosexual behavior is punishable by up to two years in prison.[^216] Despite this, about 26% of Algerians think that homosexuality should be accepted, according to the survey conducted by the [BBC News Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC_Arabic "BBC Arabic") -Arab Barometer in 2019. Algeria showed the highest LGBT acceptance compared to other Arab countries where the survey was conducted.[^217]

[Human Rights Watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch "Human Rights Watch") has accused the Algerian authorities of using the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic") as an excuse to prevent pro-democracy movements and protests in the country, leading to the arrest of youths as part of [social distancing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_distancing "Social distancing").[^218]

## Economy

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/GDP_per_capita_development_in_Algeria.png/500px-GDP_per_capita_development_in_Algeria.png)

GDP per capita development in Algeria

Algeria's currency is the [dinar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_dinar "Algerian dinar") (DZD). The economy remains dominated by the state, a legacy of the country's socialist post-independence development model. In June 2024 The World Bank's 2024 report marks a turning point for Algeria, which joins the select club of upper-middle-income countries. This economic rise, the result of an ambitious development strategy, places the country in the same category as emerging powers such as China, Brazil and Turkey [^221] [^222] [^223] In recent years, the Algerian government has halted the privatisation of state-owned industries and imposed restrictions on imports and foreign involvement in its economy.[^224] These restrictions are just starting to be lifted off recently although questions about Algeria's slowly-diversifying economy remain.[^225] [^226]

Algeria has struggled to develop industries outside hydrocarbons in part because of high costs and an inert state bureaucracy. The government's efforts to diversify the economy by attracting foreign and domestic investment outside the energy sector have done little to reduce high youth unemployment rates or to address housing shortages.[^18] The country is facing a number of short-term and medium-term problems, including the need to diversify the economy, strengthen political, economic and financial reforms, improve the business climate and reduce inequalities among regions.[^173]

A wave of economic protests in February and March 2011 prompted the Algerian government to offer more than $23 billion in public grants and retroactive salary and benefit increases. Public spending has increased by 27% annually during the past five years. The 2010–14 public-investment programme will cost US$286 billion, 40% of which will go to human development.[^173]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/A_Market_in_Algeria.jpg/250px-A_Market_in_Algeria.jpg)

Street market in Algeria

Thanks to strong hydrocarbon revenues, Algeria has a cushion of $173 billion in [foreign currency reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_currency_reserves "Foreign currency reserves") and a large hydrocarbon stabilization fund. In addition, Algeria's [external debt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_external_debt "List of countries by external debt") is extremely low at about 2% of GDP.[^18] The economy remains very dependent on hydrocarbon wealth, and, despite high foreign exchange reserves (US$178 billion, equivalent to three years of imports), current expenditure growth makes Algeria's budget more vulnerable to the risk of prolonged lower hydrocarbon revenues.[^227]

Algeria has not joined the [WTO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WTO "WTO"), despite several years of negotiations but is a member of the [Greater Arab Free Trade Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Arab_Free_Trade_Area "Greater Arab Free Trade Area"),[^228] the [African Continental Free Trade Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Continental_Free_Trade_Area "African Continental Free Trade Area"),[^229] and has an association agreement with the European Union.[^230] [^231]

Turkish direct investments have accelerated in Algeria, with total value reaching $5 billion. As of 2022, the number of Turkish companies present in Algeria has reached 1,400. In 2020, despite the pandemic, more than 130 Turkish companies were created in Algeria.[^232]

### Oil and natural resources

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Algeria_pipelines_map.jpg/250px-Algeria_pipelines_map.jpg)

Pipelines across Algeria

Algeria, whose economy is reliant on petroleum, has been an [OPEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OPEC "OPEC") member since 1969. Its crude oil production stands at around 1.1 million barrels/day, but it is also a major gas producer and exporter, with important links to Europe.[^233] Hydrocarbons have long been the backbone of the economy, accounting for roughly 60% of budget revenues, 30% of GDP, and 87.7% [^234] of export earnings. Algeria has the [10th-largest reserves of natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_natural_gas_proven_reserves "List of countries by natural gas proven reserves") in the world and is the [sixth-largest gas exporter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_natural_gas_exports "List of countries by natural gas exports"). The U.S. [Energy Information Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_Information_Administration "Energy Information Administration") reported that in 2005, Algeria had 4.5 trillion cubic metres (160×10 ~~^~~ <sup>12</sup> cu ft) of proven [natural gas reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas_reserves "Natural gas reserves").[^235] It also ranks [16th in oil reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_proven_oil_reserves "List of countries by proven oil reserves").[^18]

Non-hydrocarbon growth for 2011 was projected at 5%. To cope with social demands, the authorities raised expenditure, especially on basic food support, employment creation, support for SMEs, and higher salaries. High hydrocarbon prices have improved the current account and the already large international reserves position.[^227]

Income from oil and gas rose in 2011 as a result of continuing high oil prices, though the trend in production volume is downward.[^173] Production from the oil and gas sector in terms of volume continues to decline, dropping from 43.2 million tonnes to 32 million tonnes between 2007 and 2011. Nevertheless, the sector accounted for 98% of the total volume of exports in 2011, against 48% in 1962,[^236] and 70% of budgetary receipts, or US$71.4 billion.[^173]

The Algerian national oil company is [Sonatrach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonatrach "Sonatrach"), which plays a key role in all aspects of the oil and natural gas sectors in Algeria. All foreign operators must work in partnership with Sonatrach, which usually has majority ownership in production-sharing agreements.[^237]

Access to [biocapacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biocapacity "Biocapacity") in Algeria is lower than world average. In 2016, Algeria had 0.53 global hectares [^238] of biocapacity per person within its territory, much less than the world average of 1.6 global hectares per person.[^239] In 2016, Algeria used 2.4 global hectares of biocapacity per person – their [ecological footprint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_footprint "Ecological footprint") of consumption. This means they use just under 4.5 times as much biocapacity as Algeria contains. As a result, Algeria is running a biocapacity deficit.[^238] In April 2022, diplomats from Italy and Spain held talks after Rome's move to secure large volume of Algerian gas stoked concerns in Madrid.[^240] Under the deal between Algeria's Sonatrach and Italy's Eni, Algeria will send an additional 9 billion cubic metres of gas to Italy by next year and in 2024.[^241]

### Research and alternative energy sources

Algeria has invested an estimated 100 billion dinars towards developing research facilities and paying researchers. This development programme is meant to advance alternative energy production, especially solar and wind power.[^242] Algeria is estimated to have the largest solar energy potential in the Mediterranean, so the government has funded the creation of a solar science park in Hassi R'Mel. Currently, Algeria has 20,000 research professors at various universities and over 780 research labs, with state-set goals to expand to 1,000. Besides solar energy, areas of research in Algeria include space and satellite telecommunications, nuclear power and medical research.

### Labour market

The overall rate of unemployment was 11.8% in 2023.[^243] The government strengthened in 2011 the job programmes introduced in 1988, in particular in the framework of the programme to aid those seeking work (Dispositif d'Aide à l'Insertion Professionnelle).[^173]

Despite a decline in total unemployment, youth and women unemployment is high.[^227]

### Tourism

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Giants_-_Rock_towers_in_the_Algerian_Sahara.jpg/250px-Giants_-_Rock_towers_in_the_Algerian_Sahara.jpg)

[Djanet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djanet "Djanet")

The development of the tourism sector in Algeria had previously been hampered by a lack of facilities, but since 2004 a broad tourism development strategy has been implemented resulting in many hotels of a high modern standard being built.

There are several [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO") [World Heritage Sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites "World Heritage Sites") in Algeria [^244] which includes [Al Qal'a of Beni Hammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Qal%27a_of_Beni_Hammad "Al Qal'a of Beni Hammad"), the first capital of the [Hammadid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hammadid "Hammadid") empire; [Tipasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tipasa "Tipasa"), a Phoenician and later Roman town; [Djémila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dj%C3%A9mila "Djémila") and [Timgad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timgad "Timgad"), both [Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") ruins; [M'Zab Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%27Zab_Valley "M'Zab Valley"), a limestone valley containing a large urbanised [oasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oasis "Oasis"); and the [Casbah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casbah "Casbah") of Algiers, an important citadel. The only natural [World Heritage Site](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Site "World Heritage Site") in Algeria is the [Tassili n'Ajjer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tassili_n%27Ajjer "Tassili n'Ajjer"), a mountain range.

### Transport

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Autoroute_est_ouest_ghomri2.JPG/250px-Autoroute_est_ouest_ghomri2.JPG)

The main highway connecting the Moroccan to the Tunisian border was a part of the Cairo–Dakar Highway project.

Two trans-African automobile routes pass through Algeria:

- [Cairo-Dakar Highway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cairo-Dakar_Highway "Cairo-Dakar Highway")
- [Algiers-Lagos Highway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algiers-Lagos_Highway "Algiers-Lagos Highway")

The Algerian road network is the densest in Africa; its length is estimated at 180,000 km (110,000 mi) of highways, with more than 3,756 structures and a paving rate of 85%. This network has been complemented by the [East-West Highway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria_East%E2%80%93West_Highway "Algeria East–West Highway"), a major infrastructure project completed in 2023. It is a three-way, 1,216-kilometre-long (756 mi) highway, linking [Annaba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annaba "Annaba") in the extreme east to the [Tlemcen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tlemcen "Tlemcen") in the far west. Algeria is also crossed by the [Trans-Sahara Highway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Sahara_Highway "Trans-Sahara Highway"), which is now completely paved. This road is supported by the Algerian government to increase trade between the six countries crossed: Algeria, [Mali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mali "Mali"), [Niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger "Niger"), [Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigeria "Nigeria"), [Chad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chad "Chad"), and [Tunisia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunisia "Tunisia").

## Demographics

Algeria has a population of an estimated 47.4 million,[^11] [^245] of which 75% [^246] to 85% are [Arabs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabs "Arabs") and [Arabized Berbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabized_Berber "Arabized Berber").[^18] [^247] [^248] The majority of the population is of Berber ancestry, though only around 20% identify as Berber.[^249] At the outset of the 20th century, its population was approximately 4 million.[^250] About 90% of Algerians live in the northern, coastal area; the inhabitants of the Sahara desert are mainly concentrated in [oases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oasis "Oasis"), although some 1.5 million remain [nomadic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomad "Nomad") or partly nomadic. 28.1% of Algerians are under the age of 15.[^18]

Between 90,000 and 165,000 [Sahrawis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahrawi_people "Sahrawi people") from [Western Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Sahara "Western Sahara") live in the [Sahrawi refugee camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahrawi_refugee_camps "Sahrawi refugee camps"),[^251] [^252] in the western Algerian Sahara desert.[^253] There are also more than 4,000 [Palestinian refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_refugee "Palestinian refugee"), who are well integrated and have not asked for assistance from the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR).[^251] [^252] In 2009, 35,000 [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_Chinese "Overseas Chinese") migrant workers lived in Algeria.[^254]

The largest concentration of Algerian migrants outside Algeria is in France, which has reportedly over 1.7 million [Algerians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerians "Algerians") of up to the second generation.[^255]

There are also many [foreign communities in Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_communities_in_Algeria "Foreign communities in Algeria"), though these do not make up a significant portion of the population.

### Ethnic groups

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Tenus_traditionnelles_alg%C3%A9riennes_27.png/250px-Tenus_traditionnelles_alg%C3%A9riennes_27.png)

Some of Algeria's traditional clothes

[Arabs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabs "Arabs") and indigenous [Berbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berbers "Berbers") as well as [Phoenicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicians "Phoenicians"), [Romans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome"), [Vandals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vandals "Vandals"), [Byzantine Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Greeks "Byzantine Greeks"), [Turks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_people "Turkish people"), various [Sub-Saharan Africans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africans "Sub-Saharan Africans"), and French have contributed to the history and culture of Algeria.[^257] Descendants of [Andalusi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Andalus "Al-Andalus") refugees are also present in the population of Algiers and other cities.[^258] Moreover, Spanish was spoken by these [Aragonese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aragon "Aragon") and [Castillian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Castilian_people "Castilian people") [Morisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moriscos "Moriscos") descendants deep into the 18th century, and even [Catalan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_language "Catalan language") was spoken at the same time by [Catalan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalonia "Catalonia") [Morisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moriscos "Moriscos") descendants in the small town of Grish El-Oued.[^259]

Centuries of [Arab migrations to the Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_migrations_to_the_Maghreb "Arab migrations to the Maghreb") since the seventh century shifted the demographic scope in Algeria. Estimates vary based on different sources. The majority of the population of Algeria is ethnically [Arab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabs "Arabs"), constituting between 75% [^246] [^260] [^261] [^262] and 80% [^263] [^264] [^265] to 85% [^266] [^267] of the population. [Berbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berbers "Berbers") who make up between 15% [^18] and 20% [^264] [^263] [^268] to 24% [^260] [^261] [^262] of the population are divided into many groups with varying languages. The largest of these are the [Kabyles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabyle_people "Kabyle people"), who live in the [Kabylie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabylie "Kabylie") region east of Algiers, the [Chaoui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaoui_people "Chaoui people") of Northeast Algeria, the [Tuaregs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuareg_people "Tuareg people") in the southern desert and the [Shenwa people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chenouas "Chenouas") of North Algeria.[^269] During the colonial period, there was a large (10% in 1960) [^270] [European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Europe "Ethnic groups in Europe") population who became known as *[Pied-Noirs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pied-Noir "Pied-Noir")*. They were primarily of French, Spanish and [Italian origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Algerians "Italian Algerians"). Almost all of this population left during the war of independence or immediately after its end.[^271]

### Languages

[Modern Standard Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic "Modern Standard Arabic") and [Berber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_language "Berber language") are the official languages.[^272] [Algerian Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_Arabic "Algerian Arabic") (Darja) is the language used by the majority of the population. Colloquial Algerian Arabic has some Berber loanwords which represent 8% to 9% of its vocabulary.[^273]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Tizi_Ouzou_Tasdawit.jpg/250px-Tizi_Ouzou_Tasdawit.jpg)

Signs in the University of Tizi Ouzou in three languages: Arabic, Berber, and French

[Berber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_language "Berber language") has been recognised as a "national language" by the constitutional amendment of 8 May 2002.[^274] [Kabyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabyle_language "Kabyle language"), the predominant Berber language, is taught and is partially co-official (with a few restrictions) in parts of [Kabylie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabylie "Kabylie"). Kabyle has a significant [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic"), [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language"), [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin"), [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language"), [Phoenician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenician_language "Phoenician language") and [Punic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punic_language "Punic language") substratum, and Arabic loanwords represent 35% of the total Kabyle vocabulary.[^275] In February 2016, the Algerian constitution passed a resolution that made Berber an official language alongside Arabic. Algeria emerged as a bilingual state after 1962.[^276] Colloquial [Algerian Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_Arabic "Algerian Arabic") is spoken by about 83% of the population and Berber by 27%.[^277]

Although French has no official status in Algeria, it has one of the largest Francophone populations in the world,[^278] and French is widely used in government, media (newspapers, radio, local television), and both the education system (from primary school onwards) and academia due to [Algeria's colonial history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_rule_in_Algeria "French rule in Algeria"). It can be regarded as a [lingua franca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lingua_franca "Lingua franca") of Algeria. In 2008, 11.2 million Algerians could read and write in French.[^279] In 2013, it was estimated that 60% of the population could speak or understand French.[^280] In 2022, it was estimated that 33% of the population was Francophone.[^281]

The use of [English in Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_in_Algeria "English in Algeria"), though limited in comparison to the previously mentioned languages, has increased due to globalisation.[^282] [^283] In 2022 it was announced that English would be taught in elementary schools.[^284]

### Religion

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/%D9%85%D8%B3%D8%AC%D8%AF_%D8%A7%D9%84%D8%A3%D9%85%D9%8A%D8%B1_%D8%B9%D8%A8%D8%AF_%D8%A7%D9%84%D9%82%D8%A7%D8%AF%D8%B1_%D9%83%D9%85%D8%A7_%D9%84%D9%85_%D8%AA%D8%B1%D8%A7%D9%87_%D9%85%D9%86_%D9%82%D8%A8%D9%84_%D9%82%D8%B3%D9%86%D8%B7%D9%8A%D9%86%D8%A9.jpg/250px-%D9%85%D8%B3%D8%AC%D8%AF_%D8%A7%D9%84%D8%A3%D9%85%D9%8A%D8%B1_%D8%B9%D8%A8%D8%AF_%D8%A7%D9%84%D9%82%D8%A7%D8%AF%D8%B1_%D9%83%D9%85%D8%A7_%D9%84%D9%85_%D8%AA%D8%B1%D8%A7%D9%87_%D9%85%D9%86_%D9%82%D8%A8%D9%84_%D9%82%D8%B3%D9%86%D8%B7%D9%8A%D9%86%D8%A9.jpg)

Emir Abdelkader Mosque in Constantine

[Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") is the predominant religion in Algeria, with its adherents, mostly Sunnis, accounting for 99% of the population according to a 2021 CIA *[World Factbook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Factbook "World Factbook")* estimate,[^18] and 98.4% according to [Pew Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pew_Research_Center "Pew Research Center") in 2020.[^285] There are about 290,000 [Ibadis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibadi "Ibadi") in the M'zab Valley in the region of [Ghardaia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghardaia "Ghardaia").

Prior to independence, Algeria was home to more than 1.3 million Christians (mostly of [European ancestry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pied-Noir "Pied-Noir")).[^286] Most of the Christian settlers left to France after the country's independence.[^287] [^288] Today, estimates of the [Christian population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_in_Algeria "Christianity in Algeria") range from 100,000 to 200,000.[^289] Algerian citizens who are Christians predominantly belong to [Protestant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestantism "Protestantism") denominations, which have seen increased pressure from the government in recent years including many forced closures.[^289]

According to the [Arab Barometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Barometer "Arab Barometer") in 2018–2019, the vast majority of Algerians (99.1%) continue to identify as Muslim.[^290] The June 2019 [Arab Barometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Barometer "Arab Barometer") - [BBC News](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC_News "BBC News") report found that the percentage of Algerians identifying as non-religious has grown from around 8% in 2013 to around 15% in 2018.[^291] The Arab Barometer December 2019, found that the growth in the percentage of Algerians identifying as non-religious is largely driven by young Algerians, with roughly 25% describing themselves as non-religious.[^292] However, the 2021 Arab Barometer report found that those who said they were not religious among Algerians has decreased, with just 2.6% identifying as non-religious. In that same report, 69.5% of Algerians identified as religious and another 27.8% identifying as somewhat religious.[^290] [^293]

Algeria has given the [Muslim world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_world "Muslim world") a number of prominent thinkers, including [Emir Abdelkader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emir_Abdelkader "Emir Abdelkader"), [Abdelhamid Ben Badis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdelhamid_Ben_Badis "Abdelhamid Ben Badis"), [Mouloud Kacem Naît Belkacem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouloud_Kacem_Na%C3%AEt_Belkacem "Mouloud Kacem Naît Belkacem"), [Malek Bennabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malek_Bennabi "Malek Bennabi") and [Mohamed Arkoun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohamed_Arkoun "Mohamed Arkoun").

### Health

In 2018, Algeria had the highest numbers of physicians in the Maghreb region (1.72 per 1,000 people), nurses (2.23 per 1,000 people), and dentists (0.31 per 1,000 people). Access to "improved water sources" was around 97.4% of the population in urban areas and 98.7% of the population in the rural areas. Some 99% of Algerians living in urban areas, and around 93.4% of those living in rural areas, had access to "improved sanitation". According to the World Bank, Algeria is making progress towards its goal of "reducing by half the number of people without sustainable access to improved drinking water and basic sanitation by 2015". Given Algeria's young population, policy favours preventive health care and clinics over hospitals. In keeping with this policy, the government maintains an immunisation programme. However, poor sanitation and unclean water still cause [tuberculosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuberculosis "Tuberculosis"), [hepatitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hepatitis "Hepatitis"), [measles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measles "Measles"), [typhoid fever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typhoid_fever "Typhoid fever"), [cholera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cholera "Cholera") and [dysentery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysentery "Dysentery"). The poor generally receive healthcare free of charge.[^294]

Health records have been maintained in Algeria since 1882 and began adding Muslims living in the south to their vital record database in 1905 during French rule.[^295]

### Education

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/UIS_Literacy_Rate_Algeria_population_plus15_1980_2015.png/250px-UIS_Literacy_Rate_Algeria_population_plus15_1980_2015.png)

UIS literacy rate Algeria population plus 15 1985–2015

Since the 1970s, in a centralised system that was designed to significantly reduce the rate of illiteracy, the Algerian government introduced a decree by which school attendance became compulsory for all children aged between 6 and 15 years who have the ability to track their learning through the 20 facilities built since independence, now the literacy rate is around 92.6%.[^296] Since 1972, Arabic is used as the language of instruction during the first nine years of schooling. From the third year, French is taught and it is also the language of instruction for science classes. The students can also learn English, Italian, Spanish and German. In 2008, new programmes at the elementary appeared, therefore the compulsory schooling does not start at the age of six anymore, but at the age of five.[^297] Apart from the 122 private schools, the Universities of the State are free of charge. After nine years of primary school, students can go to a high school or to an educational institution. The school offers two programmes: general or technical. At the end of the third year of secondary school, students pass the exam of the baccalaureate, which allows once it is successful to pursue graduate studies in universities and institutes.[^298]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Wikipedia_Education_Program_Algeria_V2_Ceremony_%2896%29.jpg/250px-Wikipedia_Education_Program_Algeria_V2_Ceremony_%2896%29.jpg)

Students at the University of Chlef in Algeria

Education is officially compulsory for children between the ages of six and 15. In 2008, the illiteracy rate for people over 10 was 22.3%, 15.6% for men and 29.0% for women. The province with the lowest rate of illiteracy was [Algiers Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algiers_Province "Algiers Province") at 11.6%, while the province with the highest rate was [Djelfa Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djelfa_Province "Djelfa Province") at 35.5%.[^299]

Algeria has 26 universities and 67 institutions of higher education, which must accommodate a million Algerians and 80,000 foreign students in 2008. The [University of Algiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Algiers "University of Algiers"), founded in 1879, is the oldest, it offers education in various disciplines (law, medicine, science and letters). Twenty-five of these universities and almost all of the institutions of higher education were founded after the independence of the country.

Even if some of them offer instruction in [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic") like areas of law and the economy, most of the other sectors such as science and medicine continue to be provided in French and English. Among the most important universities, there are the [University of Sciences and Technology Houari Boumediene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Sciences_and_Technology_Houari_Boumediene "University of Sciences and Technology Houari Boumediene"), the [University of Mentouri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Mentouri "University of Mentouri") Constantine, and [University of Oran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Oran "University of Oran") Es-Senia. The [University of Abou Bekr Belkaïd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Abou_Bekr_Belka%C3%AFd "University of Abou Bekr Belkaïd") in Tlemcen and [University of Batna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Batna "University of Batna") Hadj Lakhdar occupy the 26th and 45th row in Africa.[^300] Algeria was ranked 115th in the [Global Innovation Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Innovation_Index "Global Innovation Index") in 2024,[^301] and kept the same rank in the index of 2025.[^302]

## Culture

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Bensari2.jpg/250px-Bensari2.jpg)

Algerian musicians in Tlemcen, Regency of Algiers; by Bachir Yellès

Modern Algerian literature, split between [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Arabic "Classical Arabic"), [Tamazight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_languages "Berber languages") and French, has been strongly influenced by the country's recent history. [Famous novelists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Algerian_writers "List of Algerian writers") of the 20th century include [Mohammed Dib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_Dib "Mohammed Dib"), [Albert Camus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Camus "Albert Camus"), [Kateb Yacine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kateb_Yacine "Kateb Yacine") and [Ahlam Mosteghanemi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahlam_Mosteghanemi "Ahlam Mosteghanemi") while [Assia Djebar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assia_Djebar "Assia Djebar") is widely translated. Among the important novelists of the 1980s were [Rachid Mimouni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rachid_Mimouni "Rachid Mimouni"), later vice-president of [Amnesty International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amnesty_International "Amnesty International"), and [Tahar Djaout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tahar_Djaout "Tahar Djaout"), murdered by an [Islamist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamist "Islamist") group in 1993 for his secularist views.[^303]

[Malek Bennabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malek_Bennabi "Malek Bennabi") and [Frantz Fanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frantz_Fanon "Frantz Fanon") are noted for their thoughts on [decolonisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decolonization "Decolonization"); [Augustine of Hippo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo "Augustine of Hippo") was born in [Tagaste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tagaste "Tagaste") (modern-day [Souk Ahras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Souk_Ahras "Souk Ahras")); and [Ibn Khaldun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun "Ibn Khaldun"), though born in [Tunis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunis "Tunis"), wrote the [Muqaddima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muqaddima "Muqaddima") while staying in Algeria. The works of the [Sanusi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senussi "Senussi") family in pre-colonial times, and of [Emir Abdelkader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emir_Abdelkader "Emir Abdelkader") and Sheikh [Ben Badis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdelhamid_Ben_Badis "Abdelhamid Ben Badis") in colonial times, are widely noted. The Latin author [Apuleius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apuleius "Apuleius") was born in [Madaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madaurus "Madaurus") (Mdaourouch), in what later became Algeria.

Contemporary [Algerian cinema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_cinema "Algerian cinema") is varied in terms of genre, exploring a wider range of themes and issues. There has been a transition from cinema which focused on the war of independence to films more concerned with the everyday lives of Algerians.[^304]

### Art

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3a/Portrait-racim.jpg)

Mohammed Racim; founder of the Algerian school for painting

Algerian painters, like [Mohammed Racim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_Racim "Mohammed Racim") and [Baya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baya_\(artist\) "Baya (artist)"), attempted to revive the prestigious Algerian past prior to French colonisation, at the same time that they have contributed to the preservation of the authentic values of Algeria. In this line, [Mohamed Temam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohamed_Temam "Mohamed Temam"), [Abdelkhader Houamel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdelkhader_Houamel "Abdelkhader Houamel") have also returned through this art, scenes from the history of the country, the habits and customs of the past and the country life. Other new artistic currents including the one of [M'hamed Issiakhem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%27hamed_Issiakhem "M'hamed Issiakhem"), [Mohammed Khadda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_Khadda "Mohammed Khadda") and [Bachir Yelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bachir_Yelles "Bachir Yelles"), appeared on the scene of Algerian painting, abandoning figurative classical painting to find new pictorial ways, to adapt Algerian paintings to the new realities of the country through its struggle and its aspirations. [Mohammed Khadda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_Khadda "Mohammed Khadda") [^305] and [M'hamed Issiakhem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%27hamed_Issiakhem "M'hamed Issiakhem") have been notable in recent years.[^305]
