**Chile**,[^5] officially the **Republic of Chile**,[^6] is a country in western [South America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_America "South America"). It is the southernmost country in the world and the closest to [Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica"), extending along a narrow strip of land between the [Andes Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andes "Andes") and the [Pacific Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Ocean "Pacific Ocean"). According to the 2024 census, Chile had an enumerated population of 18.5 million. The country covers a territorial area of 756,102 square kilometers (291,933 sq mi),[^16] [^9] sharing borders with [Peru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peru "Peru") to the north, [Bolivia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolivia "Bolivia") to the northeast, [Argentina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argentina "Argentina") to the east, and the [Drake Passage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drake_Passage "Drake Passage") to the south. It also administers several Pacific islands, including [Juan Fernández](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Islands "Juan Fernández Islands"), [Isla Salas y Gómez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isla_Salas_y_G%C3%B3mez "Isla Salas y Gómez"), [Desventuradas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desventuradas_Islands "Desventuradas Islands"), and [Easter Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Easter_Island "Easter Island"), and claims about 1,250,000 square kilometers (480,000 sq mi) of Antarctica as the [Chilean Antarctic Territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilean_Antarctic_Territory "Chilean Antarctic Territory").[^4] The capital and largest city is [Santiago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santiago "Santiago"), and the official and national language is [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language "Spanish language").

[Spain conquered and colonized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conquest_of_Chile "Conquest of Chile") the region in the mid-16th century, replacing [Inca rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incas_in_Central_Chile "Incas in Central Chile"); however, they [failed to conquer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arauco_War "Arauco War") the autonomous tribal [Mapuche people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapuche "Mapuche") who inhabited what is now south-central Chile. Chile emerged as a relatively stable authoritarian republic in the 1830s after its 1818 [declaration of independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilean_Declaration_of_Independence "Chilean Declaration of Independence") from [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain"). During the 19th century, Chile experienced significant economic and territorial growth, [putting an end to Mapuche resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupation_of_Araucan%C3%ADa "Occupation of Araucanía") in the 1880s and gaining its current northern territory in the [War of the Pacific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Pacific "War of the Pacific") (1879–83) by defeating Peru and Bolivia.

In the 20th century, until the 1970s, Chile underwent a process of [democratization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratization "Democratization") [^17] [^18] and experienced rapid population growth and [urbanization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urbanization "Urbanization"),[^19] while relying increasingly on exports from [copper mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper_mining_in_Chile "Copper mining in Chile") to support [its economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Chile "Economy of Chile").[^20] [^21] During the 1960s and 1970s, the country was marked by severe left-right [political polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_polarization "Political polarization") and turmoil, which culminated in the [11 September 1973 coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_Chilean_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1973 Chilean coup d'état") that overthrew [Salvador Allende](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvador_Allende "Salvador Allende") 's democratically elected [left-wing government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidency_of_Salvador_Allende "Presidency of Salvador Allende"), with support from the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"). This was followed by a [16-and-a-half-year right-wing military dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_dictatorship_of_Chile "Military dictatorship of Chile") led by [Augusto Pinochet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augusto_Pinochet "Augusto Pinochet"), during which the [1980 Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilean_Constitution_of_1980 "Chilean Constitution of 1980") was enacted,[^22] along with numerous political and economic reforms,[^23] and which was marked by widespread [human rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights "Human rights") violations, including more than [3,000 deaths and disappearances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights_abuses_in_Chile_under_Augusto_Pinochet "Human rights abuses in Chile under Augusto Pinochet").[^24] The regime ended in 1990, following a [referendum in 1988](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1988_Chilean_national_plebiscite "1988 Chilean national plebiscite"), and was succeeded by a [center-left coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concertaci%C3%B3n "Concertación"), which ruled until 2010.

Chile is a [high-income economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-income_economy "High-income economy") and is one of the most economically and socially stable nations in South America.[^25] Chile also performs well in the region in terms of [sustainability of the state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fragile_States_Index "Fragile States Index") and democratic development.[^26] Chile is a founding member of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), the [Community of Latin American and Caribbean States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_of_Latin_American_and_Caribbean_States "Community of Latin American and Caribbean States") (CELAC), and the [Pacific Alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Alliance "Pacific Alliance"), and joined the [OECD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OECD "OECD") in 2010.

## Etymology

There are various theories about the origin of the word *Chile*. According to 17th-century Spanish chronicler [Diego de Rosales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diego_de_Rosales "Diego de Rosales"),[^27] the [Incas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incas "Incas") called the valley of the [Aconcagua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aconcagua_River "Aconcagua River") *Chili* by corruption of the name of a [Picunche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picunche "Picunche") [tribal chief](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribal_chief "Tribal chief") (*[cacique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cacique "Cacique")*) called *Tili*, who ruled the area at the time of the Incan conquest in the 15th century.[^28] [^29] Another theory points to the similarity of the valley of the Aconcagua with that of the [Casma Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casma_Valley "Casma Valley") in Peru, where there was a town and valley named *Chili*.[^29]

Other theories say Chile may derive its name from a [Native American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas "Indigenous peoples of the Americas") word meaning either 'ends of the earth' or 'sea gulls';[^30] from the Mapuche word *chilli*, which may mean 'where the land ends'" [^31] or from the [Quechua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quechua_languages "Quechua languages") *chiri*,[^32] 'cold',[^33] or *tchili*, meaning either 'snow' [^33] [^34] or "the deepest point of the Earth".[^35] Another origin attributed to *chilli* is the onomatopoeic *cheele-cheele* —the Mapuche imitation of the warble of a bird locally known as [trile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yellow-winged_blackbird "Yellow-winged blackbird").[^31] [^36]

The Spanish [conquistadors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conquistadors "Conquistadors") heard about this name from the Incas, and the few survivors of [Diego de Almagro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diego_de_Almagro "Diego de Almagro") 's first Spanish expedition south from Peru in 1535–36 called themselves the "men of Chilli".[^31] Ultimately, Almagro is credited with the universalization of the name *Chile*, after naming the [Mapocho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapocho_River "Mapocho River") valley as such.[^29] The older spelling "Chili" was in use in English until the early 20th century before switching to "Chile".[^37]

## History

### Early history

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Momia_cultura_chinchorro_a%C3%B1o_3000_AC.jpg/250px-Momia_cultura_chinchorro_a%C3%B1o_3000_AC.jpg)

The Chinchorro mummies, the oldest of which are from around 5050 BCE

Stone tool evidence indicates humans sporadically frequented the [Monte Verde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monte_Verde "Monte Verde") valley area as long as 18,500 years ago. About 10,000 years ago, migrating [Indigenous peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas "Indigenous peoples of the Americas") settled in fertile valleys and coastal areas of what is present-day Chile. Settlement sites from very early human habitation include Monte Verde, [Cueva del Milodón](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cueva_del_Milod%C3%B3n "Cueva del Milodón") and the [Pali-Aike Crater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pali-Aike_Crater "Pali-Aike Crater") 's [lava tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lava_tube "Lava tube").[^38]

The Incas briefly extended their empire into what is now northern Chile, but the [Mapuche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapuche "Mapuche") (or Araucanians as they were known by the Spaniards) successfully resisted many attempts by the [Inca Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inca_Empire "Inca Empire") to subjugate them, despite their lack of state organization.[^39] They fought against the Sapa Inca Tupac Yupanqui and his army. The result of the bloody three-day confrontation known as the [Battle of the Maule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Maule "Battle of the Maule") was that the Inca conquest of the territories of Chile ended at the [Maule river](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maule_river "Maule river").[^40]

### Mapuche

The Mapuche are the largest Indigenous group in South America, with a population of nearly two million, primarily residing in the Central Valley of Chile, with a smaller population in Argentina. They were referred to by Spanish colonizers as Araucanians and historically they were considered one of the three main groups, the Picunche, the Mapuche, and the Huiliche, which today all identify as Mapuche. Before European contact, Mapuche society consisted mainly of scattered farming villages which were primarily led by a chief whose authority was limited to his own village. The Mapuche are notable for their long and determined resistance to foreign domination resisting both the Incan empire and Spanish colonial forces. During the 16th century, the Mapuche reorganized made alliances, and formed a resistance against the Spanish and later Chilean expansion. They maintained a level of autonomy until the late 19th century when Chile subdued their territory. Despite colonization and loss, the Mapuche have retained many aspects of their culture, identity, and tradition to this day.[^41]

### Spanish colonization

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Captaincy_General_of_Chile%2C_1775.svg/250px-Captaincy_General_of_Chile%2C_1775.svg.png)

Spain's Kingdom of Chile in 1775 according to Chilean historiography. The next year the Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata was created and the territories of the cities of Mendoza and San Juan got transferred to the new entity. 42 43 44 45

In 1520, while attempting to circumnavigate the globe, [Ferdinand Magellan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan "Ferdinand Magellan") discovered the southern passage now named after him (the [Strait of Magellan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan "Strait of Magellan")) thus becoming the first European to set foot on what is now Chile. The next Europeans to reach Chile were Diego de Almagro and his band of [Spanish conquistadors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conquistador "Conquistador"), who came from [Peru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peru "Peru") in 1535 seeking gold. The Spanish encountered various cultures that supported themselves principally through [slash-and-burn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slash-and-burn "Slash-and-burn") agriculture and hunting.[^40]

The conquest of Chile began in earnest in 1540 and was carried out by [Pedro de Valdivia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_de_Valdivia "Pedro de Valdivia"), one of [Francisco Pizarro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francisco_Pizarro "Francisco Pizarro") 's lieutenants, who founded the city of Santiago on 12 February 1541. Although the Spanish did not find the extensive gold and silver they sought, they recognized the agricultural potential of Chile's central valley, and Chile became part of the [Spanish Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Empire "Spanish Empire").[^40]

Conquest took place gradually, and the Europeans suffered repeated setbacks. A massive [Mapuche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapuche "Mapuche") insurrection that began in 1553 resulted in Valdivia's death and the destruction of many of the colony's principal settlements. Subsequent major insurrections took place in 1598 and in 1655. Each time the Mapuche and other native groups revolted, the southern border of the colony was driven northward. The abolition of [slavery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery "Slavery") by the Spanish crown in 1683 was done in recognition that enslaving the Mapuche intensified resistance rather than cowing them into submission. Despite royal prohibitions, relations remained strained from continual colonialist interference.[^46]

Cut off to the north by desert, to the south by the Mapuche, to the east by the Andes Mountains, and to the west by the ocean, Chile became one of the most centralized, homogeneous territories in Spanish America. Serving as a sort of frontier [garrison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garrison "Garrison"), the colony found itself with the mission of forestalling encroachment by both the Mapuche and Spain's European enemies, especially the [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_England "Kingdom of England") and the [Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_Republic "Dutch Republic"). [Buccaneers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buccaneer "Buccaneer") and [pirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piracy "Piracy") menaced the colony in addition to the Mapuche, as was shown by [Sir Francis Drake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_Drake "Francis Drake") 's 1578 raid on Valparaíso, the colony's principal port. Chile hosted one of the largest standing armies in the Americas, making it one of the most militarized of the Spanish possessions, as well as a [drain on the treasury of the Viceroyalty of Peru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_Situado "Real Situado").[^31]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Fundacion_de_Santiago.jpg/250px-Fundacion_de_Santiago.jpg)

Pedro Lira 's 1888 painting of the founding of Santiago by Pedro de Valdivia at Huelén Hill

The first general census was conducted by the government of [Agustín de Jáuregui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agust%C3%ADn_de_J%C3%A1uregui "Agustín de Jáuregui") between 1777 and 1778; it indicated that the population consisted of 259,646 inhabitants: 73.5% of [European descent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Latin_American "White Latin American"), 7.9% [mestizos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mestizo "Mestizo"), 8.6% [indigenous peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas "Indigenous peoples of the Americas") and 9.8% blacks. Francisco Hurtado, Governor of the province of [Chiloé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilo%C3%A9_Province "Chiloé Province"), conducted a census in 1784 and found the population consisted of 26,703 inhabitants, 64.4% of whom were whites and 33.5% of whom were natives. The Diocese of [Concepción](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concepci%C3%B3n,_Chile "Concepción, Chile") conducted a census in areas south of the [Maule river](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maule_river "Maule river") in 1812, but did not include the indigenous population or the inhabitants of the province of Chiloé. The population was estimated at 210,567, 86.1% of whom were [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Chilean "Spanish Chilean") or of European descent, 10% of whom were indigenous and 3.7% of whom were mestizos, blacks and [mulattos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mulatto "Mulatto").[^47]

A 2021 study by [Baten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%B6rg_Baten "Jörg Baten") and Llorca-Jaña shows that regions with a relatively high share of North European migrants developed faster in terms of [numeracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numeracy "Numeracy"), even if the overall number of migrants was small. This effect might be related to [externalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Externality "Externality"): the surrounding population adopted a similar behavior as the small non-European immigrant group, and new schools were created. Ironically, there might have been positive [spillover effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spillover_effects "Spillover effects") from the educational investment made by migrants, at the same time numeracy might have been reduced by the greater inequality in these regions. However, the positive effects of immigration were apparently stronger.[^48]

### Independence and nation building

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Ejercito_Libertador_cruce_de_Cordillera_de_los_andes.jpg/250px-Ejercito_Libertador_cruce_de_Cordillera_de_los_andes.jpg)

Generals José de San Martín (left) and Bernardo O'Higgins (right) during the crossing of the Andes

In 1808, [Napoleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_I "Napoleon I") 's enthronement of his brother [Joseph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Bonaparte "Joseph Bonaparte") as the [Spanish King](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_monarchy "Spanish monarchy") precipitated the drive by Chile for [independence from Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilean_War_of_Independence "Chilean War of Independence"). A national [junta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Junta_\(governing_body\) "Junta (governing body)") in the name of [Ferdinand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_VII_of_Spain "Ferdinand VII of Spain") – heir to the deposed king – was formed on 18 September 1810. The [Government Junta of Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_Junta_of_Chile_\(1810\) "Government Junta of Chile (1810)") proclaimed an autonomous government for Chile within the Spanish monarchy (in memory of this day, Chile celebrates its [National Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiestas_Patrias_\(Chile\) "Fiestas Patrias (Chile)") on 18 September each year).

After these events, a movement for total independence, under the command of [José Miguel Carrera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Miguel_Carrera "José Miguel Carrera") (one of the most renowned patriots) and his two brothers Juan José and [Luis Carrera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luis_Carrera "Luis Carrera"), soon gained a wider following. Spanish attempts to re-impose arbitrary rule during what was called the [Reconquista](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reconquista_\(Spanish_America\) "Reconquista (Spanish America)") led to a prolonged struggle, including infighting from [Bernardo O'Higgins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernardo_O%27Higgins "Bernardo O'Higgins"), who challenged Carrera's leadership.

Intermittent warfare continued until 1817. With Carrera in prison in Argentina, O'Higgins and anti-Carrera cohort [José de San Martín](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn "José de San Martín"), hero of the [Argentine War of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argentine_War_of_Independence "Argentine War of Independence"), led [an army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Army_of_the_Andes "Army of the Andes") that [crossed the Andes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crossing_of_the_Andes "Crossing of the Andes") into Chile and defeated the royalists. On 12 February 1818, [Chile was proclaimed an independent republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilean_Declaration_of_Independence "Chilean Declaration of Independence"). The political revolt brought little social change, however, and 19th-century Chilean society preserved the essence of the stratified colonial social structure, which was greatly influenced by family politics and the [Roman Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church"). A strong presidency eventually emerged, but wealthy landowners remained powerful.[^40] Bernardo O'Higgins once planned to expand Chile by liberating the [Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines "Philippines") from Spain and incorporating the islands. In this regard he tasked the Scottish naval officer, Lord Thomas Cochrane, in a letter dated 12 November 1821, expressing his plan to conquer [Guayaquil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guayaquil "Guayaquil"), the [Galapagos Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galapagos_Islands "Galapagos Islands"), and the [Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile%E2%80%93Philippines_relations "Chile–Philippines relations"). There were preparations, but the plan did not push through because O'Higgins was exiled.[^49]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Combate_Naval_Iquique-Thomas_Somerscales.jpg/250px-Combate_Naval_Iquique-Thomas_Somerscales.jpg)

The Battle of Iquique on 21 May 1879. The victory of Chile in the War of the Pacific allowed its expansion into new territories.

Chile slowly started to expand its influence and to establish its borders. By the Tantauco Treaty, the archipelago of [Chiloé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilo%C3%A9 "Chiloé") was incorporated in 1826. The economy began to boom due to the discovery of silver ore in Chañarcillo, and the growing trade of the port of Valparaíso, which led to conflict with Peru over maritime supremacy in the Pacific. At the same time, attempts were made to strengthen sovereignty in southern Chile intensifying [penetration into Araucanía](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupation_of_Araucan%C3%ADa "Occupation of Araucanía") and [colonizing Llanquihue with German immigrants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_colonization_of_Valdivia,_Osorno_and_Llanquihue "German colonization of Valdivia, Osorno and Llanquihue") in 1848. Through the founding of [Fort Bulnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fort_Bulnes "Fort Bulnes") by the [Schooner Ancud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schooner_Ancud "Schooner Ancud") under the command of [John Williams Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Williams_Wilson "John Williams Wilson"), the [Magallanes Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magallanes_Region "Magallanes Region") [started to be controlled by country in 1843](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilean_colonization_of_the_Strait_of_Magellan "Chilean colonization of the Strait of Magellan"), while the [Antofagasta Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antofagasta_Region "Antofagasta Region"), at the time in [dispute with Bolivia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Desert_border_dispute "Atacama Desert border dispute"), began to fill with people.

After the [Chilean Civil War of 1829–1830](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilean_Civil_War_of_1829%E2%80%931830 "Chilean Civil War of 1829–1830") in which the conservatives won, under the [Joaquín Prieto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joaqu%C3%ADn_Prieto "Joaquín Prieto") Administration, the [Chilean Constitution of 1833](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilean_Constitution_of_1833 "Chilean Constitution of 1833") was written and put into effect with high influence from the triple minister [Diego Portales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diego_Portales "Diego Portales"). Two other civil wars happened in Chile in the 1850s, one in [1851](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1851_Chilean_revolution "1851 Chilean revolution") and the other one in [1859](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1859_Chilean_revolution "1859 Chilean revolution").

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Territorial_losses_of_the_Republic_of_Chile_de_jure.svg/330px-Territorial_losses_of_the_Republic_of_Chile_de_jure.svg.png)

Territorial losses of the Republic of Chile de jure (by law) according to Chilean historiography 50

Toward the end of the 19th century, the government in Santiago consolidated its position in the south by the [Occupation of Araucanía](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupation_of_Araucan%C3%ADa "Occupation of Araucanía"). The [Boundary treaty of 1881 between Chile and Argentina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_treaty_of_1881_between_Chile_and_Argentina "Boundary treaty of 1881 between Chile and Argentina") confirmed Chilean sovereignty over the [Strait of Magellan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan "Strait of Magellan") but also made the country renounce its claims in the rest of East Patagonia after a [dispute that started in 1842](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Patagonia,_Tierra_del_Fuego_and_Strait_of_Magellan_Dispute "East Patagonia, Tierra del Fuego and Strait of Magellan Dispute").[^51] As a result of the [War of the Pacific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Pacific "War of the Pacific") with Peru and Bolivia (1879–83), Chile expanded its territory northward by almost one-third, eliminating Bolivia's access to the Pacific, and acquired valuable [nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caliche_\(mineral\)#Chilean_caliche "Caliche (mineral)") deposits, the exploitation of which led to an era of national affluence. Chile had joined the stand as one of the high-income countries in South America by 1870.[^52]

On 9 September 1888, Chile took possession of [Easter Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Easter_Island "Easter Island") by the signing of a mutual will agreement with the local king, thanks to the efforts of the Bishop of Tahiti, Monsignor José María Verdier since the island was constantly attacked by slave merchants. The naval officer [Policarpo Toro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Policarpo_Toro "Policarpo Toro") represented the Chilean Government and [Atamu Tekena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atamu_Tekena "Atamu Tekena") was the head of the Council of Rapanui. The Rapa Nui elders ceded sovereignty, without renouncing their titles as chiefs, the ownership of their lands, the validity of their culture and traditions on equal terms. The Rapa Nui sold nothing and were integrated in equal conditions into Chile.[^53]

The [1891 Chilean Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1891_Chilean_Civil_War "1891 Chilean Civil War") brought about a redistribution of power between the President and Congress, and Chile established a [parliamentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system") style democracy. However, the Civil War had also been a contest between those who favored the development of local industries and powerful Chilean banking interests, particularly the House of Edwards which had strong ties to foreign investors. Soon after, the country engaged in a [vastly expensive naval arms race](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argentine%E2%80%93Chilean_naval_arms_race "Argentine–Chilean naval arms race") with [Argentina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argentina "Argentina") amid escalating geopolitical competition and the [Puna de Atacama dispute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puna_de_Atacama_dispute "Puna de Atacama dispute").

The [War of the Pacific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Pacific "War of the Pacific") (1879–1883) against Peru and Bolivia resulted in Chile annexing resource-rich territory from both countries and further consolidating its status as a regional power. It subsequently emerged as a leading naval power in the Americas, even sending a ship to protest United States involvement in the [Panama crisis of 1885](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panama_crisis_of_1885 "Panama crisis of 1885"). Chile potentially threatened U.S. hegemony in the Western Hemisphere, with the two countries almost going to war during the [Baltimore crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltimore_crisis "Baltimore crisis") in 1891.[^54]

### 20th century

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Chilean_battleship_Almirante_Latorre.jpg/250px-Chilean_battleship_Almirante_Latorre.jpg)

Chile's Almirante Latorre dreadnought in 1921

The early 20th century saw Chile fully consolidate its territory and resolve long-running diplomatic and territorial disputes. Its current borders with Argentina were finalized [through British arbitration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1902_Arbitral_award_of_the_Andes_between_Argentina_and_Chile "1902 Arbitral award of the Andes between Argentina and Chile") in 1902 and a bilateral settlement of the [Puna de Atacama dispute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puna_de_Atacama_dispute "Puna de Atacama dispute") the following year. In 1904, Chile and Bolivia signed a [Treaty of Peace and Friendship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Peace_and_Friendship_\(1904\) "Treaty of Peace and Friendship (1904)") which clarified the border between both countries.

The Chilean economy partially degenerated into a system protecting the interests of a ruling [oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy "Oligarchy"). By the 1920s, the emerging middle and working classes were powerful enough to elect a reformist president, [Arturo Alessandri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arturo_Alessandri "Arturo Alessandri"), whose program was frustrated by a conservative congress. In the 1920s, [Marxist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism "Marxism") groups with strong popular support arose.[^40]

A military coup led by General [Luis Altamirano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luis_Altamirano "Luis Altamirano") in 1924 set off a period of political instability that lasted until 1932. Of the ten governments that held power in that period, the longest lasting was that of General [Carlos Ibáñez del Campo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carlos_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_del_Campo "Carlos Ibáñez del Campo"), who briefly ruled as [de facto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_facto "De facto") dictator in 1925 and then again between 1927 and 1931. These authoritarian governments were comparatively less harsh and corrupt than counterparts elsewhere in Latin America.[^55] [^56]

By relinquishing power to a democratically elected successor, Ibáñez del Campo retained the respect of a large enough segment of the population to remain a viable politician for more than thirty years, in spite of the vague and shifting nature of his ideology. When constitutional rule was restored in 1932, a strong middle-class party, the Radicals, emerged. It became the key force in coalition governments for the next 20 years. During the period of [Radical Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_Party_\(Chile\) "Radical Party (Chile)") dominance (1932–52), the state increased its role in the economy. In 1952, voters returned Ibáñez del Campo to office for another six years. [Jorge Alessandri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jorge_Alessandri "Jorge Alessandri") succeeded Ibáñez del Campo in 1958, bringing Chilean conservatism back into power democratically for another term.

The [1964 presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1964_Chilean_presidential_election "1964 Chilean presidential election") of [Christian Democrat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Democrat_Party_of_Chile "Christian Democrat Party of Chile") [Eduardo Frei Montalva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduardo_Frei_Montalva "Eduardo Frei Montalva") by an [absolute majority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majority "Majority") initiated a period of major reform. Under the slogan "Revolution in Liberty", the Frei administration embarked on far-reaching social and economic programs, particularly in education, housing, and [agrarian reform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agrarian_reform "Agrarian reform"), including rural unionization of agricultural workers. By 1967, however, Frei encountered increasing opposition from leftists, who charged that his reforms were inadequate, and from conservatives, who found them excessive. At the end of his term, Frei had not fully achieved his party's ambitious goals.[^40]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Salvador_Allende_Gossens-.jpg/250px-Salvador_Allende_Gossens-.jpg)

Salvador Allende

In the 1970 election, Senator [Salvador Allende](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvador_Allende "Salvador Allende") of the [Socialist Party of Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_Chile "Socialist Party of Chile") (then part of the " [Popular Unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_Unity_\(Chile\) "Popular Unity (Chile)") " coalition which included the Communists, Radicals, Social-Democrats, dissident Christian Democrats, the Popular Unitary Action Movement, and the Independent Popular Action),[^40] achieved a partial majority in a [plurality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plurality_\(voting\) "Plurality (voting)") of votes in a three-way contest, followed by candidates Radomiro Tomic for the Christian Democrat Party and Jorge Alessandri for the Conservative Party. Allende was not elected with an absolute majority, receiving fewer than 35% of the votes.

The [Chilean Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Congress_of_Chile "National Congress of Chile") conducted a runoff vote between the leading candidates, Allende and former president Jorge Alessandri, and, keeping with tradition, chose Allende by a vote of 153 to 35. Frei refused to form an alliance with Alessandri to oppose Allende, on the grounds that the Christian Democrats were a workers' party and could not make common cause with the right wing.[^57] [^58]

An [economic depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_depression "Economic depression") that began in 1972 was exacerbated by [capital flight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_flight "Capital flight"), plummeting private investment, and withdrawal of bank deposits in response to Allende's socialist program. Production fell and unemployment rose. Allende adopted measures including price freezes, wage increases, and tax reforms, to increase consumer spending and redistribute income downward.[^59] Joint public-private [public works](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_works "Public works") projects helped reduce unemployment.[^60] Much of the banking sector was [nationalized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalization "Nationalization"). Many enterprises within the [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper#Production "Copper"), coal, iron, [nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caliche_\(mineral\)#Chilean_caliche "Caliche (mineral)"), and steel industries were [expropriated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalization#Expropriation "Nationalization"), nationalized, or subjected to state intervention. Industrial output increased sharply and unemployment fell during the Allende administration's first year.[^60]

Allende's program included advancement of workers' interests,[^60] [^61] replacing the judicial system with "socialist legality",[^62] nationalization of banks and forcing others to bankruptcy,[^62] and strengthening "popular militias" known as MIR.[^62] Started under former President Frei, the Popular Unity platform also called for nationalization of Chile's major copper mines in the form of a constitutional amendment. The measure was passed unanimously by Congress. As a result,[^63] the [Richard Nixon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Nixon "Richard Nixon") administration [organized and inserted secret operatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_intervention_in_Chile "United States intervention in Chile") in Chile, in order to swiftly destabilize Allende's government.[^64] In addition, US financial pressure restricted international economic credit to Chile.[^65]

The economic problems were also exacerbated by Allende's public spending, financed mostly through printing money, and by poor credit ratings given by commercial banks.[^66] Simultaneously, opposition media, politicians, business guilds and other organizations helped to accelerate a campaign of domestic political and economical destabilization, some of which was backed by the United States.[^65] [^67] By early 1973, inflation was out of control. On 26 May 1973, Chile's Supreme Court, which was opposed to Allende's government, unanimously denounced Allende's *disruption of the legality of the nation*. Although illegal under the Chilean constitution, the court supported and strengthened Pinochet's soon-to-be seizure of power.[^62] [^68]

#### Pinochet era (1973–1990)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Golpe_de_Estado_1973.jpg/250px-Golpe_de_Estado_1973.jpg)

Fighter jets bombing the Presidential Palace of La Moneda during the Chilean coup of 1973

On 11 September 1973, a [military coup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilean_coup_of_1973 "Chilean coup of 1973") overthrew Allende, who apparently committed suicide as the armed forces bombarded the [presidential palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_Moneda_Palace "La Moneda Palace").[^69] [^70] The degree to which [the United States was involved in the coup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._intervention_in_Chile#1973_coup "U.S. intervention in Chile") remains debated; after Allende was overthrown, U.S. Secretary of State [Henry Kissinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Kissinger "Henry Kissinger") told [U.S. president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_the_United_States "President of the United States") [Richard Nixon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Nixon "Richard Nixon") that the United States had "helped" the coup indirectly.[^71] Historian Sebastián Hurtado contends there is no documentary evidence to support that the U.S. government was actively involved in the coordination and execution the coup; however, upon Allende's election in 1970, Kissinger had stated "I don't see why we need to stand by and watch a country go Communist due to the irresponsibility of its own people",[^72] [^73] while Nixon had expressed that the Allende government should not be consolidated and acted decisively to destabilize his government.[^73] [^74]

A military junta, led by General [Augusto Pinochet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augusto_Pinochet "Augusto Pinochet"), took control of the country. His regime was marked by widespread [human rights violations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights_violations_in_Pinochet%27s_Chile "Human rights violations in Pinochet's Chile"). Chile initiated and actively participated in [Operation Condor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Condor "Operation Condor"), a U.S.-backed campaign to suppress leftists and their sympathizers.[^75] In October 1973, at least 72 people were murdered by the [Caravan of Death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caravan_of_Death "Caravan of Death").[^76] According to the [Rettig Report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rettig_Report "Rettig Report") and [Valech Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valech_Commission "Valech Commission"), during the Pinochet regime's 15-year rule, at least 2,115 were killed,[^77] and at least 27,265 [^78] were tortured (including 88 children younger than 12 years old);[^78] many were detained, tortured, and executed at the national stadium. In 2011, Chile recognized an additional 9,800 victims, bringing the total number killed, tortured or imprisoned for political reasons to 40,018.[^79] Among the victims was internationally known poet-singer [Víctor Jara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V%C3%ADctor_Jara "Víctor Jara").

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Augusto_Pinochet_foto_oficial_coloreada.jpg/250px-Augusto_Pinochet_foto_oficial_coloreada.jpg)

Augusto Pinochet

A new Constitution was approved by a controversial [plebiscite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plebiscite "Plebiscite") on 11 September 1980, and General Pinochet became president of the republic for an eight-year term. After Pinochet obtained rule of the country, several hundred committed Chilean revolutionaries joined the [Sandinista](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandinista "Sandinista") army in [Nicaragua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicaragua "Nicaragua"), guerrilla forces in [Argentina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argentina "Argentina") or training camps in [Cuba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuba "Cuba"), Eastern Europe and Northern Africa.[^80]

In the late 1980s, largely as a result of events such as the 1982 economic collapse [^81] and mass [civil resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_resistance "Civil resistance") in 1983–1988, the government gradually permitted greater freedom of assembly, [speech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_speech "Freedom of speech"), and association, to include trade union and political activity.[^82] The government launched market-oriented reforms with [Hernán Büchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_B%C3%BCchi "Hernán Büchi") as Minister of Finance. Chile moved toward a [free market economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_market_economy "Free market economy") that saw an increase in domestic and foreign private investment, although the [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper#Production "Copper") industry and other important mineral resources were not opened to competition. In a [plebiscite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1988_Chilean_national_plebiscite "1988 Chilean national plebiscite") on 5 October 1988, Pinochet was denied a second eight-year term as president (56% against 44%). Chileans elected a new president and the majority of members of a [bicameral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicameralism "Bicameralism") congress on 14 December 1989. Christian Democrat [Patricio Aylwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patricio_Aylwin "Patricio Aylwin"), the candidate of a coalition of 17 political parties called the *[Concertación](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concertaci%C3%B3n "Concertación")*, received an absolute majority of votes (55%).[^83] President Aylwin served from 1990 to 1994, in what was considered a transition period.

### 21st century

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Izamiento_de_la_Gran_Bandera_Nacional_-_Presidentes_de_Chile.jpg/250px-Izamiento_de_la_Gran_Bandera_Nacional_-_Presidentes_de_Chile.jpg)

The first five presidents of Chile since its Transition to democracy (1990–2022), celebrating the Bicentennial of Chile

In December 1993, Christian Democrat [Eduardo Frei Ruiz-Tagle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduardo_Frei_Ruiz-Tagle "Eduardo Frei Ruiz-Tagle"), the son of previous president Eduardo Frei Montalva, led the Concertación coalition to victory with an absolute majority of votes (58%).[^84] Frei Ruiz-Tagle was succeeded in 2000 by Socialist [Ricardo Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricardo_Lagos "Ricardo Lagos"), who won the presidency in an unprecedented [runoff election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Chilean_presidential_election "1999–2000 Chilean presidential election") against [Joaquín Lavín](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joaqu%C3%ADn_Lav%C3%ADn "Joaquín Lavín") of the rightist [Alliance for Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alliance_for_Chile "Alliance for Chile").[^85] In January 2006, Chileans elected their first female president, [Michelle Bachelet Jeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michelle_Bachelet_Jeria "Michelle Bachelet Jeria"), of the Socialist Party, defeating [Sebastián Piñera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_Pi%C3%B1era "Sebastián Piñera"), of the [National Renewal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Renewal_\(Chile\) "National Renewal (Chile)") party, extending the *Concertación* governance for another four years.[^86] [^87] In January 2010, Chileans [elected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_Chilean_presidential_election "2009–10 Chilean presidential election") [Sebastián Piñera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_Pi%C3%B1era "Sebastián Piñera") as the first rightist President in 20 years, defeating former President [Eduardo Frei Ruiz-Tagle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduardo_Frei_Ruiz-Tagle "Eduardo Frei Ruiz-Tagle") of the *Concertación*, for a four-year term succeeding Bachelet. Due to term limits, [Sebastián Piñera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_Pi%C3%B1era "Sebastián Piñera") did not stand for re-election in 2013, and his term expired in March 2014 resulting in [Michelle Bachelet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michelle_Bachelet "Michelle Bachelet") returning to office.[^88] Sebastián Piñera succeeded Bachelet again in 2018 as the [President of Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Chile "President of Chile") after winning the December 2017 presidential [election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2017_Chilean_general_election "2017 Chilean general election").[^89] [^90]

On 27 February 2010, Chile was struck by an 8.8 [M <sub>w</sub>](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seismic_magnitude_scales#Mw "Seismic magnitude scales") [earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Chile_earthquake "2010 Chile earthquake"), the fifth largest ever recorded at the time. More than 500 people died (most from the ensuing [tsunami](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsunami "Tsunami")) and over a million people lost their homes. The earthquake was also followed by multiple aftershocks.[^91] Initial damage estimates were in the range of US$15–30 billion, around 10% to 15% of Chile's real gross domestic product.[^92]

Chile achieved global recognition for the successful [rescue of 33 trapped miners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Copiap%C3%B3_mining_accident "2010 Copiapó mining accident") in 2010. On 5 August 2010, the access tunnel collapsed at the San José copper and gold mine in the [Atacama Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Desert "Atacama Desert") near [Copiapó](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copiap%C3%B3 "Copiapó") in northern Chile, trapping 33 men 700 meters (2,300 ft) below ground. A rescue effort organized by the Chilean government located the miners 17 days later. All 33 men were brought to the surface two months later on 13 October 2010 over a period of almost 24 hours, an effort that was carried on live television around the world.[^93]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Marcha_Mas_Grande_De_Chile_2019_Plaza_Baquedano_Drone.jpg/250px-Marcha_Mas_Grande_De_Chile_2019_Plaza_Baquedano_Drone.jpg)

View of the 2019–2022 Chilean protests towards Plaza Baquedano, Santiago

From [2019 to 2022](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019%E2%80%9320_Chilean_protests "2019–20 Chilean protests"), Chile endured a series of nationwide protests in response to a rise in the [Santiago Metro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santiago_Metro "Santiago Metro") 's subway fare, the increased [cost of living](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost_of_living "Cost of living"), [privatization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privatization "Privatization"), and [inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_inequality "Economic inequality").[^94] On 15 November, most of the political parties represented in the National Congress signed an agreement to call a [national referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Chilean_national_referendum "2020 Chilean national referendum") in April 2020 regarding the creation of a new Constitution, later postponed to October due to the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_Chile "COVID-19 pandemic in Chile").[^95] On 25 October 2020, Chileans voted 78.28 percent in favor of a new constitution, while 21.72 percent rejected the change; voter turnout was 51 percent. An [election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Chilean_Constitutional_Convention_election "2021 Chilean Constitutional Convention election") for the members of the [Constitutional Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_Convention_\(Chile\) "Constitutional Convention (Chile)") was held in Chile between 15 and 16 May 2021;[^96] the results saw a complete rearrangement of the political system established since the end of [Pinochet's dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_dictatorship_of_Chile_\(1973%E2%80%931990\) "Military dictatorship of Chile (1973–1990)") in 1990, with various independent and leftist candidates performing strongly relative to conventional center-right and center-left parties.

On 19 December 2021, a leftist candidate, the 35-year-old former student protest leader [Gabriel Boric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabriel_Boric "Gabriel Boric"), won Chile's presidential [election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Chilean_general_election "2021 Chilean general election") to become the country's youngest leader.[^97] On 11 March 2022, Boric was sworn in as president to succeed outgoing President Sebastian Piñera.[^98] The majority of Boric's Cabinet—14 out of 24—were women, which was the first in the Western Hemisphere.[^99]

On 4 September 2022, voters rejected the new constitution proposal in the [constitutional referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Chilean_constitutional_referendum "2022 Chilean constitutional referendum"), which was put forward by the left-leaning [Constitutional Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_Convention_\(Chile\) "Constitutional Convention (Chile)").[^100] On 17 December 2023, voters rejected a second new constitution proposal in a new [constitutional referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Chilean_constitutional_referendum "2023 Chilean constitutional referendum"), written by the conservative-led [Constitutional Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_Council_\(Chile\) "Constitutional Council (Chile)").[^101] [^102] [^103]

On 14 December 2025, conservative candidate [José Antonio Kast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Antonio_Kast "José Antonio Kast") won Chile's presidential [election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Chilean_general_election "2025 Chilean general election") decisively with more than 58% of the vote. This result was widely described as marking a shift to the right in Chilean politics. In Kast's first speech as the president elect he stated "Chile will once again be free from crime, free from anguish, free from fear".[^104] [^105] On 11 March 2026, José Antonio Kast was sworn in as Chile’s president, meaning the most significant rightward shift in Chile since the return of democracy in 1990.[^106]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Natural_Regions_of_Chile.svg/250px-Natural_Regions_of_Chile.svg.png)

A long and narrow coastal [Southern Cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Cone "Southern Cone") country on the west side of the [Andes Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andes_Mountains "Andes Mountains"), Chile stretches over 4,300 km (2,670 mi) north to south, but only 350 km (217 mi) at its widest point east to west [^107] and 64 km (40 mi) at its narrowest point east to west, with an average width of 175 km (109 mi). This encompasses a large variety of climates and landscapes. It contains 756,950 square kilometers (292,260 sq mi) of land area. It is situated within the [Pacific Ring of Fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Ring_of_Fire "Pacific Ring of Fire"). Excluding its Pacific islands and Antarctic claim, Chile lies between latitudes [17°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/17th_parallel_south "17th parallel south") and [56°S](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/56th_parallel_south "56th parallel south"), and longitudes [66°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/66th_meridian_west "66th meridian west") and [75°W](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/75th_meridian_west "75th meridian west").

Chile is among the longest north–south countries in the world. If one considers only mainland territory, Chile is unique within this group in its narrowness from east to west, with the other long north–south countries (including Brazil, Russia, Canada, and the United States, among others) all being wider from east to west by a factor of more than 10. Chile also claims 1,250,000 km <sup>2</sup> (480,000 sq mi) of [Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica") as part of its territory ([Chilean Antarctic Territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilean_Antarctic_Territory "Chilean Antarctic Territory")). However, this latter claim is suspended under the terms of the [Antarctic Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctic_Treaty "Antarctic Treaty"), of which Chile is a signatory.[^108] It is the world's southernmost country that is geographically on the mainland.[^109]

Chile controls [Easter Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Easter_Island "Easter Island") and [Sala y Gómez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sala_y_G%C3%B3mez "Sala y Gómez") Island, the easternmost islands of Polynesia, which it incorporated to its territory in 1888, and the [Juan Fernández Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Islands "Juan Fernández Islands"), more than 600 km (370 mi) from the mainland. Also controlled but only temporarily inhabited (by some local fishermen) are the small islands of [San Ambrosio and San Felix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desventuradas_Islands "Desventuradas Islands"). These islands are notable because they extend Chile's claim to territorial waters out from its coast into the Pacific Ocean.[^110]

The northern [Atacama Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Desert "Atacama Desert") contains great mineral wealth, primarily [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper#Production "Copper") and [nitrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caliche_\(mineral\)#Chilean_caliche "Caliche (mineral)"). The relatively small Central Valley, which includes Santiago, dominates the country in terms of population and agricultural resources. This area is also the historical center from which Chile expanded in the late 19th century when it integrated the northern and southern regions. Southern Chile is rich in forests, grazing lands, and features a string of volcanoes and lakes. The southern coast is a labyrinth of fjords, inlets, canals, twisting peninsulas, and islands. The Andes Mountains are located on the eastern border.

### Topography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Chile_topo_en.jpg/250px-Chile_topo_en.jpg)

Topographic map of Chile

Chile is located along a highly [seismic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seismic "Seismic") and [volcanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanic "Volcanic") zone, part of the Pacific Ring of Fire, due to the subduction of the [Nazca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazca_Plate "Nazca Plate") and Antarctic plates in the [South American Plate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_American_Plate "South American Plate"). In the late [Paleozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleozoic "Paleozoic"), 251 million years ago, Chile belonged to the continental block called [Gondwana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gondwana "Gondwana"). It was just a depression that accumulated marine sediments began to rise at the end of the Mesozoic, 66 million years ago, due to the collision between the Nazca Plate and South American Plate, resulting in the Andes. The territory would be shaped over millions of years by the folding of the rocks, forming the current relief.

The Chilean relief consists of the central depression, which crosses the country longitudinally, flanked by two mountain ranges that make up about 80% of the territory: the Andes Mountains to the east-natural border with [Bolivia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolivia "Bolivia") and [Argentina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argentina "Argentina") in the [region of Atacama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Region "Atacama Region") and the [Coastal Range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilean_Coast_Range "Chilean Coast Range") west-minor height from the Andes. Chile's highest peak is the [Nevado Ojos del Salado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nevado_Ojos_del_Salado "Nevado Ojos del Salado"), at 6891.3 m, which is also the highest volcano in the world. The highest point of the Coastal Range is Vicuña Mackenna, at 3114 meters, located in the Sierra Vicuña Mackenna, the south of [Antofagasta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antofagasta_Region "Antofagasta Region"). Among the coastal mountains and the Pacific is a series of coastal plains, of variable length, which allow the settlement of coastal towns and big ports. Some areas of the plains territories encompass territory east of the Andes, and the Patagonian steppes and Magellan, or are high plateaus surrounded by high mountain ranges, such as the Altiplano or Puna de Atacama.

The [Far North](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far_North,_Chile "Far North, Chile") is the area between the northern boundary of the country and the [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_\(geometry\) "Parallel (geometry)") 26° S, covering the first three regions. It is characterized by the presence of the [Atacama Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Desert "Atacama Desert"), the most arid in the world. The desert is fragmented by streams that originate in the area known as the [pampas Tamarugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pampa_del_Tamarugal "Pampa del Tamarugal"). The Andes, split in two and whose eastern arm runs through [Bolivia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolivia "Bolivia"), has a high altitude and volcanic activity, which has allowed the formation of the Andean altiplano and salt structures as the [Salar de Atacama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salar_de_Atacama "Salar de Atacama"), due to the gradual accumulation of sediments over time.

To the south is the [Norte Chico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norte_Chico,_Chile "Norte Chico, Chile"), extending to the [Aconcagua River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aconcagua_River "Aconcagua River"). Los Andes begin to decrease its altitude to the south and closer to the coast, reaching 90 km away at the height of [Illapel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illapel "Illapel"), the narrowest part of the Chilean territory. The two mountain ranges intersect, virtually eliminating the intermediate depression. The existence of rivers flowing through the territory allows the formation of [transverse valleys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transverse_Valleys "Transverse Valleys"), where agriculture has developed strongly in recent times, while the [coastal plains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastal_plains "Coastal plains") begin to expand.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Amalia_Glacier_%2847461801572%29.jpg/250px-Amalia_Glacier_%2847461801572%29.jpg)

Amalia Glacier, located in Bernardo O'Higgins National Park

The [Central](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zona_Central,_Chile "Zona Central, Chile") area is the most populated region of the country. The coastal plains are wide and allow the establishment of cities and ports along the Pacific. The Andes maintain altitudes above 6000m but descend slowly in height to 4000 meters on average. The intermediate depression reappears becoming a fertile valley that allows agricultural development and human settlement, due to sediment accumulation. To the south, the Cordillera de la Costa reappears in the [Cordillera de Nahuelbuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cordillera_de_Nahuelbuta "Cordillera de Nahuelbuta") while glacial sediments create a series of lakes in the area of [La Frontera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_Frontera,_Chile "La Frontera, Chile").

Patagonia extends from within Reloncavi, at the height of parallel 41°S, to the south. During the [last glaciation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Llanquihue_glaciation "Llanquihue glaciation"), this area was covered by ice that strongly eroded Chilean relief structures. As a result, the intermediate depression sinks in the sea, while the coastal mountains rise to a series of archipelagos, such as [Chiloé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilo%C3%A9_Island "Chiloé Island") and the [Chonos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chonos_Archipelago "Chonos Archipelago"), disappearing in Taitao peninsula, in the parallel 47°S. The Andes mountain range loses height and erosion caused by the action of glaciers has caused [fjords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fjords "Fjords"). East of the Andes, on the continent, or north of it, on the [island of Tierra del Fuego](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego_\(main_island\) "Tierra del Fuego (main island)") are located relatively flat plains, which in the Strait of Magellan cover large areas. The Andes, as he had done previously Cordillera de la Costa, begins to break in the ocean causing a myriad of islands and islets and disappear into it, sinking and reappearing in the Southern Antilles arc and then the Antarctic Peninsula, where it is called Antartandes, in the Chilean Antarctic Territory, lying between the meridians 53°W and 90°W.

In the middle of the Pacific, the country has sovereignty over several islands of volcanic origin, collectively known as Insular Chile. The archipelago of Juan Fernandez and Easter Island is located in the fracture zone between the Nazca plate and the Pacific plate known as East Pacific Rise.

### Climate and hydrography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/K%C3%B6ppen%E2%80%93Geiger_climate_types_in_Chile.png/250px-K%C3%B6ppen%E2%80%93Geiger_climate_types_in_Chile.png)

Chile map of Köppen climate classification

The diverse [climate of Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_of_Chile "Climate of Chile") ranges from the world's driest desert in the north—the [Atacama Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Desert "Atacama Desert") —through a [Mediterranean climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_climate "Mediterranean climate") in the center, [tropical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_rainforest_climate "Tropical rainforest climate") in Easter Island,[^111] to an [oceanic climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceanic_climate "Oceanic climate"), including [alpine tundra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpine_tundra "Alpine tundra") and [glaciers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glaciers "Glaciers") in the east and south.[^24] According to the [Köppen system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification "Köppen climate classification"), Chile within its borders hosts at least eighteen major climatic subtypes.[^112] There are four seasons in most of the country: summer (December to February), autumn (March to May), winter (June to August), and spring (September to November).

Due to the characteristics of the territory, Chile is crossed by numerous rivers generally short in length and with low flow rates. They commonly extend from the [Andes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andes_Mountains "Andes Mountains") to the Pacific Ocean, flowing from East to West. Because of the [Atacama desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_desert "Atacama desert"), in the Norte Grande there are only short [endorheic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endorheic "Endorheic") character streams, except for the [Loa River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loa_River "Loa River"), the longest in the country 440 km.[^113] In the high valleys, wetland areas generate [Chungará Lake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chungar%C3%A1_Lake "Chungará Lake"), located at 4500 meters above sea level. It and the [Lauca River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lauca_River "Lauca River") are shared with [Bolivia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolivia "Bolivia"), as well as the [Lluta River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lluta_River "Lluta River"). In the center-north of the country, the number of rivers that form valleys of agricultural importance increases. Noteworthy are the Elqui with 75 km [^113] long, 142 km Aconcagua, Maipo with 250 km [^113] and its tributary, the Mapocho with 110 km, and Maule with 240 km. Their waters mainly flow from Andean snowmelt in the summer and winter rains. The major lakes in this area are the artificial lake Rapel, the Colbun Maule lagoon and the lagoon of La Laja.

[Climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") is expected to alter the frequency and severity of various natural hazards in Chile, including wildfires, floods, landslides, droughts and rising sea levels. Key sectors vulnerable to climate change impacts include agriculture, fisheries, agriculture and water security.[^114]

### Biodiversity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Araucaria_araucana_-_Parque_Nacional_Conguill%C3%ADo_por_lautaroj_-_001.jpg/250px-Araucaria_araucana_-_Parque_Nacional_Conguill%C3%ADo_por_lautaroj_-_001.jpg)

Araucaria araucana trees in Conguillío National Park

The flora and fauna of Chile are characterized by a high degree of endemism, due to its particular geography. In continental Chile, the [Atacama Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Desert "Atacama Desert") in the north and the [Andes mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andes_Mountains "Andes Mountains") to the east are barriers that have led to the isolation of flora and fauna. Add to that the enormous length of Chile (over 4,300 km (2,672 mi)) and this results in a wide range of climates and environments that can be divided into three general zones: the desert provinces of the north, central Chile, and the humid regions of the south.

The native flora of Chile consists of relatively fewer species compared to the flora of other South American countries. The northernmost coastal and central region is largely barren of vegetation, approaching the most absolute desert in the world.[^115]

On the slopes of the Andes, in addition to the scattered tola desert brush, grasses are found. The central valley is characterized by several species of cacti, the hardy [espinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acacia_caven "Acacia caven"), the [Chilean pine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Araucaria_araucana "Araucaria araucana"), the [southern beeches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nothofagus "Nothofagus") and the [copihue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copihue "Copihue"), a red bell-shaped flower that is Chile's national flower.[^115]

In southern Chile, south of the Biobío River, heavy precipitation has produced dense forests of laurels, magnolias, and various species of conifers and beeches, which become smaller and more stunted to the south.[^116]

The cold temperatures and winds of the extreme south preclude heavy forestation. Grassland is found in East [Magallanes Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magallanes_Province "Magallanes Province") and northern [Tierra del Fuego](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego_Province,_Chile "Tierra del Fuego Province, Chile") (in Patagonia). Much of the Chilean flora is distinct from that of neighboring Argentina, indicating that the Andean barrier existed during its formation.[^116]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Peruan_Condor.jpg/250px-Peruan_Condor.jpg)

Andean condor ( Vultur gryphus ), the national bird of Chile

Some of Chile's flora has an Antarctic origin due to [land bridges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_bridge "Land bridge") which formed during the Cretaceous ice ages, allowing plants to migrate from Antarctica to South America.[^117] Chile had a 2018 [Forest Landscape Integrity Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_Landscape_Integrity_Index "Forest Landscape Integrity Index") mean score of 7.37/10, ranking it 43rd globally out of 172 countries.[^118]

Just over 3,000 species of fungi are recorded in Chile,[^119] [^120] but this number is far from complete. The true total number of fungal species occurring in Chile is likely to be far higher, given the generally accepted estimate that only about 7 percent of all fungi worldwide have so far been discovered.[^121] Although the amount of available information is still very small, a first effort has been made to estimate the number of fungal species endemic to Chile, and 1995 species have been tentatively identified as possible endemics of the country.[^122]

Chile's geographical isolation has restricted the immigration of faunal life so that only a few of the many distinctive South American animals are found. Among the larger mammals are the [puma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cougar "Cougar") or cougar, the llama-like [guanaco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanaco "Guanaco") and the fox-like [chilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_American_gray_fox "South American gray fox"). In the forest region, several types of marsupials and a small deer known as the [pudu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pud%C3%BA "Pudú") are found.[^115]

There are many species of small birds, but most of the larger common Latin American types are absent. Few freshwater fish are native, but North American trout have been successfully introduced into the Andean lakes.[^115] Owing to the vicinity of the Humboldt Current, ocean waters abound with fish and other forms of marine life, which in turn support a rich variety of waterfowl, including several penguins. Whales are abundant, and some six species of seals are found in the area.[^115]
