**Finland**,[^5] or the **Republic of Finland**,[^6] is a [Nordic country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nordic_country "Nordic country") in [Northern Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Europe "Northern Europe"). It borders [Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden "Sweden") to the northwest, [Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway "Norway") to the north, and [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia") to the east, with the [Gulf of Bothnia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Bothnia "Gulf of Bothnia") to the west and the [Gulf of Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland "Gulf of Finland") to the south, opposite [Estonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estonia "Estonia"). Its capital and largest city is [Helsinki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helsinki "Helsinki"). Finland has a population of 5.7 million.[^7] The official languages are [Finnish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_language "Finnish language") and [Swedish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_language "Swedish language"), the [mother tongues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_language "First language") of 83.5 percent and 5.0 percent of the population, respectively.[^7] [^16] Finland's climate varies from [humid continental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humid_continental_climate "Humid continental climate") in the south to [boreal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boreal_climate "Boreal climate") in the north. Its land is predominantly covered by [boreal forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boreal_forest "Boreal forest"), with [over 180,000 recorded lakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_lakes_of_Finland "List of lakes of Finland").[^17] [^18]

Finland was first settled around 9000 BC after the [last Ice Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_Glacial_Period "Last Glacial Period").[^19] During the [Stone Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stone_Age "Stone Age"), various cultures emerged, distinguished by different styles of ceramics. The [Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") and [Iron Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age") were marked by contacts with other cultures in [Fennoscandia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fennoscandia "Fennoscandia") and the [Baltic region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_region "Baltic region").[^20] From the late 13th century, Finland became part of Sweden following the [Northern Crusades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Crusades "Northern Crusades"). In 1809, as a result of the [Finnish War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_War "Finnish War"), Finland was captured from Sweden and became [an autonomous grand duchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Finland "Grand Duchy of Finland") within the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"). During this period, [Finnish art flourished](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Age_of_Finnish_Art "Golden Age of Finnish Art") and an [independence movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_of_Finland "Independence of Finland") gradually developed.

Following the [Russian Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Revolution "Russian Revolution") of 1917, Finland [declared its independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_Declaration_of_Independence "Finnish Declaration of Independence"). A [civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_Civil_War "Finnish Civil War") ensued the following year, with the anticommunist [Whites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whites_\(Finland\) "Whites (Finland)") emerging victorious. Finland's status as a [republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic "Republic") was confirmed in 1919. During [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland_in_World_War_II "Finland in World War II"), Finland fought against the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") in the [Winter War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter_War "Winter War") and the [Continuation War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuation_War "Continuation War"), and later against [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") in the [Lapland War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lapland_War "Lapland War"). As a result, it lost parts of its territory to the Soviet Union but retained its independence and democracy. During the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), Finland embraced an official policy of neutrality. After the Cold War, Finland became a member of the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") in 1995 and the [Eurozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurozone "Eurozone") in 1999. Following the [Russian invasion of Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine"), Finland joined [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") in 2023.

Finland became the first country in Europe to grant [universal suffrage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_suffrage#Dates_by_country "Universal suffrage") in 1906, and the first in the world to give all adult citizens the right to run for public office.[^2] Finland remained a largely [rural and agrarian country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agrarian_country "Agrarian country") until the 1950s, when it pursued rapid industrialisation and a [Nordic-style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nordic_model "Nordic model") [welfare state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welfare_state "Welfare state"), resulting in [an advanced economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Finland "Economy of Finland") and high [per capita income](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\)_per_capita "List of countries by GDP (nominal) per capita"). The [country consistently ranks highly in international rankings across various categories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_rankings_of_Finland "International rankings of Finland"), such as education, economic competitiveness, happiness, and prosperity. [Finnish foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_Finland "Foreign relations of Finland") based on its [middle power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_power "Middle power") status emphasizes international cooperation and partnership, which has recently shifted towards closer [ties with NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland%E2%80%93NATO_relations "Finland–NATO relations"). [Finnish cultural values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Finland "Culture of Finland"), including [egalitarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egalitarianism "Egalitarianism"), [secularism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularism "Secularism"), [human rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights_in_Finland "Human rights in Finland") and [environmentalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_issues_in_Finland "Environmental issues in Finland"), are actively promoted through [membership in multiple international forums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_Finland#Multilateral_relations "Foreign relations of Finland").

## Etymology

The name *Suomi* ([Finnish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_language "Finnish language") for 'Finland') has uncertain origins, but a common etymology with *saame* (the [Sámi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A1mi_people "Sámi people")) has been suggested.[^22] [^23] In the earliest historical sources, from the 12th and 13th centuries, the term *Finland* refers to the coastal region around [Turku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turku "Turku") in the southwest of modern Finland; this region later became known as [Finland Proper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland_Proper_\(historical_province\) "Finland Proper (historical province)") in distinction from the country name Finland.[^24] [^25]

In the 14th and 15th centuries, the southwest ([Eura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eura "Eura") and Turku), the interior (around [Lake Päijänne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_P%C3%A4ij%C3%A4nne "Lake Päijänne")), and the southeast (reaching [Lake Ladoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Ladoga "Lake Ladoga")) regions of modern Finland were incorporated into the Swedish realm.[^25] The entire area was called *Finlandia et partes orientales* or *[Österland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%96sterland "Österland")* ("Eastland") by the Scandinavians.[^25] The term *Österland* was used until the 16th century.[^26] The interior was known as [Tavastia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tavastia_\(historical_province\) "Tavastia (historical province)").[^25] By the end of the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), the term *Finland* was extended to the east as well.[^27]

## History

### Prehistory

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Kivikautinen_karhunp%C3%A4%C3%A4nuija.jpg/250px-Kivikautinen_karhunp%C3%A4%C3%A4nuija.jpg)

Stone Age bear head gavel found in Paltamo, Kainuu 28 29

The area that is now Finland was settled in, at the latest, around 8500 BC during the [Stone Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stone_Age "Stone Age") towards the end of the [last glacial period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_glacial_period "Last glacial period"). The [artefacts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artifact_\(archaeology\) "Artifact (archaeology)") the first settlers left behind present characteristics that are shared with those found in [Estonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estonia "Estonia"), Russia, and Norway.[^30] The earliest people were [hunter-gatherers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hunter-gatherer "Hunter-gatherer"), using stone tools.[^31]

The first pottery appeared in 5200 BC, when the [Comb Ceramic culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comb_Ceramic_culture "Comb Ceramic culture") was introduced.[^32] The area of present-day Finland was in the western limits of the culture, which produced pottery with a distinct [comb pattern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comb_Ceramic "Comb Ceramic").[^33] The arrival of the [Corded Ware culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corded_Ware_culture "Corded Ware culture") in the south of coastal Finland between 3000 and 2500 BC may have coincided with the start of agriculture.[^34] Even with the introduction of agriculture, hunting and fishing continued to be important parts of the subsistence economy.

Based upon linguistic evidence, Finland seems to have been primarily inhabited by speakers of [Paleo-European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleo-European_languages "Paleo-European languages") languages prior to the migration of the Finno-Ugric peoples, which influenced the [Sámi languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A1mi_languages "Sámi languages") who were the first Finno-Ugric peoples to move towards Finland. These languages have been divided into [Paleo-Laplandic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleo-Laplandic_languages "Paleo-Laplandic languages") which were spoken around Lapland, and the Lakelandic languages spoken in most of modern-day Finland.[^35] [^36]

In the [Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age"), permanent all-year-round cultivation and [animal husbandry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_husbandry "Animal husbandry") spread, but the cold climate slowed the change.[^37] The [Seima-Turbino phenomenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seima-Turbino_phenomenon "Seima-Turbino phenomenon") brought the first bronze artefacts to the region and possibly also the [Finno-Ugric languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finno-Ugric_languages "Finno-Ugric languages").[^37] [^38] Commercial contacts that had so far mostly been to Estonia started to extend to Scandinavia. Domestic manufacture of bronze artefacts started 1300 BC.[^39]

In the [Iron Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age"), population grew. [Finland Proper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland_Proper "Finland Proper") was the most densely populated area. Commercial contacts in the [Baltic Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_Sea "Baltic Sea") region grew and extended during the eighth and ninth centuries. Main exports from Finland were furs, slaves, [castoreum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Castoreum "Castoreum"), and falcons to European courts. Imports included silk and other fabrics, jewelry, [Ulfberht swords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulfberht_swords "Ulfberht swords"), and, in lesser extent, glass. Production of iron started approximately in 500 BC.[^40] At the end of the ninth century, indigenous artefact culture, especially weapons and women's jewelry, had more common local features than ever before. This has been interpreted to be expressing common Finnish identity.[^41]

An early form of [Finnic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnic_languages "Finnic languages") spread to the Baltic Sea region approximately 1900 BC. Common Finnic language was spoken around [Gulf of Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland "Gulf of Finland") 2000 years ago. The dialects from which the modern-day Finnish language was developed came into existence during the Iron Age.[^42] Contacts with the ancient [Baltic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_peoples "Baltic peoples") and eastern [Germanic peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanic_peoples "Germanic peoples") greatly influenced the [Proto-Finnic language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Finnic_language "Proto-Finnic language").[^33] Although distantly related, the [Sámi people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A1mi_people "Sámi people") retained the hunter-gatherer lifestyle longer than the Finns. The Sámi cultural identity and the [Sámi languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A1mi_languages "Sámi languages") have survived in [Lapland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lapland_\(Finland\) "Lapland (Finland)"), the northernmost province.

### Swedish era

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Suomenlinna.jpg/250px-Suomenlinna.jpg)

Now lying within Helsinki, Suomenlinna is a UNESCO World Heritage Site consisting of an inhabited 18th-century sea fortress built on six islands. It is one of Finland's most popular tourist attractions.

The 12th and 13th centuries were a violent time in the northern Baltic Sea. The [Livonian Crusade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livonian_Crusade "Livonian Crusade") was ongoing and the [Finnish tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_tribes "Finnish tribes") such as the [Tavastians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tavastians "Tavastians") and [Karelians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karelia_\(historical_province_of_Finland\) "Karelia (historical province of Finland)") were in [frequent conflicts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Finnish_wars "Early Finnish wars") with [Novgorod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Novgorod "Republic of Novgorod") and with each other. Also, during the 12th and 13th centuries several crusades from the Catholic realms of the Baltic Sea area were made against the Finnish tribes. [Danes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danes "Danes") waged at least three crusades to Finland, in 1187 or slightly earlier,[^43] in 1191 and in 1202,[^44] and [Swedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedes "Swedes"), possibly the so-called [second Crusade to Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Swedish_Crusade "Second Swedish Crusade"), in 1249 against Tavastians and the [third Crusade to Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_Swedish_Crusade "Third Swedish Crusade") in 1293 against the Karelians. The so-called [first Crusade to Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Swedish_Crusade "First Swedish Crusade"), possibly in 1155, most likely never occurred.[^45]

As a result of the Crusades, mostly with the Second Swedish Crusade led by [Birger Jarl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birger_Jarl "Birger Jarl"), and the [colonization of some Finnish coastal areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_colonisation_of_Finland "Swedish colonisation of Finland") with Christian [Swedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish-speaking_population_of_Finland "Swedish-speaking population of Finland") during the Middle Ages,[^46] Finland gradually became part of the kingdom of Sweden and the sphere of influence of the [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church").[^47] Under Sweden, Finland was annexed as part of the cultural order of [Western Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Europe "Western Europe").[^48] The Swedes built fortresses in [Häme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%A4me "Häme") and [Turku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turku "Turku"), while a Swedish royal council was instituted, an administrative structure and fiscal apparatus was created, and law codes were codified during the reigns of [Magnus Ladulås](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnus_Ladul%C3%A5s "Magnus Ladulås") (1275–1290) and [Magnus Eriksson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnus_Eriksson "Magnus Eriksson") (1319–1364).[^26] As a result, the Finnish lands were firmly integrated into the Swedish realm.[^26]

[Swedish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland_Swedish "Finland Swedish") was the dominant language of the nobility, administration, and education; [Finnish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_language "Finnish language") was chiefly a language for the [peasantry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peasant "Peasant"), clergy, and local [courts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Court "Court") in predominantly Finnish-speaking areas.[^49] [^50] During the [Protestant Reformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestant_Reformation "Protestant Reformation"), the [Finns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finns "Finns") gradually converted to [Lutheranism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lutheranism "Lutheranism").[^51] The end of the [Kalmar Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalmar_Union "Kalmar Union") ushered in an era of religious, social, and economic changes.[^52] [Gustav Vasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Vasa "Gustav Vasa") (r. 1523–1560) made his second son [Johan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_III_of_Sweden "John III of Sweden") the [duke of Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_of_Finland "Duke of Finland"), while [Gustav Adolf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus "Gustavus Adolphus") (r. 1611–1632) created the office of [governor-general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governor-General_of_Finland "Governor-General of Finland") for Finland as part of his restructuring of the administration of the Swedish realm.[^53]

In the 16th century, a bishop and Lutheran Reformer [Mikael Agricola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikael_Agricola "Mikael Agricola") published the first written works in Finnish;[^54] and Finland's current capital city, [Helsinki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helsinki "Helsinki"), was founded by King [Gustav Vasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_I_of_Sweden "Gustav I of Sweden") in 1555.[^55] The first university in Finland, the [Royal Academy of Turku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Turku "Royal Academy of Turku"), was established by Queen [Christina of Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christina_of_Sweden "Christina of Sweden") at the proposal of Count [Per Brahe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Per_Brahe_the_Younger "Per Brahe the Younger") in 1640.[^56] [^57]

The Finns reaped a reputation in the [Thirty Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War "Thirty Years' War") (1618–1648) as a well-trained [cavalrymen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavalrymen "Cavalrymen") called " [Hakkapeliitta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hakkapeliitta "Hakkapeliitta") ".[^58] Finland suffered a severe [famine in 1695–1697](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Famine_of_Finland_\(1695%E2%80%931697\) "Great Famine of Finland (1695–1697)"), during which about one third of the Finnish population died,[^59] and a [devastating plague occurred a few years later](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_plague_during_the_Great_Northern_War "The plague during the Great Northern War").

In the 18th century, wars between Sweden and Russia twice led to the occupation of Finland by Russian forces, times known to the Finns as the [Greater Wrath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Wrath "Greater Wrath") (1714–1721) and the [Lesser Wrath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russo-Swedish_War_\(1741%E2%80%931743\) "Russo-Swedish War (1741–1743)") (1742–1743).[^60] [^59] It is estimated that almost an entire generation of young men was lost during the Great Wrath, due mainly to the destruction of homes and farms, and the burning of Helsinki.[^61]

### Grand Duchy of Finland

The Swedish era ended with the [Finnish War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_War "Finnish War") of 1809. On 29 March 1809, after being conquered by the armies of [Alexander I of Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_I_of_Russia "Alexander I of Russia"), Finland became [an autonomous grand duchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Finland "Grand Duchy of Finland") within the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"), as recognised by the [Diet of Porvoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diet_of_Porvoo "Diet of Porvoo").[^62] This situation continued until the end of 1917.[^60] In 1812, Alexander I incorporated the Russian [province of Vyborg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vyborg_Governorate "Vyborg Governorate") into the Grand Duchy of Finland. In 1854, Finland became involved in Russia's involvement in the [Crimean War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crimean_War "Crimean War") when the British and French navies bombed the Finnish coast and [Åland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%85land "Åland") during the so-called [Åland War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%85land_War "Åland War").[^63]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Suomineito_%28Isto%29.jpg/250px-Suomineito_%28Isto%29.jpg)

Edvard Isto, The Attack, 1899. The Russian eagle is attacking the Finnish Maiden, trying to steal her book of laws

Although Swedish was still widely spoken, the Finnish language began to gain recognition during this period. From the 1860s, a strong Finnish [nationalist movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_nationalism "Ethnic nationalism"), known as the [Fennoman movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fennoman_movement "Fennoman movement"), grew. As a leading Fennoman philosopher and statesman, [J.V. Snellman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johan_Vilhelm_Snellman "Johan Vilhelm Snellman") played a decisive role in the 19th-century Finnish national movement by championing the official status of the Finnish language and spearheading the introduction of the [Finnish markka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_markka "Finnish markka") in 1865, thereby strengthening the autonomy of the Grand Duchy of Finland.[^63] [^64] Milestones included the publication of what would become Finland's [national epic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_epic "National epic"), the *[Kalevala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalevala "Kalevala")*, in 1835 and the legal equality of the Finnish language with Swedish in 1892. In the spirit of [Adolf Ivar Arwidsson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Ivar_Arwidsson "Adolf Ivar Arwidsson") – "we are not Swedes, we do not want to become Russians, so let us be Finns" – a Finnish national identity was established.[^65] Nevertheless, there was no real independence movement in Finland until the early 20th century.[^66]

The [Finnish famine of 1866–1868](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_famine_of_1866%E2%80%931868 "Finnish famine of 1866–1868") occurred after freezing temperatures in early September devastated crops and killed around 15% of the population, making it one of the worst [famines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Famine "Famine") in European history.[^67] The famine led the Russian Empire to relax financial regulations, and investment increased in the following decades. Economic development was rapid.[^68] The [gross domestic product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_domestic_product "Gross domestic product") (GDP) per capita was still half of that of the United States and a third of that of Britain.[^68]

From 1899 to 1917, the Russian Empire pursued [a policy of Russification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russification_of_Finland "Russification of Finland"), which was suspended between 1905 and 1908. In 1906, [universal suffrage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_suffrage "Universal suffrage") was introduced in the Grand Duchy of Finland. However, relations between the Grand Duchy of Finland and the Russian Empire soured when the Russian government began to take steps to restrict Finland's special status and autonomy. For example, universal suffrage was virtually meaningless in practice, as the [tsar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsar "Tsar") did not have to approve any of the laws passed by the Finnish parliament. The desire for independence gained ground, first among radical [liberals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberalism "Liberalism") [^69] and [socialists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialism "Socialism"), partly driven by a declaration called the *[February Manifesto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/February_Manifesto "February Manifesto")* by the last tsar of the Russian Empire, [Nicholas II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia "Nicholas II of Russia"), on 15 February 1899.[^70]

### Civil war and early independence

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/The_victory_parade_of_the_White_Army_1918.jpg/250px-The_victory_parade_of_the_White_Army_1918.jpg)

Finnish military leader and statesman C. G. E. Mannerheim as general officer leading the White Victory Parade at the end of the Finnish Civil War in Helsinki, 1918

After the [February Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/February_Revolution "February Revolution") of 1917, Finland's position as a Grand Duchy under the rule of the Russian Empire was questioned. The [Finnish parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Finland "Parliament of Finland"), controlled by the [Social Democrats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Finland "Social Democratic Party of Finland"), passed the so-called [Power Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_of_Finland#Power_act "Independence of Finland") to give the parliament supreme authority. This was rejected by the [Russian Provisional Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Provisional_Government "Russian Provisional Government"), which decided to dissolve the parliament.[^71] New elections were held in which the right-wing parties won by a small majority. Some social democrats refused to accept the result, claiming that the dissolution of parliament and the subsequent elections were extra-legal. The two almost equally powerful political blocs, the right-wing parties and the Social Democratic Party, were deeply divided.

The [October Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/October_Revolution "October Revolution") in Russia changed the geopolitical situation once again. Suddenly the right-wing parties in Finland began to reconsider their decision to block the transfer of supreme executive power from the Russian government to Finland when the [Bolsheviks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolsheviks "Bolsheviks") came to power in Russia. The right-wing government, led by Prime Minister [P. E. Svinhufvud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P._E._Svinhufvud "P. E. Svinhufvud"), presented the [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_Declaration_of_Independence "Finnish Declaration of Independence") on 4 December 1917, which was officially approved by the Finnish Parliament on 6 December. The [Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Soviet_Federative_Socialist_Republic "Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic") (RSFSR), led by [Vladimir Lenin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin "Vladimir Lenin") was the first country to recognise Finland's independence on 4 January 1918.[^72]

On 27 January 1918, the government began to disarm the Russian forces in [Ostrobothnia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostrobothnia_\(historical_province\) "Ostrobothnia (historical province)"). The socialists took control of southern Finland and Helsinki, but the white government continued in exile in [Vaasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaasa "Vaasa").[^73] [^74] This led to a short but bitter [civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_Civil_War "Finnish Civil War"). The [Whites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Guard_\(Finland\) "White Guard (Finland)"), backed by [Imperial Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Empire "German Empire"), prevailed over the [Reds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Guards_\(Finland\) "Red Guards (Finland)") [^75] and their self-proclaimed [Finnish Socialist Workers' Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_Socialist_Workers%27_Republic "Finnish Socialist Workers' Republic").[^76] After the war, tens of thousands of Reds were interned in camps where thousands were executed or died of malnutrition and disease. A deep social and political enmity was sown between the Reds and the Whites that would last until the [Winter War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter_War "Winter War") and beyond.[^77] [^78] The civil war and the activist expeditions to Soviet Russia in 1918–1920, known as the " [Kinship Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heimosodat "Heimosodat") ", strained relations with the East.[^79] [^80]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Helsinki_Olympic_stadium_and_stadium_tower%2C_1938_%2829438954721%29.jpg/250px-Helsinki_Olympic_stadium_and_stadium_tower%2C_1938_%2829438954721%29.jpg)

Helsinki Olympic Stadium in 1938

After a [brief experiment with monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Finland_\(1918\) "Kingdom of Finland (1918)"), when an attempt to make [Prince Frederick Charles of Hesse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince_Frederick_Charles_of_Hesse "Prince Frederick Charles of Hesse") the king of Finland failed, a [republican constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Finland "Constitution of Finland") was adopted and Finland became a [presidential republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_republic "Presidential republic"), with [K. J. Ståhlberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K._J._St%C3%A5hlberg "K. J. Ståhlberg") elected as its first president on 25 July 1919.[^81] A [liberal nationalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_nationalism "Civic nationalism") with a legal background, Ståhlberg anchored the state in [liberal democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_democracy "Liberal democracy"), promoted the [rule of law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_of_law "Rule of law") and initiated internal reforms.[^82] Finland was also one of the first European countries to strongly promote [women's equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women%27s_rights "Women's rights"), with [Miina Sillanpää](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miina_Sillanp%C3%A4%C3%A4 "Miina Sillanpää") becoming the first female minister in Finnish history in [Väinö Tanner's cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanner_Cabinet "Tanner Cabinet") in 1926–1927.[^83] The Finnish-Russian border was established in 1920 by the [Treaty of Tartu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Tartu_\(Finland%E2%80%93Russia\) "Treaty of Tartu (Finland–Russia)"), which largely followed the historical border but gave Finland [Pechenga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pechengsky_District "Pechengsky District") ([Finnish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_language "Finnish language"): *Petsamo*) and its [Barents Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barents_Sea "Barents Sea") port.[^60] Finnish democracy survived Soviet coup attempts and the anti-communist [Lapua movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lapua_movement "Lapua movement").

In 1917 there were three million people in the country. After the civil war, a credit-based [land reform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_reform "Land reform") was introduced, increasing the proportion of the population with access to capital.[^68] About 70% of the workforce was employed in agriculture and 10% in industry.[^84]

### World War II

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Kolmen_valtakunnan_rajapyykki_27.4.1945.png/250px-Kolmen_valtakunnan_rajapyykki_27.4.1945.png)

Finnish troops raise a flag on the three-country cairn in April 1945 at the close of the World War II in Finland.

The [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") and [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") signed the [Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molotov%E2%80%93Ribbentrop_Pact "Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact") on 23 August 1939, which divided Europe into spheres of influence between the two dictatorships.[^85] In accordance with the pact, the Soviet Union launched the [Winter War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter_War "Winter War") on 30 November 1939 in order to annex Finland.[^86] [^87] The [Finnish Democratic Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_Democratic_Republic "Finnish Democratic Republic") was set up by [Joseph Stalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Stalin "Joseph Stalin") at the beginning of the war to govern Finland after Soviet conquest.[^88] There was widespread international condemnation of the unprovoked attack and it led to the Soviet Union being expelled from the [League of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/League_of_Nations "League of Nations").[^89] The [Red Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Army "Red Army") was defeated in numerous battles, most notably the [Battle of Suomussalmi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Suomussalmi "Battle of Suomussalmi").[^90] After two months of negligible progress on the battlefield, as well as heavy losses in men and material,[^91] Soviet forces began to advance in February and [reached Vyborg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Vyborg_Bay_\(1940\) "Battle of Vyborg Bay (1940)") ([Finnish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_language "Finnish language"): *Viipuri*) in March. The [Moscow Peace Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow_Peace_Treaty "Moscow Peace Treaty") was signed on 12 March 1940, and the war ended the following day. Finland had defended its independence, but ceded 9% of its territory to the Soviet Union.[^92] [^93] Between 1939 and 1944, some 400,000 people were [evacuated from Karelia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evacuation_of_Finnish_Karelia "Evacuation of Finnish Karelia").

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Finnish_areas_ceded_in_1944.png/250px-Finnish_areas_ceded_in_1944.png)

Areas ceded by Finland to the Soviet Union during World War II. The Porkkala land lease was returned to Finland in 1956.

Hostilities resumed in June 1941 with the [Continuation War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuation_War "Continuation War"), when Finland allied itself with Germany following the [latter's invasion of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa "Operation Barbarossa"); the main aim was to regain the territory lost to the Soviets barely a year earlier.[^94] Finnish troops [occupied Eastern Karelia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_military_administration_in_Eastern_Karelia "Finnish military administration in Eastern Karelia") from 1941 to 1944 and assisted the German Army in the [Siege of Leningrad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Leningrad "Siege of Leningrad").[^95] The massive Soviet [Vyborg–Petrozavodsk offensive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vyborg%E2%80%93Petrozavodsk_offensive "Vyborg–Petrozavodsk offensive") in the summer of 1944 led to a breakthrough until the Finns finally repulsed it at [Tali–Ihantala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Tali%E2%80%93Ihantala "Battle of Tali–Ihantala"). This partial Soviet success led to a stalemate and later an [armistice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow_Armistice "Moscow Armistice"). This was followed by the [Lapland War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lapland_War "Lapland War") of 1944–1945, when Finland fought retreating German forces in northern Finland.

The [Armistice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow_Armistice "Moscow Armistice") and [treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finno-Soviet_Treaty_of_1948 "Finno-Soviet Treaty of 1948") signed with the Soviet Union in 1944 and 1948 included Finnish obligations, restraints, and reparations, as well as further territorial concessions. As a result of the two wars, Finland lost 12% of its land area, 20% of its industrial capacity, its second largest city, [Vyborg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vyborg "Vyborg") ([Finnish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_language "Finnish language"): *Viipuri*), and the ice-free port of [Liinakhamari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liinakhamari "Liinakhamari") ([Finnish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_language "Finnish language"): *Liinahamari*).[^93] The Finns lost around 97,000 soldiers and were forced to pay war reparations initially set at $300 million in 1938 prices, later adjusted to $226.5 million.[^96] However, the country avoided occupation by Soviet forces and managed to retain its independence. Along with [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Britain "Great Britain"), Finland emerged from the war as one of the only European countries to have taken part in hostilities that was never occupied and managed to preserve its democracy throughout.[^97]

In the decades following World War II, the [Communists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Finland "Communist Party of Finland") were a strong political party. Furthermore, the Soviet Union persuaded Finland to refuse [Marshall Plan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Plan "Marshall Plan") aid. However, in the hope of preserving Finland's independence, the United States provided secret development aid and supported the [Social Democratic Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Finland "Social Democratic Party of Finland").[^98]

### After the war

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/2018_Helsinki%2C_Finland_%2841401402010%29.jpg/250px-2018_Helsinki%2C_Finland_%2841401402010%29.jpg)

Alvar Aalto 's Finlandia Hall hosted the Conference on Security and Co-operation in Europe in 1975.

The development of trade with the Western powers, such as the United Kingdom, and the payment of reparations to the Soviet Union led to Finland's transformation from a primarily [agrarian society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agrarian_society "Agrarian society") to an industrialised one. [Valmet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valmet "Valmet"), originally a shipyard and then several metal workshops, was established to produce materials for war reparations. After the reparations were paid, Finland continued to trade with the Soviet Union as part of [bilateral trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilateral_trade "Bilateral trade").

In 1950, 46% of Finnish workers were employed in agriculture and a third lived in urban areas, but new jobs in manufacturing, services and trade quickly attracted people to the cities.[^99] The average number of births per woman fell from a [baby boom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baby_boom "Baby boom") peak of 3.5 in 1947 to 1.5 in 1973. As the baby boomers entered the workforce, the economy failed to create jobs fast enough and hundreds of thousands emigrated to more industrialised Sweden, with emigration peaking in 1969 and 1970.[^99] Finland participated in trade liberalisation in the [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank"), the [International Monetary Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund "International Monetary Fund") and the [General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Agreement_on_Tariffs_and_Trade "General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade").

During the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), Finland officially embraced a policy of [neutrality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutral_country "Neutral country"). The [YYA Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finno-Soviet_Treaty_of_1948 "Finno-Soviet Treaty of 1948") (Finno-Soviet Pact of Friendship, Cooperation and Mutual Assistance) recognized Finland's desire to remain outside great-power conflicts. From 1956 president [Urho Kekkonen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urho_Kekkonen "Urho Kekkonen") had a virtual monopoly on relations with the Soviet Union, which was crucial to his continued popularity. In politics, there was a tendency to avoid any policy or statement that could be interpreted as anti-Soviet. This phenomenon was dubbed " [Finlandisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finlandisation "Finlandisation") " by the West German press.[^100]

A [market economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_economy "Market economy") was maintained in Finland. Various industries benefited from trade privileges with the Soviets. Economic growth was rapid in the post-war period, and by 1975 Finland's GDP per capita was the 15th highest in the world. During the 1970s and 1980s, Finland built one of the most extensive [welfare states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welfare_state "Welfare state") in the world. In 1973, Finland negotiated a treaty with the [European Economic Community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Economic_Community "European Economic Community") (EEC) that reduced tariffs, enhancing trade relations.[^101]

Miscalculated macroeconomic decisions, a [banking crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_banking_crisis_of_1990s "Finnish banking crisis of 1990s"), the collapse of its largest trading partner, the Soviet Union, and a global economic downturn caused a deep recession in Finland in the [early 1990s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_1990s_depression_in_Finland "Early 1990s depression in Finland"). The recession bottomed out in 1993 and Finland enjoyed more than a decade of steady economic growth.[^102] After the collapse of the Soviet Union, Finland began to integrate more closely with the West.[^103] Finland [joined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1995_enlargement_of_the_European_Union "1995 enlargement of the European Union") the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") in 1995 and the [euro zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euro_zone "Euro zone") in 1999. Much of the economic growth of the late 1990s was fuelled by the success of mobile phone manufacturer [Nokia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nokia "Nokia").[^48] [^104]

### 21st century

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Presidentti_Sauli_Niinist%C3%B6_ja_p%C3%A4%C3%A4ministeri_Sanna_Marinin_tiedotustilaisuus_15.5.2022_%2852073724707%29.jpg/250px-Presidentti_Sauli_Niinist%C3%B6_ja_p%C3%A4%C3%A4ministeri_Sanna_Marinin_tiedotustilaisuus_15.5.2022_%2852073724707%29.jpg)

Prime Minister Sanna Marin and President Sauli Niinistö at the press conference announcing Finland's intent to apply to NATO on 2022

The Finnish people elected [Tarja Halonen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarja_Halonen "Tarja Halonen") in the [2000 Presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2000_Finnish_presidential_election "2000 Finnish presidential election"), making her the first female President of Finland.[^105] Her predecessor, President [Martti Ahtisaari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martti_Ahtisaari "Martti Ahtisaari"), later won the [Nobel Peace Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize "Nobel Peace Prize") in 2008. The [2008 financial crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_financial_crisis "2008 financial crisis") paralysed Finland's exports in 2008, leading to weaker economic growth throughout the decade.[^106] [^107] [Sauli Niinistö](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sauli_Niinist%C3%B6 "Sauli Niinistö") was elected President of Finland from 2012 until 2024, when [Alexander Stubb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Stubb "Alexander Stubb") took over.[^108] [^109]

Finnish support for [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") rose sharply after the [Russian invasion of Ukraine in 2022](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine"). Before February 2022, opinion polls showed a narrow but decisive majority against NATO membership;[^110] by April, a supermajority was in favour of membership.[^111] [^112] [^113] [^114] On 11 May 2022, Finland signed a mutual security pact with the United Kingdom.[^115] On 12 May, Finland's president and Prime Minister called for NATO membership "without delay".[^116] Subsequently, on 17 May, the Finnish Parliament voted 188–8 in favour of Finland's accession to NATO.[^117] [Finland became a member of NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland%E2%80%93NATO_relations "Finland–NATO relations") on 4 April 2023.[^118]
