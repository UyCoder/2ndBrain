**Oman**,[^5] officially the **Sultanate of Oman**,[^6] is a country in the southeastern [Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula") in [West Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Asia "West Asia"). It shares land borders with [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia"), the [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates"), and [Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemen "Yemen"). Oman's coastline faces the [Arabian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Sea "Arabian Sea") to the southeast and the [Gulf of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman "Gulf of Oman") on the northeast. The [exclaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclaves "Exclaves") of [Madha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madha "Madha") and [Musandam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musandam_Governorate "Musandam Governorate") are surrounded by the United Arab Emirates on their land borders, while Musandam's coastal boundaries are formed by the [Strait of Hormuz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Hormuz "Strait of Hormuz") and the Gulf of Oman. It has a population of approximately 5.46 million and an area of 315,331 km <sup>2</sup> (121,750 sq mi).[^10] [Muscat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscat "Muscat") is the capital and largest city.

Oman is the oldest independent state in the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world"),[^16] [^17] and has been continuously ruled by the [Al Bu Said dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Bu_Said_dynasty "Al Bu Said dynasty") since 1744.[^18] Beginning in the 17th century, Oman became a [regional power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power") with [an empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omani_Empire "Omani Empire") that competed with the [Portuguese Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire") and [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") for influence in the Persian Gulf and the Indian Ocean.[^16] At its peak in the 19th century, Omani influence and control extended across the Strait of Hormuz to what later became Iran and Pakistan, and as far south as [Zanzibar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zanzibar "Zanzibar").[^19] In the 20th century, Oman came under the influence of the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") while *de jure* remaining sovereign.[^20]

Oman's oil reserves are ranked as the 22nd largest globally.[^16] [^21] In 2010, the [United Nations Development Programme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme "United Nations Development Programme") recognised Oman as the most improved country in the world in terms of development during the preceding 40 years.[^22] Less reliant on fossil fuels than other Persian Gulf states, a portion of Oman's economy involves [tourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourism_in_Oman "Tourism in Oman"), and trade of fish, [dates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Date_palm "Date palm") and other agricultural produce. The [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank") classifies Oman as a [high-income economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-income_economy "High-income economy"). As of 2024, Oman ranks as the 42nd most peaceful country in the world in the [Global Peace Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Peace_Index "Global Peace Index").[^23]

Oman is an [absolute monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy "Absolute monarchy") ruled by a [Sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan "Sultan"), with power passed down through the male line. [Qaboos bin Said](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qaboos_bin_Said "Qaboos bin Said") served as sultan from 1970 until his death in 2020. His reign saw a rise in the country's living standards, the abolition of slavery, the end of the [Dhofar Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhofar_Rebellion "Dhofar Rebellion"), and the promulgation of [Oman's constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_Statute_of_Oman "Basic Statute of Oman").[^24] In 2020, [Haitham bin Tariq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haitham_bin_Tariq "Haitham bin Tariq") succeeded Qaboos as the [Sultan of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan_of_Oman "Sultan of Oman").[^25] Oman is a member of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League"), the [Gulf Cooperation Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Cooperation_Council "Gulf Cooperation Council"), the [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement"), and the [Organisation of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation").

## Etymology

The oldest known written mention of "Oman" is found on a tomb in the [Mleiha Archeological Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mleiha_Archaeological_Centre "Mleiha Archaeological Centre") in the [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates"), dated to around 220 BCE, where it is written as 𐩲𐩣𐩬 in [Ancient South Arabian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_South_Arabian_script "Ancient South Arabian script") and 𐡏𐡌𐡍 in [Aramaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aramaic_alphabet "Aramaic alphabet").[^26] The name "Oman" is believed to predate [Pliny the Elder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder") 's reference to "Omana" [^27] and [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy "Claudius Ptolemy") 's reference to "Omanon". (Ὄμανον ἐμπόριον in [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language")).[^28]

Beginning in the 19th century, most writers localised Omana with [Sohar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sohar "Sohar").[^29]. This changed, however, with the advent of archaeology [^30].

The city or region is commonly etymologised in Arabic as deriving from *ʿāmin* or *ʿamūn*, meaning 'settled' people, in contrast to the nomadic [Bedouins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bedouin "Bedouin").[^29] While some theories propose an eponymous founder—such as Oman bin Ibrahim al-Khalil, Oman bin Siba' bin Yaghthan bin Ibrahim, and Oman bin Qahtan—others suggest that "Oman" originates from a valley in [Ma'rib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ma%27rib "Ma'rib"), [Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemen "Yemen"). This valley is presumed to be the ancestral homeland of the [Azd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azd "Azd"), an ancient Bedouin tribe mentioned in [pre-Islamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Islamic_Arabia "Pre-Islamic Arabia") inscriptions, particularly in [Sabaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabaic "Sabaic") inscriptions from the reign of [Sha'r Awtar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sha%27r_Awtar "Sha'r Awtar") ([210](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/210 "210") – [230](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/230 "230") CE).[^31]

Oman has been known by other names. The ancient [Sumerians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumer "Sumer") and the [Mesopotamians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia") called it [Magan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magan_\(civilization\) "Magan (civilization)"), a reference to the [Magan civilisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magan_\(civilization\) "Magan (civilization)"), while the Persians called it Mazun, derived from the word "Muzn", meaning clouds and abundant flowing water, which explains the establishment and prosperity of agriculture in Oman since ancient times and the stability and civilisation that accompanied it. The name "Oman" is mentioned in Arabic sources as an independent region. [Al-Istakhri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Istakhri "Istakhri") and [Ibn Hawqal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Hawqal "Ibn Hawqal") referred to this in their sayings: "Oman is a region with independent regions with spacious people." [Ibn Khaldun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun "Ibn Khaldun") was more explicit in his definition of Oman. He mentioned it among the Arab regions that emerged as independent states in the Arabian Peninsula, namely Yemen, the Hijaz, Hadhramaut, Ash Shihr, and Oman. He described its system of government, saying that it was a "sole sultanic region." [^32]

## History

### Prehistory

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/World_Heritage_Grave_Al_Ayn_Oman.JPG/250px-World_Heritage_Grave_Al_Ayn_Oman.JPG)

The Archaeological Sites of Bat, Al-Khutm and Al-Ayn in Ad Dhahirah, built in the 3rd millennium BCE and a UNESCO World Heritage Site

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Lia_sites.JPG/250px-Lia_sites.JPG)

Late Iron Age sites in Oman

Al Aybut Al Awal, in the [Dhofar Governorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhofar "Dhofar") of Oman, a site was discovered in 2011 containing more than 100 surface scatters of stone tools, belonging to a regionally specific African [lithic industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithic_industry "Lithic industry") —the late [Nubian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nubia "Nubia") Complex—known previously only from the northeast and [Horn of Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horn_of_Africa "Horn of Africa"). Two optically stimulated luminescence age estimates place the Arabian Nubian Complex at 106,000 years old. This supports the proposition that early human populations moved from Africa into Arabia during the [Late Pleistocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Pleistocene "Late Pleistocene").[^33]

In recent years surveys have uncovered Palaeolithic and Neolithic sites on the eastern coast. Main Palaeolithic sites include Saiwan-Ghunaim in the Barr al-Hikman.[^34] Archaeological remains are particularly numerous for the Bronze Age [Umm an-Nar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umm_an-Nar "Umm an-Nar") and [Wadi Suq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wadi_Suq_culture "Wadi Suq culture") periods. At the archaeological sites of Bat, Al-Janah, and Al-Ayn wheel-turned pottery, hand-made stone vessels, metals industry artifacts, and monumental architecture have been preserved.[^35]

### Ancient history

#### Magan civilisation

[Sumerian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumerian_language "Sumerian language") tablets referred to Oman as " [Magan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magan_\(civilization\) "Magan (civilization)") " [^36] [^37] and in the [Akkadian language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akkadian_language "Akkadian language") "Makan",[^38] a name that links Oman's ancient copper resources.[^39] This is a clear reference to the Magan civilisation, an ancient region in what is now modern day Oman and [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates"), which was referred to in [Sumerian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumer "Sumer") [cuneiform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuneiform_\(script\) "Cuneiform (script)") texts of around 2300 BCE and existed until 550 BCE as a source of [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper") and [diorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diorite "Diorite") for [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia").

#### Iron Age to pre-Islamic period

During the 8th century BCE, it is believed that the Yaarub, the descendant of [Qahtan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qahtanite "Qahtanite"), ruled the entire region of Yemen, including Oman. Wathil bin Himyar bin [Abd-Shams (Saba)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheba "Sheba") bin Yashjub ([Yaman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemen "Yemen")) bin Yarub bin [Qahtan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joktan "Joktan") later ruled Oman.[^40] It is thus believed that the Yaarubah were the first settlers in Oman from Yemen.[^41]

### Persian period

In the 1970s and 1980s, scholars like [John C. Wilkinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_C._Wilkinson "John C. Wilkinson") [^42] believed by virtue of oral history that in the 6th century BCE, the [Achaemenids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") exerted control over the Omani peninsula, most likely ruling from a coastal centre such as [Suhar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suhar "Suhar").[^43] The northern half of Oman was presumably was part of the [Maka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maka_\(satrapy\) "Maka (satrapy)") [^44] [satrapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satrapy "Satrapy") of the Persian [Achaemenid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire"). By the time of the [conquests of Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conquests_of_Alexander_the_Great "Conquests of Alexander the Great"), the satrapy may have existed in some form.

Central Oman has its own indigenous Samad Late Iron Age cultural assemblage named eponymously from [Samad al-Shan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samad_al-Shan "Samad al-Shan"). In the northern part of the Oman Peninsula the [Recent Pre-Islamic Period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Islamic_Arabia "Pre-Islamic Arabia") begins in the 3rd century BCE and extends into the 3rd century CE. Whether or not Persians brought south-eastern Arabia under their control is a moot point, since the lack of Persian archeological finds speak against this belief. [Armand-Pierre Caussin de Perceval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armand-Pierre_Caussin_de_Perceval "Armand-Pierre Caussin de Perceval") suggests that Shammir bin Wathil bin Himyar recognised the authority of [Cyrus the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great "Cyrus the Great") over Oman in 536 BCE.[^40]

The Omani Kingdom was subdued by the [Sasanian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sasanian_Empire "Sasanian Empire") 's forces under [Vahrez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vahrez "Vahrez") during the [Aksumite–Persian wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aksumite%E2%80%93Persian_wars "Aksumite–Persian wars"). The 4,000-strong Sasanian garrison was headquartered at Jamsetjerd/Jamshedgird (modern Jebel Gharabeh, also known as Felej al-Sook) [^45]

### Azd tribal migration

Over centuries, From the 2nd half of the 1st millennium BCE, waves of Semitic speaking peoples migrated from central and western Arabia to the east. The most important of these migrating tribes are known as [Azd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azd "Azd"). they made a living by fishing, farming, herding or stock breeding. Further, many present-day Omani families trace their ancestral roots to other parts of Arabia. Arab migration to Oman started from northern-western and south-western Arabia and those who chose to settle had to compete with the indigenous population for the best arable land. When Arab tribes started to migrate to Oman, there were two distinct groups. One group, a segment of the [Azd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azd "Azd") tribe migrated from [Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemen "Yemen") in 120 [^46] /200 CE following the collapse of [Marib Dam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marib_Dam "Marib Dam"), while the other group migrated a few centuries before the birth of Islam from [Nejd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nejd "Nejd") (present-day [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia")), named [Nizari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nizar_ibn_Ma%27ad "Nizar ibn Ma'ad"). Other historians believe that the Yaarubah from Qahtan, which belong to an older branch, were the first settlers of Oman from Yemen, and then came the Azd.[^41]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Ruins6.JPG/250px-Ruins6.JPG)

Ruins of Khor Rori, built between 100 BCE and 100 CE

The Azd settlers in Oman are descendants of Nasr bin Azd and were later known as "the Al-Azd of Oman".[^46] Seventy years after the first Azd migration, another branch of [Alazdi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azd "Azd") under Malik bin Fahm, the founder of Kingdom of [Tanukhites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanukhids "Tanukhids") on the west of [Euphrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euphrates "Euphrates"), is believed to have settled in Oman.[^46] According to Al-Kalbi, Malik bin Fahm was the first settler of Alazd.[^47] He is said to have first settled in [Qalhat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qalhat "Qalhat"). By this account, Malik, with an armed force of more than 6000 men and horses, fought against the [Marzban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marzban "Marzban"), who served an ambiguously named Persian king in the battle of Salut in Oman and eventually defeated the Persian forces.[^41] [^48] [^49] [^50] This account is, however, semi-legendary and seems to condense multiple centuries of migration and conflict as well as an amalgamation of various traditions from not only the Arab tribes but also the region's original inhabitants.[^48] [^51] [^52]

During the 7th century CE, Omanis came in contact with and accepted [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam").[^53] [^54] The conversion of Omanis to Islam is ascribed to [Amr ibn al-As](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amr_ibn_al-As "Amr ibn al-As"), who was sent by the Islamic prophet [Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad "Muhammad") during the [Expedition of Zaid ibn Haritha (Hisma)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expedition_of_Zaid_ibn_Haritha_\(Hisma\) "Expedition of Zaid ibn Haritha (Hisma)"). Amr was dispatched to meet with Jaifer and Abd, the sons of Julanda who ruled Oman. They appear to have readily embraced Islam.[^55]

### Imamate of Oman

Omani [Azd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azd "Azd") used to travel to [Basra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basra "Basra") for trade, which was a centre of Islam, during the [Umayyad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad "Umayyad") empire. Omani Azd were granted a section of Basra, where they could settle and attend to their needs. Many of the Omani Azd who settled in Basra became wealthy merchants and, under their leader [al-Muhallab ibn Abi Sufra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Muhallab_ibn_Abi_Sufra "Al-Muhallab ibn Abi Sufra"), started to expand their influence of power eastwards towards [Khorasan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Khorasan "Greater Khorasan").

[Ibadism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibadism "Ibadism") originated in Basra through its founder, [Abd Allah ibn Ibad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdallah_ibn_Ibad "Abdallah ibn Ibad"),[^56] as a moderate branch of the Kharijites, an Islamic sect that split from the [Muhakkima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhakkima "Muhakkima"). These groups initially supported Imam [Ali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ali "Ali") during the [First Fitna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Fitna "First Fitna") but turned away after rejecting arbitration at the [Battle of Siffin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Siffin "Battle of Siffin") in AD 657. around the year 650; the Omani Azd in Iraq would subsequently adopt this as their predominant faith. Later, [al-Hajjaj](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Hajjaj_ibn_Yusuf "Al-Hajjaj ibn Yusuf"), the governor of Iraq, came into conflict with the Ibadis, which forced them back to Oman. Among those who returned was the scholar [Jaber bin Zaid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C4%81bir_ibn_Zayd "Jābir ibn Zayd"). His return (and the return of many other scholars) greatly enhanced the Ibadhi movement in Oman.[^57] Alhajjaj also made an attempt to subjugate Oman, then ruled by Suleiman and Said (the sons of Abbad bin Julanda). Alhajjaj dispatched Mujjaah bin Shiwah, who was confronted by Said bin Abbad. This confrontation devastated Said's army, after which he and his forces retreated to the [Jebel Akhdar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jebel_Akhdar_\(Oman\) "Jebel Akhdar (Oman)") (mountains). Mujjaah and his forces went after Said, successfully flushing them out from hiding in Wadi Mastall. Mujjaah later moved towards the coast, where he confronted Suleiman bin Abbad. The battle was won by Suleiman's forces. Alhajjaj, however, sent another force (under Abdulrahman bin Suleiman); he eventually won the war, taking over the governance of Oman.[^58] [^59] [^60]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Bahla_Fort-109699.jpg/250px-Bahla_Fort-109699.jpg)

Bahla Fort, a UNESCO World Heritage site, was built between 12th and 15th c. by the Nabhani dynasty.

The first elective [Imamate of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imamate_of_Oman "Imamate of Oman") is believed to have been established shortly after the fall of the [Umayyad Dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") in 750/755 CE, when Janaħ bin ʕibadah Alħinnawi was elected.[^57] [^61] Other scholars claim that Janaħ bin Ibadah served as a [Wāli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W%C4%81li "Wāli") (governor) under the [Umayyad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") dynasty (and later ratified the Imamate), and that Julanda bin Masud was the first elected Imam of Oman, in 751 CE.[^62] [^63] The first Imamate reached its peak power in the ninth century CE.[^57] The Imamate established a maritime empire whose fleet controlled the Gulf, during the time when trade with the [Abbasid Dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate"), the Far East, and Africa flourished.[^64] The authority of the Imams started to decline due to power struggles, the constant interventions of Abbasid, and the rise of the [Seljuk Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seljuk_Empire "Seljuk Empire").[^65] [^62]

### Nabhani dynasty

During the 11th and 12th centuries, the Omani coast was in the sphere of influence of the [Seljuk Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seljuk_Empire "Seljuk Empire"). They were expelled in 1154, when the Nabhani dynasty came to power.[^65] The Nabhanis ruled as *muluk*, or kings, while the Imams were reduced to largely symbolic significance. The capital of the dynasty was [Bahla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bahla "Bahla").[^66] The Banu Nabhan controlled the trade in frankincense on the overland route via [Sohar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sohar "Sohar") to the [Yabrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yabrin "Yabrin") oasis, and then north to Bahrain, Baghdad and Damascus.[^67] The mango-tree was introduced to Oman during the time of Nabhani dynasty, by ElFellah bin Muhsin.[^41] [^68] The Nabhani dynasty started to deteriorate in 1507 when Portuguese colonisers captured the coastal city of [Muscat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscat,_Oman "Muscat, Oman"), and gradually extended their control along the coast up to [Sohar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sohar "Sohar") in the north and down to [Sur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sur,_Oman "Sur, Oman") in the southeast.[^69] Other historians argue that the Nabhani dynasty ended earlier in 1435 CE when conflicts between the dynasty and [Hinawis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinawi "Hinawi") arose, which led to the restoration of the elective Imamate.[^41]

### Portuguese era

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/MASCATE_Ant%C3%B3nio_Bocarro.png/250px-MASCATE_Ant%C3%B3nio_Bocarro.png)

Portuguese depiction of Muscat. The Portuguese Empire ruled Oman for 143 years (1507–1650)

A decade after [Vasco da Gama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama "Vasco da Gama") succeeded in his voyage around the [Cape of Good Hope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope "Cape of Good Hope") and to India in 1497–1498, [Afonso de Albuquerque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque "Afonso de Albuquerque") captured Hormuz and Portugal governed Oman for a 143-year period, from 1507 to 1650.[^70] In need of an outpost to protect their sea lanes, the Portuguese built up and fortified Muscat. Remnants of Portuguese architectural style still exist. Later, several more Omani cities were colonised in the early 16th century by the Portuguese, to control the entrances of the [Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf") and trade in the region as part of a web of fortresses in the region, from [Basra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basra "Basra") to [Hormuz Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hormuz_Island "Hormuz Island").[^70]

The Ottomans contested the Persian Gulf and Oman from the Portuguese during the sixteenth century. In 1552 an [Ottoman fleet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Navy "Ottoman Navy") briefly [captured the fort in Muscat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capture_of_Muscat_\(1552\) "Capture of Muscat (1552)"), during their fight for control of the Persian Gulf and the Indian Ocean, but soon departed after destroying the surroundings of the fortress.[^71] [^72] [^73] At the [Battle of the Gulf of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Gulf_of_Oman "Battle of the Gulf of Oman"), the Portuguese decisively routed an Ottoman fleet.[^74]

Later in the 17th century, using its bases in Oman, Portugal engaged in the [largest naval battle ever fought in the Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_off_Hormuz_\(1625\) "Battle off Hormuz (1625)"). The [Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire") force fought against a combined armada of the [Dutch East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company "Dutch East India Company") (VOC) and [English East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_East_India_Company "English East India Company") supported by the Safavid empire. The result of the battle was a draw but it resulted in the loss of Portuguese influence in the Gulf.[^75]

### Yaruba dynasty (1624–1744)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Areas_under_Omani_influence_18th-19th_century.svg/250px-Areas_under_Omani_influence_18th-19th_century.svg.png)

Following the expulsion of the Portuguese Empire, Oman became one of the powers in the western Indian Ocean from 1698 onwards. 76

During the 17th century, the Omanis were reunited by the [Yaruba Imams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yaruba_dynasty "Yaruba dynasty"). Nasir bin Murshid became the first Yaarubah Imam in 1624, when he was elected in [Rustaq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rustaq "Rustaq").[^77] Imam Nasir and his successor succeeded in the 1650s in expelling the Portuguese from their coastal domains in Oman.[^57] The Omanis over time established a maritime empire that pursued the Portuguese and expelled them from all their possessions in East Africa, which were then incorporated into the Omani domains. To capture Zanzibar [Saif bin Sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saif_bin_Sultan "Saif bin Sultan"), the [Imam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imam "Imam") of Oman, pressed down the [Swahili Coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swahili_Coast "Swahili Coast"). A major obstacle to his progress was [Fort Jesus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fort_Jesus "Fort Jesus"), housing the garrison of a Portuguese settlement at [Mombasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mombasa "Mombasa"). After a two-year siege, the fort fell to Imam Saif bin Sultan in 1698. Saif bin Sultan occupied Bahrain in 1700. The rivalry within the house of Yaruba over power after the death of Imam Sultan in 1718 weakened the dynasty. With the power of the Yaruba Dynasty dwindling, Imam Saif bin Sultan II eventually asked for help against his rivals from [Nader Shah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nader_Shah "Nader Shah") of Persia. A Persian force arrived in March 1737 to aid Saif. From their base at Julfar, the Persian forces eventually rebelled against the Yaruba in 1743. The Persian empire then tried to take possession of the coast of Oman until 1747.[^57] [^78]

### 18th and 19th centuries

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Dares_222.jpg/250px-Dares_222.jpg)

The Sultan's Palace in Zanzibar, which was once Oman's capital and residence of its sultans

After the Omanis expelled the [Persians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persians "Persians"), [Ahmed bin Sa'id Albusaidi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmad_bin_Said_al-Busaidi "Ahmad bin Said al-Busaidi") became the elected Imam of Oman on 20 November 1744, with [Rustaq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rustaq "Rustaq") serving as the capital. His dynasty, the Al Bu Said, continues to rule Oman into the year 2026. Since the revival of the Imamate, the Omanis continued with the elective system but, provided that the person is deemed qualified, gave preference to a member of the ruling family.[^79]

Following Imam Ahmed's death in 1783, his son, [Said bin Ahmed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Said_bin_Ahmad_Al_Busaidi "Said bin Ahmad Al Busaidi"), became the elected Imam. Said's son, [Hamed bin Said](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamad_bin_Said "Hamad bin Said"), overthrew the governor of Muscat and soon obtained the possession of most of the fortresses in the region, giving himself the title of "Seyyid". Afterwards, Seyyid [Sultan bin Ahmed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan_bin_Ahmad "Sultan bin Ahmad"), the uncle of Seyyid Hamed, took over power. Sultan's son, Seyyid [Said bin Sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Said_bin_Sultan "Said bin Sultan"), succeeded him.[^80] [^81]

During the entire 19th century, in addition to Imam Said bin Ahmed, who retained the title until he died in 1803, [Azzan bin Qais](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azzan_bin_Qais "Azzan bin Qais") was the only elected Imam of Oman. His rule started in 1868. However, the British refused to accept Imam Azzan as a ruler, as he was viewed as inimical to their interests. This view played an instrumental role in supporting the deposition of Imam Azzan in 1871 by his cousin, [Sayyid Turki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turki_bin_Said "Turki bin Said"), a son of the late Sayyid Said bin Sultan and brother of [Sultan Barghash of Zanzibar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barghash_bin_Said_of_Zanzibar "Barghash bin Said of Zanzibar"), who Britain deemed to be more acceptable.[^82]

Oman's Imam Sultan, defeated ruler of Muscat, was granted sovereignty over [Gwadar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gwadar "Gwadar"), an area of modern-day Pakistan.[^1] [^83]

#### British protectorate status

The [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") was keen to dominate southeast Arabia to stifle the growing power of other European states and to curb the Omani maritime power that grew during the 17th century.[^84] [^64] The British empire over time, starting from the late 18th century, began to establish a series of treaties with the sultans with the objective of advancing British political and economic interest in Muscat, while granting the sultans military protection.[^64] [^84] In 1798, the first treaty between the British [East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_India_Company "East India Company") and the Albusaidi dynasty was signed by Sayyid Sultan bin Ahmed. The treaty aimed to block commercial competition of the French and the Dutch as well as obtain a concession to build a British factory at [Bandar Abbas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bandar_Abbas "Bandar Abbas").[^85] [^57] [^86] A second treaty was signed in 1800, which stipulated that a British representative shall reside at the port of Muscat and manage all external affairs with other states.[^86] As the Omani Empire weakened, the British influence over Muscat grew throughout the nineteenth century.[^76]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Muscat_Squadron.png/250px-Muscat_Squadron.png)

A [British naval squadron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Navy "Royal Navy") in [Muscat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscat "Muscat") c,1870 [^87]

In 1854, a deed of cession of the Omani [Kuria Muria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuria_Muria "Kuria Muria") islands to Britain was signed by the sultan of Muscat and the British government.[^88] The British government achieved predominating control over Muscat, which, for the most part, impeded competition from other nations.[^89] Between 1862 and 1892, the Political Residents, [Lewis Pelly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_Pelly "Lewis Pelly") and Edward Ross, played an instrumental role in securing British supremacy over the Persian Gulf and Muscat by a system of indirect governance.[^82] By the end of the 19th century, and with the loss of its African dominions and its revenues, British influence increased to the point that the sultans became heavily dependent on British loans and signed declarations to consult the British government on all important matters.[^84] [^90] [^91] [^92] The Sultanate thus came *de facto* under the British sphere.[^91] [^93]

Zanzibar was a valuable property as the main [slave market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean_slave_trade "Indian Ocean slave trade") of the Swahili Coast as well as being a major producer of cloves, and became an increasingly important part of the Omani empire, a fact reflected by the decision of the Sayyid [Sa'id bin Sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sa%27id_ibn_Sultan "Sa'id ibn Sultan"), to make it the capital of the empire in 1837. In 1856, under British arbitration, Zanzibar and Muscat became two different sultanates.[^94]

#### Treaty of Seeb

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Maskat_%26_Oman_map.png/250px-Maskat_%26_Oman_map.png)

The split between the interior region (orange) and the coastal region (red) of Oman and Muscat

The [Hajar Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hajar_Mountains "Hajar Mountains"), of which the [Jebel Akhdar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jebel_Akhdar_\(Oman\) "Jebel Akhdar (Oman)") is a part, separate the country into two distinct regions: the interior, and the coastal area dominated by the capital, Muscat. The British imperial development over Muscat and Oman during the 19th century led to the renewed revival of the cause of the Imamate in the interior of Oman, which has appeared in cycles for more than 1,200 years in Oman.[^64] The British Political Agent, who resided in Muscat, owed the alienation of the interior of Oman to the vast influence of the British government over Muscat, which he described as being completely self-interested and without any regard to the social and political conditions of the locals.[^95] In 1913, Imam Salim Alkharusi instigated an anti-Muscat rebellion that lasted until 1920 when the Sultanate established peace with the Imamate by signing the [Treaty of Seeb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Seeb "Treaty of Seeb"). The treaty was brokered by Britain, which had no economic interest in the interior of Oman during that point of time. The treaty granted autonomous rule to the Imamate in the interior of Oman and recognised the sovereignty of the coast of Oman, the [Sultanate of Muscat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscat_and_Oman "Muscat and Oman").[^84] [^96] [^97] [^98] In 1920, Imam Salim Alkharusi died and Muhammad Alkhalili was elected.[^57]

On 10 January 1923, an agreement between the Sultanate and the British government was signed in which the Sultanate had to consult with the British [political agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_officer_\(British_Empire\) "Political officer (British Empire)") residing in Muscat and obtain the approval of the [High Government of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Raj "British Raj") to extract oil in the Sultanate.[^99] On 31 July 1928, the [Red Line Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Line_Agreement "Red Line Agreement") was signed between Anglo-Persian Company (later renamed British Petroleum), Royal Dutch/Shell, Compagnie Française des Pétroles (later renamed Total), Near East Development Corporation (later renamed ExxonMobil) and Calouste Gulbenkian (an Armenian businessman) to collectively produce oil in the post- [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") region, which included the Arabian peninsula, with each of the four major companies holding 23.75 percent of the shares while [Calouste Gulbenkian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calouste_Gulbenkian "Calouste Gulbenkian") held the remaining 5 percent shares. The agreement stipulated that none of the signatories was allowed to pursue the establishment of oil concessions within the agreed on area without including all other stakeholders. In 1929, the members of the agreement established [Iraq Petroleum Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq_Petroleum_Company "Iraq Petroleum Company") (IPC).[^100] On 13 November 1931, Sultan Taimur bin Faisal abdicated.[^101]

### Reign of Sultan Said (1932–1970)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Said_bin_Taimur_%28cropped%29.png/250px-Said_bin_Taimur_%28cropped%29.png)

Sultan Said bin Taimur ruled from 1932 to 1970

[Said bin Taimur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Said_bin_Taimur "Said bin Taimur") became the sultan of Muscat officially on 10 February 1932. The rule of sultan Said, a very complex character, was backed by the British government, and has been characterised as being [feudal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudal "Feudal"), [reactionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactionary "Reactionary") and isolationist.[^98] [^64] [^91] [^102] The British government maintained vast administrative control over the Sultanate as the defence secretary and chief of intelligence, chief adviser to the sultan and all ministers except for two were British.[^91] [^103] In 1937, an agreement between the sultan and [Iraq Petroleum Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq_Petroleum_Company "Iraq Petroleum Company") (IPC), a consortium of oil companies that was 23.75% British owned, was signed to grant oil concessions to IPC. After failing to discover oil in the Sultanate, IPC was intensely interested in some promising geological formations near [Fahud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahud "Fahud"), an area located within the Imamate. IPC offered financial support to the sultan to raise an armed force against any potential resistance by the Imamate.[^104] [^105]

Upon the outbreak of [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), the sultan of Oman declared war on Germany on September 10, 1939.[^106] During the war, Oman had a strategic role in the defence of the United Kingdom's trade routes. Oman was never attacked during the war. In 1943, the [Royal Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Air_Force "Royal Air Force") established stations on [Masirah Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masirah_Island "Masirah Island") ([RAF Masirah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RAF_Masirah "RAF Masirah")) and at [Ras al Hadd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ras_al_Hadd "Ras al Hadd"). [Air-sea rescue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air-sea_rescue "Air-sea rescue") units were also stationed in Oman. [No. 244 Squadron RAF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No._244_Squadron_RAF "No. 244 Squadron RAF") flew [Bristol Blenheim V](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bristol_Blenheim_V "Bristol Blenheim V") [light bombers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_bomber "Light bomber") and [Vickers Wellington XIIIs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vickers_Wellington_XIII "Vickers Wellington XIII") out of RAF Masirah on anti-submarine duties in the Gulf of Oman and the northern [Arabian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Sea "Arabian Sea"), while [No. 209 Squadron RAF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No._209_Squadron_RAF "No. 209 Squadron RAF"), [No. 265 Squadron RAF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No._265_Squadron_RAF "No. 265 Squadron RAF"), and [No. 321 Squadron RAF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No._321_Squadron_RAF "No. 321 Squadron RAF") flew [Consolidated PBY Catalinas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consolidated_PBY_Catalina "Consolidated PBY Catalina") out of Umm Ruşayş on Masirah Island.[^107] On October 16, 1943, the German [U-Boat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U-Boat "U-Boat") *[U-533](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_submarine_U-533 "German submarine U-533")* was sunk in the Gulf of Oman after being struck by [depth charges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depth_charge "Depth charge") dropped by a Bristol Blenheim from No. 244 Squadron RAF. The wreck settled at a depth of 108 meters (354 feet) approximately 25 nautical miles (46 kilometres) off the [Fujairah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fujairah "Fujairah") coast. 52 crew members died, with the sole survivor, *[Matrosengefreiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrosengefreiter "Matrosengefreiter")* Günther Schmidt, taken aboard HMIS *Hiravati* near [Khor Fakkan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khor_Fakkan "Khor Fakkan") and made a [prisoner of war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prisoner_of_war "Prisoner of war"). The wreck is now a popular recreational diving site.[^108] [^109]

The December 1951 Treaty of Friendship, Commerce and Navigation (covering commerce, oil reserves and navigation) between Oman and the United Kingdom recognised the Sultanate of Muscat and Oman as a fully independent state.

In 1955, the [exclave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enclave_and_exclave "Enclave and exclave") coastal [Makran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makran "Makran") strip acceded to Pakistan and was made a district of its [Balochistan province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balochistan_\(Pakistan\) "Balochistan (Pakistan)"), while [Gwadar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gwadar "Gwadar") remained in Oman. On 8 September 1958, Pakistan [purchased the Gwadar enclave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gwadar_Purchase "Gwadar Purchase") from Oman for [US$](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_dollar "United States dollar") 3 million.[^2] [^110] Gwadar then became a [tehsil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tehsil "Tehsil") in the Makran district.

#### Jebel Akhdar War

Sultan Said bin Taimur expressed his interest in occupying the Imamate right after the death of Imam Alkhalili, thus taking advantage of any potential instability that might occur within the Imamate when elections were due, to the British government.[^111] The British political agent in Muscat believed that the only method of gaining access to the oil reserves in the interior was by assisting the sultan in taking over the Imamate.[^112] In 1946, the British government offered arms and ammunition, auxiliary supplies and officers to prepare the sultan to attack the interior of Oman.[^113] In May 1954, Imam Alkhalili died and [Ghalib Alhinai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghalib_Alhinai "Ghalib Alhinai") was elected Imam.[^114] Relations between the Sultan Said bin Taimur, and Imam Ghalib Alhinai frayed over their dispute about oil concessions.

In December 1955, Sultan Said bin Taimur sent troops of the Muscat and Oman Field Force to occupy the main centres in Oman, including [Nizwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nizwa "Nizwa"), the capital of the Imamate of Oman, and [Ibri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibri "Ibri").[^96] [^115] The Omanis in the interior led by Imam Ghalib Alhinai, Talib Alhinai, the brother of the Imam and the Wali (governor) of Rustaq, and Suleiman bin Hamyar, who was the Wali (governor) of Jebel Akhdar, defended the Imamate in the [Jebel Akhdar War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jebel_Akhdar_War "Jebel Akhdar War") against British-backed attacks by the Sultanate. In July 1957, the Sultan's forces were withdrawing, but they were repeatedly ambushed, sustaining heavy casualties.[^96] Sultan Said, however, with the intervention of British infantry (two companies of the [Cameronians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameronians_\(Scottish_Rifles\) "Cameronians (Scottish Rifles)")), armoured car detachments from the British Army and [RAF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RAF "RAF") aircraft, was able to suppress the rebellion.[^116] The Imamate's forces retreated to the inaccessible [Jebel Akhdar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jebel_Akhdar_\(Oman\) "Jebel Akhdar (Oman)").[^116] [^104]

Colonel [David Smiley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Smiley "David Smiley"), who had been seconded to organise the Sultan's Armed Forces, managed to isolate the mountain in autumn 1958 and found a route to the plateau from Wadi Bani Kharus.[^117] On 4 August 1957, the British Foreign Secretary gave the approval to carry out air strikes without prior warning to the locals residing in the interior of Oman.[^102] Between July and December 1958, the British RAF made 1,635 raids, dropping 1,094 tons and firing 900 rockets at the interior of Oman targeting insurgents, mountain top villages, water channels and crops.[^91] [^102] On 27 January 1959, the Sultanate's forces occupied the mountain in a surprise operation.[^117] Imam Ghalib, his brother Talib and Sulaiman managed to escape to [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia"), where the Imamate's cause was promoted until the 1970s.[^117] The exiled partisans of the now abolished Imamate of Oman presented the case of Oman to the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League") and the United Nations.[^118] [^119] On 11 December 1963, the UN General Assembly decided to establish an Ad-Hoc Committee on Oman to study the 'Question of Oman' and report back to the General Assembly.[^120] The UN General Assembly adopted the 'Question of Oman' resolution in 1965, 1966 and again in 1967 that called upon the British government to cease all repressive action against the locals, end British control over Oman and reaffirmed the inalienable right of the Omani people to self-determination and independence.[^121] [^122] [^93] [^123] [^124] [^125]

#### Dhofar War

In the [Dhofar War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhofar_War "Dhofar War"), which began in 1963, pro- [Soviet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") forces were pitted against government troops. As the rebellion threatened the Sultan's control of [Dhofar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhofar "Dhofar"), Sultan Said bin Taimur was deposed in a [bloodless coup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1970_Omani_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1970 Omani coup d'état") in 1970 by his son [Qaboos bin Said](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qaboos_bin_Said "Qaboos bin Said") with [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") support. Qaboos expanded the [Sultan of Oman's Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan_of_Oman%27s_Armed_Forces "Sultan of Oman's Armed Forces"), modernised the state's administration and introduced social reforms. The uprising was finally put down in 1976 with the help of forces from Jordan, Saudi Arabia, Iran, Pakistan and Britain.

### Modern history (1970–present)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Qaboos_bin_Said_profile_1983_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Qaboos_bin_Said_profile_1983_%28cropped%29.jpg)

Sultan Qaboos bin Said, whose reign saw a rise in living standards and development, the abolition of slavery, the end of the Dhofar Rebellion, and the promulgation of Oman's constitution.

After deposing his father in 1970, [Sultan Qaboos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan_Qaboos "Sultan Qaboos") opened up the country, removed "Muscat and" from the country's name, embarked on economic reforms, and followed a policy of modernisation marked by increased spending on health, education and welfare.[^126] Saudi Arabia invested in the development of the Omani education system, sending Saudi teachers on its own expense.[^127] [^128] [Slavery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean_slave_trade "Indian Ocean slave trade"), once a cornerstone of the country's trade and development, was outlawed in 1970.[^129]

In 1971, Oman joined the United Nations, along with Bahrain, Qatar and the United Arab Emirates.[^130]

In 1981, Oman became a founding member of the six-nation [Gulf Cooperation Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Cooperation_Council "Gulf Cooperation Council"). Political reforms were eventually introduced. The country adopted its present [national flag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_Oman "Flag of Oman") in 1995, resembling the previous flag but with a thicker stripe. In 1997, a royal decree was issued granting women the right to vote, and stand for election to the Majlis al-Shura, the [Consultative Assembly of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consultative_Assembly_of_Oman "Consultative Assembly of Oman"). Two women were duly elected to the body. In 2002, voting rights were extended to all citizens over the age of 21, and the first elections to the Consultative Assembly under the new rules were held in 2003. In 2004, the Sultan appointed Oman's first female minister with portfolio, [Sheikha Aisha bint Khalfan bin Jameel al-Sayabiyah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aisha_bint_Khalfan_bin_Jameel "Aisha bint Khalfan bin Jameel"), to the post of National Authority for Industrial Craftsmanship.[^131] Despite these changes, there was little change to the actual political makeup of the government. The Sultan continued to rule by decree. Nearly 100 suspected Islamists were arrested in 2005 and 31 people were convicted of trying to overthrow the government. They were ultimately pardoned in June of the same year.[^16]

Before the [Beijing Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics "2008 Summer Olympics"), Oman became the stop of the Middle East's [torch relay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics_torch_relay "2008 Summer Olympics torch relay") on 14 April 2008, covering 20 kilometres.[^132]

Inspired by the [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring") uprisings that were taking place throughout the region, [protests occurred in Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_Omani_protests "2011 Omani protests") during the early months of 2011. While they did not call for the ousting of the regime, demonstrators demanded political reforms, improved living conditions and the creation of more jobs. They were dispersed by riot police in February 2011. Sultan Qaboos reacted by promising jobs and benefits. In October 2011, elections were held to the Consultative Assembly, to which Sultan Qaboos promised greater powers. The following year, the government began a crackdown on internet criticism. In September 2012, trials began of 'activists' accused of posting "abusive and provocative" criticism of the government online. Six were given jail terms.[^133]

In 2013, Oman achieved its status as the elimination of [malaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaria "Malaria") diagnoses, according to the [World Health Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization "World Health Organization") (WHO).[^134]

Qaboos, at the time the Arab world's longest-serving ruler, died on 10 January 2020.[^135] He was succeeded by his first cousin [Haitham bin Tariq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haitham_bin_Tariq "Haitham bin Tariq").[^136] On 12 January 2021, Haitham named his eldest son, [Theyazin bin Haitham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theyazin_bin_Haitham "Theyazin bin Haitham"), as the country's first [crown prince](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crown_Prince_of_Oman "Crown Prince of Oman") and heir to the throne with an amendment to the [Basic Statute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_Statute_of_Oman "Basic Statute of Oman").[^137]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Elphinstone_Inlet.webp/250px-Elphinstone_Inlet.webp.png)

Topographic map of the Musandam Peninsula, near the Strait of Hormuz, and showing the Elphinstone Inlet and Malcolm's Inlet

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Oman-Oasis.jpg/250px-Oman-Oasis.jpg)

An oasis in Oman

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/SurOman.jpg/250px-SurOman.jpg)

The coast of Sur, Oman

Oman lies between latitudes [16th parallel north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16th_parallel_north "16th parallel north") and [28th parallel north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/28th_parallel_north "28th parallel north"), and longitudes [52nd meridian east](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/52nd_meridian_east "52nd meridian east") and [60th meridian east](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/60th_meridian_east "60th meridian east"). A gravel desert plain covers most of central Oman, with mountain ranges along the north ([Hajar Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hajar_Mountains "Hajar Mountains")) and southeast coast ([Dhofar Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhofar_Mountains "Dhofar Mountains")),[^138] [^139] where the country's main cities are located: the capital city [Muscat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscat,_Oman "Muscat, Oman"), [Sohar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sohar "Sohar") and [Sur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sur,_Oman "Sur, Oman") in the north, and [Salalah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salalah "Salalah") in the south and [Musandam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musandam_Governorate "Musandam Governorate"). Oman's climate is hot and dry in the interior and humid along the coast.

The peninsula of [Musandam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musandam_Peninsula "Musandam Peninsula") (Musandem), strategically located on the [Strait of Hormuz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Hormuz "Strait of Hormuz"), is an [exclave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enclave_and_exclave "Enclave and exclave") separated from the rest of Oman by the [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates").[^140]

[Madha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madha "Madha"), another exclave, is an enclave within UAE territory located halfway between the Musandam Peninsula and the main body of Oman.[^140] Madha, part of the Musandam governorate, covers approximately 75 square kilometres (29 sq mi). Madha's boundary was settled in 1969, with the north-east corner of Madha barely 10 metres (33 ft) from the [Fujairah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fujairah "Fujairah") road. Within the Madha exclave is a UAE [enclave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enclave_and_exclave "Enclave and exclave") called [Nahwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nahwa "Nahwa"), belonging to the [Emirate of Sharjah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Sharjah "Emirate of Sharjah"), situated about 8 kilometres (5.0 mi) west of the town of New Madha, and consisting of about forty houses with a clinic and telephone exchange.[^141]

The central desert of Oman is a source of [meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite "Meteorite") for scientific analysis.[^142]

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Water_stress%2C_top_countries_%282022%29.svg/250px-Water_stress%2C_top_countries_%282022%29.svg.png)

Oman was the fifteenth most water stressed country in the world in 2022.

Like the rest of the Persian Gulf, Oman generally has one of the hottest climates in the world—with summer temperatures in Muscat and northern Oman averaging 30 to 40 °C (86.0 to 104.0 °F).[^143] Oman receives [little rainfall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Oman "Geography of Oman"), with annual rainfall in Muscat averaging 100 mm (3.9 in), occurring mostly in January. In the south, the [Dhofar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhofar "Dhofar") Mountains area near Salalah has a tropical-like climate and receives seasonal rainfall from late June to late September as a result of monsoon winds from the Indian Ocean, leaving the summer air saturated with cool moisture and heavy fog.[^144] Summer temperatures in [Salalah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salalah "Salalah") range from 20 to 30 °C (68.0 to 86.0 °F)—relatively cool compared to northern Oman.[^145]

The mountain areas receive more rainfall, and annual rainfall on the higher parts of the [Jabal Akhdar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jebel_Akhdar_\(Oman\) "Jebel Akhdar (Oman)") probably exceeds 400 millimetres (16 in).[^146] Low temperatures in the mountainous areas leads to snow cover once every few years.[^147] Some parts of the coast, particularly near the island of [Masirah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masirah "Masirah"), sometimes receive no rain at all within the course of a year. The climate is generally very hot, with temperatures reaching around 54 °C (129.2 °F) (peak) in the hot season, from May to September.[^148]

On 26 June 2018, the village of [Qurayyat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qurayyat,_Oman "Qurayyat, Oman") set the record for highest minimum temperature in a 24-hour period, 42.6 °C (108.7 °F).[^149]

In terms of climate action, major challenges remain to be solved, per the United Nations Sustainable Development 2019 index. The per-capita CO <sub>2</sub> emissions from energy and embodied in fossil fuel exports rates are very high, while imported CO <sub>2</sub> emissions and percent of population affected by climate-related disasters are low.[^150]

#### Wadis

Oman possesses many [wadis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wadi "Wadi") (Arabic term for river valley) that can temporarily fill with water when rain occurs.

### Biodiversity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Nakhalfarms.jpg/250px-Nakhalfarms.jpg)

Nakhal palm tree farms in Oman's Al Batinah Region

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/The-Worlds-Most-Isolated-and-Distinct-Whale-Population-Humpback-Whales-of-the-Arabian-Sea-pone.0114162.s001.tif/lossy-page1-250px-The-Worlds-Most-Isolated-and-Distinct-Whale-Population-Humpback-Whales-of-the-Arabian-Sea-pone.0114162.s001.tif.jpg)

Non-migratory Arabian Sea humpback whales off Dhofar

[Desert shrub](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert_shrub "Desert shrub") and desert grass, common in southern Arabia, are found in Oman, but vegetation is sparse in the interior plateau, which is largely [gravel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravel "Gravel") desert. The greater monsoon rainfall in Dhofar and the mountains makes the growth there more luxuriant during summer; [coconut palms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coconut_palm "Coconut palm") grow plentifully on the coastal plains of Dhofar and [frankincense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankincense "Frankincense") is produced in the hills, with abundant [oleander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oleander "Oleander") and varieties of [acacia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acacia "Acacia"). The Hajar Mountains are a distinct [ecoregion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecoregion "Ecoregion"), the highest points in eastern Arabia, with [wildlife](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildlife "Wildlife") that includes the [Arabian tahr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_tahr "Arabian tahr").

[Indigenous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_\(ecology\) "Indigenous (ecology)") [mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mammal "Mammal") include the [leopard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leopard "Leopard"), [hyena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyena "Hyena"), [fox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fox "Fox"), [wolf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf "Wolf"), [hare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hare "Hare"), [oryx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oryx "Oryx") and [ibex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibex "Ibex"). Birds include the vulture, eagle, stork, bustard, [Arabian partridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_partridge "Arabian partridge"), [bee eater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bee-eater "Bee-eater"), falcon and sunbird. In 2001, Oman had nine endangered species of mammals, five endangered types of birds,[^151] and nineteen threatened [plant species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_species "Plant species"). [Decrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decree "Decree") have been passed to protect endangered species, including the [Arabian leopard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_leopard "Arabian leopard"), [Arabian oryx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_oryx "Arabian oryx"), [mountain gazelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain_gazelle "Mountain gazelle"), [goitered gazelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goitered_gazelle "Goitered gazelle"), [Arabian tahr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_tahr "Arabian tahr"), [green sea turtle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_sea_turtle "Green sea turtle"), [hawksbill turtle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawksbill_turtle "Hawksbill turtle") and [olive ridley turtle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olive_ridley_turtle "Olive ridley turtle"). However, the [Arabian Oryx Sanctuary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Oryx_Sanctuary "Arabian Oryx Sanctuary") is the first site ever to be deleted from [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO") 's World Heritage List, following the government's 2007 decision to reduce the site's area by 90% to clear the way for oil prospectors.[^152]

Local and national entities have noted unethical treatment of animals in Oman. In particular, stray dogs (and to a lesser extent, stray cats) are often the victims of torture, abuse or neglect.[^153] The only approved method of decreasing the stray dog population is shooting by police officers. The Oman government has refused to implement a spay and neuter programme or create any animal shelters in the country. Cats, while seen as more acceptable than dogs, are nevertheless also viewed as pests and frequently die of starvation or illness.[^154] [^155]

In recent years, Oman has become a popular spot for [whale watching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whale_watching "Whale watching"), highlighting the critically endangered Arabian [humpback whale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humpback_whale "Humpback whale"), [sperm whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sperm_whale "Sperm whale") and [pygmy blue whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pygmy_blue_whale "Pygmy blue whale").[^156]

## Politics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Oman-Muscat-16-Sultans-Palace-2.JPG/250px-Oman-Muscat-16-Sultans-Palace-2.JPG)

The Sultan's Al Alam Palace in Old Muscat

Oman is a [unitary state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_state "Unitary state") and an [absolute monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy "Absolute monarchy"),[^157] in which all legislative, executive and judiciary power ultimately rests in the hands of the hereditary Sultan. Consequently, [Freedom House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_House "Freedom House") has routinely rated the country "Not Free".[^158]

The sultan is the head of state and directly controls the foreign affairs and defence portfolios.[^159] He has absolute power and issues [laws by decree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_by_decree "Rule by decree").[^160] [^161]

According to [International IDEA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Institute_for_Democracy_and_Electoral_Assistance "International Institute for Democracy and Electoral Assistance") 's Global State of Democracy (GSoD) Indices and Democracy Tracker, Oman performs in the low to mid-range on overall democratic measures, with particular weaknesses in political participation, including electoral participation and civil society.[^162] [^163] [^164]

### Legal system

Oman is an [absolute monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy "Absolute monarchy"), with the Sultan's word having the force of law. The judiciary branch is subordinate to the Sultan. According to Oman's constitution, [Sharia law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharia_law "Sharia law") is one of the sources of legislation. Sharia court departments within the civil court system are responsible for family-law matters, such as divorce and inheritance.

While ultimate power is concentrated in the Sultan [^24] and Oman does not have an official [separation of powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_powers "Separation of powers"),[^24] the late Sultan Qaboos declined to grant the full title Minister of Defence, Minister of Foreign Affairs and Minister of Finance to the ministers exercising those responsibilities, preferring to keep them within the Royal Domain. The current Sultan Haitham has granted the ministers responsible of those portfolios the full titles, whilst elevating the defence portfolio to that of a deputy prime minister.[^24] Since 1970 all legislation has been promulgated through royal decrees, including the 1996 Basic Law.[^24] The Sultan appoints the ministers, the judges, and can grant pardons and commute sentences.[^24] The Sultan's authority is inviolable and the Sultan expects total subordination to his will.[^24]

The administration of justice is highly personalised, with limited due process protections, especially in political and security-related cases.[^165] The [Basic Statute of the State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_Statute_of_Oman "Basic Statute of Oman") [^166] is supposedly the cornerstone of the Omani legal system and it operates as a constitution for the country. The Basic Statute was issued in 1996 and thus far has been amended only twice: in 2011,[^167] in response to [protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_Omani_protests "2011 Omani protests"); and in 2021, to create the position of [Crown Prince of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crown_Prince_of_Oman "Crown Prince of Oman").

Though Oman's legal code theoretically protects civil liberties and personal freedoms, both are regularly ignored by the regime.[^24] Women and children face legal discrimination in many areas.[^24] Women are excluded from certain state benefits, such as housing loans, and are refused equal rights under the personal status law.[^24] Women also experience restrictions on their self-determination in respect to health and reproductive rights.[^24]

The Omani legislature is the bicameral [Council of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Oman "Council of Oman"), consisting of an upper chamber, the [Council of State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_State_\(Oman\) "Council of State (Oman)") (Majlis ad-Dawlah) and a lower chamber, the [Consultative Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consultative_Assembly_\(Oman\) "Consultative Assembly (Oman)") (Majlis al-Shura).[^168] Political parties are banned, as are any affiliations based on religion.[^161] The upper chamber has 71 members, appointed by the Sultan from among prominent Omanis; it has only advisory powers.[^169] The 84 members of the Consultative Assembly are elected by universal suffrage to serve four-year terms.[^169] The members are appointed for three-year terms, which may be renewed once.[^168] The last elections were held on [29 October 2023](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Omani_general_election "2023 Omani general election"), and the next is due in October 2027. Oman's national anthem, *[As-Salam as-Sultani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/As-Salam_as-Sultani "As-Salam as-Sultani")* is dedicated to former Sultan Qaboos.

### Foreign relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Prime_Minister_Keir_Starmer_meets_Haitham_bin_Tariq%2C_the_Sultan_of_Oman_for_a_bilateral_meeting_in_10_Downing_Street%2C_London%2C_United_Kingdom_6_August_2024_-_2.jpg/250px-thumbnail.jpg)

Sultan Haitham bin Tariq with British Prime Minister Keir Starmer, August 2024

Since 1970, Oman has pursued a moderate foreign policy, and has expanded its diplomatic relations dramatically. Oman is among the very few Arab countries that have maintained friendly ties with [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran "Iran").[^172] [^173] [Yusuf bin Alawi bin Abdullah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yusuf_bin_Alawi_bin_Abdullah "Yusuf bin Alawi bin Abdullah") is the Sultanate's Minister Responsible for Foreign Affairs.

Oman allowed the British [Royal Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Navy "Royal Navy") and [Indian Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Navy "Indian Navy") access to the port facilities of [Al Duqm Port & Drydock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Duqm_Port_%26_Drydock "Al Duqm Port & Drydock").[^174]

### Military

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Portsmouth_korvet_Al-Shamikh_Oman_18-10-2011_15-15-51.png/250px-Portsmouth_korvet_Al-Shamikh_Oman_18-10-2011_15-15-51.png)

Royal Navy of Oman Khareef-class corvette, Al-Shamikh

[SIPRI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stockholm_International_Peace_Research_Institute "Stockholm International Peace Research Institute") 's estimation of Oman's military and security expenditure as a percentage of GDP in 2020 was 11 percent, making it the world's highest rate in that year, higher than [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia") (8.4 percent).[^175] Oman's on-average military spending as a percentage of GDP between 2016 and 2018 was around 10 percent, while the world's average during the same period was 2.2 percent.[^176]

Oman's military manpower totalled 44,100 in 2006, including 25,000 men in the army, 4,200 sailors in the navy, and an air force with 4,100 personnel. The [Royal Household](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Household "Royal Household") maintained 5,000 Guards, 1,000 in Special Forces, 150 sailors in the Royal Yacht fleet, and 250 pilots and ground personnel in the Royal Flight squadrons. Oman also maintains a modestly sized paramilitary force of 4,400 men.[^177]

The [Royal Army of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Army_of_Oman "Royal Army of Oman") had 25,000 active personnel in 2006, plus a small contingent of Royal Household troops. Despite a comparative large military spending, it has been relatively slow to modernise its forces. Oman has a relatively limited number of tanks, including 6 [M60A1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M60A1 "M60A1"), 73 [M60A3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M60A3 "M60A3") and 38 [Challenger 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Challenger_2 "Challenger 2") main battle tanks, as well as 37 aging [Scorpion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FV101_Scorpion "FV101 Scorpion") light tanks.[^177]

The [Royal Air Force of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Air_Force_of_Oman "Royal Air Force of Oman") has approximately 4,100 men, with 36 combat aircraft and no armed helicopters. Combat aircraft include 20 aging [Jaguars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SEPECAT_Jaguar "SEPECAT Jaguar"), 12 [Hawk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BAE_Systems_Hawk "BAE Systems Hawk") Mk 203s, 4 Hawk Mk 103s and 12 [PC-9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PC-9 "PC-9") turboprop trainers with a limited combat capability. It has one squadron of 12 [F-16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F-16 "F-16") C/D aircraft. Oman also has 4 [A202-18 Bravos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FFA_AS-202_Bravo "FFA AS-202 Bravo") and 8 [MFI-17B Mushshaqs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PAC_MFI-17_Mushshak "PAC MFI-17 Mushshak").[^177]

The [Royal Navy of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Navy_of_Oman "Royal Navy of Oman") had 4,200 men in 2000, and is headquartered at [Seeb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seeb "Seeb"). It has bases at Ahwi, Ghanam Island, [Mussandam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mussandam "Mussandam") and [Salalah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salalah "Salalah"). In 2006, Oman had ten surface combat vessels. These included two 1,450-ton [*Qahir* class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qahir-class_corvette "Qahir-class corvette") [corvettes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corvette "Corvette"), and eight ocean-going [patrol boats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrol_boat "Patrol boat"). The Omani Navy had one 2,500-ton [*Nasr al Bahr* class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasr_al_Bahr "Nasr al Bahr") LSL (240 troops, 7 tanks) with a helicopter deck. Oman also had at least four [landing craft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landing_craft "Landing craft").[^177] Oman ordered three [*Khareef* class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khareef-class_corvette "Khareef-class corvette") corvettes from the [VT Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VT_Group "VT Group") for £400 million in 2007. They were built at [Portsmouth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portsmouth "Portsmouth").[^178] In 2010 Oman spent US$4.074 billion on military expenditures, 8.5% [of the gross domestic product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_military_expenditure_share_of_GDP "List of countries by military expenditure share of GDP").[^179] The sultanate has a long history of association with the British military and defence industry.[^180] According to [SIPRI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SIPRI "SIPRI"), Oman was the 23rd largest arms importer from 2012 to 2016.[^181]

### Human rights

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Portrait_Of_Dr._Talib_Al_Mamari.jpg/250px-Portrait_Of_Dr._Talib_Al_Mamari.jpg)

Talib Al Mamari, a former member of the Consultative Assembly of Oman, who was arrested in 2013 after participating in a peaceful protest as a mediator. The UN Working Group on Arbitrary Detention (WGAD) deemed his detention arbitrary and a violation of his right to freedom of expression.

Torture methods in use in Oman include [mock execution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mock_execution "Mock execution"), beating, [hooding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hooding "Hooding"), solitary confinement, subjection to extremes of temperature and to constant noise, abuse and humiliation.[^182] [^183] There have been numerous reports of torture and other inhumane forms of punishment perpetrated by Omani security forces on protesters and detainees.[^184] Several prisoners detained in 2012 complained of sleep deprivation, extreme temperatures and solitary confinement.[^185] [Homosexuality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_Oman "LGBT rights in Oman") is criminalised within Oman.[^186]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Mohammed_Al-fazari.jpeg/250px-Mohammed_Al-fazari.jpeg)

Mohammed Al-Fazari, an exiled Omani writer and journalist now living in the UK, is an author whose books are banned in Oman. He is also the founder and EIC of Muwatin. 187

The Omani government decides who can or cannot be a journalist and this permission can be withdrawn at any time.[^188] Censorship and [self-censorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-censorship "Self-censorship") are a constant factor.[^188] Omanis have limited access to political information through the media.[^189] Access to news and information can be problematic: journalists have to be content with news compiled by the official news agency on some issues.[^188] Through a decree by the Sultan, the government has now extended its control over the media to blogs and other websites.[^188] Omanis cannot hold a public meeting without the government's approval.[^188] Omanis who want to set up a non-governmental organisation of any kind need a licence.[^188] The Omani government does not permit the formation of independent [civil society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_society "Civil society") associations.[^184] [Human Rights Watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch "Human Rights Watch") issued in 2016, that an Omani court sentenced three journalists to prison and ordered the permanent closure of their newspaper, over an article that alleged corruption in the judiciary.[^190]

Omani law prohibits criticism of the Sultan and government in any form or medium.[^188] Oman's police do not need [search warrants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Search_warrants "Search warrants") to enter people's homes.[^188] The law does not provide citizens with the right to change their government.[^188] The Sultan retains ultimate authority on all foreign and domestic issues.[^188] Government officials are not subject to financial disclosure laws.[^188] Criticism of government figures and politically objectionable views have been suppressed.[^188] Publication of books is limited and the government restricts their importation and distribution, as with other media products.[^188]

Until 2023, Omani citizens needed government permission to marry foreigners.[^185] In April 2023, the law was changed by a royal decree, allowing Omani citizens to marry foreigners without government permission.[^191] According to HRW, women in Oman face discrimination.[^187]

The plight of [domestic workers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domestic_worker "Domestic worker") in Oman is a taboo subject.[^192] [^193] In 2011, the [Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines "Philippines") government determined that out of all the countries in the Middle East, only Oman and Israel qualify as safe for Filipino migrants.[^194] Migrant workers remained insufficiently protected against exploitation.[^195]

## Economy

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/GDP_per_capita_development_in_Oman.svg/500px-GDP_per_capita_development_in_Oman.svg.png)

Historical development of real GDP per capita in Oman

Oman's Basic Statute of the State expresses in Article 11 that the "national economy is based on justice and the principles of a [free economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_economy "Market economy") ".[^196] By regional standards, Oman has a relatively diversified economy, but remains dependent on oil exports. In terms of monetary value, mineral fuels accounted for 82.2 percent of total product exports in 2018.[^197] Tourism is the fastest-growing industry in Oman. Other sources of income, agriculture and industry, are small in comparison and account for less than 1% of the country's exports, but diversification is seen as a priority by the government. Agriculture, often [subsistence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subsistence "Subsistence") in its character, produces [dates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenix_dactylifera "Phoenix dactylifera"), [limes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lime_\(fruit\) "Lime (fruit)"), [grains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cereal "Cereal") and vegetables, but with less than 1% of the country under [cultivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tillage "Tillage"), Oman is likely to remain a net importer of food.

Oman's socio-economic structure is described as being hyper-centralised [rentier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rentier_state "Rentier state") welfare state.[^198] The largest 10 percent of corporations in Oman are the employers of almost 80 percent of Omani nationals in the private sector. Half of the private sector jobs are classified as elementary. One third of employed Omanis are in the private sector, while the remaining majority are in the public sector.[^199] A hyper-centralised structure produces a monopoly-like economy.[^198]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/MSM_Main.JPG/250px-MSM_Main.JPG)

Muscat Securities Market

Since a slump in oil prices in 1998, Oman has made active plans to diversify its economy and is placing a greater emphasis on other areas of industry, namely tourism and infrastructure. Oman had a 2020 Vision to diversify the economy established in 1995, which targeted a decrease in oil's share to less than 10 percent of GDP by 2020, but it was rendered obsolete in 2011. Oman then established 2040 Vision.[^198] A [free-trade agreement with the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman%E2%80%93United_States_Free_Trade_Agreement "Oman–United States Free Trade Agreement") took effect 1 January 2009, which eliminated tariff barriers on all consumer and industrial products and provided strong protections for foreign businesses investing in Oman.[^200] [Tourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourism_in_Oman "Tourism in Oman"), another source of Oman's revenue, is on the rise.[^201]

Oman's [foreign workers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Migrant_workers_in_the_Gulf_region "Migrant workers in the Gulf region") send an estimated US$10 billion annually to their home states in Asia and Africa, more than half of them earning a monthly wage of less than US$400.[^203] The largest foreign community is from the Indian states of [Kerala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerala "Kerala"), [Tamil Nadu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamil_Nadu "Tamil Nadu"), [Karnataka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karnataka "Karnataka"), [Maharashtra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maharashtra "Maharashtra"), [Gujarat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gujarat "Gujarat") and [Punjab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punjab,_India "Punjab, India"),[^204] representing more than half of the entire workforce in Oman. Salaries for overseas workers are known to be less than those for Omani nationals, though still two to five times higher than for the equivalent job in India.[^203]

In terms of foreign direct investment (FDI), total investments in 2017 exceeded US$24 billion. The highest share of FDI went to the oil and gas sector, which represented around US$13 billion (54.2 percent), followed by [financial intermediation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_intermediary "Financial intermediary"), which represented US$3.66 billion (15.3 percent). FDI is dominated by the United Kingdom with an estimated value of US$11.56 billion (48 percent), followed by the UAE, with US$2.6 billion (10.8 percent), and Kuwait with US$1.1 billion (4.6 percent).[^202]

In 2018, Oman had a budget deficit of 32 percent of total revenue and a government debt-to-GDP ratio of 47.5 percent.[^205] [^206] Oman's military spending to GDP between 2016 and 2018 averaged 10 percent, while the world's average during the same period was 2.2 percent.[^207] Oman's health spending to GDP in 2015 and 2016 averaged 4.3 percent, while the world's average during the same period was 10 percent.[^208] Oman's research and development spending between 2016 and 2017 averaged 0.24 percent, which is significantly lower than the world's average (2.2 percent) during the same period.[^209] Oman's government spending on education to GDP in 2016 was 6.11 percent, while the world's average was 4.8 percent (2015).[^210]

### Oil and gas

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Sohar_flickr01.jpg/250px-Sohar_flickr01.jpg)

Petrochemical tanks in Sohar

Oman's proven reserves of petroleum total about 5.5 billion barrels, 25th largest in the world.[^172] Oil is extracted and processed by [Petroleum Development Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum_Development_Oman "Petroleum Development Oman") (PDO), with proven oil reserves holding approximately steady, although oil production has been declining.[^215] [^216] The [Ministry of Energy and Minerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Energy_and_Minerals_\(Oman\) "Ministry of Energy and Minerals (Oman)") is responsible for all oil and gas infrastructure and projects in Oman.[^217] Following the [1970s energy crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1970s_energy_crisis "1970s energy crisis"), Oman doubled their oil output between 1979 and 1985.[^218]

In 2018, oil and gas represented 71 percent of the government's revenues.[^205] In 2016, oil and gas share of the government's revenue represented 72 percent.[^219] The government's reliance on oil and gas as a source of income dropped by 1 percent from 2016 to 2018. Oil and gas sector represented 30.1 percent of the nominal GDP in 2017.[^220]

Between 2000 and 2007, production fell by more than 26%, from 972,000 to 714,800 [barrels per day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barrels_per_day "Barrels per day").[^221] Production has recovered to 816,000 barrels in 2009, and 930,000 barrels per day in 2012.[^221] Oman's natural gas reserves are estimated at 849.5 billion cubic metres, ranking 28th in the world, and production in 2008 was about 24 billion cubic metres per year.[^172]

In September 2019, Oman was confirmed to become the first Middle Eastern country to host the [International Gas Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Gas_Union "International Gas Union") Research Conference (IGRC 2020). This 16th iteration of the event will be held between 24 and 26 February 2020, in collaboration with [Oman LNG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman_LNG "Oman LNG"), under the auspices of the [Ministry of Energy and Minerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Energy_and_Minerals_\(Oman\) "Ministry of Energy and Minerals (Oman)").[^222]

### Industry, innovation and infrastructure

In industry, innovation and infrastructure, Oman is still faced with "significant challenges", as per United Nations Sustainable Development Goals index, as of 2019. Oman has scored high on the rates of internet use, mobile broadband subscriptions, logistics performance and on the average of top 3 university rankings. Meanwhile, Oman scored low on the rate of scientific and technical publications and on research & development spending.[^150] Oman's manufacturing value added to GDP rate in 2016 was 8.4 percent, which is lower than the average in the Arab world (9.8 percent) and world average (15.6 percent). In terms of research & development expenditures to GDP, Oman's share was on average 0.20 percent between 2011 and 2015, while the world's average during the same period was 2.11 percent.[^223] The majority of firms in Oman operate in the oil and gas, construction and trade sectors.[^224]

Oman is refurbishing and expanding the ports infrastructure in Muscat, Duqm, Sohar and Salalah to expand tourism, local production and export shares. Oman is also expanding its downstream operations by constructing a refinery and petrochemical plant in Duqm with a 230,000 barrels per day capacity projected for completion by 2021.[^202] The majority of industrial activity in Oman takes place in eight industrial states and four free-zones. The industrial activity is mainly focused on mining-and-services, petrochemicals and construction materials.[^224] The largest employers in the private-sector are the construction, wholesale-and-retail and manufacturing sectors, respectively. Construction accounts for nearly 48 percent of the total labour force, followed by wholesale-and-retail, which accounts for around 15 percent of total employment and manufacturing, which accounts for around 12 percent of employment in the private sector. The percentage of Omanis employed in the construction and manufacturing sectors is nevertheless low, as of 2011 statistics.[^199]

Oman, as per [Global Innovation Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Innovation_Index "Global Innovation Index") (2019) report, scores "below expectations" in innovation relative to countries classified under high income.[^226] In 2025, Oman ranked 69th out of 144 countries in innovation index, which takes into consideration factors, such as, political environment, education, infrastructure and business sophistication.[^227] [^228] Innovation, technology-based growth and economic diversification are hindered by an economic growth that relies on infrastructure expansion, which heavily depends on a high percentage of 'low-skilled' and 'low-wage' foreign labour. Another challenge to innovation is the [Dutch disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_disease "Dutch disease") phenomenon, which creates an oil and gas investment lock-in, while relying heavily on imported products and services in other sectors. Such a locked-in system hinders local business growth and global competitiveness in other sectors, and thus impedes economic diversification.[^224] The inefficiencies and bottlenecks in business operations that are a result of heavy dependence on natural resources and 'addiction' to imports in Oman suggest a 'factor-driven economy'.[^199] A third hindrance to innovation in Oman is an economic structure that is heavily dependent on few large firms, while granting few opportunities for SMEs to enter the market, which impedes healthy market-share competition between firms.[^224] The ratio of patent applications per million people was 0.35 in 2016 and the [MENA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MENA "MENA") region average was 1.50, while the 'high-income' countries' average was approximately 48.0 during the same year.[^229] Oman was ranked 74th in the [Global Innovation Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Innovation_Index "Global Innovation Index") in 2024.[^230]

### Agriculture and fishing

Oman's fishing industry contributed 0.78 percent to the GDP in 2016. Fish exports between 2000 and 2016 grew from US$144 million to US$172 million, an increase of 19.4 percent. The main importer of Omani fish in 2016 was [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam"), which imported almost US$80 million (46.5 percent) in value,[^231] and the second biggest importer was the [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates"), which imported around US$26 million (15 percent). The other main importers are Saudi Arabia, [Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil "Brazil") and [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China"). Oman's consumption of fish is almost two times the world's average. The ratio of exported fish to total fish captured in tons fluctuated between 49 and 61 percent between 2006 and 2016. Omani strengths in the fishing industry comes from having a good market system, a long coastline (3,165 km) and wide water area. However, Oman lacks sufficient infrastructure, research and development, quality and safety monitoring, together with a limited contribution by the fishing industry to GDP.[^232]

[Dates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Date_\(fruit\) "Date (fruit)") represent 80 percent of all fruit crop production. Further, date farms employ 50 percent of the total agricultural area in the country. Oman's estimated production of dates in 2016 is 350,000 tons, making it the 9th largest producer of dates. Oman's total export of dates was US$12.6 million in 2016, almost equivalent to Oman's total imported value of dates, which was US$11.3 million in 2016. The main importer is India (around 60 percent of all imports). Oman's date exports remained steady between 2006 and 2016. Oman is considered to have good infrastructure for date production and support provision to cultivation and marketing, but lacks innovation in farming and cultivation, industrial coordination in the supply chain and encounter high losses of unused dates.[^232]

### Tourism

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Bustan-palace.jpg/250px-Bustan-palace.jpg)

Al-Bustan Palace Hotel, Muscat

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Wahiba_Sands_%2833%29.jpg/250px-Wahiba_Sands_%2833%29.jpg)

Sharqiya Sands

Tourism in Oman has grown considerably recently, and it is expected to be one of the largest industries in the country.[^233] The World Travel & Tourism Council stated that Oman is the fastest growing tourism destination in the Middle East.[^234]

Tourism contributed 2.8 percent to the Omani GDP in 2016. It grew from RO 505 million (US$1.3 billion) in 2009 to RO 719 million (US$1.8 billion) in 2017 (+42.3 percent growth). Citizens of the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC), including Omanis who are residing outside of Oman, represent the highest ratio of all tourists visiting Oman, estimated to be 48 percent. The second highest number of visitors come from other Asian countries, who account for 17 percent of the total number of visitors.[^232] A challenge to tourism development in Oman is the reliance on the government-owned firm, Omran, as a key actor to develop the tourism sector, which potentially creates a market [barrier-to-entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barrier_to_entry "Barrier to entry") of private-sector actors and a [crowding out](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crowding_out_\(economics\) "Crowding out (economics)") effect. Another key issue to the tourism sector is deepening the understanding of the ecosystem and biodiversity in Oman to guarantee their protection and preservation.[^224]

[Ecotourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecotourism "Ecotourism") is a growing segment of Omani tourism.[^235] One site in particular – [Ras al-Jinz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ras_al-Jinz "Ras al-Jinz"), also known as "Turtle Beach" – is a popular destination due to the annual nesting of the critically endangered [Hawksbill Turtle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawksbill_sea_turtle "Hawksbill sea turtle"), the endangered [Green Turtle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_sea_turtle "Green sea turtle"), the [Olive Ridley Turtle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olive_ridley_sea_turtle "Olive ridley sea turtle"), and the [Loggerhead Turtle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loggerhead_sea_turtle "Loggerhead sea turtle").[^236]

Oman has one of the most diverse environments in the Middle East with various tourist attractions and is particularly well known for adventure and [cultural tourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_tourism "Cultural tourism").[^201] [^237] [Muscat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscat "Muscat"), the capital of Oman, was named the second best city to visit in the world in 2012 by the travel guide publisher [Lonely Planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lonely_Planet "Lonely Planet").[^238] Muscat also was chosen as the Capital of Arab Tourism of 2012.[^239]

In November 2019, Oman made the rule of [visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Travel_visa "Travel visa") on arrival an exception and introduced the concept of e-visa for tourists from all nationalities. Under the new laws, visitors were required to apply for the visa in advance.[^240]

By 2020, Oman's population exceeded 4.5 million.[^243] The [total fertility rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_fertility_rate "Total fertility rate") in 2020 was estimated to be 2.8 children born per woman; this rate has been rapidly decreasing in recent years.[^244] About half of the population lives in [Muscat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscat,_Oman "Muscat, Oman") and the Batinah coastal plain northwest of the capital. Omanis of Balochi descent typically use the surname/ [nisba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nisba_\(onomastics\) "Nisba (onomastics)") *[Al-Balushi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Balushi "Al-Balushi")*.[^249] [Gwadar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gwadar "Gwadar"), a region of Balochistan, was a Colony of Oman for more than a century. In the 1960s, Pakistan took over the land, with many Omanis still there.[^250]

### Languages

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Sultan_Qaboos_Highway.jpg/330px-Sultan_Qaboos_Highway.jpg)

Arabic and English road sign in Oman

[Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic") is the official and most widely spoken language of Oman. It belongs to the [Semitic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semitic_languages "Semitic languages") branch of the [Afroasiatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages "Afroasiatic languages") family.[^196] There are several [dialects of Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialects_of_Arabic "Dialects of Arabic") spoken, all part of the [Peninsular Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peninsular_Arabic "Peninsular Arabic") family: [Dhofari Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhofari_Arabic "Dhofari Arabic") (also known as Dhofari, Zofari) is spoken in [Salalah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salalah "Salalah") and the surrounding coastal regions (the [Dhofar Governorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhofar "Dhofar"));[^251] [Gulf Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Arabic "Gulf Arabic") is spoken in parts bordering the [UAE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates"); whereas [Omani Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omani_Arabic "Omani Arabic"), distinct from the Gulf Arabic of eastern Arabia and Bahrain, is spoken in Central Oman, although with recent oil wealth and mobility has spread over other parts of the Sultanate.

According to the U.S. [Central Intelligence Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency "Central Intelligence Agency") (CIA), the main languages spoken in Oman besides Arabic are English, [Malayalam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malayalam "Malayalam"), [Baluchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balochi_language "Balochi language") ([Southern Baluchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makrani_dialect "Makrani dialect")), [Urdu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urdu "Urdu"), [Tamil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamil_language "Tamil language"), [Bengali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bengali_language "Bengali language"), [Hindi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindi "Hindi"), [Tulu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulu_language "Tulu language") and various other Indian languages, due to the influx of Pakistani migrants and foreign workers.[^172] English is widely spoken in the business community and is taught at school from an early age. Almost all signs and writings appear in both Arabic and English at tourist sites.[^201] [Sindhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sindhi_language "Sindhi language") language and its various dialects like ([Jadgali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jadgali_language "Jadgali language"), [Lawati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luwati_language "Luwati language"), [Maimani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memoni_language "Memoni language"), [Kutchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kutchi_language "Kutchi language"), Al Saigh) are also spoken in Oman.[^252] [^253] [^254] [^255] Additionally, [Swahili](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swahili "Swahili") is sometimes spoken among Omanis of [Zanzibari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zanzibar "Zanzibar") descent.[^19]

Today, the [Mehri language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mehri_language "Mehri language") is limited in its distribution to the area around [Salalah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salalah "Salalah"), in [Zafar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zafar,_Yemen "Zafar, Yemen") and westward into the Yemen. But until the 18th or 19th century it was spoken further north, perhaps into Central Oman.[^256] [^257] Endangered indigenous languages in Oman include [Kumzari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kumzari_language "Kumzari language"), [Bathari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bathari_language "Bathari language"), [Harsusi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harsusi_language "Harsusi language"), [Hobyot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hobyot_language "Hobyot language"), [Jibbali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jibbali_language "Jibbali language") and [Mehri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mehri_language "Mehri language").[^258] [Omani Sign Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omani_Sign_Language "Omani Sign Language") is the language of the deaf community.

### Religion

[Religion in Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_in_Oman "Religion in Oman") (2020) [^259]

1. [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Oman "Islam in Oman") (85.9%)
2. [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_in_Oman "Christianity in Oman") (6.40%)
3. [Hinduism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism_in_Oman "Hinduism in Oman") (5.70%)
4. [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism_in_the_Middle_East "Buddhism in the Middle East") (0.80%)

6. Other (0.20%)

Even though the Oman government does not keep statistics on religious affiliation, statistics from the US's Central Intelligence Agency state that adherents of [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Oman "Islam in Oman") are in the majority at 85.9 percent, while 6.4 percent are [Christians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_in_Oman "Christianity in Oman"), 5.7 percent [Hindus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism_in_Oman "Hinduism in Oman"), 0.8 percent [Buddhists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism_in_the_Middle_East "Buddhism in the Middle East"), and fewer than 0.1 percent are [Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Oman "History of the Jews in Oman"); members of other religious affiliations comprise 1 percent and the unaffiliated 0.2 percent.

Virtually all Omanis are [Muslims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslims "Muslims"); these predominantly follow the [Ibadi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibadi "Ibadi") [school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_schools_and_branches "Islamic schools and branches") of Islam,[^260] [^261] followed by the [Shafi\`i](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shafi%60i "Shafi`i") school of [Sunni Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunni_Islam "Sunni Islam") followed in Northern Oman, particularly in the [Al Batinah North](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Batinah_North_Governorate "Al Batinah North Governorate") and [Al Batinah South](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Batinah_South_Governorate "Al Batinah South Governorate") governorates, with a very small minority following the [Twelver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twelver "Twelver") school of [Shia Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shia_Islam "Shia Islam") practiced by Omani [Ajam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajam "Ajam") tribes. Most non-Muslims in Oman are foreign workers, variously adhering to [Jainism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jainism "Jainism"), [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism"), [Zoroastrianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoroastrianism "Zoroastrianism"), [Sikhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sikhism "Sikhism"), [Hinduism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism"), or [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity"). Christian communities are centred in the major urban areas of [Muscat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscat,_Oman "Muscat, Oman"), [Sohar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sohar "Sohar") and [Salalah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salalah "Salalah"). These include [Catholic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church"), [Eastern Orthodox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church "Eastern Orthodox Church") and various [Protestant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestant "Protestant") congregations, organising along linguistic and ethnic lines. More than 50 different Christian groups, fellowships and assemblies are active in the Muscat metropolitan area, formed by migrant workers from Southeast Asia.

There are also communities of ethnic Indian Hindus and Christians, as well as a small Sikh community.[^262]

### Education

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Dhofar_University.jpg/250px-Dhofar_University.jpg)

Dhofar University in Salalah

Oman scored high as of 2019 on the percentage of students who complete lower secondary school and on the literacy rate between the age of 15 and 24, 99.7 percent and 98.7 percent, respectively. However, Oman's net primary school enrolment rate in 2019, which is 94.1 percent, is rated as "challenges remain" by the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (UNSDG) standard. Oman's overall evaluation in quality of education, according to UNSDG, is 94.8 ("challenges remain") as of 2019.[^150]

Oman's higher education produces a surplus in humanitdhoies and liberal arts, while it produces an insufficient number in technical and scientific fields and required skill-sets to meet the market demand.[^224] Further, sufficient human capital creates a business environment that can compete with, partner or attract foreign firms. Accreditation standards and mechanisms with a quality control that focuses on input assessments, rather than output, are areas of improvement in Oman, according to the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014 report.[^224] The transformation Index BTI 2018 report on Oman recommends that the education curriculum should focus more on the "promotion of personal initiative and critical perspective".[^198] Oman was ranked 84th in the [Global Innovation Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Innovation_Index "Global Innovation Index") in 2020, down from 80th in 2019.[^263] [^264] [^265] [^266]

The adult literacy rate in 2010 was 86.9 percent.[^267]

According to the [Webometrics Ranking of World Universities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Webometrics_Ranking_of_World_Universities "Webometrics Ranking of World Universities"), the top-ranking universities in the country are [Sultan Qaboos University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan_Qaboos_University "Sultan Qaboos University") (1678th worldwide), Dhofar University (6011th) and the [University of Nizwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Nizwa "University of Nizwa") (6093rd).[^268]

### Health

Since 2003, Oman's undernourished share of the population has dropped from 11.7 percent to 5.4 percent in 2016, but the rate remains high: double the level of high-income economies (2.7 percent) in 2016.[^269] The UNSDG targets zero hunger by 2030.[^270] Oman's coverage of essential health services in 2015 was 77 percent, which is relatively higher than the world's average of approximately 54 percent during the same year, but lower than high-income economies' level (83 percent) in 2015.[^271]

Since 1995, the percentage of Omani children who receive key vaccines has consistently been very high (above 99 percent). As for road incident death rates, Oman's rate has been decreasing since 1990, from 98.9 per 100,000 individuals to 47.1 per 100,000 in 2017, however, the rate remains significantly above average, which was 15.8 per 100,000 in 2017.[^272] Oman's health spending to GDP between 2015 and 2016 averaged 4.3 percent, while the world's average during the same period averaged 10 percent.[^208]

As for mortality due to air pollution (household and ambient air pollution), Oman's rate was 53.9 per 100,000 population as of 2016.[^273] However, in 2019 the WHO ranked Oman as the least polluted country in the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world"), with a score of 37.7 in the [pollution index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_Pollution_Index "Air Pollution Index"). The country ranked 112th in Asia among the list of highest polluted countries.[^274]

Life expectancy at birth in Oman was estimated to be 76.1 years in 2010.[^275] As of 2010, there were an estimated 2.1 physicians and 2.1 hospital beds per 1,000 people.[^275] In 1993, 89 percent of the population had access to health care services. In 2000, 99 percent of the population had access to health care services.[^276] In 2000, Oman's health system was ranked number 8 by the WHO.[^277]

### Currency

The **Omani rial** (Arabic: ريال عُماني; ISO code: **OMR**) is the official currency of the Sultanate of Oman. Issued by the [Central Bank of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Bank_of_Oman "Central Bank of Oman") (CBO), the rial is subdivided into **1,000 baisa** (also written *baiza*, بيسة) — making it one of only a handful of currencies in the world divided into 1,000 rather than 100 subunits. As of 2025, the Omani rial is the **third-highest-valued currency unit in the world**, after the Kuwaiti dinar and the Bahraini dinar.

## Culture

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/DhowMuscat.jpg/250px-DhowMuscat.jpg)

The traditional dhow, an enduring symbol of Oman 278

Outwardly, Oman shares many of the cultural characteristics of its Arab neighbours, particularly those in the [Gulf Cooperation Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Cooperation_Council "Gulf Cooperation Council").[^279] Despite these similarities, important factors make Oman unique in the Middle East.[^279] These result as much from geography and history as from culture and economics.[^279] The relatively recent and artificial nature of the [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)") in Oman makes it difficult to describe a national culture;[^279] however, sufficient cultural heterogeneity exists within its national boundaries to make Oman distinct from other Arab States of the Persian Gulf.[^279] Oman's cultural diversity is greater than that of its Arab neighbours, given its historical expansion to the Swahili Coast and the Indian Ocean.[^279]

Oman has a long tradition of shipbuilding, as maritime travel played a major role in the Omanis' ability to stay in contact with the civilisations of the ancient world. [Sur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sur,_Oman "Sur, Oman") was one of the most famous shipbuilding cities of the Indian Ocean. An [al Ghanja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghanjah "Ghanjah") ship takes one whole year to build. Other types of Omani ship include As Sunbouq and Al Badan.[^280]

### Dress

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Khanjar.jpg/250px-Khanjar.jpg)

Khanjar, the traditional dagger of Oman ( c. 1924 )

The male [national dress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_dress "National dress") in Oman consists of the *[dishdasha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dishdasha "Dishdasha")*, a simple, ankle-length, collarless gown with long sleeves.[^281] Worn by nearly every Omani male, it is most frequently white in colour but may also appear in a variety of other colours. Its main adornment, a [tassel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tassel "Tassel") (*furakha or farakh*) sewn into the neckline, can be impregnated with perfume.[^282] Underneath the dishdasha, men wear a plain, wide strip of cloth wrapped around the body from the waist down. The most noted regional differences in dishdasha designs are the style with which they are embroidered, which varies according to age group.[^281] On extremely formal occasions a black or beige cloak called a [*bisht*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bisht_\(clothing\) "Bisht (clothing)") may cover the dishdasha. The embroidery edging the cloak is often in silver or gold thread and it is intricate in detail.[^282]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Sultanate_of_Oman_Display_Brisbane_Mall-41_%286930110464%29.jpg/250px-Sultanate_of_Oman_Display_Brisbane_Mall-41_%286930110464%29.jpg)

An Omani man wearing a beige dishdasha, a Musar with a Khanjar worn at his waist, holding an assa.

Omani men wear two types of headdress:

- the *ghutra*, also called "Musar" a square piece of woven wool or cotton fabric of a single colour, decorated with various embroidered patterns. Occasionally, an accessory similar to the [Agal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agal_\(accessory\) "Agal (accessory)") called the [Khizam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khizam "Khizam") is tied upon the Musar.[^283]
- the *kummah*, a cap that is the head dress worn during leisure hours.[^281]

Some men carry the *assa*, a stick, which can have practical uses or is simply used as an accessory during formal events. Omani men, on the whole, wear sandals on their feet.[^282]

The [Khanjar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khanjar "Khanjar") (dagger) forms part of the national dress and men wear the [Khanjar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khanjar "Khanjar") on all formal public occasions and festivals.[^281] It is traditionally worn at the waist. Sheaths may vary from simple covers to ornate silver or gold-decorated pieces.[^282] A depiction of a Khanjar appears on the national flag.[^281]

Omani women wear eye-catching national costumes, with distinctive regional variations. All costumes incorporate vivid colours and vibrant embroidery and decorations. The Omani women's traditional costume comprises several garments: the *kandoorah*, which is a long tunic whose sleeves or *radoon* are adorned with hand-stitched embroidery of various designs. The *dishdasha* is worn over a pair of loose-fitting trousers, tight at the ankles, known as a *[sirwal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirwal "Sirwal")*. Women also wear a head shawl most commonly referred to as the *lihaf*.[^284]

As of 2014 women reserve wearing their traditional dress for special occasions, and instead wear a loose black cloak called an *[abaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abaya "Abaya")* over their personal choice of clothing, whilst in some regions, particularly amongst the Bedouin, the *[burqa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burqa "Burqa")* is still worn.[^284] Women wear *[hijab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hijab "Hijab")*, and though some women cover their faces and hands, most do not. The Sultan has forbidden the covering of faces in public office.[^278]

### Art

Traditional art in Oman stems from its long heritage of material culture. Art movements in the 20th century reveal that the art scene in Oman began with early practices that included a range of tribal handicrafts and self-portraiture in painting since the 1960s.[^285] However, since the inclusion of several Omani artists in international collections, art exhibitions, and events, such Alia Al Farsi, the first Omani artist to show at the last [Venice Biennale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venice_Biennale "Venice Biennale") and Radhika Khimji, the first Omani artist to exhibit at both the [Marrakesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arts_in_Marrakech_\(AiM\)_International_Biennale "Arts in Marrakech (AiM) International Biennale") and [Haiti Ghetto biennale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghetto_Biennale "Ghetto Biennale"), Oman's position as a newcomer to the contemporary art scene in recent years has been more important for Oman's international exposure.[^286]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Aflaj_Gallery_in_The_National_Museum_Oman%2C.jpg/330px-Aflaj_Gallery_in_The_National_Museum_Oman%2C.jpg)

Ancient irrigation system and water channels. 287 Aflaj Gallery, The National Museum of Oman.

Bait Muzna Gallery is the first art gallery in Oman. Established in 2000 by Sayyida Susan Al Said, Bait Muzna has served as a platform for emerging Omani artists to showcase their talent and place themselves on the wider art scene. In 2016, Bait Muzna opened a second space in [Salalah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salalah "Salalah") to branch out and support [art film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_film "Art film") and the [digital art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_art "Digital art") scene. The gallery has been primarily active as an art consultancy.[^286] [^288] The Omani Society for Fine Arts, established in 1993, offers educational programmes, workshops and artist grants for practitioners across varied disciplines.[^286]

The Sultanate's flagship cultural institution, the [National Museum of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Museum_\(Oman\) "National Museum (Oman)"), opened on 30 July 2016 with 14 permanent galleries. It showcases national heritage from the earliest human settlement in Oman two million years ago through to the present day. The museum takes a further step by presenting information on the material in [Arabic Braille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_Braille "Arabic Braille") script for the visually impaired, the first museum to do this in the Gulf region.[^286] [Bait Al Zubair Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bait_Al_Zubair "Bait Al Zubair") is a private, family-funded museum that opened its doors to the public in 1998. In 1999, the museum received [Sultan Qaboos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qaboos_bin_Said_al_Said "Qaboos bin Said al Said") ' Award for Architectural Excellence. Bait Al Zubair displays the family's collection of Omani artifacts.[^289]

### Music and cinema

The [Music of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_of_Oman "Music of Oman") is extremely diverse due to Oman's imperial legacy. There are over 130 different forms of traditional Omani songs and dances, with the Oman Centre for Traditional Music being created in 1984 to preserve them.[^290] All of Oman's traditional music is sustained by oral transmission between generations, and each region has its own unique forms. There are songs for special occasions, songs for [seafarers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sailor "Sailor"), [fishermen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fisherman "Fisherman"), [Bedouins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bedouin "Bedouin") and mountain farmers. Traditional songs originating from the desert are often in praise of the [camel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camel "Camel"), which has been described in the [Holy Quran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quran "Quran"). Al Taghrud, sung while riding camels, and Al Taariq, a Bedouin song with two singers taking turns to sing verses, are two of the best examples of traditional desert songs.[^291]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/01.13.SFLF.WDC.23jun05_%2823320043%29.jpg/250px-01.13.SFLF.WDC.23jun05_%2823320043%29.jpg)

An Omani Razfa dance

Omani traditional dances include Al-Razha, a traditional war dance characterised by its use of the sword and its exchange of poetry between men.[^292] [Al Razfa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yowlah "Yowlah"), also known as the Yowlah, is performed by men, where two rows form to create a dual chorus that delivers chants and poetry in an antiphonal style, accompanied by drums and other instruments. The music and poetry performed during the dance is referred to as *Harbiya* music, with Omani Harbiya groups frequently performing at weddings, cultural festivals, national celebrations, and community gatherings.[^293]

In 1985, Sultan Qaboos founded the Royal Oman Symphony Orchestra. Instead of engaging foreign musicians, he decided to establish an orchestra made up of Omanis.[^294] On 1 July 1987 at the Al Bustan Palace Hotel's Oman Auditorium the Royal Oman Symphony Orchestra gave its inaugural concert.[^295] In popular music, a seven-minute music video about Oman went viral, achieving 500,000 views on YouTube within 10 days of being released on YouTube in November 2015. The a cappella production features three of the region's most popular talents: Kahliji musician Al Wasmi, Omani poet Mazin Al-Haddabi and actress Buthaina Al Raisi.[^296]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Sur-Cinema_%283%29.JPG/250px-Sur-Cinema_%283%29.JPG)

Cinema in Sur

The [cinema of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cinema_of_Oman "Cinema of Oman") is very small, there being only one Omani film *Al-Boom* (2006) as of 2007. Oman Arab Cinema Company LLC is the single largest motion picture exhibitor chain in Oman.[^297]

### Media

The government has continuously held a monopoly on television in Oman. [Oman TV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultanate_of_Oman_Television "Sultanate of Oman Television") is the only state-owned national television channel broadcaster in Oman. Oman TV broadcasts four HD channels, including Oman TV General, Oman TV Sport, Oman TV Live and Oman TV Cultural.[^298] Although private ownership of radio and television stations is permitted, Oman has only one privately owned television channel.[^299] Majan TV is the first private TV channel in Oman. It began broadcasting in January 2009. However, Majan TV's official channel website was last updated in early 2010.[^300] The public has access to foreign broadcasts since the use of satellite receivers is allowed.[^299] [^301]

Oman Radio is the first and only state-owned radio channel.[^299] It began broadcasting on 30 July 1970.[^302] It operates both Arabic and English networks. Other private channels include Hala FM, Hi FM, Al-Wisal, Virgin Radio Oman FM and Merge. In early 2018, Muscat Media Group (MMG) launched a new private radio station.[^303] [^304] [^305]

Oman has nine main newspapers, five in Arabic and four in English.[^306]

The media landscape in Oman has been continuously described as restrictive, censored, and subdued.[^307] The Ministry of Information censors politically, culturally, or sexually offensive material in domestic or foreign media. The press freedom group [Reporters Without Borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reporters_Without_Borders "Reporters Without Borders") ranked the country 127th out of 180 countries on its 2018 World Press Freedom Index. In 2016, the government drew international criticism for suspending the newspaper *[Azamn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azamn "Azamn")* and arresting three journalists after a report on corruption in the country's judiciary. *Azamn* was not allowed to reopen in 2017 although an appeal court ruled in late 2016 that the paper can resume operating.[^308]

### Cuisine

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Traditional_Omani_Food.jpg/250px-Traditional_Omani_Food.jpg)

Traditional Omani food

Omani cuisine is diverse and has been influenced by many cultures. Omanis usually eat their main daily meal at midday, while the evening meal is lighter. During [Ramadan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramadan "Ramadan"), dinner is typically served after the [Taraweeh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taraweeh "Taraweeh") prayers, sometimes as late as 11 pm.

Arsia, a festival meal served during celebrations, consists of mashed rice and meat (sometimes chicken). Another popular festival meal, shuwa, consists of meat cooked very slowly (sometimes for up to 2 days) in an underground clay oven. Fish is often used in main dishes too, and the [kingfish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_trevally "Giant trevally") is a popular ingredient. Mashuai is a meal consisting of a whole spit-roasted kingfish served with lemon rice. Rukhal bread is a thin, round bread eaten at any meal, typically served with Omani honey for breakfast or crumbled over curry for dinner. The Omani [halwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halva "Halva") is a very popular sweet, consisting of cooked raw sugar with nuts. There are many different flavours, the most popular ones being black halwa (original) and saffron halwa. Halwa is considered a symbol of Omani hospitality, traditionally served with coffee. As is the case with most [Arab states of the Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_states_of_the_Persian_Gulf "Arab states of the Persian Gulf"), alcohol is only available over the counter to non-Muslims.

### Sports

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/MuscatGulfCup2.jpg/250px-MuscatGulfCup2.jpg)

Oman hosted and won the 19th Arabian Gulf Cup

In October 2004, the Omani government set up a Ministry of Sports Affairs to replace the General Organisation for Youth, Sports and Cultural Affairs. The [19th Arabian Gulf Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/19th_Arabian_Gulf_Cup "19th Arabian Gulf Cup") took place in [Muscat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscat,_Oman "Muscat, Oman"), from 4 to 17 January 2009 and was won by the [Omani national football team](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman_national_football_team "Oman national football team"). The [23rd Arabian Gulf Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/23rd_Arabian_Gulf_Cup "23rd Arabian Gulf Cup") that took place in [Kuwait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuwait "Kuwait"), from 22 December 2017 until 5 January 2018 with Oman winning their second title, defeating the [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates_national_football_team "United Arab Emirates national football team") in the final.

Oman's traditional sports are [dhow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhow "Dhow") racing, [horse racing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horse_racing "Horse racing"), [camel racing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camel_racing "Camel racing"), [bull fighting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bull_fighting "Bull fighting") and [falconry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falconry "Falconry").[^309] Association football, [basketball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basketball "Basketball"), [waterskiing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waterskiing "Waterskiing") and [sandboarding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandboarding "Sandboarding") [^310] are among the sports that have emerged quickly and gained popularity among the younger generation.[^309] Oman, along with [Fujairah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fujairah "Fujairah") in the [UAE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UAE "UAE"), are the only regions in the Middle East that have a variant of [bullfighting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bullfighting "Bullfighting"), known as 'bull-butting', organised within their territories.[^311] Al-Batena area in Oman is specifically prominent for such events.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Flickr_-_tpower1978_-_World_Cup_Qualifiers_%287%29.jpg/250px-Flickr_-_tpower1978_-_World_Cup_Qualifiers_%287%29.jpg)

2010 FIFA World Cup Qualifiers Round 3 match between Oman and Japan at the Royal Oman Police Stadium on 7 June 2008 in Muscat, Oman

The [Oman Olympic Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman_Olympic_Committee "Oman Olympic Committee") played a major part in organising the highly successful 2003 Olympic Days, which were of great benefit to the sports associations, clubs, and young participants. The [football](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_football "Association football") association took part, along with the [handball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handball "Handball"), [basketball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basketball "Basketball"), [rugby union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rugby_union "Rugby union"), [field hockey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_hockey "Field hockey"), [volleyball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volleyball "Volleyball"), [athletics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_of_athletics "Sport of athletics"), swimming and [tennis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tennis "Tennis") associations. In 2010 Muscat hosted the [2010 Asian Beach Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Asian_Beach_Games "2010 Asian Beach Games"). Oman featured a men's national team in [beach volleyball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beach_volleyball "Beach volleyball") that competed at the [2018–2020 AVC Beach Volleyball Continental Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2018%E2%80%932020_AVC_Beach_Volleyball_Continental_Cup "2018–2020 AVC Beach Volleyball Continental Cup").[^312]

Oman also hosts tennis tournaments each year. The [Sultan Qaboos Sports Complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan_Qaboos_Sports_Complex "Sultan Qaboos Sports Complex") stadium contains a 50-metre swimming pool that is used for international tournaments. The Tour of Oman, a professional cycling 6-day stage race, takes place in February. Oman hosted the Asian [2011 FIFA Beach Soccer World Cup qualifiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_FIFA_Beach_Soccer_World_Cup_qualification_\(AFC\) "2011 FIFA Beach Soccer World Cup qualification (AFC)"), where 11 teams competed for three spots at the [FIFA World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FIFA_Beach_Soccer_World_Cup "FIFA Beach Soccer World Cup"). Oman hosted the Men's and Women's [2012 Beach Handball World Championships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Beach_Handball_World_Championships "2012 Beach Handball World Championships") at the Millennium Resort in Mussanah, from 8 to 13 July.[^313] The first " [El Clasico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Cl%C3%A1sico "El Clásico") " to be played outside of Spain, was played on 14 March 2014, at the [Sultan Qaboos Sports Complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan_Qaboos_Sports_Complex "Sultan Qaboos Sports Complex").[^314]

Oman has competed repeatedly for a position in the [FIFA World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup "FIFA World Cup"), but have not yet qualified to compete in the tournament. In [cricket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cricket "Cricket"), Oman qualified for the [2016 ICC World Twenty20](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_ICC_World_Twenty20 "2016 ICC World Twenty20") and the 2021 T20 Cricket World Cup. On 25 June 2021, it was confirmed that Oman will co-host the [2021](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_ICC_Men%27s_T20_World_Cup "2021 ICC Men's T20 World Cup") edition of the [ICC Men's T20 World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ICC_Men%27s_T20_World_Cup "ICC Men's T20 World Cup") alongside the United Arab Emirates.[^315] In 2024, Oman participated in the [2024 Touch Rugby World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Touch_World_Cup#2024_-_England_\(Nottingham\) "Touch World Cup") in [Nottingham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nottingham "Nottingham"), which was its first participation in an international rugby tournament.[^316]
