**Uzbekistan**,[^1] officially the **Republic of Uzbekistan**,[^2] is a [doubly landlocked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landlocked_country#Doubly_landlocked "Landlocked country") country located in [Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia"). It is bordered by [Kazakhstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan "Kazakhstan") to the [north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan%E2%80%93Uzbekistan_border "Kazakhstan–Uzbekistan border"), [Kyrgyzstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan "Kyrgyzstan") to the [northeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan%E2%80%93Uzbekistan_border "Kyrgyzstan–Uzbekistan border"), [Tajikistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tajikistan "Tajikistan") to the [southeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tajikistan%E2%80%93Uzbekistan_border "Tajikistan–Uzbekistan border"), [Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afghanistan "Afghanistan") to the [south](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afghanistan%E2%80%93Uzbekistan_border "Afghanistan–Uzbekistan border"), and [Turkmenistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkmenistan "Turkmenistan") to the [southwest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkmenistan%E2%80%93Uzbekistan_border "Turkmenistan–Uzbekistan border"). It is one of the only 2 [doubly landlocked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubly_landlocked "Doubly landlocked") countries in the world, with the other being [Liechtenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liechtenstein "Liechtenstein"). The country has a population of more than 38.2 million, making it the most populous Central Asian republic. Uzbekistan is a member of the [Organization of Turkic States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_of_Turkic_States "Organization of Turkic States"). [Uzbek (both in Cyrillic and Turkic scripts)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbek_language "Uzbek language"), spoken by the [Uzbek people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbeks "Uzbeks") who form nearly 85% of the population, is the official language of the country. [Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language "Russian language") and [Tajik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tajik_language "Tajik language") are significant minority languages with the former serving as a [Lingua franca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lingua_franca "Lingua franca") (albeit declining). Tajik is primarily spoken by Tajiks who live in the southern parts of the country like [Samarkand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarkand "Samarkand") and [Bukhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bukhara "Bukhara").

The first recorded settlers in Uzbekistan were [Eastern Iranian nomads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Iranian_peoples "Eastern Iranian peoples"), known as [Scythians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scythians "Scythians"), who founded kingdoms in [Khwarazm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khwarazm "Khwarazm"), [Sogdiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sogdia "Sogdia"), and [Bactria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bactria "Bactria") in the 8th–6th centuries BC, as well as in [Fergana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Fergana "Kingdom of Fergana") and [Margiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margiana "Margiana") in the 3rd century BC – 6th century AD.[^16] The area was incorporated into the [Achaemenid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") and, after a period of [Greco-Bactrian rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom "Greco-Bactrian Kingdom"), was part of the [Sasanian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sasanian_Empire "Sasanian Empire") until the [Muslim conquest of Persia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_Persia "Muslim conquest of Persia") in the seventh century. The [early Muslim conquests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Muslim_conquests "Early Muslim conquests") and the subsequent [Samanid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samanid_Empire "Samanid Empire") resulted in the spread of Islam among the local people. During this period, cities began to grow rich from the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road"), and became a center of the [Islamic Golden Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age "Islamic Golden Age"). The local [Khwarazmian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khwarazmian_Empire "Khwarazmian Empire") was destroyed in the [Mongol invasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_conquest_of_Khwarezmia "Mongol conquest of Khwarezmia") in the 13th century, leading to a dominance by Mongol peoples. [Timur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timur "Timur") in the 14th century established the [Timurid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timurid_Empire "Timurid Empire"). The Timurid capital of [Samarkand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarkand "Samarkand") became a center of science under the rule of [Ulugh Beg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulugh_Beg "Ulugh Beg"), giving birth to the [Timurid Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timurid_Renaissance "Timurid Renaissance"). The territories of the [Timurid dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timurid_dynasty "Timurid dynasty") were conquered by [Kipchak Shaybanids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shaybanids "Shaybanids") in the 16th century. Most of [Central Asia was gradually incorporated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_conquest_of_Central_Asia "Russian conquest of Central Asia") into the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") during the 19th century, with Tashkent becoming the political center of [Russian Turkestan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Turkestan "Russian Turkestan"). In 1924, [national delimitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_delimitation_in_the_Soviet_Union#National_delimitation_in_Central_Asia "National delimitation in the Soviet Union") created the [Uzbek Soviet Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbek_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Uzbek Soviet Socialist Republic") as a [republic of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_the_Soviet_Union "Republic of the Soviet Union"). During the Soviet era, Uzbekistan became a leading producer of farm products such as cotton and melons, while also making significant advances in science and technology. It declared [independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_Day_\(Uzbekistan\) "Independence Day (Uzbekistan)") as the Republic of Uzbekistan in 1991 following the [Soviet collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_collapse "Soviet collapse").

Uzbekistan is a [secular state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secular_state "Secular state"), with a [semi-presidential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-presidential "Semi-presidential") government. Uzbekistan comprises 12 [regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regions_of_Uzbekistan "Regions of Uzbekistan") (vilayats), Tashkent City, and the [autonomous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomy "Autonomy") republic of [Karakalpakstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakalpakstan "Karakalpakstan"). While [NGOs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-governmental_organization "Non-governmental organization") have defined Uzbekistan as "an [authoritarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") state with limited [civil rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights "Civil and political rights") ",[^17] [^18] significant reforms under Uzbekistan's second president, [Shavkat Mirziyoyev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shavkat_Mirziyoyev "Shavkat Mirziyoyev"), have been made following the death of the first president, [Islam Karimov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_Karimov "Islam Karimov"). Owing to these reforms, relations with neighbors Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan and Afghanistan have drastically improved.[^19] [^20] [^21] [^22] The country also holds strong ties with other Asian countries like [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China"), [Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey"), [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia") and [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"). In recent years, Uzbekistan's [ties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan%E2%80%93European_Union_relations "Uzbekistan–European Union relations") with the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") have grown rapidly as well.[^23]

The [Uzbek economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Uzbekistan "Economy of Uzbekistan") is undergoing a gradual transition to a [market economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_economy "Market economy"), with foreign trade policy being based on [import substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Import_substitution_industrialization "Import substitution industrialization"). In September 2017, the country's currency became fully convertible at market rates. Uzbekistan is a major producer and exporter of [cotton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton "Cotton"). With gigantic power-generation facilities from the Soviet era and an ample supply of [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas"), Uzbekistan has become the largest electricity producer in Central Asia.[^24] Uzbekistan is a member of the [Commonwealth of Independent States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States "Commonwealth of Independent States") (CIS), [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") (UN) and the [Shanghai Cooperation Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Cooperation_Organisation "Shanghai Cooperation Organisation") (SCO).

## Etymology

The name *Uzbegistán* appears in the 16th century [Tarikh-i Rashidi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirza_Muhammad_Haidar_Dughlat "Mirza Muhammad Haidar Dughlat").[^25]

The origin of the word Uzbek remains disputed.

1. "free", "independent" or "own master/leader", requiring an [amalgamation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amalgamation_\(linguistics\) "Amalgamation (linguistics)") of *uz* ([Turkic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_language "Turkic language"): "own"), *beg* ("master" or "leader") [^26]
2. [eponymously](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eponym "Eponym") named after [Oghuz Khagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oghuz_Khagan "Oghuz Khagan"), also known as *Oghuz Beg* [^26]
3. A contraction of *uğuz*, earlier *oğuz*, that is, the [oghuz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oghuz_\(tribe\) "Oghuz (tribe)"), or "tribe", amalgamated with *bek* " [oguz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oghuz_\(tribe\) "Oghuz (tribe)") -leader".[^27]
4. Some scholars connect the name *Uzbek* to [Özbeg Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%96zbeg_Khan "Özbeg Khan"), the ruler of the Golden Horde, whence the Uzbeks migrated into what is now Uzbekistan in the 16th century.

All four have the middle syllable/phoneme being [cognate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognate_\(linguistics\) "Cognate (linguistics)") with the Turkic title *[beg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beg_\(title\) "Beg (title)")*.

The name of the country was often spelled Ўзбекистон in Uzbek Cyrillic or Узбекистан in Russian during Soviet rule.

## History

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Kaunakes_Bactria_Louvre_AO31917.jpg/250px-Kaunakes_Bactria_Louvre_AO31917.jpg)

Female statuette wearing the kaunakes. Chlorite and limestone, Bactria, beginning of the second millennium BC.

The region has been referred to by many names over the millennia. The name Uzbekistan first appears in 16th century literature.[^25] Other names for the region include: [Transoxiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transoxonia "Transoxonia"), [Turkestan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkestan "Turkestan"), and [Bukhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khanate_of_Bukhara "Khanate of Bukhara"). In the 14th century the region served as the birthplace, home, and capital of [Tamerlane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamerlane "Tamerlane"). Under Tamerlane, the region was a part of the [Timurid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timurid_Empire "Timurid Empire") which extended from the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea") to the [Arabian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Sea "Arabian Sea"), and to just outside of [Delhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delhi "Delhi"), India.

### Prehistory and ancient history

Central Asia was shaped by multiple [Indo-European migrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-European_migrations "Indo-European migrations"). During early [antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_history "Ancient history"), the region was inhabited by nomadic [Scythian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scythians "Scythians") tribes who came from the [Eurasian Steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Steppe "Eurasian Steppe"), which includes modern Uzbekistan, sometime during the first millennium BC. When these nomadic tribes settled in the region they built an extensive irrigation system along the rivers.[^28] At this time, cities such as Bukhoro ([Bukhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bukhara "Bukhara")) and Samarqand ([Samarkand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarkand "Samarkand")) emerged as centres of government and high culture.[^28] By the fifth century BC, the [Bactrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bactria "Bactria"), [Sogdian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sogdia "Sogdia"), and [Tokharian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuezhi "Yuezhi") states dominated the region.[^28]

As [East Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asia "East Asia") began to develop its silk trade with the West, using an extensive network of cities and rural settlements in the province of [Transoxiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transoxiana "Transoxiana"), and further east in what is today [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang"), the Sogdian intermediaries became the wealthiest of these merchants. As a result of this trade on what became known as the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Route "Silk Route"), Bukhara and Samarkand eventually became extremely wealthy cities, and at times [Transoxiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transoxiana "Transoxiana") (Mawarannahr) was one of the most influential and powerful provinces of antiquity.[^28]

The [Achaemenid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") emperors [Cyrus the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great "Cyrus the Great"), and later [Darius the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darius_the_Great "Darius the Great"), exerted control over the [Amu Darya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amu_Darya "Amu Darya"), incorporating [Bactria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bactria "Bactria") and [Chorasmia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chorasmia "Chorasmia") as [satrapies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satrapies "Satrapies"). Historical Iranian texts later record the region as [Turan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turan "Turan"). In 327 BC, Macedonian ruler [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") conquered the [Persian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") provinces of Sogdiana and Bactria, which contained the territories of modern Uzbekistan. Popular resistance to the conquest was fierce, causing Alexander's army to be bogged down in the region that became the northern part of the Macedonian [Greco-Bactrian Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom "Greco-Bactrian Kingdom"). The kingdom was replaced with the Yuezhi-dominated [Kushan Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kushan_Empire "Kushan Empire") in the first century BC. For many centuries thereafter the region of Uzbekistan was ruled by the [Hephthalites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hephthalites "Hephthalites") and [Sassanid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sassanid "Sassanid") Empires, as well as by other empires, for example, those formed by the Turkic [Gokturk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gokturk "Gokturk") peoples.

### Medieval history

The [Muslim conquests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Muslim_conquests "Early Muslim conquests") from the seventh century onward saw the [Arabs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabs "Arabs") bring [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") to Uzbekistan. In the same period, Islam began to take root among the nomadic [Turkic peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_people "Turkic people").

In the eighth century, Transoxiana, the territory between the [Amudarya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amu_Darya "Amu Darya") and [Syrdarya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syr_Darya "Syr Darya") rivers, was conquered by the Arabs ([Qutayba ibn Muslim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qutayba_ibn_Muslim "Qutayba ibn Muslim")), becoming a focal point soon after the [Islamic Golden Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age "Islamic Golden Age").

In the ninth and tenth centuries, Transoxiana was brought into the [Samanid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samanid_Empire "Samanid Empire"). In the tenth century it was gradually dominated by the Turkic-ruled [Karakhanids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakhanids "Karakhanids"), as well as their [Seljuk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seljuks "Seljuks") overseers.[^29]

The [Mongol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire") conquest under [Genghis Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genghis_Khan "Genghis Khan") during the 13th century brought change to the region. The invasions of [Bukhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bukhara "Bukhara"), [Samarkand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarkand "Samarkand"), [Urgench](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%B6ne%C3%BCrgen%C3%A7 "Köneürgenç") and other cities resulted in [mass murders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Destruction_under_the_Mongol_Empire "Destruction under the Mongol Empire") and unprecedented destruction, which saw parts of [Khwarezmia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khwarazmian_Empire "Khwarazmian Empire") being completely razed.[^30]

Following the death of Genghis Khan in 1227, the empire was divided among his four sons and family members. Despite the potential for serious fragmentation, an orderly succession continued for several generations. Control of most of Transoxiana stayed in the hands of the direct descendants of [Chagatai Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chagatai_Khan "Chagatai Khan"), the second son of Genghis Khan. Orderly succession, prosperity, and internal peace prevailed in the Chagatai lands, and the Mongol Empire as a whole remained a strong and united kingdom, known as the [Golden Horde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Horde "Golden Horde").[^31]

### Timurid period

One tribal chieftain, [Timur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timur "Timur") (Tamerlane),[^32] emerged from struggles in the 1380s as the dominant force in Transoxiana. Although he was not a descendant of Genghis Khan, Timur became the *de facto* ruler of Transoxiana and proceeded to conquer all of western Central Asia, [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran "Iran"), the [Caucasus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caucasus "Caucasus"), [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia"), [Asia Minor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia_Minor "Asia Minor"), and the southern steppe region north of the [Aral Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aral_Sea "Aral Sea"). He also invaded Russia before dying during an invasion of [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") in 1405.[^31] Timur was also known for his extreme brutality and his conquests were accompanied by [genocidal massacres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genocidal_massacre "Genocidal massacre") in the cities he occupied.[^33]

Timur initiated the last flowering of Transoxiana by gathering together numerous artisans and scholars from the vast lands he had conquered into his capital, Samarkand, thus imbuing his empire with a rich Perso-Islamic culture. During his reign and the reigns of his immediate descendants, a wide range of religious and palatial construction masterpieces were undertaken in Samarkand and other population centres.[^34]

Tamerlane also established an exchange of medical discoveries and became the patron of physicians, scientists and artists from neighbouring countries such as India;[^35] his grandson [Ulugh Beg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulugh_Beg "Ulugh Beg") was one of the world's first great astronomers. It was during the Timurid dynasty that Turkic, in the form of the [Chaghatai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chagatai_language "Chagatai language") dialect, became a literary language in its own right in Transoxiana, although the Timurids were Persianate in culture. The greatest Chaghataid writer, [Ali-Shir Nava'i](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ali-Shir_Nava%27i "Ali-Shir Nava'i"), was active in the city of [Herat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herat "Herat") (now in northwestern Afghanistan) in the second half of the 15th century.[^31]

### Uzbek period

The Timurid state quickly split in half after the death of Timur. The chronic internal fighting of the Timurids attracted the attention of the Uzbek nomadic tribes living to the north of the Aral Sea. In 1501, the Uzbek forces began a wholesale invasion of Transoxiana.[^31] The [slave trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bukhara_slave_trade "Bukhara slave trade") in the [Emirate of Bukhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Bukhara "Emirate of Bukhara") became prominent and was firmly established at this time.[^36] The Khanate of Bukhara was eventually invaded by the foreign government of Persia in 1740, and then became a part of the Persian empire of the day.

Before the arrival of the [Russians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians "Russians"), present-day Uzbekistan was divided between the [Emirate of Bukhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Bukhara "Emirate of Bukhara") and the [khanates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khanate "Khanate") of [Khiva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khanate_of_Khiva "Khanate of Khiva") and [Kokand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khanate_of_Kokand "Khanate of Kokand").

### Russian conquest

In the 19th century, the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") began to expand and spread into [Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia"). There were 210,306 Russians living in Uzbekistan in 1912.[^37] The " [Great Game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Game "Great Game") " period is generally regarded as running from approximately 1813 to the [Anglo-Russian Convention of 1907](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Russian_Convention_of_1907 "Anglo-Russian Convention of 1907"). A second, less intensive phase followed the [Bolshevik Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/October_Revolution "October Revolution") of 1917. At the start of the 19th century, there were some 3,200 kilometres (2,000 mi) separating [British India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_India "British India") and the outlying regions of [Tsarist Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Russia "Imperial Russia"). Much of the land between was unmapped. In the early 1890s, [Sven Hedin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sven_Hedin "Sven Hedin") passed through Uzbekistan during his first expedition.

### Soviet period

By the beginning of 1920, Central Asia was firmly in the hands of Russia and, despite some early [resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basmachi_movement "Basmachi movement") to the [Bolsheviks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolsheviks "Bolsheviks"), Uzbekistan and the rest of Central Asia became a part of the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"). On 27 October 1924, the [Uzbek Soviet Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbek_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Uzbek Soviet Socialist Republic") was created. From 1941 to 1945, during [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), 1,433,230 people from Uzbekistan fought in the [Red Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Army "Red Army") against [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany"). As many as 263,005 Uzbek soldiers died in the battlefields of the [Eastern Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Front_\(World_War_II\) "Eastern Front (World War II)"), and 32,670 went missing in action.[^38]

During the [Soviet–Afghan War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet%E2%80%93Afghan_War "Soviet–Afghan War"), about 65,000 Uzbek troops fought in neighbouring [Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Afghanistan "Democratic Republic of Afghanistan"). At least 1,500 lost their lives and thousands more were paralysed.[^39]

### Independence

On 20 June 1990, Uzbekistan declared its state sovereignty. On 31 August 1991, Uzbekistan declared independence after the [failed coup attempt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1991_Soviet_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_attempt "1991 Soviet coup d'état attempt") in Moscow. 1 September was proclaimed National Independence Day. The Soviet Union was [dissolved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union "Dissolution of the Soviet Union") on 26 December of that year. [Islam Karimov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_Karimov "Islam Karimov"), previously first secretary of the Communist Party of Uzbekistan since 1989, was elected president of the Uzbek Soviet Socialist Republic in 1990. After the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991, he was elected president of independent Uzbekistan.[^40] An authoritarian ruler, Karimov died in September 2016.[^41] He was replaced by his long-time [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Uzbekistan "Prime Minister of Uzbekistan"), [Shavkat Mirziyoyev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shavkat_Mirziyoyev "Shavkat Mirziyoyev"), on 14 December of the same year.[^42] On 6 November 2021, Mirziyoyev was sworn into his second term in office, after gaining a landslide victory in the presidential [election.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Uzbek_presidential_election "2021 Uzbek presidential election")[^43] [^44]

## Geography and climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Uzbekistan_satellite_photo.jpg/250px-Uzbekistan_satellite_photo.jpg)

Satellite image of Uzbekistan (March 2005)

Uzbekistan has an area of 448,978 square kilometres (173,351 sq mi).[^8] It is the 56th largest country in the world by area and the 40th by population.[^45] Among the [CIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States "Commonwealth of Independent States") countries, it is the fourth largest by area and the second largest by population.[^46]

Uzbekistan lies between latitudes [37°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/37th_parallel_north "37th parallel north") and [46° N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/46th_parallel_north "46th parallel north"), and longitudes [56°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/56th_meridian_east "56th meridian east") and [74° E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/74th_meridian_east "74th meridian east"). It stretches 1,425 kilometres (885 mi) from west to east and 930 kilometres (580 mi) from north to south. Bordering [Kazakhstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan "Kazakhstan") and the [Aralkum Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aralkum_Desert "Aralkum Desert") (former [Aral Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aral_Sea "Aral Sea")) to the north and northwest, [Turkmenistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkmenistan "Turkmenistan") and [Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afghanistan "Afghanistan") to the southwest, [Tajikistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tajikistan "Tajikistan") to the southeast, and [Kyrgyzstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan "Kyrgyzstan") to the northeast, Uzbekistan is one of the largest [Central Asian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia") states and the only Central Asian state to border all the other four. Uzbekistan also shares a short border (less than 150 km or 93 mi) with [Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afghanistan "Afghanistan") to the south. Uzbekistan is home to six terrestrial ecoregions: [Alai-Western Tian Shan steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alai-Western_Tian_Shan_steppe "Alai-Western Tian Shan steppe"), [Gissaro-Alai open woodlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gissaro-Alai_open_woodlands "Gissaro-Alai open woodlands"), [Badghyz and Karabil semi-desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Badghyz_and_Karabil_semi-desert "Badghyz and Karabil semi-desert"), [Central Asian northern desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asian_northern_desert "Central Asian northern desert"), [Central Asian riparian woodlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asian_riparian_woodlands "Central Asian riparian woodlands"), and [Central Asian southern desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asian_southern_desert "Central Asian southern desert").[^47]

Uzbekistan is a hot, dry, [landlocked country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landlocked_country "Landlocked country").[^48] The second doubly landlocked country is [Liechtenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liechtenstein "Liechtenstein"). In addition, due to its location within a series of [endorheic basins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endorheic_basin "Endorheic basin"), none of its rivers lead to the sea. Less than 10% of its territory is intensively cultivated irrigated land in river valleys and oases. The [Aral Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aral_Sea "Aral Sea"), which has been largely desiccated by cotton production established in the Soviet era, is considered one of the world's worst environmental disasters.[^49] The rest is the vast [Kyzylkum Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyzylkum_Desert "Kyzylkum Desert") and mountains.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Koppen-Geiger_Map_UZB_present.svg/330px-Koppen-Geiger_Map_UZB_present.svg.png)

Köppen climate classification

As published in its book of records in 2007,[^46] the generally accepted highest point in Uzbekistan is [Khazret Sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khazret_Sultan "Khazret Sultan") at 4,643 metres (15,233 ft) above sea level.[^50]

The climate in Uzbekistan is continental, with little [precipitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation "Precipitation") expected annually (100–200 millimetres, or 3.9–7.9 inches). The average summer high [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") tends to be 40 °C (104 °F), while the average winter low temperature is around −23 °C (−9 °F).[^51]

### Environment

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Karakalpakstan_Kyzyl_Qala_Cotton_Picking.jpg/250px-Karakalpakstan_Kyzyl_Qala_Cotton_Picking.jpg)

Cotton picking near Kyzyl-Kala, Karakalpakstan

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Water_stress%2C_top_countries_%282022%29.svg/250px-Water_stress%2C_top_countries_%282022%29.svg.png)

Uzbekistan was the thirteenth most water stressed country in the world in 2022.

Uzbekistan has a rich and diverse natural environment. However, decades of [Soviet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") policies in pursuit of greater [cotton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton "Cotton") production have resulted in a catastrophic scenario with the agricultural industry being the main contributor to the pollution and devastation of both air and water in the country.[^52]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/AralSea1989_2014.jpg/250px-AralSea1989_2014.jpg)

Comparison of the Aral Sea between 1989 and 2014

The [Aral Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aral_Sea "Aral Sea") was once the fourth-largest inland sea on Earth, humidifying the surrounding air and irrigating the arid land.[^53] Since the 1960s, when the overuse of the Aral Sea water began, it has shrunk to about 10% of its former area and divided into parts, with only the southern part of the narrow western lobe of the [South Aral Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Aral_Sea "South Aral Sea") remaining permanently in Uzbekistan. Much of the water was and continues to be used for the [irrigation of cotton fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton_production_in_Uzbekistan "Cotton production in Uzbekistan"),[^54] a crop requiring a large amount of water to grow.[^55]

Due to the Aral Sea loss, high salinity and contamination of the soil with [heavy elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_elements "Heavy elements") are especially widespread in [Karakalpakstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakalpakstan "Karakalpakstan"), the region of Uzbekistan adjacent to the Aral Sea. The bulk of the nation's water resources is used for farming, which accounts for nearly 84% of the water use and contributes to high [soil salinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_salinity "Soil salinity"). Heavy use of [pesticides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pesticide "Pesticide") and [fertilisers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertiliser "Fertiliser") for cotton growing further aggravates [soil contamination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_contamination "Soil contamination").[^51]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Suv-ombori.gif/250px-Suv-ombori.gif)

Map of flooded areas as a result of the collapse of the Sardoba Reservoir

According to the UNDP (United Nations Development Programme), [climate risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_risk "Climate risk") management in Uzbekistan should consider its ecological safety.[^56]

Numerous oil and gas deposits have been discovered in the south of the country.[^57] [^58]

Uzbekistan has also been home to seismic activity, as evidenced by the [1902 Andijan earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1902_Andijan_earthquake "1902 Andijan earthquake"), [2011 Fergana Valley earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_Fergana_Valley_earthquake "2011 Fergana Valley earthquake"), and [1966 Tashkent earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1966_Tashkent_earthquake "1966 Tashkent earthquake").[^59]

A dam collapse at [Sardoba Reservoir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sardoba_Reservoir "Sardoba Reservoir") in May 2020 flooded 35,000 hectares of land. Six people died and 111,000 evacuated with recovery estimates over 1.5 trillion som. The devastation extended into areas inside neighbouring [Kazakhstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan "Kazakhstan").[^60] [^61]

#### Air pollution

The air pollution situation in Uzbekistan throughout the 21st century has been characterised by steady deterioration, reflecting a long history of environmental problems. The increase in pollution began with local domestic emissions in the early 20th century, but Soviet industrialisation made a significant contribution, especially the evacuation of enterprises in the 1940s and the subsequent development of heavy industry, oil refining and chemical production in the 1950s–1980s. The decline in the level of the Aral Sea has created an additional source of salt and dust storms. In the post-Soviet period, the structure of pollution shifted: in the 2000s, the dominant factor was the growth of motor transport and the use of low-quality fuel, and in the 2010s, it was the increase in PM2.5 concentrations due to the construction boom and climatic conditions.

By 2020–2025, the combination of transport emissions, domestic heating, dust and meteorological inversions had led to a systemic environmental crisis, with Tashkent regularly ranking among the most polluted cities in the world. According to the [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank_Group "World Bank Group"), the average annual pollution level in the capital is more than six times higher than the [WHO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization "World Health Organization") recommended level (5 μg/m³).[^62]

### Responses to climate change

Uzbekistan’s socioeconomic situation and geographic location make it highly vulnerable to the effects of [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") —particularly [water stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_scarcity "Water scarcity") —as much of the country consists of vast [desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert "Desert") plains and [arid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aridity "Aridity") regions.[^63] According to a [risk assessment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk_assessment "Risk assessment") by [The World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank_Group "World Bank Group"), more than a fifth of Uzbekistan’s projected 2030 population (8 million people) will live in areas classified as very high climate‑risk (p. 11).[^63] The country has made progress in the agricultural sector, risk management, and adaptation and resilience strategies, but important gaps remain in the resilience of [forest ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_ecology "Forest ecology"), [water‑resource](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_resources "Water resources") management, business and financial [continuity planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_continuity_planning "Business continuity planning"), and disaster communication.[^63] Uzbekistan also lacks an overarching national climate policy, limiting climate action to sector‑specific efforts.[^63]

Climate impacts on agriculture are already visible: crops have been affected differentially by temperature and water‑stress changes.[^63] Maize, potatoes, rice, watermelons, and wheat may experience positive yield changes, while cotton—the country’s second most important crop—and high‑revenue fruit crops such as apples, cherries, and apricots are expected to face negative effects (p. 7).[^63]

At the same time, reforms are underway to support a transition toward a [lower‑carbon economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-carbon_economy "Low-carbon economy"), even as economic growth remains dependent on natural resources.[^63] Key solutions include expanding access to financing for lower‑income populations and improving access to safe sanitation, [universal health care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_health_care "Universal health care"), [information and communication technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_and_communications_technology "Information and communications technology"), and social protections (p. 1).[^63]

## Politics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Tashkent%2C_Paque_Navoi_3.jpg/250px-Tashkent%2C_Paque_Navoi_3.jpg)

The Legislative Chamber of Uzbekistan (Lower House)

![Portrait of Shavkat Mirziyoyev](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Shavkat_Mirziyoyev_official_portrait_%28cropped_2%29.jpg/330px-Shavkat_Mirziyoyev_official_portrait_%28cropped_2%29.jpg)

Portrait of Shavkat Mirziyoyev

[Shavkat Mirziyoyev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shavkat_Mirziyoyev "Shavkat Mirziyoyev")  
[President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Uzbekistan "President of Uzbekistan")

After Uzbekistan declared independence from the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") in 1991, an election was held, and [Islam Karimov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_Karimov "Islam Karimov") was elected as the [first President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_Uzbekistan "List of Presidents of Uzbekistan") of Uzbekistan on 29 December 1991. The elections of the [Oliy Majlis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliy_Majlis "Oliy Majlis") (Parliament or Supreme Assembly) were held under a resolution adopted by the 16th Supreme Soviet in 1994. In that year, the Supreme Soviet was replaced by the Oliy Majlis. The third elections for the bicameral 150-member Oliy Majlis, the Legislative Chamber, and the 100-member Senate for five-year terms, were held on 27 December 2009. The second elections were held from December 2004 to January 2005. The Oliy Majlis was unicameral up to 2004. Its size increased from 69 deputies (members) in 1994 to 120 in 2004–05 and currently stands at 150.

[Islam Karimov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_Karimov "Islam Karimov") ’s first presidential term was extended to 2000 through a referendum. He was subsequently re-elected in 2000, 2007, and 2015, each time winning over 90% of the vote. However, most international observers refused to participate in the electoral process and dismissed the results as failing to meet democratic standards.

A 2002 referendum introduced a [bicameral parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicameralism "Bicameralism"), consisting of a lower house (the [Oliy Majlis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliy_Majlis "Oliy Majlis")) and an upper house (the [Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senate_of_Uzbekistan "Senate of Uzbekistan")), with members of the lower house serving as full-time legislators. Elections for the new parliament were held on 26 December 2002.

Following Karimov’s death on 2 September 2016, the Oliy Majlis appointed Prime Minister Shavkat Mirziyoyev as interim president.[^64] While the constitution designated Senate Chairman [Nigmatilla Yuldashev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigmatilla_Yuldashev "Nigmatilla Yuldashev") as the rightful successor, he declined the position, citing Mirziyoyev’s extensive experience. In the December 2016 presidential election, Mirziyoyev was officially elected with 88.6% of the vote and was sworn in on 14 December.[^65] Deputy Prime Minister [Abdulla Aripov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdulla_Aripov "Abdulla Aripov") then succeeded him as prime minister.[^66]

As president, Mirziyoyev replaced most of Karimov’s officials and called for the inclusion of younger, patriotic individuals in government. Over time, he distanced himself from Karimov’s policies, visiting various regions and cities to oversee reforms. [Analysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_commentator "Color commentator") and [Western media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_media "Western media") have compared his leadership style to that of [Deng Xiaoping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping "Deng Xiaoping") and [Mikhail Gorbachev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev "Mikhail Gorbachev"), describing his tenure as a potential "Uzbek Spring".[^67] [^68] [^20]

According to [International IDEA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Institute_for_Democracy_and_Electoral_Assistance "International Institute for Democracy and Electoral Assistance") ’s Global State of Democracy (GSoD) Indices and Democracy Tracker, Uzbekistan performs in the low range on overall democratic measures, with particular weaknesses in local democracy, judicial independence and free political parties.[^69] [^70] [^71] The [V-Dem Democracy Indices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V-Dem_Democracy_Indices "V-Dem Democracy Indices") described Uzbekistan as a closed [autocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy") in 2024.[^72]

### Foreign relations

Uzbekistan joined the [Commonwealth of Independent States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States "Commonwealth of Independent States") in December 1991. However, it is opposed to reintegration and withdrew from the CIS collective security arrangement in 1999. Since that time, Uzbekistan has participated in the CIS peacekeeping force in Tajikistan and in UN-organised groups to help resolve the Tajikistan and Afghanistan conflicts, both of which it sees as posing threats to its own stability.

Previously close to Washington (which gave Uzbekistan half a billion dollars in aid in 2004, about a quarter of its military budget), the government of Uzbekistan has recently restricted American military use of the airbase at [Karshi-Khanabad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karshi-Khanabad "Karshi-Khanabad") for air operations in neighbouring Afghanistan.[^73] Uzbekistan was an active supporter of U.S. efforts against worldwide terrorism.[^74]

The relationship between Uzbekistan and the United States began to deteriorate after the so-called " [colour revolutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colour_revolutions "Colour revolutions") " in [Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgia_\(country\) "Georgia (country)") and Ukraine (and to a lesser extent [Kyrgyzstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan "Kyrgyzstan")). When the U.S. joined in a call for an independent international investigation of the bloody events at [Andijan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andijan_massacre "Andijan massacre"), the relationship further declined, and President Islam Karimov changed the political alignment of the country to bring it closer to Russia and China.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Secretary_Kerry_Meets_With_President_Karimov_at_the_President%27s_Residential_Compound_in_Samarkand_%2822052330394%29.jpg/250px-Secretary_Kerry_Meets_With_President_Karimov_at_the_President%27s_Residential_Compound_in_Samarkand_%2822052330394%29.jpg)

President Islam Karimov with U.S. Secretary of State John Kerry in Samarkand in November 2015

In late July 2005, the government of Uzbekistan ordered the United States to vacate an airbase in Karshi-Kanabad (near Uzbekistan's border with Afghanistan) within 180 days.[^75] Karimov had offered use of the base to the U.S. shortly after [9/11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/9/11 "9/11"). It is also believed by some Uzbeks that the protests in Andijan were brought about by the UK and U.S. influences in the area of Andijan.[^75] This is another reason for the hostility between Uzbekistan and the West.

Uzbekistan is a member of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") (UN) (since 2 March 1992), the [Euro-Atlantic Partnership Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euro-Atlantic_Partnership_Council "Euro-Atlantic Partnership Council") (EAPC), [Partnership for Peace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partnership_for_Peace "Partnership for Peace") (PfP), and the [Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_for_Security_and_Co-operation_in_Europe "Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe") (OSCE). It belongs to the [Organisation of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation") (OIC) and the [Economic Cooperation Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_Cooperation_Organization "Economic Cooperation Organization") (ECO) (comprising the five Central Asian countries, [Azerbaijan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azerbaijan "Azerbaijan"), Iran, Turkey, Afghanistan, and Pakistan). In 1999, Uzbekistan joined the [GUAM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GUAM "GUAM") alliance (Georgia, Ukraine, Azerbaijan and [Moldova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moldova "Moldova")), which was formed in 1997 (making it GUUAM), but pulled out of the organisation in 2005.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/SCO_meeting_%282022-09-16%29.jpg/250px-SCO_meeting_%282022-09-16%29.jpg)

Leaders present at the SCO summit in Samarkand, Uzbekistan, in 2022

Uzbekistan is also a member of the [Shanghai Cooperation Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Cooperation_Organisation "Shanghai Cooperation Organisation") (SCO) and hosts the SCO's Regional Anti-Terrorist Structure (RATS) in Tashkent. Uzbekistan joined the new [Central Asian Cooperation Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asian_Cooperation_Organisation "Central Asian Cooperation Organisation") (CACO) in 2002. The CACO consists of Uzbekistan, [Tajikistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tajikistan "Tajikistan"), [Kazakhstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan "Kazakhstan"), and Kyrgyzstan. It is a founding member of, and remains involved in, the [Central Asian Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asian_Union "Central Asian Union"), formed with Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan, and joined in March 1998 by Tajikistan.

In December 1994 Uzbekistan applied for the [World Trade Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Trade_Organization "World Trade Organization") membership and received an observer status to start the accession process. The Working Party on the Accession of Uzbekistan to the WTO held its fourth meeting on 7 July 2020 — almost 15 years after its last formal meeting.[^76]

In September 2006, [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO") presented Islam Karimov an award for Uzbekistan's preservation of its rich culture and traditions.[^77] Despite criticism, this seems to be a sign of improving relationships between Uzbekistan and the West.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Vladimir_Putin_and_Shavkat_Mirziyoyev_%282024_Victory_Day%29.jpg/250px-Vladimir_Putin_and_Shavkat_Mirziyoyev_%282024_Victory_Day%29.jpg)

President Shavkat Mirziyoyev with Russian president Vladimir Putin before the Moscow Victory Day Parade in Moscow on 9 May 2024

The month of October 2006 also saw a decrease in the isolation of Uzbekistan from the West. The [EU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") announced that it was planning to send a delegation to Uzbekistan to talk about human rights and liberties, after a long period of hostile relations between the two. Although it is equivocal about whether the official or unofficial version of the [Andijan Massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andijan_Massacre "Andijan Massacre") is true, the EU is evidently willing to ease its economic sanctions against Uzbekistan. Nevertheless, it is generally assumed among Uzbekistan's population that the government will stand firm in maintaining its close ties with the [Russian Federation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Federation "Russian Federation") and in its theory that the 2004–2005 protests in Uzbekistan were promoted by the US and UK.

In January 2008, [Lola Karimova-Tillyaeva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lola_Karimova-Tillyaeva "Lola Karimova-Tillyaeva") was appointed to her current role as Uzbekistan's ambassador to [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO"). Karimova-Tillyaeva and her team have been instrumental in promoting inter-cultural dialogue by increasing European society's awareness of Uzbekistan's cultural and historical heritage.

### Human rights

[Non-governmental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-governmental_organization "Non-governmental organization") human rights organisations, such as [IHF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Helsinki_Federation_for_Human_Rights "International Helsinki Federation for Human Rights"), [Human Rights Watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch "Human Rights Watch"), [Amnesty International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amnesty_International "Amnesty International"), as well as the [United States Department of State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State "United States Department of State") and the [Council of the European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_the_European_Union "Council of the European Union"), characterise Uzbekistan as "an authoritarian state with limited civil rights" [^17] and express profound concern about "wide-scale violation of virtually all basic human rights".[^78] According to the reports, the most widespread violations include torture, arbitrary arrests, and various restrictions on freedoms, such as those of religion, speech, press, association, and assembly. It has also been reported that forced sterilisation of rural Uzbek women has been sanctioned by the government.[^79] [^80] The reports maintain that the violations are most often committed against members of religious organisations, independent journalists, human rights activists and political activists, including members of the banned opposition parties. As of 2015, reports on violations on human rights in Uzbekistan indicated that violations were still going on without any improvement.[^81] The [Freedom House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_House "Freedom House") has consistently ranked Uzbekistan near the bottom of its Freedom in the World ranking since the country's founding in 1991. In the 2018 report, Uzbekistan was one of the 11 worst countries for [Political Rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_Rights "Political Rights") and [Civil Liberties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_Liberties "Civil Liberties").[^82]

The [2005 civil unrest in Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2005_civil_unrest_in_Uzbekistan "2005 civil unrest in Uzbekistan"), which resulted in several hundred people being killed, is viewed by many as a landmark event in the history of human rights abuse in Uzbekistan.[^83] [^84] [^85] Concern has been expressed and requests for an independent investigation of the events has been made by the United States,[^86] the European Union,[^87] the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"),[^88] the OSCE Chairman-in-Office and the OSCE Office for Democratic Institutions and Human Rights.[^89]

The government of Uzbekistan is accused of unlawful termination of human life and of denying its citizens [freedom of assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_assembly "Freedom of assembly") and freedom of expression. The government vehemently rebuffs the accusations.[^90] In addition, some officials claim that "an [information war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_warfare "Information warfare") on Uzbekistan has been declared" and the human rights violations in Andijan are invented by the enemies of Uzbekistan as a convenient pretext for intervention in the country's internal affairs.[^91] Male and female [homosexuality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_Uzbekistan "LGBT rights in Uzbekistan") is illegal in Uzbekistan.[^92] Punishment ranges from a fine to 3 years in prison.[^93]

There are an estimated 1.2 million modern slaves in Uzbekistan,[^94] most working in the cotton industry. The government allegedly forces state employees to pick cotton in the autumn months.[^95] World Bank loans have been connected to projects that use child labour and forced labour practices in the cotton industry.[^96]

### Recent developments

Karimov died in 2016 and his successor Shavkat Mirziyoyev is considered by most to be pursuing a less autocratic path by increasing co-operation with human rights NGOs,[^97] [^98] scheduling Soviet-style [exit visas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exit_visa "Exit visa") to be abolished in 2019,[^99] and reducing sentences for certain misdemeanor offences.[^100]

The Amnesty International report on the country for 2017–2018 found some remnant repressive measures and lack of rule of law in eradicating modern slavery.[^101] In February 2020, the United Nations announced that Uzbekistan had made "major progress" on stamping out forced labour in its cotton harvest as 94% of pickers worked voluntarily.[^102]

## Economy

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Real_GDP_per_capita_development_of_Uzbekistan.svg/250px-Real_GDP_per_capita_development_of_Uzbekistan.svg.png)

Development of real GDP per capita

Uzbekistan mines 80 tons of gold annually, seventh in the world. In 2015, Uzbekistan's gold production was 102 metric tons.[^109] Uzbekistan's copper deposits rank tenth in the world and its uranium deposits twelfth. The country's uranium production ranks seventh globally.[^110] [^111] [^112] The Uzbek national gas company, [Uzbekneftegaz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekneftegas "Uzbekneftegas"), ranks 11th in the world in natural gas production with an annual output of 60 to 70 billion cubic metres (2.1–2.5 trillion cubic feet). The country has significant untapped reserves of oil and gas: there are 194 deposits of hydrocarbons in Uzbekistan, including 98 condensate and natural gas deposits and 96 gas condensate deposits.[^113] [^114]

Uzbekistan improved marginally in the *2020 Ease of Doing Business* ranking by the [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank").[^115] The largest corporations involved in Uzbekistan's energy sector are the [China National Petroleum Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_National_Petroleum_Corporation "China National Petroleum Corporation") (CNPC), [Petronas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petronas "Petronas"), the [Korea National Oil Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korea_National_Oil_Corporation "Korea National Oil Corporation"), [Gazprom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gazprom "Gazprom"), [Lukoil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lukoil "Lukoil"), and [Uzbekneftegaz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekneftegas "Uzbekneftegas").

Along with many [Commonwealth of Independent States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States "Commonwealth of Independent States") or CIS economies, Uzbekistan's economy declined during the first years of transition and then recovered after 1995, as the cumulative effect of policy reforms began to be felt.[^116] It has shown robust growth, rising by 4% per year between 1998 and 2003 and accelerating thereafter to 7%–8% per year. According to IMF estimates,[^117] the GDP in 2008 will be almost double its value in 1995 (in constant prices). Since 2003, annual inflation rates varied, reaching almost 40% in 2010 and less than 20% in 2019.[^118]

Uzbekistan has a GNI per capita of US$2,020 in 2018 dollars, resulting in a [PPP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity "Purchasing power parity") equivalent of US$7,230.[^119] Economic production is concentrated in commodities. In 2011, Uzbekistan was the world's seventh-largest producer and fifth-largest exporter of [cotton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton "Cotton") [^120] as well as the seventh-largest world producer of gold. It is also a regionally significant producer of natural gas, coal, copper, oil, silver and uranium.[^121]

[Agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture_in_Uzbekistan "Agriculture in Uzbekistan") employs 27% of Uzbekistan's labour force and contributes 17.4% of its GDP (2012 data).[^46] Cultivable land is 4.4 million hectares, or about 10% of Uzbekistan's total area. While official unemployment is very low, underemployment – especially in rural areas – is estimated to be at least 20%.[^122] [Cotton production in Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton_production_in_Uzbekistan "Cotton production in Uzbekistan") is important to the national economy of the country.[^54] Uzbek cotton is used to make banknotes in South Korea.[^123] Uzbek cotton exports have become the cause of a scandal related to the Russian-Ukrainian war and sanctions imposed on the Russian military industry. According to the Organized Crime and Corruption Reporting Project (OCCRP), Vlast, and iStories, after 24 February 2022, Uzbekistan significantly increased its exports of cotton pulp and nitrocellulose to Russia, key components for the manufacture of explosives and gunpowder. According to Ekonomichna Pravda, at least two large Uzbek exporters have been working with Russian military-industrial complex enterprises. Documents from the Federal Tax Service of the Russian Federation confirm that at least three Russian companies – Bina Group, Khimtrade, and Lenakhim – sold imported cotton pulp in Russia to military plants under US sanctions.[^124] [^125] [^126] The use of child labour in Uzbekistan has led several companies, including Tesco,[^127] C&A,[^128] Marks & Spencer, Gap, and H&M, to boycott Uzbek cotton.[^129]

The country has a considerable production of carrots as well.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Yodgorlik_Silk_Factory_%285982822980%29.jpg/250px-Yodgorlik_Silk_Factory_%285982822980%29.jpg)

Yodgorlik silk factory

Facing a multitude of economic challenges upon acquiring independence, the government adopted an evolutionary reform strategy, with an emphasis on state control, reduction of imports and self-sufficiency in energy. Since 1994, the state-controlled media have repeatedly proclaimed the success of this "Uzbekistan Economic Model" [^130] and suggested that it is a unique example of a smooth transition to the market economy while avoiding shock, pauperism and stagnation. As of 2019, Uzbekistan's economy is one of the most diversified in Central Asia which makes the country an attractive economic partner for China.[^131]

The gradualist reform strategy has involved postponing significant macroeconomic and structural reforms. The state in the hands of the [bureaucracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_class "New class") has remained a dominant influence in the economy. Corruption permeates the society and grows more rampant over time.[^132] A February 2006 report by the [International Crisis Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Crisis_Group "International Crisis Group") suggests that revenues earned from key exports, especially [cotton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton "Cotton"), [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold "Gold"), [maize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maize "Maize") and increasingly gas, are distributed among a very small circle of the ruling elite, with little or no benefit for the populace at large.[^133] The early-2010s high-profile corruption scandals involving government contracts and large international companies, notably [TeliaSonera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecom_corruption_scandal "Telecom corruption scandal"), have shown that businesses are particularly vulnerable to corruption when operating in Uzbekistan.[^134]

According to the [Economist Intelligence Unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economist_Intelligence_Unit "Economist Intelligence Unit"), "the government is hostile to allowing the development of an independent private sector, over which it would have no control".[^135]

The economic policies have repelled foreign investment, which is the lowest per capita in the CIS.[^136] For years, the largest barrier to foreign companies entering the Uzbekistan market has been the difficulty of converting currency. In 2003 the government accepted the obligations of Article VIII under the [International Monetary Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund "International Monetary Fund") (IMF) [^137] providing for full currency convertibility. However, strict currency controls and the tightening of borders have lessened the effect of this measure.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Urgut_Sunday_market_bread_sellers.JPG/250px-Urgut_Sunday_market_bread_sellers.JPG)

Bread sellers in Urgut

Uzbekistan experienced rampant [inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation "Inflation") of around 1000% per year immediately after independence (1992–1994). Stabilisation efforts implemented with guidance from the IMF [^138] paid off. The inflation rates were brought down to 50% in 1997 and then to 22% in 2002. Since 2003 annual inflation rates averaged less than 10%.[^117] Tight economic policies in 2004 resulted in a drastic reduction of inflation to 3.8% (although alternative estimates based on the price of a true [market basket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_basket "Market basket") put it at 15%).[^139] The inflation rates moved up to 6.9% in 2006 and 7.6% in 2007 but have remained in the single-digit range.[^140]

The government of Uzbekistan restricts foreign imports in many ways, including high import duties. Excise taxes are applied in a highly discriminatory manner to protect locally produced goods,[^141] although the excises taxes were removed for foreign cars in 2020.[^142] Official tariffs are combined with unofficial, discriminatory charges resulting in total charges amounting to as much as 100 to 150% of the actual value of the product, making imported products virtually unaffordable.[^143] [Import substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Import_substitution "Import substitution") is an officially declared policy and the government proudly reports a reduction by a factor of two in the volume of consumer goods imported. A number of CIS countries are officially exempt from Uzbekistan import duties. Uzbekistan has a Bilateral Investment Treaty with fifty other countries.[^144]

The [Republican Stock Exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent_Stock_Exchange "Tashkent Stock Exchange") (RSE) opened in 1994. The stocks of all Uzbek joint stock companies (around 1,250) are traded on RSE. The number of listed companies as of January 2013 exceeds 110. Securities market volume reached 2 trillion in 2012, and the number is rapidly growing due to the rising interest by companies of attracting necessary resources through the capital market. According to Central Depository as of January 2013 par value of outstanding shares of Uzbek emitters exceeded 9 trillion.

Thanks in part to the recovery of world market prices of gold and cotton (the country's key export commodities), expanded natural gas and some manufacturing exports, and increasing labour migrant transfers, the current account turned into a large surplus (between 9% and 11% of GDP from 2003 to 2005). In 2018, foreign exchange reserves, including gold, totalled around US$25 billion.[^145]

Uzbekistan’s IT sector is one of the fastest-growing parts of the national economy. By 2024, [information and communication technologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_and_communications_technology "Information and communications technology") accounted for around 2.1% of GDP, with steady growth since 2020 and IT service exports reaching about $1 billion. The sector’s expansion is supported by [IT-park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IT_Park_Uzbekistan "IT Park Uzbekistan"), education programs, and the entry of foreign companies.[^146] In 2024, the digital ecosystem Uzum became the country’s first technology [unicorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicorn_\(finance\) "Unicorn (finance)"), valued at over $1 billion, increasing its valuation to $1.5 billion in 2025.[^147]

Foreign exchange reserves amounted in 2010 to US$13 billion.[^148]

Uzbekistan is predicted to be one of the fastest-growing economies in the world (top 26) in future decades, according to a survey by global bank HSBC.[^149] Uzbekistan was ranked 79th in the [Global Innovation Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Innovation_Index "Global Innovation Index") in 2025.[^150] [^151]

## Demographics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Jeunes_Mari%C3%A9s_dans_le_parc_dAk_Saray_%28Shahrisabz%29_%286018352949%29.jpg/250px-Jeunes_Mari%C3%A9s_dans_le_parc_dAk_Saray_%28Shahrisabz%29_%286018352949%29.jpg)

As of 2022, Uzbekistan has the largest population of countries in Central Asia. Its 36 million citizens comprise nearly half the region's total population.[^152] The population of Uzbekistan is very young though it is slowly aging. 23.1% of its people are younger than 16 (2020 estimate).[^122] According to official sources, [Uzbeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbeks "Uzbeks") are a majority (84.5%) of the population. Other ethnic groups include [Tajiks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C4%81jik_people "Tājik people") 4.8%, [Kazakhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhs "Kazakhs") 2.4%, [Karakalpaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakalpaks "Karakalpaks") 2.2%, [Russians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians "Russians") 2.1% and [Tatars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatars "Tatars") 0.5% as of 2021.[^5]

Ethnic composition of the population of Uzbekistan in 2021 as per official estimates:

- [Uzbeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbeks "Uzbeks"), 29.2 million people, or 84.4% of the population;
- [Tajiks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tajiks "Tajiks"), 1.7 million people;

- [Russians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians "Russians"), 720.3 thousand people;
- [Kyrgyz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_people "Kyrgyz people"), 291.6 thousand people;
- [Turkmens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkmens "Turkmens"), 206.2 thousand people;
- [Tatars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatars "Tatars"), 187.3 thousand people;
- [Koreans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koreans "Koreans"), 174.2 thousand people;
- [Ukrainians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ukrainians "Ukrainians"), 67.9 thousand people;
- [Armenians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenians "Armenians"), 50.2 thousand people;
- [Azerbaijanis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azerbaijanis "Azerbaijanis"), 41.2 thousand people;
- [Belarusians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belarusians "Belarusians"), 18.5 thousand people;
- Other nationalities: 376.2 thousand people.[^153]

The percentage of the ethnic Tajik population in Uzbekistan is controversial. While official state tabulations put the number around 5%, the number is said to be an understatement and Western academics put the number at approximately 25–30%.[^154] [^155] [^156] [^157] Uzbekistan has an [ethnic Korean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koryo-saram "Koryo-saram") population that was [forcibly relocated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deportation_of_Koreans_in_the_Soviet_Union "Deportation of Koreans in the Soviet Union") to the region by Stalin from the [Soviet Far East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Far_East "Russian Far East") in 1937–1938. There are also small groups of [Armenians in Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenians_in_Uzbekistan "Armenians in Uzbekistan"), mostly in Tashkent and Samarkand.

The nation is 96% Muslim (mostly [Sunni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunnis "Sunnis"), with a [Shi'a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shi%27a "Shi'a") minority), 2.3% [Eastern Orthodox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox "Eastern Orthodox") and 1.7% other faiths. The U.S. State Department's International Religious Freedom Report 2004 reports that 0.2% of the population are [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist "Buddhist") (these being ethnic Koreans). [Bukharan Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bukharan_Jews "Bukharan Jews") have lived in Central Asia, mostly in Uzbekistan, for thousands of years. There were 94,900 [Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jew "Jew") in Uzbekistan in 1989 [^158] (about 0.5% of the population according to the [1989 census](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Uzbekistan "Ethnic groups in Uzbekistan")), but since the [dissolution of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union "Dissolution of the Soviet Union"), most Central Asian Jews left the region for the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany"), or [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel"). Fewer than 5,000 Jews remained in Uzbekistan in 2007 and they live primarily in Tashkent, Samarkand, and Bukhara.[^159]

[Russians in Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians_in_Uzbekistan "Russians in Uzbekistan") represented 5.5% of the total population in 1989. During the Soviet period, Russians and [Ukrainians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ukrainians "Ukrainians") constituted more than half the population of [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent").[^160] The country counted nearly 1.5 million Russians, 12.5% of the population, in the 1970 census.[^161] After the dissolution of the Soviet Union, significant emigration of ethnic Russians has taken place, mostly for economic reasons.[^162]

In the 1940s, the Crimean Tatars, along with the [Volga Germans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volga_Germans "Volga Germans"), Chechens, Pontic [^163] Greeks, Kumaks and many other nationalities were [deported](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_transfer_in_the_Soviet_Union "Population transfer in the Soviet Union") to Central Asia. Approximately 100,000 [Crimean Tatars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crimean_Tatars "Crimean Tatars") continue to live in Uzbekistan.[^164] The number of [Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greeks_in_Uzbekistan "Greeks in Uzbekistan") in Tashkent has decreased from 35,000 in 1974 to about 12,000 in 2004.[^165] The majority of [Meskhetian Turks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meskhetian_Turks "Meskhetian Turks") left the country after [the pogrom in the Fergana valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fergana_massacre "Fergana massacre") in June 1989.[^166]

Almost 10% of Uzbekistan's labour force works abroad, mostly in Russia and [Kazakhstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan "Kazakhstan").[^167] [^168]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d1/Nukus_Art_Museum.JPG/250px-Nukus_Art_Museum.JPG)

Nukus Art Museum named after Savicky.

Uzbekistan has a 100% literacy rate among adults older than 15 (2019 estimate).[^169]

Life expectancy in Uzbekistan is 75 years average. 72 years among men and 78 years among women.[^170]

President Shavkat Mirziyoyev signed a law in March 2020 that demands a national census take place at least every 10 years.[^171] The population has not been officially counted in over 30 years. In November 2020, the first census was cancelled due to concerns about coronavirus and the sheer size of the task. It now has been scheduled for 2025–2026, with the results expected to be published in 2027.[^172]

### Religion

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Le_minaret_et_la_mosqu%C3%A9e_Kalon_%28Boukhara%2C_Ouzb%C3%A9kistan%29_%285658826884%29.jpg/250px-Le_minaret_et_la_mosqu%C3%A9e_Kalon_%28Boukhara%2C_Ouzb%C3%A9kistan%29_%285658826884%29.jpg)

Mosque of Bukhara

Religion in Uzbekistan ([ARDA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_of_Religion_Data_Archives "Association of Religion Data Archives") 2025 est.) [^173]

1. [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") (94.3%)
2. [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity") (1.17%)
3. [None](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreligion "Irreligion") (4.26%)
4. Other (0.29%)

Uzbekistan is a secular country and Article 61 of its constitution states that religious organisations and associations shall be separated from the state and equal before law. The state shall not interfere in the activity of religious associations.[^174] [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Uzbekistan "Islam in Uzbekistan") is the dominant religion in Uzbekistan, although Soviet power (1924–1991) discouraged the expression of religious belief, and it was repressed during its existence as a [Soviet Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"). The CIA Factbook (2004) estimates that [Muslims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslims "Muslims") constitute 88% of the population, while 9% of the population follow [Russian Orthodox Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church_in_Uzbekistan "Russian Orthodox Church in Uzbekistan"), 3% other religions and non-religious,[^175] while a 2020 [Pew Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pew_Research_Center "Pew Research Center") projection stated that Uzbekistan's population is 96.7% Muslim and [Christians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_in_Uzbekistan "Christianity in Uzbekistan") (mostly [Russian Orthodox Christians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church_in_Uzbekistan "Russian Orthodox Church in Uzbekistan")) comprised 2.3% of the population (630,000).[^176] An estimated 93,000 [Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jews "Jews") lived in the country in the early 1990s.[^177] In addition, there are about 7,400 Zoroastrians left in Uzbekistan, mostly in Tajik areas like [Khojand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khojand "Khojand").[^178]

Despite the predominance of Islam and its rich history in the country, the practice of the faith is far from monolithic. Uzbeks have practiced many versions of Islam. The conflict of Islamic tradition with various agendas of [reform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reform_movement "Reform movement") or [secularisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularisation "Secularisation") throughout the 20th century has left a wide variety of Islamic practices in [Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia").[^177]

The end of Soviet control in Uzbekistan in 1991 did not bring an immediate upsurge of religion-associated [fundamentalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamentalism "Fundamentalism"), as many had predicted, but rather a gradual re-acquaintance with the precepts of the Islamic faith and a gradual resurgence of [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Uzbekistan "Islam in Uzbekistan") in the country.[^179] However, since 2015 there has been a slight increase in [Islamist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamism "Islamism") activity, with small organisations such as the [Islamic Movement of Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Movement_of_Uzbekistan "Islamic Movement of Uzbekistan") declaring allegiance to [Daesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daesh "Daesh") and contributing fighters abroad,[^180] although the [terror threat in Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrorism_in_Uzbekistan "Terrorism in Uzbekistan") itself remains low.[^181]

#### Jewish community

The Jewish community in the Uzbek lands lived for centuries, with occasional hardships during the reigns of certain rulers. During the rule of [Tamerlane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamerlane "Tamerlane") in the 14th century, [Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jews "Jews") contributed greatly to his efforts to rebuild [Samarkand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarkand "Samarkand"), and a great Jewish centre was established there.[^182]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Bukharan_Jews_%28before_1899%29.jpg/250px-Bukharan_Jews_%28before_1899%29.jpg)

Bukharan Jews, c. 1899

After the area came under Russian rule in 1868, Jews were granted equal rights with the local Muslim population.[^182] In that period some 50,000 Jews lived in Samarkand and 20,000 in [Bukhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bukhara "Bukhara").[^182]

After the Russian revolutions in 1917 and the establishment of the Soviet regime, Jewish religious life (as with all religions) became restricted. By 1935 only one synagogue out of 30 remained in Samarkand. Similarly only one synagogue was operational in Bukhara and in Tashkent. Young children were not allowed to enter synagogues. In spite of this underground Jewish community life continued during the Soviet era.[^182]

By 1970 there were 103,000 Jews registered in the [Uzbek SSR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbek_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Uzbek Soviet Socialist Republic").[^182] Since the 1980s most of the Jews of Uzbekistan emigrated to Israel or to the United States of America.[^183] A small community of several thousand remained in the country as of 2013: some 7,000 lived in Tashkent, 3,000 in Bukhara and 700 in Samarkand.[^184]

### Languages

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Adib-i_sani.jpg/250px-Adib-i_sani.jpg)

A page in Uzbek language written in Nastaʿlīq script printed in Tashkent in 1911

The Uzbek language is one of the [Turkic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_languages "Turkic languages"). It belongs to the [Karluk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karluk_languages "Karluk languages") branch of the Turkic language family, which also includes the [Uyghur language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghur_language "Uyghur language"). It is the only official national language and since 1992 is officially written in the [Latin alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_alphabet "Latin alphabet").[^185]

Before the 1920s, the written language of Uzbeks was called Turki (known to Western scholars as [Chagatai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chagatai_language "Chagatai language")) and used the [Nastaʿlīq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasta%CA%BFl%C4%ABq "Nastaʿlīq") script. In 1926 the Latin alphabet was introduced and went through several revisions throughout the 1930s. Finally, in 1940, the [Cyrillic alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrillic_alphabets "Cyrillic alphabets") was introduced by Soviet authorities and was used until the fall of Soviet Union. In 1993 Uzbekistan shifted back to the Latin script ([Uzbek alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbek_alphabet "Uzbek alphabet")), which was modified in 1996 and is being taught in schools since 2000. Educational establishments teach only the Latin notation. At the same time, the Cyrillic notation is common among the older generation.[^186] Even though the Cyrillic notation of Uzbek has now been abolished for official documents, it is still used by a number of some newspapers and websites.

[Karakalpak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakalpak_language "Karakalpak language"), belonging to the [Kipchak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kipchak_languages "Kipchak languages") branch of the Turkic language family and thus closer to [Kazakh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_language "Kazakh language"), is spoken by half a million people, primarily in the [Republic of Karakalpakstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakalpakstan "Karakalpakstan"), and has an official status in that territory.

Although the [Russian language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language "Russian language") is not an official language in the country, it is widely used in many fields as a second official de-facto language. Digital information from the government is bilingual.[^187] [^188] [^189] The country is also home to approximately one million native Russian speakers. Signs throughout the country are both in Uzbek and Russian.[^190] [^191] [^192] [^193] [^194]

The [Tajik language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tajik_language "Tajik language") (a variety of [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_language "Persian language")) is widespread in the cities of [Bukhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bukhara "Bukhara") and [Samarkand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarkand "Samarkand") because of their relatively large population of ethnic [Tajiks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tajik_people "Tajik people").[^156] [^154] [^155] It is also found in large pockets in the [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent") region, and [Kasansay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kosonsoy "Kosonsoy"), [Chust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chust,_Uzbekistan "Chust, Uzbekistan"), [Rishtan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rishton,_Uzbekistan "Rishton, Uzbekistan") and [Sokh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sokh_District "Sokh District") in [Ferghana Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fergana "Fergana"), as well as in [Burchmulla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burchmulla "Burchmulla"), [Ahangaran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Okhangaron_District "Okhangaron District"), Baghistan in the middle [Syr Darya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syr_Darya "Syr Darya") district, and finally in, [Shahrisabz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shahrisabz "Shahrisabz"), [Qarshi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qarshi "Qarshi"), [Kitab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kitob_District "Kitob District") and the river valleys of Kafiringan and Chaganian, forming altogether, approximately 25–30% of the population of Uzbekistan.[^154] [^155] [^156]

In April 2020, a draft bill was introduced in Uzbekistan to regulate the exclusive use of the Uzbek language in government affairs. Under this legislation, government workers could incur fines for doing work in languages other than Uzbek. Though unsuccessful, it was met with criticism by the [Russian Ministry of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(Russia\) "Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Russia)") spokeswoman, [Maria Zakharova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maria_Zakharova "Maria Zakharova").[^195] In response, a group of Uzbek intellectuals signed an open letter arguing for the instatement of Russian as an official language alongside Uzbek, citing historical ties, the large Russian-speaking population in Uzbekistan, and the usefulness of Russian in higher education, together with the argument that only Russian language opened the communication with the other peoples of the region and the literature of the outside world.[^196] The Cyrillic Uzbek alphabet is still widely used, and 862 Russian-language schools are functioning in the country, compared to 1,100 in 1991, despite the fact that the Russian minority there has decreased from 1,7 million in 1990 to nearly 700,000 in 2022. In business, the Russian language outpaces Uzbek. Many Uzbeks in urban areas, as of 2019, are feeling more comfortable to speak in Russian, while Uzbek is more present in the agricultural regions. Uzbek did not manage to become a state language, and many blame the [intelligentsia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligentsia "Intelligentsia").[^197]

On September 15, 2020, citizenship amendments came into force that required foreigners to have proficiency in the Uzbek language before becoming citizens.[^198]

## Communications

According to the official source report, as of 10 March 2008, the number of cellular phone users in Uzbekistan reached 7 million, up from 3.7 million on 1 July 2007.[^199] Mobile users in 2017 were more than 24 million.[^200] The largest mobile operator in terms of number of subscribers is MTS-Uzbekistan (former [Uzdunrobita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzdunrobita "Uzdunrobita") and part of Russian Mobile TeleSystems) and it is followed by Beeline (part of Russia's Beeline) and UCell (ex Coscom) (originally part of the U.S. MCT Corp., now a subsidiary of the Nordic/Baltic telecommunication company [TeliaSonera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TeliaSonera "TeliaSonera") AB).[^201]

As of 2019, the estimated number of internet users was more than 22 million [^202] or about 52% of the population.[^203]

### Censorship

[Censorship in Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_media_in_Uzbekistan#Censorship "Mass media in Uzbekistan") exists and in October 2012 the government toughened [internet censorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_censorship "Internet censorship") by blocking access to proxy servers.[^204] [Reporters Without Borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reporters_Without_Borders "Reporters Without Borders") has named Uzbekistan's government an "Enemy of the Internet" and government control over the internet has increased dramatically since the start of the [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring").[^205]

The press in Uzbekistan practices [self-censorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-censorship "Self-censorship") and foreign journalists have been gradually expelled from the country since the [Andijan massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andijan_massacre "Andijan massacre") of 2005 when government troops fired into crowds of protesters killing 187 according to official reports and estimates of several hundred by unofficial and witness accounts.[^205]

## Transportation

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Toshkent_Railway_Station.jpg/250px-Toshkent_Railway_Station.jpg)

Central Station of Tashkent

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Afrosiyob_Express_Train_in_Station_-_Samarkand_-_Uzbekistan_%287502824436%29_%283%29.jpg/250px-Afrosiyob_Express_Train_in_Station_-_Samarkand_-_Uzbekistan_%287502824436%29_%283%29.jpg)

The Afrosiyob high-speed train

[Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent"), the nation's capital and largest city, has a four-line [metro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent_Metro "Tashkent Metro") built in 1977, and expanded in 2001 after ten years' independence from the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"). Uzbekistan and Kazakhstan are currently the only two countries in Central Asia with a subway system. It is promoted as one of the cleanest systems in the former Soviet Union.[^206] The stations are exceedingly ornate. For example, the station *[Kosmonavtlar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kosmonavtlar_\(Tashkent_Metro\) "Kosmonavtlar (Tashkent Metro)")* built in 1984 is decorated using a [space travel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_spaceflight "Human spaceflight") theme to recognise the achievements of humankind in space exploration and to commemorate the role of [Vladimir Dzhanibekov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Dzhanibekov "Vladimir Dzhanibekov"), the Soviet [cosmonaut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmonaut "Cosmonaut") of Uzbek origin. A statue of Vladimir Dzhanibekov stands near a station entrance.

There are government-operated trams and buses running across the city. There are also many taxis, registered and unregistered. Uzbekistan has plants that produce modern cars. The car production is supported by the government and the Korean auto company [Daewoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daewoo "Daewoo"). In May 2007 [UzDaewooAuto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UzDaewooAuto "UzDaewooAuto"), the car maker, signed a strategic agreement with General Motors-Daewoo Auto and Technology ([GMDAT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GM_Daewoo "GM Daewoo"), see [GM Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GM_Uzbekistan "GM Uzbekistan") also).[^207] The government bought a stake in Turkey's Koc in [SamKochAvto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SamKochAvto "SamKochAvto"), a producer of small buses and lorries. Afterward, it signed an agreement with [Isuzu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isuzu "Isuzu") Motors of Japan to produce Isuzu buses and lorries.[^208]

Train links connect many towns in Uzbekistan, as well as neighbouring former republics of the Soviet Union. Moreover, after independence two fast-running train systems were established. Uzbekistan launched the first [high-speed railway in Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent%E2%80%93Samarkand_high-speed_rail_line "Tashkent–Samarkand high-speed rail line") in September 2011 between [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent") and [Samarqand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarqand "Samarqand"). The new high-speed electric train [Talgo 250](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talgo_250 "Talgo 250"), called *Afrosiyob*, was manufactured by [Patentes Talgo S.L.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talgo "Talgo") (Spain) and took its first trip from Tashkent to Samarkand on 26 August 2011.[^209]

A large aircraft manufacturing plant was built during the Soviet era – [Tashkent Chkalov Aviation Manufacturing Plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent_Aviation_Production_Association "Tashkent Aviation Production Association") or ТАПОиЧ in Russian. The plant originated during World War II, when production facilities were evacuated south and east to avoid capture by advancing Nazi forces. Until the late 1980s, the plant was one of the leading aeroplane production centres in the USSR. With dissolution of the Soviet Union, its manufacturing equipment became outdated; most of the workers were laid off. Now it produces only a few planes a year, but with interest from Russian companies growing, there are rumours of production-enhancement plans.

### Civil Aviation

After the declaration of independence of the Republic of Uzbekistan, in accordance with the Decree of the President of the Republic of Uzbekistan dated January 28, 1992, the National Airline [Uzbekistan Airways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan_Airways "Uzbekistan Airways") was created on the basis of the Civil Aviation Department of Uzbekistan, which was under the jurisdiction of the former Ministry of [Civil aviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_aviation "Civil aviation") of the Union. Air transport is one of the leading sectors of the economy of Uzbekistan, serves the development of international, economic, diplomatic, cultural ties of the country with the outside world.[^210] In [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent"), [Nukus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nukus "Nukus"), [Samarkand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarkand "Samarkand"), [Bukhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bukhara "Bukhara"), [Urgench](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urgench "Urgench"), [Termez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Termez "Termez"), [Karshi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karshi "Karshi"), [Namangan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Namangan "Namangan"), [Fergana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fergana "Fergana"), [Navoi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navoi "Navoi") there are airports equipped with modern equipment. [Tashkent International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent_International_Airport "Tashkent International Airport") is the largest international airport in the [Central Asian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asian "Central Asian") region. The airports of [Bukhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bukhara "Bukhara"), [Samarkand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarkand "Samarkand"), [Urgenchalso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urgench "Urgench") have international status. The National Airline [Uzbekistan Airways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan_Airways "Uzbekistan Airways") regularly performs 20 international flights. 44 representative offices have been opened in the cities of [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe"), [America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/America "America"), [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia") and the [CIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States "Commonwealth of Independent States") countries.[^211] Over the years of independence, the Government of the Republic of Uzbekistan has allocated 1 billion 200 million dollars to the [aviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aviation "Aviation") industry. Investments in the amount of US dollars were made and a developed modern infrastructure was built. All [international flights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_flight "International flight") are carried out on [Boeing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing "Boeing") 767/757, A-310, U-85 aircraft. The national airline [Uzbekistan Airways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan_Airways "Uzbekistan Airways") cooperates with large European companies [Airbus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airbus "Airbus") Industry, American companies [Boeing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing "Boeing"), Russian design bureau Ilyushin, German and French firms in various fields.

## Military

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Ukbekistani_troops.jpg/250px-Ukbekistani_troops.jpg)

Uzbek troops during a cooperative operation exercise

With 120,000 servicemen, Uzbekistan possesses the largest armed forces in [Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia"). Military structure is largely inherited from the [Turkestan Military District](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkestan_Military_District "Turkestan Military District") of the [Soviet Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Ground_Forces "Soviet Ground Forces").[^212] The Uzbek Armed Forces' equipment is standard, mostly consisting of post-Soviet inheritance and newly crafted Russian and some American equipment.

The Government has accepted the arms control obligations of the former Soviet Union, acceded to the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (as a non-nuclear state), and supported an active programme by the U.S. [Defence Threat Reduction Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defense_Threat_Reduction_Agency "Defense Threat Reduction Agency") (DTRA) in western Uzbekistan ([Nukus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nukus "Nukus") and [Vozrozhdeniye Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vozrozhdeniye_Island "Vozrozhdeniye Island")). The Government of Uzbekistan spends about 3.7% of GDP on the military but has received a growing infusion of Foreign Military Financing (FMF) and other security assistance funds since 1998.

Following 11 September 2001 terrorist attacks in the U.S., Uzbekistan approved the [U.S. Central Command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Central_Command "U.S. Central Command") 's request for access to an air base, the [Karshi-Khanabad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karshi-Khanabad "Karshi-Khanabad") airfield, in southern Uzbekistan. However, Uzbekistan demanded that the U.S. withdraw from the airbases after the [Andijan massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andijan_massacre "Andijan massacre") and the U.S. reaction to this massacre. The last US troops left Uzbekistan in November 2005.[^213] In 2020, it was revealed that the former US base was contaminated with radioactive materials which may have resulted in unusually high cancer rates in US personnel stationed there. Despite this, the government of Uzbekistan has denied this statement, claiming that there has never been such a case.[^214]

On 23 June 2006, Uzbekistan became a full participant in the [Collective Security Treaty Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_Security_Treaty_Organization "Collective Security Treaty Organization") (CSTO), but informed the CSTO to suspend its membership in June 2012.[^215]

## Culture

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Taschkent_-_Art_of_Uzbekistan.jpg/250px-Taschkent_-_Art_of_Uzbekistan.jpg)

Traditional Uzbek pottery

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Theatre_Alisher_Navoi.JPG/250px-Theatre_Alisher_Navoi.JPG)

Navoi Opera Theater in Tashkent

Uzbekistan has a wide mix of ethnic groups and cultures, with the [Uzbek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbeks "Uzbeks") being the majority group. In 1995 about 71% of Uzbekistan's population was Uzbek. The chief minority groups were Russians (8%), [Tajiks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tajiks "Tajiks") (3–4.7%),[^154] [^155] [^156] [^157] [Kazakhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhs "Kazakhs") (4%), [Tatars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatars "Tatars") (2.5%) and [Karakalpaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakalpaks "Karakalpaks") (2%). It is said, however, that non-Uzbeks decline as Russians and other minority groups slowly leave and Uzbeks return from other parts of the former [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union").

Embroidery from Uzbekistan

When Uzbekistan gained independence in 1991, there was concern that [Muslim fundamentalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_fundamentalism "Muslim fundamentalism") would spread across the region.[^216] The expectation was that a country long denied freedom of religious practice would undergo a very rapid increase in the expression of its dominant faith.

According to a 2009 Pew Research report, Uzbekistan's population is 96.3% Muslim; around 54% identifies as non-denominational Muslim, 18% as Sunni and 1% as Shia. Furthermore, 11% say they belong to a Sufi order.[^217]

### Media

Mass media in Uzbekistan have undergone significant transformations since the country's independence in 1991. The government initially maintained strict control over all forms of media, with the state owning almost all [print media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Print_media "Print media") and broadcasting outlets. However, since the early 2000s, there has been a gradual shift toward a more open media environment, although state [influence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence "Sphere of influence") remains strong.[^218] [^219]

The [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Uzbekistan "Government of Uzbekistan") controls major television channels, radio stations, and print publications, while the internet, though growing, faces censorship. Despite these constraints, the rise of [independent media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independent_media "Independent media") outlets and online platforms has slowly contributed to a diversification of information sources in the country. Nevertheless, journalists continue to face harassment, and [self-censorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-censorship "Self-censorship") remains a significant issue in Uzbekistan's media landscape.[^218]

### Music

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Boukhara_4696a.jpg/250px-Boukhara_4696a.jpg)

Silk and Spice Festival in Bukhara

Central Asian classical music is called [Shashmaqam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shashmaqam "Shashmaqam"), which arose in [Bukhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bukhara "Bukhara") in the late 16th century when that city was a regional capital.[^220] Shashmaqam is closely related to [Azerbaijani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azerbaijan "Azerbaijan") [Mugam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mugam "Mugam") and [Uyghur muqam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghur_muqam "Uyghur muqam").[^221] The name, which translates as *six maqams*, refers to the structure of the music, which contains six sections in six different [Musical modes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_mode "Musical mode"), similar to classical [Persian traditional music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_traditional_music "Persian traditional music"). Interludes of spoken [Sufi poetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sufi_poetry "Sufi poetry") interrupt the music, typically beginning at a lower register and gradually ascending to a climax before calming back down to the beginning tone.

### Education

Uzbekistan has a high [literacy rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literacy_rate "Literacy rate"), with 99.9% of adults above the age of 15 being able to read and write.[^222] However, with only 76% of the under-15 population currently enrolled in education <sup>(and only 20% of the 3–6 year olds attending pre-school)</sup>, this figure may drop in the future. Students attend school Monday through Saturday during the school year, and education officially concludes at the end of the 11th grade.

Uzbekistan has encountered severe budget shortfalls in its education programme. The education law of 1992 began the process of theoretical reform, but the physical base has deteriorated and curriculum revision has been slow. Corruption within the education system is rampant, with students from wealthier families routinely bribing teachers and school executives to achieve high grades without attending school, or undertaking official examinations.[^223]

Public spending on education in Uzbekistan in 2019 amounted to 5.6% of GDP and 6.2% of GDP in 2021. In 2019, of the total expenditure on education, the government spent the largest share on [secondary education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_education "Secondary education") (about 65%), [preschool education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preschool_education "Preschool education") (21%), [higher education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher_education "Higher education") (10%) and [secondary special education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_special_education "Secondary special education") (4%).

In 2023, [UNICEF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNICEF "UNICEF") supported the [Government of Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Uzbekistan "Government of Uzbekistan") in developing a Roadmap for Education Reform, which aims to bring together the efforts of the government, development partners, teachers, parents, students and school communities to achieve the outlined strategic goals of transformative quality education.[^224]

Several universities, including [Westminster University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westminster_International_University_in_Tashkent "Westminster International University in Tashkent"), [Turin University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turin_University "Turin University"), [Management University Institute of Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Management_Development_Institute_of_Singapore_in_Tashkent "Management Development Institute of Singapore in Tashkent"), [Bucheon University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bucheon_University "Bucheon University") in [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent"), [TEAM University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TEAM_University_Tashkent "TEAM University Tashkent") and [Inha University Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inha_University_Tashkent "Inha University Tashkent") maintain a campus in Tashkent offering English language courses across several disciplines. The [Russian-language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language "Russian language") high education is provided by most national universities, including foreign [Moscow State University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow_State_University "Moscow State University") and [Gubkin Russian State University of Oil and Gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gubkin_Russian_State_University_of_Oil_and_Gas "Gubkin Russian State University of Oil and Gas"), maintaining campuses in Tashkent. As of 2019, [Webster University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Webster_University "Webster University"), in partnership with the Ministry of Education (now Ministry of Higher Education, Science and Innovation), has opened a graduate school offering an MBA in Project Management and a MA in Teaching English as a Second Language (TESL).

There are three Islamic institutes and an academy in Uzbekistan. They are Tashkent islamic institute, Mir Arab high school, School of hadith knowledge, International islamic academy of Uzbekistan.

### Cuisine

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Plov.jpg/250px-Plov.jpg)

*[Palov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilaf "Pilaf")*

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Uzbek_Manti_%28bright%29.jpg/250px-Uzbek_Manti_%28bright%29.jpg)

Uzbek manti

Uzbek cuisine is influenced by local [agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture"); since there is a great deal of grain farming in Uzbekistan, bread and noodles are of importance and Uzbek cuisine has been characterised as "noodle-rich". [Mutton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamb_and_mutton "Lamb and mutton") is a popular variety of meat due to the abundance of [sheep](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheep "Sheep") in the country and it is part of various Uzbek dishes.[^226]

Uzbekistan's signature dish is [*palov*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilaf "Pilaf") (or *plov*), a main course typically made with rice, meat, carrots, and onions, though it was not available to ordinary people until the 1930s. There are many regional variations of the dish. Often the fat found near the sheep tail, *qurdiuq*, is used. In the past, the cooking of *palov* was reserved for men, but the Soviets allowed women to cook it as well. Since then, it seems, the old gender roles have been restored.[^227]

Other notable national dishes include [shurpa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chorba "Chorba"), a soup made of large pieces of fatty meat (usually mutton), and fresh vegetables;[^228] [norin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naryn_\(dish\) "Naryn (dish)") and *[laghman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laghman_\(food\) "Laghman (food)")*, noodle-based dishes that may be served as a soup or a main course;[^229] [manti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manti_\(food\) "Manti (food)"), [chuchvara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joshpara "Joshpara"), and *[somsa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samsa_\(food\) "Samsa (food)")*, stuffed pockets of [dough](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dough "Dough") served as an appetiser or a main course; [dimlama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimlama "Dimlama"), a meat and vegetable stew; and various [kebabs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kebab "Kebab"), usually served as a main course.

[Green tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_tea "Green tea") is the national hot beverage consumed throughout the day; [teahouses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahouse "Teahouse") (*chaikhanas*) are of cultural importance.[^230] [Black tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_tea "Black tea") is preferred in [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent"), but both green and black teas are consumed daily, without milk or sugar. Tea always accompanies a meal, but it is also a drink of hospitality that is automatically offered: green or black to every guest.[^231] [Ayran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayran "Ayran"), a chilled yogurt drink, is popular in summer.[^232]

The use of alcohol is less widespread than in the West, but wine is comparatively popular for a Muslim nation as Uzbekistan is largely secular. Uzbekistan has 14 wineries, the oldest and most famous being the Khovrenko Winery in [Samarkand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarkand "Samarkand") (established in 1927).[^233] A number of vineyards in and around Tashkent are also growing in popularity, including Chateau Hamkor.[^234]

### Sport

Before Uzbekistan's independence in 1991, the country was part of the Soviet Union [football](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union_national_football_team "Soviet Union national football team"), [rugby union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union_national_rugby_union_team "Soviet Union national rugby union team"), [basketball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union_men%27s_national_basketball_team "Soviet Union men's national basketball team"), [ice hockey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union_men%27s_national_ice_hockey_team "Soviet Union men's national ice hockey team"), and handball national teams. After independence, Uzbekistan created its own [football](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan_national_football_team "Uzbekistan national football team"), [rugby union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan_national_rugby_union_team "Uzbekistan national rugby union team"), [basketball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan_men%27s_national_basketball_team "Uzbekistan men's national basketball team") and [futsal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan_national_futsal_team "Uzbekistan national futsal team") national teams.

#### Football

[Football](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_football "Association football") is the most popular sport in Uzbekistan. Uzbekistan's premier football league is the [Uzbek Super League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan_Super_League "Uzbekistan Super League"), which has consisted of 16 teams since 2015. The current champions (2022) are [FC Pakhtakor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakhtakor_Tashkent_FK "Pakhtakor Tashkent FK"). [Pakhtakor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakhtakor_Tashkent_FK "Pakhtakor Tashkent FK") holds the record for the most Uzbekistan champion titles, having won the league ten times. Uzbekistan's football clubs regularly participate in the [AFC Champions League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AFC_Champions_League "AFC Champions League") and the [AFC Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AFC_Cup "AFC Cup"). [FC Nasaf Qarashi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FC_Nasaf "FC Nasaf") won the [AFC Cup in 2011](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_AFC_Cup "2011 AFC Cup"), the first international club cup for Uzbek football.[^235] [^236]

In 2025, Uzbek international [footballer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Footballer "Footballer") [Abdukodir Khusanov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdukodir_Khusanov "Abdukodir Khusanov"), a [center-back](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center-back "Center-back"), transferred from [Lens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RC_Lens "RC Lens") to [Manchester City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchester_City "Manchester City") on a four-and-a-half-year deal, becoming the first Uzbek player to compete in the [Premier League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_League "Premier League").[^237]

#### Cycling

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Bunyodkor_stadium2_b.jpg/250px-Bunyodkor_stadium2_b.jpg)

Milliy Stadium in Tashkent

Uzbekistan is home to former racing cyclist [Djamolidine Abdoujaparov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djamolidine_Abdoujaparov "Djamolidine Abdoujaparov"). Abdoujaparov won the [green jersey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_jersey "Green jersey") points contest in the [Tour de France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tour_de_France "Tour de France") three times.[^238] Abdoujaparov was a specialist at winning stages in tours or one-day races when the bunch or [peloton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peloton "Peloton") would finish together. He would often 'sprint' in the final kilometer and had a reputation as being dangerous in these bunch sprints as he would weave from side to side. This reputation earned him the nickname 'The Terror of Tashkent'.[^239]

#### Wrestling

[Artur Taymazov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artur_Taymazov "Artur Taymazov") won Uzbekistan's inaugural wrestling medal at the [2000 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2000_Summer_Olympics "2000 Summer Olympics"), followed by three Olympic gold medals in Men's 120 kg in [2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2004_Summer_Olympics "2004 Summer Olympics"), [2008](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics "2008 Summer Olympics") and [2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics "2012 Summer Olympics"). His 2008 gold was taken away in 2017 after a re-testing of samples from the Beijing Games and Taymazov was later stripped of his London 2012 Olympic gold medal after re-analysis of stored samples in 2019.[^240] His London gold had made him the most successful freestyle competitor in Olympic history. He is the 60th athlete to be disqualified from the London Olympics after the event.[^241]

Uzbekistan is the home of the International [Kurash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurash "Kurash") Association.[^242] Kurash is an internationalised and modernised form of traditional Uzbek wrestling.

#### Boxing

[Boxing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxing "Boxing") is also one of the most popular sports in Uzbekistan. Uzbekistan has four Olympic champions, winners of two silver and eight bronze medals at the Olympic Games in this sport. The country also has more medal winners in boxing at the Asian Games, the [Asian Championships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_Boxing_Championships "Asian Boxing Championships") and the [World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBA_World_Boxing_Championships "IBA World Boxing Championships"). At the [2016 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_Summer_Olympics "2016 Summer Olympics"), Uzbekistan [boxing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxing_at_the_2016_Summer_Olympics "Boxing at the 2016 Summer Olympics") team took first place in the medal standings in boxing, winning 3 gold, 2 silver and 2 bronze medals. At the [2024 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Summer_Olympics "2024 Summer Olympics"), the Uzbekistan boxing team performed even better: five uzbek boxers [were awarded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxing_at_the_2024_Summer_Olympics "Boxing at the 2024 Summer Olympics") gold medals.[^243]

[Ruslan Chagaev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruslan_Chagaev "Ruslan Chagaev") is a former professional boxer representing Uzbekistan in the WBA. He won the WBA champion title in 2007 after defeating Nikolai Valuev.[^244] Chagaev defended his title twice before losing it to Vladimir Klitschko in 2009. Another young talented boxer [Hasanboy Dusmatov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasanboy_Dusmatov "Hasanboy Dusmatov"), light flyweight champion at the [2016 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_Summer_Olympics "2016 Summer Olympics"), won the [Val Barker Trophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Val_Barker_Trophy "Val Barker Trophy") for the outstanding male boxer of Rio 2016 on 21 August 2016.[^245] On 21 December 2016 Dusmatov was honoured with the AIBA Boxer of the Year award at a 70-year anniversary event of [AIBA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Boxing_Association "International Boxing Association").[^246]

#### Ice hockey

[Humo Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humo_Tashkent "Humo Tashkent"), a professional ice hockey team, was established in 2019 with the aim of joining the [Kontinental Hockey League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kontinental_Hockey_League "Kontinental Hockey League") (KHL).[^247] Humo joined the second-tier [Supreme Hockey League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Hockey_League "Supreme Hockey League") (VHL) for the 2019–20 season. Humo play their games at the [Humo Ice Dome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humo_Ice_Dome "Humo Ice Dome"), which cost over €175 million in construction; both the team and arena derive their name from the mythical [Huma bird](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huma_bird "Huma bird"), a symbol of happiness and freedom.[^248] [Uzbekistan Hockey Federation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan_Hockey_Federation "Uzbekistan Hockey Federation") (UHF) began preparation for forming a national ice hockey team and joining [IIHF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Ice_Hockey_Federation "International Ice Hockey Federation") competitions.[^249]

#### Tennis

[Tennis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tennis "Tennis") is a very popular sport in Uzbekistan, especially after Uzbekistan's sovereignty in 1991. Uzbekistan has its own Tennis Federation called the "UTF" (Uzbekistan Tennis Federation), created in 2002.[^250] Uzbekistan also hosts an International WTA tennis tournament, the "Tashkent Open", held in Uzbekistan's capital city. This tournament has been held since 1999, and is played on outdoor hard courts. The most notable active players from Uzbekistan are [Denis Istomin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denis_Istomin "Denis Istomin") and [Akgul Amanmuradova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akgul_Amanmuradova "Akgul Amanmuradova").[^251]

#### Chess

[Chess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chess "Chess") is quite popular in Uzbekistan. The country boasts [Rustam Kasimdzhanov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rustam_Kasimdzhanov "Rustam Kasimdzhanov"), who was the [FIDE World Chess Champion in 2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FIDE_World_Chess_Championship_2004 "FIDE World Chess Championship 2004"), and many junior players like the 2021 [World Rapid Chess Champion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Rapid_Chess_Championship "World Rapid Chess Championship") [Nodirbek Abdusattorov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nodirbek_Abdusattorov "Nodirbek Abdusattorov") and the [Chess World Cup 2025](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chess_World_Cup_2025 "Chess World Cup 2025") winner [Javokhir Sindarov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Javokhir_Sindarov "Javokhir Sindarov").[^252] [^253] The Uzbek team consisting of GM [Nodirbek Abdusattorov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nodirbek_Abdusattorov "Nodirbek Abdusattorov"), GM [Nodirbek Yakubboev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nodirbek_Yakubboev "Nodirbek Yakubboev"), GM [Javokhir Sindarov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Javokhir_Sindarov "Javokhir Sindarov"), GM [Shamsiddin Vokhidov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shamsiddin_Vokhidov "Shamsiddin Vokhidov") and GM [Jahongir Vakhidov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jahongir_Vakhidov "Jahongir Vakhidov") won gold at the [44th Chess Olympiad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/44th_Chess_Olympiad "44th Chess Olympiad") in Chennai.[^254]
