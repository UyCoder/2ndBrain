**Myanmar**,[^5] officially the **Republic of the Union of Myanmar** [^6] and also referred to as **Burma** (the official English name until 1989), is a country in northwest [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia"). It is the largest country by area in [Mainland Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland_Southeast_Asia "Mainland Southeast Asia") and has a population of about 55 million. It is bordered by [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") and [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh") to the northwest, [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") to the northeast, [Laos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laos "Laos") and [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand") to the east and southeast, and the [Andaman Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andaman_Sea "Andaman Sea") and the [Bay of Bengal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal "Bay of Bengal") to the south and southwest. The country's capital city is [Naypyidaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naypyidaw "Naypyidaw"), while [its largest city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_cities_and_largest_towns_in_Myanmar "List of cities and largest towns in Myanmar") is [Yangon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yangon "Yangon") (formerly Rangoon).[^14]

Early civilisations in the area included the [Tibeto-Burman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibeto-Burman "Tibeto-Burman") -speaking [Pyu city-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyu_city-states "Pyu city-states") in [Upper Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_Myanmar "Upper Myanmar") and the [Mon kingdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_kingdoms "Mon kingdoms") in [Lower Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_Myanmar "Lower Myanmar").[^21] In the 9th century, the [Bamar people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamar_people "Bamar people") entered the upper [Irrawaddy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrawaddy_River "Irrawaddy River") valley, and following the establishment of the [Pagan Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pagan_Kingdom "Pagan Kingdom") in the 1050s, the [Burmese language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_language "Burmese language") and [culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Myanmar "Culture of Myanmar") and [Theravada Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism_in_Myanmar "Buddhism in Myanmar") slowly became dominant in the country. The [Pagan Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pagan_Kingdom "Pagan Kingdom") fell to [Mongol invasions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Mongol_invasion_of_Burma "First Mongol invasion of Burma"), and several warring states emerged. In the 16th century, reunified by the [Taungoo dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taungoo_dynasty "Taungoo dynasty"), the country became the largest empire in the [history of Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Southeast_Asia "History of Southeast Asia") for a short period.[^22] The early 19th-century [Konbaung dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konbaung_dynasty "Konbaung dynasty") ruled over an area that included modern Myanmar and briefly controlled [Assam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahom_kingdom "Ahom kingdom"), the [Lushai Hills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mizo_Chieftainship "Mizo Chieftainship"), and [Manipur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manipur_Kingdom "Manipur Kingdom") as well. The British [East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_India_Company "East India Company") seized control of the administration of Myanmar after three [Anglo-Burmese Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Burmese_Wars "Anglo-Burmese Wars") in the 19th century, and the country became a [British colony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_rule_in_Burma "British rule in Burma"). After a brief [Japanese occupation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_Burma "Japanese occupation of Burma"), Myanmar was reconquered by the Allies. In 1948, Myanmar declared [independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_Day_\(Myanmar\) "Independence Day (Myanmar)") under the terms of the [Burma Independence Act 1947](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burma_Independence_Act_1947 "Burma Independence Act 1947").

Myanmar's post-independence history has been checkered by continuous unrest and conflict. A [coup d'état in 1962](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1962_Burmese_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1962 Burmese coup d'état") resulted in a [military dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_dictatorship "Military dictatorship") under the [Burma Socialist Programme Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burma_Socialist_Programme_Party "Burma Socialist Programme Party"). The [8888 Uprising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8888_Uprising "8888 Uprising") in 1988 resulted in a transition to a [multi-party system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-party_system "Multi-party system") two years later, but the country's [post-uprising military junta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Peace_and_Development_Council "State Peace and Development Council"), led by members of the [Tatmadaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatmadaw "Tatmadaw"), refused to cede power and continued to rule the country directly until 2011, when the junta was officially dissolved following the [2010 general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Myanmar_general_election "2010 Myanmar general election"). A nominally [civilian government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_authority "Civil authority") was installed and longtime political prisoners like [Aung San Suu Kyi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi "Aung San Suu Kyi") were released, but the military retained power over key government operations. The [2015 general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015_Myanmar_general_election "2015 Myanmar general election") led to a transition of power to the [National League for Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_League_for_Democracy "National League for Democracy"), although the country's treatment of its [ethnic minorities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ethnic_groups_in_Myanmar "List of ethnic groups in Myanmar"), particularly the [Rohingya genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohingya_genocide "Rohingya genocide"), continued to be a source of international tension.[^23] Following the [2020 general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Myanmar_general_election "2020 Myanmar general election"), in which the NLD won a clear majority in both houses, the Tatmadaw again seized power in the [2021 coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Myanmar_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "2021 Myanmar coup d'état"). The coup led to [widespread protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_protests_\(2021%E2%80%93present\) "Myanmar protests (2021–present)") and has been marked by violent [political repression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_repression "Political repression") by the military, as well as the outbreak of a [civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_civil_war_\(2021%E2%80%93present\) "Myanmar civil war (2021–present)"). The military again arrested Aung San Suu Kyi in order to remove her from public life. The country transitioned to a nominally civilian government in 2026.

Myanmar is a member of the [East Asia Summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asia_Summit "East Asia Summit"), [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement"), [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN"), and [BIMSTEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BIMSTEC "BIMSTEC"). Myanmar is a Dialogue Partner of the [Shanghai Cooperation Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Cooperation_Organization "Shanghai Cooperation Organization"). The country is very rich in [natural resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_resource "Natural resource"), such as [jade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jade "Jade"), [gems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gems "Gems"), [oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum"), [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas"), [teak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teak_in_Myanmar "Teak in Myanmar") and other [minerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineral "Mineral"), as well as endowed with [renewable energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renewable_energy "Renewable energy"), having the highest [solar power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_power "Solar power") potential compared to other countries of the Great [Mekong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mekong "Mekong") Subregion.[^24] However, Myanmar has long suffered from [instability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instability "Instability"), factional violence, [corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corruption_in_Myanmar "Corruption in Myanmar"), poor infrastructure, [colonial exploitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_exploitation "Colonial exploitation"), and issues with [human development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index").[^25] In 2013, its GDP (nominal) stood at US$56.7 billion and its GDP ([PPP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity "Purchasing power parity")) at US$221.5 billion.[^26] The [income gap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_inequality "Economic inequality") in Myanmar is among the widest in the world, as a large proportion of the [economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Myanmar "Economy of Myanmar") is controlled by [cronies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cronyism "Cronyism") of the [military junta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Administration_Council "State Administration Council").[^27] Myanmar is one of the [least developed countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_developed_countries "Least developed countries") in the world.[^20]

The country remains riven by ethnic strife among its [myriad ethnic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ethnic_groups_in_Myanmar "List of ethnic groups in Myanmar"), and has one of the world's [longest-running ongoing civil wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_conflict "Myanmar conflict"). The [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") and several other organisations have reported consistent and systemic [human rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights_in_Myanmar "Human rights in Myanmar") violations in the country.[^28] Since 2021, more than 600,000 people have been displaced across Myanmar due to the [civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_civil_war_\(2021%E2%80%93present\) "Myanmar civil war (2021–present)") post-coup, with more than three million people in dire need of humanitarian assistance.[^29] According to the [United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_High_Commissioner_for_Refugees "United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees") (UNHCR), there are over 1.3 million people counted as refugees and asylum seekers, and 3.5 million people displaced internally as of December 2024.[^30]

## Etymology

The name of the country has been a matter of dispute and disagreement, particularly in the early 21st century, focusing mainly on the political legitimacy of those using *Myanmar* versus *Burma*.[^31] [^32] Both names derive from the earlier [Burmese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_language "Burmese language") *Mranma* or *Mramma*, an [ethnonym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnonym "Ethnonym") for the majority [Burman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamar_people "Bamar people") ethnic group, of uncertain etymology.[^33] The terms are also popularly thought to derive from [Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit") *Brahma Desha*, 'land of [Brahma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahma "Brahma") '.[^34]

In 1989, the [military government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_government "Military government") [officially changed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_renamed_places_in_Myanmar "List of renamed places in Myanmar") the English names of places, dating back to [Burma's colonial period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_rule_in_Burma "British rule in Burma") or earlier, to romanized versions of their official Burmese names, including that of the country itself: *Burma* became *Myanmar*. The renaming remains a contested issue.[^35] Many political and ethnic opposition groups and countries continue to use *Burma* because they do not recognise the legitimacy or authority of the military government.[^36]

The country's official full name is "Republic of the Union of Myanmar" ([Burmese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_language "Burmese language"): ပြည်ထောင်စုသမ္မတ မြန်မာနိုင်ငံတော်, **Pyihtaungsu Thamada Myanma Naingngantaw**, pronounced [\[pjìdàʊɴzṵ θàɴməda̰ mjəmà nàɪɴŋàɴdɔ̀\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Burmese "Help:IPA/Burmese")). Countries that do not officially recognise that name use the long form "Union of Burma" instead.[^14] [^37] In English, the country is popularly known as either *Burma* or *Myanmar*. In Burmese, the pronunciation depends on the [register](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_\(sociolinguistics\) "Register (sociolinguistics)") used and is either **Bama** (pronounced [\[bəmà\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Burmese "Help:IPA/Burmese")) or **Myamah** (pronounced [\[mjəmà\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Burmese "Help:IPA/Burmese")).[^35]

Official [United States foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_foreign_policy "United States foreign policy") retains *Burma* as the country's name although the [State Department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Department "State Department") 's website lists the country as *Burma (Myanmar)*.[^38] The [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") uses *Myanmar*, as does the [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN") and as do [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia"),[^39] [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia"), [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany"),[^40] [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China"), [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"), [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh"), [Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway "Norway"),[^41] [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan"),[^42] [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland"),[^43] [Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada "Canada") [^44] and [Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ukraine "Ukraine").[^45] Most English-speaking international news media refer to the country by the name *Myanmar*, including the [BBC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC "BBC"),[^46] [CNN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNN "CNN"),[^47] [Al Jazeera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Jazeera "Al Jazeera"),[^48] [Reuters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuters "Reuters"),[^49] and the [Australian Broadcasting Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_Broadcasting_Corporation "Australian Broadcasting Corporation") ([ABC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ABC_News_\(Australia\) "ABC News (Australia)"))/ [Radio Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_Australia "Radio Australia").[^50] Myanmar is known by a name deriving from *Burma* in [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language "Spanish language"), [Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_language "Italian language"), [Romanian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanian_language "Romanian language"), and [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language").[^51] French-language media consistently use *Birmanie*.[^52] [^53]

There are at least nine different pronunciations of the English name *Myanmar*, and no single one is standard. Pronunciations with two syllables are found most often in major British and American dictionaries.[^7] Dictionaries—such as [Collins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collins_English_Dictionary "Collins English Dictionary") —and other sources also report pronunciations with three syllables.[^8] [^54]

As [John Wells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_C._Wells "John C. Wells") explains,[^55] the English spellings of both Myanmar and Burma assume a [non-rhotic variety of English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhoticity_in_English "Rhoticity in English"), in which the phonemic /ər/ and /ɑːr/ (as in B **ur** ma, Myanm **ar**) are produced and received phonetically as /ɜː/ and /ɑː/, bearing more resemblance to the original Burmese pronunciation. However, as an English speaker's interpretation of rhoticity is dependent on their dialect, both rhotic and non-rhotic pronunciations of either name are correct, not due to any actual assimilation of Burmese phonetic features.

## History

### Prehistory

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Pyu_city-states_map.svg/250px-Pyu_city-states_map.svg.png)

Pyu city-states, c. 8th century

Archaeological evidence shows that *[Homo erectus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_erectus "Homo erectus")* lived in the region now known as Myanmar as early as 750,000 years ago, with no more *erectus* finds after 75,000 years ago.[^56] The first evidence of *[Homo sapiens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_sapiens "Homo sapiens")* is dated to about 25,000 BP with discoveries of stone tools in central Myanmar.[^57] Evidence of [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") age domestication of plants and animals and the use of polished stone tools dating to sometime between 10,000 and 6,000 BCE has been discovered in the form of [cave paintings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cave_painting "Cave painting") in [Padah-Lin Caves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Padah-Lin_Caves "Padah-Lin Caves").[^58]

The [Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") arrived c. 1500 BCE when people in the region were turning copper into bronze, growing rice and domesticating poultry and pigs; they were among the first people in the world to do so.[^59] Human remains and artefacts from this era were discovered in [Monywa District](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monywa_District "Monywa District") in the [Sagaing Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagaing_Region "Sagaing Region").[^60] The [Iron Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age") began around 500 BCE with the emergence of iron-working settlements in an area south of present-day [Mandalay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandalay "Mandalay").[^61] Evidence also shows the presence of rice-growing settlements of large villages and small towns that traded with their surroundings as far as China between 500 BCE and 200 CE.[^62] Iron Age Burmese cultures also had influences from outside sources such as [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") and [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand"), as seen in their funerary practices concerning child burials. This indicates some form of communication between groups in Myanmar and other places, possibly through trade.[^63]

### Early city-states

Around the 2nd century BCE the first-known [city-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City-state "City-state") emerged in central Myanmar. These city-states were founded as part of the southward migration by the [Tibeto-Burman-speaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibeto-Burman_languages "Tibeto-Burman languages") [Pyu people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyu_people "Pyu people") from present-day [Yunnan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yunnan "Yunnan").[^64] The Pyu culture was heavily influenced by trade with [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"), importing [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") as well as other cultural, architectural and political concepts, which would have an enduring influence on later Burmese culture and political organisation.[^65]

By the 9th century, several city-states had sprouted across the land: the Pyu in the central dry zone, Mon along the southern coastline and Arakanese along the western littoral. The balance was upset when the Pyu came under repeated attacks from [Nanzhao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanzhao "Nanzhao") between the 750s and the 830s. In the mid-to-late 9th century the [Bamar people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamar_people "Bamar people") founded a small settlement at [Bagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bagan "Bagan"). It was one of several competing city-states until the late 10th century, when it grew in authority and grandeur.[^66]

### Pagan Kingdom

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Bagan%2C_Burma.jpg/250px-Bagan%2C_Burma.jpg)

Pagodas and kyaungs in present-day Bagan, the capital of the Pagan Kingdom

[Pagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pagan_Kingdom "Pagan Kingdom") gradually grew to absorb its surrounding states until the 1050s–1060s when [Anawrahta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anawrahta "Anawrahta") founded the Pagan Kingdom, the first ever unification of the Irrawaddy valley and its periphery. In the 12th and 13th centuries, the Pagan Empire and the [Khmer Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Empire "Khmer Empire") were two main powers in mainland Southeast Asia.[^67] The [Burmese language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_language "Burmese language") and culture gradually became dominant in the upper Irrawaddy valley, eclipsing the [Pyu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyu_language_\(Burma\) "Pyu language (Burma)"), [Mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_language "Mon language") and [Pali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pali "Pali") norms by the late 12th century.[^68] [Theravada Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theravada_Buddhism "Theravada Buddhism") slowly began to spread to the village level, although [Tantric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vajrayana "Vajrayana"), [Mahayana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahayana "Mahayana"), [Hinduism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism"), and [folk religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_folk_religion "Burmese folk religion") remained heavily entrenched. Pagan's rulers and wealthy built over 10,000 [Buddhist temples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist_temple "Buddhist temple") in the Pagan capital zone alone. Repeated Mongol invasions in the late 13th century toppled the four-century-old kingdom in 1287.[^68]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/MysticalMraukU.jpg/250px-MysticalMraukU.jpg)

Temples at Mrauk U

Pagan's collapse was followed by 250 years of political fragmentation that lasted well into the 16th century. Like the Burmans four centuries earlier, [Shan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_people "Shan people") migrants who arrived with the Mongol invasions stayed behind. Several competing [Shan States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_States "Shan States") came to dominate the entire northwestern to eastern arc surrounding the Irrawaddy valley. The valley too was beset with petty states until the late 14th century when two sizeable powers, [Ava Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ava_Kingdom "Ava Kingdom") and [Hanthawaddy kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanthawaddy_kingdom "Hanthawaddy kingdom"), emerged. In the west, a politically fragmented Arakan was under competing influences of its stronger neighbours until the [Kingdom of Mrauk U](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Mrauk_U "Kingdom of Mrauk U") unified the Arakan coastline for the first time in 1437. The kingdom was a protectorate of the [Bengal Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bengal_Sultanate "Bengal Sultanate") at different time periods.[^69]

In the 14th and 15th centuries, Ava fought [wars of unification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forty_Years%27_War "Forty Years' War") but could never quite reassemble the lost empire. Having held off Ava, the [Mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_people "Mon people") -speaking Hanthawaddy entered its golden age, and Arakan went on to become a power in its own right for the next 350 years. In contrast, constant warfare left Ava greatly weakened, and it slowly disintegrated from 1481 onward. In 1527, the Confederation of Shan States conquered Ava and ruled Upper Myanmar until 1555.

Like the Pagan Empire, Ava, [Hanthawaddy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanthawaddy "Hanthawaddy"), and the Shan states were all [multi-ethnic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-ethnic "Multi-ethnic") polities. Despite the wars, cultural synchronisation continued. This period is considered a golden age for [Burmese culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_culture "Burmese culture"). [Burmese literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_literature "Burmese literature") "grew more confident, popular, and stylistically diverse", and the second generation of Burmese law codes, as well as the earliest [pan-Burma chronicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_chronicles "Burmese chronicles") emerged.[^70] Hanthawaddy monarchs introduced religious reforms that later spread to the rest of the country.[^71]

### Taungoo and Konbaung dynasties

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Elefante_Portugues.jpg/250px-Elefante_Portugues.jpg)

Portuguese ruler mounting an Elephant and his soldiers. Philips, Jan Caspar (draughtsman and engraver)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Map_of_Taungoo_Empire_%281580%29.png/250px-Map_of_Taungoo_Empire_%281580%29.png)

Toungoo Empire under Bayinnaung in 1580

Political unification returned in the mid-16th century, through the efforts of [Taungoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toungoo_dynasty "Toungoo dynasty"), a former vassal state of Ava. Taungoo's young, ambitious King [Tabinshwehti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabinshwehti "Tabinshwehti") defeated the more powerful Hanthawaddy in the [Toungoo–Hanthawaddy War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toungoo%E2%80%93Hanthawaddy_War "Toungoo–Hanthawaddy War"). His successor [Bayinnaung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayinnaung "Bayinnaung") went on to conquer a vast swath of mainland Southeast Asia, including the Shan states, [Lan Na](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lan_Na "Lan Na"), Manipur, [Mong Mao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mong_Mao "Mong Mao"), the [Ayutthaya Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayutthaya_Kingdom "Ayutthaya Kingdom"), [Lan Xang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lan_Xang "Lan Xang"), and southern Arakan. However, the largest empire in the history of Southeast Asia unravelled soon after Bayinnaung died in 1581, and the empire collapsed by 1599. Ayutthaya seized Tenasserim and Lan Na, and Portuguese mercenaries established [Portuguese rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_rule "Portuguese rule") at [Thanlyin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thanlyin "Thanlyin") (Syriam).

The dynasty regrouped and defeated the Portuguese in 1613 and Siam in 1614. It restored a smaller, more manageable kingdom, encompassing [Lower Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_Myanmar "Lower Myanmar"), [Upper Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_Myanmar "Upper Myanmar"), [Shan states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_states "Shan states"), [Lan Na](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lan_Na "Lan Na") and upper [Tenasserim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tenasserim_Division "Tenasserim Division"). The restored Toungoo kings created a legal and political framework whose basic features continued well into the 19th century. The crown completely replaced the hereditary chieftainships with appointed governorships in the entire Irrawaddy valley and greatly reduced the hereditary rights of Shan chiefs. Its trade and secular administrative reforms built a prosperous economy for more than 80 years. From the 1720s onward, the kingdom was beset with repeated [Meithei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meitei_people "Meitei people") raids into Upper Myanmar and a nagging rebellion in Lan Na. In 1740, the Mon of Lower Myanmar founded the [Restored Hanthawaddy Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restored_Hanthawaddy_Kingdom "Restored Hanthawaddy Kingdom"). Hanthawaddy forces sacked Ava in 1752, ending the 266-year-old Toungoo Dynasty.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Shwedagon_pagoda.jpg/250px-Shwedagon_pagoda.jpg)

A British 1825 lithograph of Shwedagon Pagoda shows British occupation during the First Anglo-Burmese War.

After the fall of Ava, the [Konbaung–Hanthawaddy War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konbaung%E2%80%93Hanthawaddy_War "Konbaung–Hanthawaddy War") involved one resistance group under [Alaungpaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaungpaya "Alaungpaya") defeating the Restored Hanthawaddy, and by 1759, he had reunited all of Myanmar and Manipur and driven out the French and the British, who had provided arms to Hanthawaddy. By 1770, Alaungpaya's heirs had subdued much of Laos and fought and won the [Burmese–Siamese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese%E2%80%93Siamese_War_\(1765%E2%80%931767\) "Burmese–Siamese War (1765–1767)") against [Ayutthaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayutthaya_Kingdom "Ayutthaya Kingdom") and the [Sino-Burmese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Burmese_War "Sino-Burmese War") against [Qing China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_China "Qing China").[^72]

With Burma preoccupied by the Chinese threat, Ayutthaya recovered its territories by 1770 and went on to capture Lan Na by 1776. Burma and Siam went to war until 1855, but the result was a stalemate, exchanging [Tenasserim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tenasserim_Island "Tenasserim Island") (to Burma) and Lan Na (to Ayutthaya). Faced with a powerful China and a resurgent Ayutthaya in the east, King [Bodawpaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bodawpaya "Bodawpaya") turned west, acquiring Arakan (1785), Manipur (1814), and Assam (1817). It was the second-largest empire in Burmese history, but also one with a long, ill-defined border with [British India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_India "British India").[^73]

In 1826, Burma lost Arakan, [Manipur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manipur "Manipur"), Assam, and Tenasserim to the British in the [First Anglo-Burmese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Anglo-Burmese_War "First Anglo-Burmese War"). In 1852, the British easily seized Lower Burma in the [Second Anglo-Burmese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Anglo-Burmese_War "Second Anglo-Burmese War"). King [Mindon Min](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mindon_Min "Mindon Min") tried to modernise the kingdom and in 1875 narrowly avoided annexation by ceding the [Karenni States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karenni_States "Karenni States"). The British, alarmed by the consolidation of [French Indochina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Indochina "French Indochina"), annexed the remainder of the country in the [Third Anglo-Burmese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_Anglo-Burmese_War "Third Anglo-Burmese War") in 1885.

Konbaung kings extended Restored Toungoo's administrative reforms and achieved unprecedented levels of internal control and external expansion. For the first time in history, the Burmese language and culture came to predominate the entire Irrawaddy valley. The evolution and growth of Burmese literature and theatre continued, aided by an extremely high adult male literacy rate for the era (half of all males and 5% of females).[^74] Nonetheless, the extent and pace of reforms were uneven and ultimately proved insufficient to stem the advance of British colonialism.

### British Burma (1885–1948)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/British_forces_arrival_mandalay1885.jpg/250px-British_forces_arrival_mandalay1885.jpg)

The landing of British forces in Mandalay after the last of the Anglo-Burmese Wars, which resulted in the abdication of the last Burmese monarch, King Thibaw Min

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/British_3-inch_mortars_supporting_the_19th_Indian_Division%27s_advance_along_the_Mawchi_Road%2C_east_of_Toungoo_in_Burma%2C_1944._IND4723.jpg/250px-British_3-inch_mortars_supporting_the_19th_Indian_Division%27s_advance_along_the_Mawchi_Road%2C_east_of_Toungoo_in_Burma%2C_1944._IND4723.jpg)

British troops firing a mortar on the Mawchi road, July 1944

In the 19th century, Burmese rulers sought to maintain their traditional influence in the western areas of Assam, Manipur and Arakan. Pressing them, however, was the [British East India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_East_India "British East India") Company, which was expanding its interests eastwards over the same territory. Over the next 60 years, diplomacy, raids, treaties and compromises, known collectively as the [Anglo-Burmese Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Burmese_Wars "Anglo-Burmese Wars"), continued until Britain proclaimed control over most of Burma.[^75] With the fall of Mandalay, all of Burma came under British rule, being [annexed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_Anglo-Burmese_War#Annexation_and_resistance "Third Anglo-Burmese War") on 1 January 1886.

Throughout the colonial era, many Indians arrived as soldiers, civil servants, construction workers, and traders and, along with the [Anglo-Burmese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Burmese "Anglo-Burmese") community, dominated commercial and civil life in Burma. [Rangoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rangoon "Rangoon") became the capital of British Burma and an important port between [Calcutta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcutta "Calcutta") and [Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore "Singapore"). Burmese resentment was strong and was vented in violent riots that periodically paralysed Rangoon until the 1930s.[^76] Some of the discontent was caused by a disrespect for Burmese culture and traditions. [Buddhist monks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist_monks "Buddhist monks") became the vanguards of the independence movement. [U Wisara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U_Wisara "U Wisara"), an activist monk, died in prison after a 166-day hunger strike.[^77]

On 1 April 1937, Burma became a separately administered colony of Britain, and [Ba Maw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ba_Maw "Ba Maw") became the first Prime Minister and Premier of Burma. Ba Maw was an outspoken advocate for Burmese self-rule, and he opposed the participation of Britain, and by extension Burma, in [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"). He resigned from the Legislative Assembly and was arrested for sedition. In 1940, before [Japan formally entered the war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan_during_World_War_II "Japan during World War II"), [Aung San](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aung_San "Aung San") formed the [Burma Independence Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burma_Independence_Army "Burma Independence Army") in Japan.

As a major battleground, Burma was devastated during World War II by the [Japanese invasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_invasion_of_Burma "Japanese invasion of Burma"). Within months after they entered the war, Japanese troops had advanced on Rangoon, and the British administration had collapsed. A [Burmese Executive Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_Burma "Japanese occupation of Burma") headed by Ba Maw was established by the Japanese in August 1942. [Wingate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orde_Wingate "Orde Wingate") 's British [Chindits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chindits "Chindits") were formed into [long-range penetration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-range_penetration "Long-range penetration") groups trained to operate deep behind Japanese lines.[^78] A similar American unit, [Merrill's Marauders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merrill%27s_Marauders "Merrill's Marauders"), followed the Chindits into the Burmese jungle in 1943.[^79]

Beginning in late 1944, Allied troops launched a [series of offensives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burma_campaign_1944 "Burma campaign 1944") that led to the [end of Japanese rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burma_campaign_1944%E2%80%9345 "Burma campaign 1944–45") in July 1945. The battles were intense, with much of Burma laid waste by the fighting. Overall, the Japanese lost some 150,000 men in Burma, with 1,700 prisoners taken.[^80] Although many Burmese fought initially for the Japanese as part of the Burma Independence Army, many Burmese, mostly from the ethnic minorities, served in the British Burma Army.[^81] The [Burma National Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burma_National_Army "Burma National Army") and the Arakan National Army fought with the Japanese from 1942 to 1944 but switched allegiance to the Allied side in 1945. Overall, 170,000 to 250,000 Burmese civilians died during World War II.[^82]

Following World War II, [Aung San](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aung_San "Aung San") negotiated the [Panglong Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panglong_Agreement "Panglong Agreement") with ethnic leaders that guaranteed the independence of Burma as a unified state. [Aung Zan Wai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aung_Zan_Wai "Aung Zan Wai"), Pe Khin, [Bo Hmu Aung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bo_Hmu_Aung "Bo Hmu Aung"), Sir Maung Gyi, Sein Mya Maung, [Myoma U Than Kywe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myoma_U_Than_Kywe "Myoma U Than Kywe") were among the negotiators of the historic [Panglong Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panglong_Conference "Panglong Conference"), which was negotiated with Bamar leader General Aung San and other ethnic leaders in 1947. In 1947, Aung San became Deputy Chairman of the Executive Council of Burma, a transitional government. But in July 1947, political rivals [^83] [assassinated Aung San](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_Martyrs%27_Day "Burmese Martyrs' Day") and several cabinet members.[^84]

### Independence (1948–1962)

On 4 January 1948, the nation became an independent republic, under the terms of the [Burma Independence Act 1947](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burma_Independence_Act_1947 "Burma Independence Act 1947"). The new country was named the *Union of Burma*, with [Sao Shwe Thaik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sao_Shwe_Thaik "Sao Shwe Thaik") as its first president and [U Nu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U_Nu "U Nu") as its first prime minister. Unlike most other former British colonies and overseas territories, Burma did not become a member of the [Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations"). A [bicameral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicameral "Bicameral") parliament was formed, consisting of a [Chamber of Deputies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies_\(Burma\) "Chamber of Deputies (Burma)") and a [Chamber of Nationalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chamber_of_Nationalities "Chamber of Nationalities"),[^85] and [multi-party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-party "Multi-party") elections were held in [1951–1952](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1951%E2%80%9352_Burmese_general_election "1951–52 Burmese general election"), [1956](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1956_Burmese_general_election "1956 Burmese general election") and [1960](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1960_Burmese_general_election "1960 Burmese general election").

The geographical area Burma encompasses today can be traced to the [Panglong Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panglong_Agreement "Panglong Agreement"), which combined Burma Proper, which consisted of Lower Burma and Upper Burma, and the Frontier Areas, which had been administered separately by the British.[^86]

In 1961, [U Thant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U_Thant "U Thant"), the Union of Burma's Permanent Representative to the United Nations and former secretary to the prime minister, was elected [Secretary-General of the United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secretary-General_of_the_United_Nations "Secretary-General of the United Nations"), a position he held for ten years.[^87]

When the non-Burman ethnic groups pushed for autonomy or federalism, alongside having a weak civilian government at the centre, the military leadership staged a coup d'état in 1962. Though incorporated in the 1947 Constitution, successive military governments construed the use of the term ' [federalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalism "Federalism") ' as being anti-national, anti-unity and pro-disintegration.[^88]

### Military rule (1962–2011)

On 2 March 1962, the military led by General [Ne Win](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ne_Win "Ne Win") [took control of Burma through a coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1962_Burmese_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1962 Burmese coup d'état"), and the government has been under direct or indirect control by the military since then. Between 1962 and 1974, Myanmar was ruled by a [revolutionary council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_Revolutionary_Council "Union Revolutionary Council") headed by the general. Almost all aspects of society (business, media, production) were [nationalised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalised "Nationalised") or brought under government control under the [Burmese Way to Socialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_Way_to_Socialism "Burmese Way to Socialism"),[^89] which combined Soviet-style nationalisation and [central planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_planning "Economic planning").

A [new constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Myanmar "Constitution of Myanmar") of the [Socialist Republic of the Union of Burma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_Republic_of_the_Union_of_Burma "Socialist Republic of the Union of Burma") was adopted in 1974. Until 1988, the country was ruled as a [one-party system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-party_system "One-party system"), with the general and other military officers resigning and ruling through the [Burma Socialist Programme Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burma_Socialist_Programme_Party "Burma Socialist Programme Party") (BSPP).[^90] During this period, Myanmar became one of the world's most impoverished countries.[^91] There were sporadic protests against military rule during the Ne Win years, and these were almost always violently suppressed. On 7 July 1962, the government broke up [demonstrations at Rangoon University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1962_Rangoon_University_protests "1962 Rangoon University protests"), killing 15 students.[^89] In 1974, the military violently suppressed [anti-government protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U_Thant_funeral_crisis "U Thant funeral crisis") at the funeral of U Thant. Student protests in 1975, 1976, and 1977 were quickly suppressed by overwhelming force.[^90]

In 1988, unrest over economic mismanagement and political oppression by the government led to widespread pro-democracy demonstrations throughout the country, known as the [8888 Uprising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8888_Uprising "8888 Uprising"). Security forces killed thousands of demonstrators, and General [Saw Maung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saw_Maung "Saw Maung") staged a coup d'état and formed the [State Law and Order Restoration Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Law_and_Order_Restoration_Council "State Law and Order Restoration Council") (SLORC). In 1989, SLORC declared martial law after widespread protests. The military government finalised plans for People's Assembly elections on 31 May 1989.[^92] SLORC changed the country's official English name from the "Socialist Republic of the Union of Burma" to the "Union of Myanmar" on 18 June 1989 by enacting the adaptation of the expression law.

In May 1990, the government held free multiparty elections for the first time in almost 30 years, and the [National League for Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_League_for_Democracy "National League for Democracy") (NLD), the party of Aung San Suu Kyi, won [^93] [earning 392 out of a total 492 seats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1990_Myanmar_general_election "1990 Myanmar general election") (i.e., 80% of the seats). However, the military junta refused to cede power [^94] and continued to rule the nation, first as SLORC and, from 1997, as the State Peace and Development Council (SPDC) until its dissolution in March 2011. General [Than Shwe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Than_Shwe "Than Shwe") took over the Chairmanship – effectively the position of Myanmar's top ruler – from General Saw Maung in 1992 and held it until 2011.[^95]

On 23 June 1997, Myanmar was admitted into the Association of Southeast Asian Nations. On 27 March 2006, the military junta, which had moved the national capital from Yangon to a site near [Pyinmana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyinmana "Pyinmana") in November 2005, officially named the new capital [Naypyidaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naypyidaw "Naypyidaw"), meaning "city of the kings".[^96]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/2007_Myanmar_protests_7.jpg/250px-2007_Myanmar_protests_7.jpg)

Protesters in Yangon during the 2007 Saffron Revolution with a banner that reads non-violence: national movement in Burmese. In the background is Shwedagon Pagoda.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Cyclone_Nargis_-Myanmar-3May2008.jpg/250px-Cyclone_Nargis_-Myanmar-3May2008.jpg)

Cyclone Nargis in southern Myanmar, May 2008

In August 2007, an increase in the price of fuel led to the [Saffron Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saffron_Revolution "Saffron Revolution") led by Buddhist monks, who were dealt with harshly by the government.[^97] The government cracked down on them on 26 September 2007, with reports of barricades at the [Shwedagon Pagoda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shwedagon_Pagoda "Shwedagon Pagoda") and monks killed. There were also rumours of disagreement within the Burmese armed forces, but none were confirmed. The military crackdown against unarmed [protesters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protest "Protest") was widely condemned as part of the [international reactions to the Saffron Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_reactions_to_the_Saffron_Revolution "International reactions to the Saffron Revolution") and led to an increase in economic sanctions against the [Burmese Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Myanmar "Politics of Myanmar").

In May 2008, [Cyclone Nargis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclone_Nargis "Cyclone Nargis") caused extensive damage in the densely populated rice-farming delta of the [Irrawaddy Division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrawaddy_Division "Irrawaddy Division").[^98] It was the worst natural disaster in Burmese history with reports of an estimated 200,000 people dead or missing, damages totalled to 10 billion US dollars, and as many as 1 million were left homeless.[^99] In the critical days following this disaster, Myanmar's [isolationist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolationist "Isolationist") government was accused of hindering United Nations recovery efforts.[^100] [Humanitarian aid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanitarian_aid "Humanitarian aid") was requested, but concerns about foreign military or intelligence presence in the country delayed the entry of United States military planes delivering medicine, food, and other supplies.[^101]

In early August 2009, [a conflict broke out](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2009_Kokang_incident "2009 Kokang incident") in Shan State in northern Myanmar. For several weeks, junta troops fought against ethnic minorities including the [Han Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kokang_Chinese "Kokang Chinese"),[^102] [Wa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wa_people "Wa people"), and [Kachin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jingpo_people "Jingpo people").[^103] [^104] During 8–12 August, the first days of the conflict, as many as 10,000 Burmese civilians fled to Yunnan in neighbouring China.[^103] [^104] [^105]

### Civil wars

[Civil wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_war "Civil war") have been a constant feature of Myanmar's socio-political landscape since the attainment of independence in 1948. These wars are predominantly struggles for ethnic and sub-national autonomy, with the areas surrounding the ethnically Bamar central districts of the country serving as the primary geographical setting of conflict. Foreign journalists and visitors require a special travel permit to visit the areas in which Myanmar's civil wars continue.[^106]

In October 2012, the ongoing conflicts in Myanmar included the [Kachin conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kachin_conflict "Kachin conflict"),[^107] between the Pro-Christian [Kachin Independence Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kachin_Independence_Army "Kachin Independence Army") and the government;[^108] a civil war between the [Rohingya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohingya "Rohingya") Muslims,[^109] and the government and non-government groups in [Rakhine State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rakhine_State "Rakhine State");[^110] and a conflict between the [Shan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_people "Shan people"),[^111] [Lahu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lahu_people "Lahu people"), and [Karen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karen_people "Karen people") [^112] [^113] minority groups, and the government in the eastern half of the country. In addition, [al-Qaeda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Qaeda "Al-Qaeda") signalled an intention to become involved in Myanmar.[^114]

Armed conflict between [ethnic Chinese rebels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_National_Democratic_Alliance_Army "Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army") and the [Myanmar Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_Armed_Forces "Myanmar Armed Forces") resulted in the [Kokang offensive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015_Kokang_offensive "2015 Kokang offensive") in February 2015. The conflict had forced 40,000 to 50,000 civilians to flee their homes and seek shelter on the Chinese side of the border.[^115] During the incident, the government of China was accused of giving military assistance to the [ethnic Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kokang_Chinese "Kokang Chinese") rebels.[^116] Clashes between Burmese troops and local insurgent groups have continued, fuelling tensions between China and Myanmar.[^117]

### Period of liberalisation, 2011–2021

The military-backed Government had promulgated a ["Roadmap to Discipline-flourishing Democracy"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roadmap_to_democracy "Roadmap to democracy") in 1993, but the process appeared to stall several times, until 2008 when the Government published a new draft national constitution, and organised a (flawed) national referendum which adopted it. The new constitution provided for the election of a national assembly with the powers to appoint a president, while practically ensuring army control at all levels.[^118]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Barack_Obama_and_Hillary_Clinton_at_home_of_Aung_San_Suu_Kyi.jpg/250px-Barack_Obama_and_Hillary_Clinton_at_home_of_Aung_San_Suu_Kyi.jpg)

U.S. President Barack Obama and Secretary of State Hillary Clinton with Aung San Suu Kyi and her staff at her home in Yangon, 2012

A [general election in 2010](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Myanmar_general_election "2010 Myanmar general election") – the first for twenty years – was boycotted by the [NLD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_League_for_Democracy "National League for Democracy"). The military-backed [Union Solidarity and Development Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_Solidarity_and_Development_Party "Union Solidarity and Development Party") declared victory, stating that it had been favoured by 80 per cent of the votes; fraud, however, was alleged.[^119] [^120] A nominally civilian government was then formed, with retired general [Thein Sein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thein_Sein "Thein Sein") as president.[^121]

A series of liberalising political and economic actions – or reforms – then took place. By the end of 2011, these included the release of pro-democracy leader Aung San Suu Kyi from house arrest, the establishment of the [National Human Rights Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_National_Human_Rights_Commission "Myanmar National Human Rights Commission"), the granting of general amnesties for more than 200 political prisoners, new labour laws that permitted labour unions and strikes, a relaxation of press censorship, and the regulation of currency practices.[^122] In response, [United States Secretary of State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State "United States Secretary of State") [Hillary Clinton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hillary_Clinton "Hillary Clinton") visited Myanmar in December 2011 – the first visit by a US Secretary of State in more than fifty years [^123] – meeting both President Thein Sein and opposition leader Aung San Suu Kyi.[^124]

[Aung San Suu Kyi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi "Aung San Suu Kyi") 's [NLD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_League_for_Democracy "National League for Democracy") party participated in the 2012 by-elections, facilitated by the government's abolition of the laws that previously barred it.[^125] In the April 2012 [by-elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Myanmar_by-elections "2012 Myanmar by-elections"), the NLD won 43 of the 45 available seats. The 2012 by-elections were also the first time that international representatives were allowed to monitor the voting process in Myanmar.[^126]

Myanmar's improved international reputation was indicated by [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN") 's approval of Myanmar's bid for the position of ASEAN chair in 2014.[^127]

#### 2015 general elections

General elections [were held on 8 November 2015](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015_Myanmar_general_election "2015 Myanmar general election"). These were the first openly contested elections held in Myanmar since the 1990 general election (which was annulled [^128]). The results gave the NLD an [absolute majority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermajority "Supermajority") of seats in both chambers of the [national parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_parliament "National parliament"), enough to ensure that its candidate would become president, while NLD leader [Aung San Suu Kyi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi "Aung San Suu Kyi") is constitutionally barred from the presidency.[^128] [^129]

The new parliament convened on 1 February 2016,[^130] and on 15 March 2016, [Htin Kyaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Htin_Kyaw "Htin Kyaw") was elected as the first non-military president since the military coup of 1962.[^131] On 6 April 2016, [Aung San Suu Kyi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi "Aung San Suu Kyi") assumed the newly created role of [state counsellor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Counsellor_of_Myanmar "State Counsellor of Myanmar"), a role akin to a [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Myanmar "Prime Minister of Myanmar").[^132]

### Coup d'état and civil war

In Myanmar's 2020 parliamentary election, the ostensibly ruling [National League for Democracy (NLD)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_League_for_Democracy "National League for Democracy"), the party of State Counsellor [Aung San Suu Kyi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi "Aung San Suu Kyi"), competed with various other smaller parties – particularly the military-affiliated [Union Solidarity and Development Party (USDP)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_Solidarity_and_Development_Party "Union Solidarity and Development Party").[^133] Suu Kyi's NLD won the [2020 Myanmar general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Myanmar_general_election "2020 Myanmar general election") on 8 November in a landslide.[^133] [^134] [^135] The USDP, regarded as a proxy for the military, suffered a "humiliating" defeat [^136] [^137] – even worse than in 2015 [^137] – capturing only 33 of the 476 elected seats.[^135] [^136]

As the election results began emerging, the USDP rejected them, urging a new election with the military as observers.[^133] [^137] More than 90 other smaller parties contested the vote, including more than 15 who complained of irregularities. Despite this, election observers declared there were no major irregularities.[^136] [^135] [^138] However, despite the election commission validating the NLD's overwhelming victory,[^138] the USDP and Myanmar's military persistently alleged fraud.[^139] [^140] [^141] [^142] [^143] In January 2021, just before the new parliament was to be sworn in, the NLD announced that Suu Kyi would retain her State Counsellor role in the upcoming government. [^144]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/We_want_our_leader_free_Daw_Aung_San_Su_Kyi.jpg/250px-We_want_our_leader_free_Daw_Aung_San_Su_Kyi.jpg)

Protesters against the military coup in Myanmar

In the early morning of 1 February 2021, the day parliament was set to convene, the [Tatmadaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatmadaw "Tatmadaw"), Myanmar's military, detained Suu Kyi and other members of the ruling party.[^136] [^145] [^146] The military handed power to military chief [Min Aung Hlaing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Min_Aung_Hlaing "Min Aung Hlaing") and declared a state of emergency for one year [^147] [^145] and began closing the borders, restricting travel and electronic communications nationwide.[^146] The military announced it would replace the existing election commission with a new one, and a military media outlet indicated new elections would be held in about one year – though the military avoided making an official commitment to that.[^146] The military expelled NLD party Members of Parliament from the capital city, [Naypyidaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naypyidaw "Naypyidaw").[^146] By 15 March 2021, the military leadership continued to extend martial law into more parts of Yangon, while security forces killed 38 people in a single day of violence.[^148]

By the second day of the coup, thousands of protesters were marching in the streets of Yangon, and other protests erupted nationwide, largely halting commerce and transportation. Despite the military's arrests and killings of protesters, the first weeks of the coup found growing public participation, including groups of civil servants, teachers, students, workers, monks and religious leaders – even normally disaffected ethnic minorities.[^149] [^150] [^146]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Myanmar_civil_war.svg/250px-Myanmar_civil_war.svg.png)

Military situation in Myanmar as of 4 April 2025. Areas controlled by the Tatmadaw are highlighted in pink.

The coup was immediately condemned by the [United Nations Secretary General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Secretary_General "United Nations Secretary General") and leaders of democratic nations. The U.S. threatened sanctions on the military and its leaders, including a "freeze" of US$1 billion of their assets in the U.S.[^149] [^146] [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"), [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan"), [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh"), [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia"), [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam"), [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand"), the [Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines "Philippines") and [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") refrained from criticizing the military coup.[^151] [^152] [^153] [^154] A [United Nations Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council") resolution called for the release of Aung San Suu Kyi and the other detained leaders [^149] [^146] – a position shared by the [United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_High_Commissioner_for_Human_Rights "United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights").[^146]

International development and aid partners – business, non-governmental, and governmental – hinted at suspension of partnerships with Myanmar. Banks were closed and [social media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_media "Social media") communications platforms, including [Facebook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facebook "Facebook") and [Twitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twitter "Twitter"), removed Tatmadaw postings. Protesters appeared at Myanmar embassies in foreign countries.[^149] [^146] The National Unity Government then declared the formation of an armed wing on 5 May 2021, a date that is often cited as the start of a [full-scale civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_civil_war_\(2021%E2%80%93present\) "Myanmar civil war (2021–present)"). This armed wing was named the [People's Defence Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Defence_Force_\(Myanmar\) "People's Defence Force (Myanmar)") (PDF) to protect its supporters from military junta attacks and as a first step towards a Federal Union Army. The civil war is ongoing as of 2026.[^155]

## Geography

Myanmar has a total area of 678,500 square kilometres (262,000 sq mi). It lies between latitudes [9°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/9th_parallel_north "9th parallel north") and [29°N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/29th_parallel_north "29th parallel north"), and longitudes [92°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/92nd_meridian_east "92nd meridian east") and [102°E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/102nd_meridian_east "102nd meridian east"). Myanmar is bordered in the northwest by the [Chittagong Division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chittagong_Division "Chittagong Division") of [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh") and the [Mizoram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mizoram "Mizoram"), Manipur, [Nagaland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nagaland "Nagaland") and [Arunachal Pradesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arunachal_Pradesh "Arunachal Pradesh") states of India. Its north and northeast border is with the [Tibet Autonomous Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region "Tibet Autonomous Region") and [Yunnan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yunnan "Yunnan") for a Sino-Myanmar border total of 2,185 km (1,358 mi). It is bounded by [Laos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laos "Laos") and [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand") to the southeast. Myanmar has 1,930 km (1,200 mi) of contiguous coastline along the [Bay of Bengal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal "Bay of Bengal") and [Andaman Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andaman_Sea "Andaman Sea") to the southwest and the south, which forms one quarter of its total perimeter.[^14]

In the north, the [Hengduan Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hengduan_Mountains "Hengduan Mountains") form the border with China. [Hkakabo Razi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hkakabo_Razi "Hkakabo Razi"), located in [Kachin State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kachin_State "Kachin State"), at an elevation of 5,881 metres (19,295 ft), is the highest point in Myanmar.[^156] Many mountain ranges, such as the [Rakhine Yoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rakhine_Yoma "Rakhine Yoma"), the [Bago Yoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bago_Yoma "Bago Yoma"), the [Shan Hills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_Hills "Shan Hills") and the [Tenasserim Hills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tenasserim_Hills "Tenasserim Hills") exist within Myanmar, all of which run north-to-south from the [Himalayas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himalayas "Himalayas").[^157] The mountain chains divide Myanmar's three river systems, which are the [Irrawaddy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrawaddy_River "Irrawaddy River"), [Salween (Thanlwin)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salween_River "Salween River"), and the [Sittaung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sittaung_River "Sittaung River") rivers.[^158] The Irrawaddy River, Myanmar's longest river at nearly 2,170 kilometres (1,348 mi), flows into the [Gulf of Martaban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Martaban "Gulf of Martaban"). Fertile plains exist in the valleys between the [mountain chains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain_chain "Mountain chain").[^157] The majority of Myanmar's population lives in the [Irrawaddy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrawaddy_River "Irrawaddy River") valley, which is situated between the [Rakhine Yoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rakhine_Yoma "Rakhine Yoma") and the [Shan Plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_Plateau "Shan Plateau").

Myanmar is one of the most seismically prone countries in the world.[^159] The [Sagaing Fault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagaing_Fault "Sagaing Fault") between the [Indian Plate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_plate "Indian plate") and the [Eurasian Plate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_plate "Eurasian plate") runs north–south through the center of the country.[^159] There have been many small and some big [earthquakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_earthquakes_in_Myanmar "List of earthquakes in Myanmar") with a 7–8 magnitude.[^159]

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Koppen-Geiger_Map_MMR_present.svg/500px-Koppen-Geiger_Map_MMR_present.svg.png)

Myanmar map of Köppen climate classification

Much of the country lies between the [Tropic of Cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer "Tropic of Cancer") and the [Equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator"). It lies in the [monsoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsoon "Monsoon") region of Asia, with its coastal regions receiving over 5,000 mm (196.9 in) of rain annually. Annual [rainfall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation_\(meteorology\) "Precipitation (meteorology)") in the [delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/River_delta "River delta") region is approximately 2,500 mm (98.4 in), while average annual rainfall in the dry zone in central Myanmar is less than 1,000 mm (39.4 in). The northern regions of Myanmar are the coolest, with average temperatures of 21 °C (70 °F). Coastal and delta regions have an average maximum temperature of 32 °C (89.6 °F).[^158] Previously and currently analysed data, as well as future projections on changes caused by [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Myanmar "Climate change in Myanmar") predict serious consequences to development for all economic, productive, social, and environmental sectors in Myanmar.[^162] In order to combat the hardships ahead and do its part to help [combat climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combat_climate_change "Combat climate change") Myanmar has displayed interest in expanding its use of renewable energy and lowering its level of carbon emissions. Groups involved in helping Myanmar with the transition and move forward include the [UN Environment Programme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UN_Environment_Programme "UN Environment Programme"), Myanmar Climate Change Alliance, and the [Ministry of Natural Resources and Environmental Conservation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Natural_Resources_and_Environmental_Conservation "Ministry of Natural Resources and Environmental Conservation") which directed in producing the final draft of the Myanmar national climate change policy that was presented to various sectors of the Myanmar government for review.[^163]

In April 2015, it was announced that the [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank") and Myanmar would enter a full partnership framework aimed at improving access to electricity and other basic services for about six million people, and expected to benefit three million pregnant women and children through improved health services.[^164] Acquired funding and proper planning has allowed Myanmar to better prepare for the impacts of climate change by enacting programs which teach its people new farming methods, rebuild its infrastructure with materials resilient to natural disasters, and transition various sectors towards reducing greenhouse gas emissions.[^165]

### Biodiversity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Kaw_Gon_towards_Shwe_Kyar_Pwint.jpg/250px-Kaw_Gon_towards_Shwe_Kyar_Pwint.jpg)

The limestone landscape of Kayin State

Myanmar is a [biodiverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiverse "Biodiverse") country with more than 16,000 [plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant "Plant"), 314 [mammal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mammal "Mammal"), 1131 [bird](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bird "Bird"), 293 [reptile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reptile "Reptile"), and 139 [amphibian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amphibian "Amphibian") species, and 64 terrestrial [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystems "Ecosystems") including tropical and subtropical vegetation, seasonally inundated wetlands, shoreline and tidal systems, and alpine ecosystems. Myanmar houses some of the largest intact natural ecosystems in [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia"), but the remaining ecosystems are under threat from land use intensification and over-exploitation. According to the [IUCN Red List of Ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUCN_Red_List_of_Ecosystems "IUCN Red List of Ecosystems") categories and criteria, more than a third of Myanmar's land area has been converted to [anthropogenic ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropogenic_biome "Anthropogenic biome") over the last 2–3 centuries, and nearly half of its ecosystems are threatened. Despite large gaps in information for some ecosystems, there is a large potential to develop a comprehensive [protected area network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protected_area_network "Protected area network") that protects its terrestrial biodiversity.[^166]

Myanmar continues to perform badly in the global [Environmental Performance Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_Performance_Index "Environmental Performance Index") (EPI) with an overall ranking of 153 out of 180 countries in 2016, among the worst in the [South Asian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Asia "South Asia") region. The environmental areas where Myanmar performs worst (i.e., highest ranking) are [air quality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_quality "Air quality") (174), health impacts of [environmental issues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_issues "Environmental issues") (143), and [biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity "Biodiversity") and [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat "Habitat") (142). Myanmar performs best (i.e., lowest ranking) in [environmental impacts of fisheries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_impacts_of_fisheries "Environmental impacts of fisheries") (21) but with declining [fish stocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fish_stocks "Fish stocks"). Despite several issues, Myanmar also ranks 64 and scores very well (i.e., a high percentage of 93.73%) in environmental effects of the agricultural industry because of an excellent management of the [nitrogen cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle "Nitrogen cycle").[^167] [^168] Myanmar is one of the most highly vulnerable countries to [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change"); this poses several social, political, economic and foreign policy challenges to the country.[^169]

Myanmar's slow economic growth has contributed to the preservation of much of its environment and ecosystems. [Forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest "Forest"), including dense tropical growth and valuable [teak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teak "Teak") in lower Myanmar, cover over 49% of the country, including areas of [acacia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acacia "Acacia"), [bamboo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamboo "Bamboo"), [ironwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopea_odorata "Hopea odorata") and *[Magnolia champaca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnolia_champaca "Magnolia champaca")*. [Coconut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coconut "Coconut") and [betel palm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betel_palm "Betel palm") and rubber have been introduced. In the [highlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highland "Highland") of the north, oak, pine, and various rhododendrons cover much of the land.[^170]

Heavy logging since the new 1995 forestry law went into effect has seriously reduced forest area and wildlife habitat.[^171] The lands along the coast support all varieties of tropical fruits and once had large areas of [mangroves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_Coast_mangroves "Myanmar Coast mangroves") although much of the protective mangroves have disappeared. In much of central Myanmar (the dry zone), [vegetation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetation "Vegetation") is sparse and stunted.

Typical jungle animals, particularly [tigers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiger "Tiger"), occur sparsely in Myanmar. In upper Myanmar, there are [rhinoceros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhinoceros "Rhinoceros"), [wild water buffalo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wild_water_buffalo "Wild water buffalo"), [clouded leopard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clouded_leopard "Clouded leopard"), [wild boars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wild_boar "Wild boar"), [deer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deer "Deer"), [antelope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antelope "Antelope"), and [elephants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elephant "Elephant"), which are also tamed or bred in captivity for use as work animals, particularly in the [lumber industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lumber_industry "Lumber industry"). Smaller mammals are also numerous, ranging from [gibbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibbon "Gibbon") and [monkeys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monkey "Monkey") to [flying foxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flying_foxes "Flying foxes"). The abundance of [birds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bird "Bird") is notable with over 800 species, including [parrots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rose-ringed_parakeet "Rose-ringed parakeet"), [myna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myna "Myna"), [peafowl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peafowl "Peafowl"), [red junglefowl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_junglefowl "Red junglefowl"), [weaverbirds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weaverbirds "Weaverbirds"), [crows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crow "Crow"), [herons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heron "Heron"), and [barn owl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_barn_owl "Eastern barn owl"). Among reptile species there are [crocodiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crocodile "Crocodile"), [geckos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gecko "Gecko"), [cobras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobra "Cobra"), [Burmese pythons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_python "Burmese python"), and [turtles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turtle "Turtle"). Hundreds of species of freshwater fish are wide-ranging, plentiful and are very important food sources.[^172]

## Government and politics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/President_and_Vice_Presidents_of_Myanmar_%282026%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-President_and_Vice_Presidents_of_Myanmar_%282026%29_%28cropped%29.jpg)

President Min Aung Hlaing (center) flanked by first and second vice-presidents, respectively, Nyo Saw (left) and Nan Ni Ni Aye (right) at the Pyidaungsu Hluttaw in 2026

Myanmar operates as a [unitary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_state "Unitary state") [assembly-independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assembly-independent "Assembly-independent") [republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic "Republic") under its [2008 constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Constitution_of_Myanmar "2008 Constitution of Myanmar"). The country is governed as a [parliamentary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system") with a [bicameral legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicameral_legislature "Bicameral legislature") (with an executive [president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Myanmar "President of Myanmar") accountable to the legislature), with 25% of the legislators appointed by the military and the rest elected in general elections.[^173]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Myanmar-Lower-House-Parliament.jpg/250px-Myanmar-Lower-House-Parliament.jpg)

Assembly of the Union (Pyidaungsu Hluttaw)

The legislature, called the [Assembly of the Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Union "Assembly of the Union"), is bicameral and made up of two houses: The 224-seat upper [House of Nationalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Nationalities "House of Nationalities") and the 440-seat lower [House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Representatives_\(Myanmar\) "House of Representatives (Myanmar)"). The upper house consists of 168 members who are directly elected and 56 who are appointed by the [Burmese Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_Armed_Forces "Burmese Armed Forces"). The lower house consists of 330 members who are directly elected and 110 who are appointed by the armed forces. The President of Myanmar acts as the [head of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_state "Head of state") and the [head of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_government "Head of government") and is elected by the Assembly of the Union.[^173]

### Political culture

The major political party is the military-backed [Union Solidarity and Development Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_Solidarity_and_Development_Party "Union Solidarity and Development Party"). The [National League for Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_League_for_Democracy "National League for Democracy") was previously a major political party, but it was banned by the junta government in 2023.[^174]

Myanmar's army-drafted constitution was approved in a [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Burmese_constitutional_referendum "2008 Burmese constitutional referendum") in May 2008. The results, 92.4% of the 22 million voters with an official turnout of 99%, are considered suspect by many international observers and by the National League of Democracy with reports of widespread [fraud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_fraud "Electoral fraud"), ballot stuffing, and voter intimidation.[^175] The [elections of 2010](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Myanmar_general_election "2010 Myanmar general election") resulted in a victory for the military-backed Union Solidarity and Development Party. Various foreign observers questioned the fairness of the elections.[^120] [^176] [^177] One criticism of the election was that only government-sanctioned political parties were allowed to contest in it and the popular [National League for Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_League_for_Democracy "National League for Democracy") was declared illegal.[^178] However, immediately following the elections, the government ended the house arrest of the democracy advocate and leader of the National League for Democracy, [Aung San Suu Kyi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi "Aung San Suu Kyi"),[^179] and her ability to move freely around the country is considered an important test of the military's movement toward more openness.[^178]

The Tatmadaw again seized power in a [2021 coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Myanmar_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "2021 Myanmar coup d'état"), which led to the rule of the [National Defence and Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Defence_and_Security_Council_\(Myanmar\) "National Defence and Security Council (Myanmar)") (NDSC) and its new military junta, the [State Administration Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Administration_Council "State Administration Council") (SAC).[^145] The coup, which was widely condemned by the [international community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_community "International community"), led to [continuous ongoing widespread protests in Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021%E2%80%932022_Myanmar_protests "2021–2022 Myanmar protests") and has been marked by violent [political repression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_repression "Political repression") by the military, as well as the outbreak of a [civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_civil_war_\(2021%E2%80%93present\) "Myanmar civil war (2021–present)").[^180] The military again arrested Aung San Suu Kyi in order to remove her from public life, and charged her with crimes ranging from [corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corruption_in_Myanmar "Corruption in Myanmar") to violation of [COVID-19](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_Myanmar "COVID-19 pandemic in Myanmar") protocols; all of the charges against her are "politically motivated" according to independent observers.[^181] The SAC imposed a state of emergency from 2021 to 2025, after which it transferred power back to the NDSC. In the [V-Dem Democracy Indices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V-Dem_Democracy_Indices "V-Dem Democracy Indices"), Myanmar ranks amongst the lowest as a "closed autocracy," ranking at 174th and 176th out of 179 countries in electoral democracy and liberal democracy indices respectively.[^182] The country transitioned to a nominally civilian government in 2026.

### Foreign relations

Though the country's foreign relations, particularly with [Western nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_nations "Western nations"), have historically been strained, the situation has markedly improved since the reforms following the 2010 elections.[^183] After years of diplomatic isolation and economic and military sanctions,[^184] the United States relaxed curbs on foreign aid to Myanmar in November 2011 [^124] and announced the resumption of diplomatic relations on 13 January 2012 [^185] The [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") has placed sanctions on Myanmar, including an [arms embargo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arms_embargo "Arms embargo"), cessation of [trade preferences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade_preference "Trade preference"), and suspension of all aid with the exception of [humanitarian aid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanitarian_aid "Humanitarian aid").[^186]

Sanctions imposed by the United States and European countries against the former military government, coupled with boycotts and other direct pressure on corporations by supporters of the democracy movement, have resulted in the withdrawal from the country of most U.S. and many European companies.[^187] Despite Western isolation, Asian corporations have generally remained willing to continue investing in the country and to initiate new investments, particularly in natural resource extraction. The country has close relations with neighbouring India and China, with several Indian and Chinese companies operating in the country. Under India's [Act East policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Look_East_policy_\(India\) "Look East policy (India)"), fields of co-operation between India and Myanmar include [remote sensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_sensing "Remote sensing"),[^188] oil and gas exploration,[^189] information technology,[^190] hydropower [^191] and construction of ports and buildings.[^192] Myanmar also has close political relations with Vietnam [^193] and Japan.[^194] [^195]

In June 2013, Myanmar held its first ever summit, the [World Economic Forum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Economic_Forum "World Economic Forum") on East Asia 2013. A regional spinoff of the annual [World Economic Forum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Economic_Forum "World Economic Forum") in [Davos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davos "Davos"), Switzerland, the summit was held on 5–7 June and attended by 1,200 participants, including 10 heads of state, 12 ministers and 40 senior directors from around the world.[^196]

On 19 January 2025, Reuters reported that [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN") had told Myanmar's military government to prioritise ceasefire, "urging the junta to start dialogue and end hostilities immediately" and "to allow unhindered humanitarian access", citing Malaysia's foreign minister [Mohamad Hasan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohamad_Hasan_\(politician\) "Mohamad Hasan (politician)").[^197] The ASEAN chair's statement denounced "the continued acts of violence against civilians and public facilities and urged all parties involved" to "immediately halt indiscriminate violence, exercise utmost restraint, ensure the protection and safety of all civilians and civilian infrastructures, and create a conducive environment for the delivery of humanitarian assistance and inclusive national dialogue".[^198]

### Military

Since the late 1950s, Myanmar's military has had major roles in Myanmar's politics.[^199]<sup><span title="Page: 23">: 23</span></sup>

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Myanmar_Air_Force_MiG-29_MRD.jpg/250px-Myanmar_Air_Force_MiG-29_MRD.jpg)

A Myanmar Air Force Mikoyan MiG-29 multirole fighter

Myanmar has received extensive military aid from China in the past.[^200] Myanmar has been a member of ASEAN since 1997. Though it gave up its turn to hold the ASEAN chair and host the [ASEAN Summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN_Summit "ASEAN Summit") in 2006, it chaired the forum and hosted the summit in 2014.[^201] In November 2008, Myanmar's political situation with neighbouring Bangladesh became tense as they began searching for natural gas in a disputed block of the Bay of Bengal.[^201] Controversy surrounding the Rohingya population also remains an issue between Bangladesh and Myanmar.[^202]

Myanmar's armed forces are known as the [Tatmadaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatmadaw "Tatmadaw"), which numbers 488,000. The Tatmadaw comprises the [Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_Army "Myanmar Army"), the [Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_Navy "Myanmar Navy"), and the [Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_Air_Force "Myanmar Air Force"). The country [ranked twelfth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_number_of_military_and_paramilitary_personnel "List of countries by number of military and paramilitary personnel") in the world for its number of active troops in service.[^37] The military is very influential in Myanmar, with all top cabinet and ministry posts usually held by military officials. Official figures for military spending are not available. Estimates vary widely because of uncertain exchange rates, but Myanmar's military forces' expenses are high.[^203] Myanmar imports most of its weapons from Russia, Ukraine, China, and India.

Myanmar is building a research [nuclear reactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_reactor "Nuclear reactor") near [Pyin Oo Lwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyin_Oo_Lwin "Pyin Oo Lwin") with help from Russia. It is one of the signatories of the nuclear [non-proliferation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-proliferation "Non-proliferation") pact since 1992 and a member of the [International Atomic Energy Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency "International Atomic Energy Agency") (IAEA) since 1957. The military junta had informed the IAEA in September 2000 of its intention to construct the reactor.[^204] [^205] In 2010 as part of the [leaked diplomatic cables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_diplomatic_cables_leak "United States diplomatic cables leak"), Myanmar was suspected of using North Korean construction teams to build a fortified surface-to-air missile facility.[^206] As of 2019, the United States [Bureau of Arms Control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureau_of_Arms_Control,_Verification,_and_Compliance "Bureau of Arms Control, Verification, and Compliance") assessed that Myanmar is not in violation of its obligations under the [Non-Proliferation Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Proliferation_Treaty "Non-Proliferation Treaty") but that the Myanmar government had a history of non-transparency on its nuclear programs and aims.[^207]

Until 2005, the [United Nations General Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly "United Nations General Assembly") annually adopted a detailed resolution about the situation in Myanmar by consensus.[^208] [^209] [^210] [^211] But in 2006 a divided United Nations General Assembly voted through a resolution that strongly called upon the government of Myanmar to end its systematic violations of human rights.[^212] In January 2007, Russia and China vetoed a draft resolution before the [United Nations Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council") [^213] calling on the government of Myanmar to respect human rights and begin a democratic transition. South Africa also voted against the resolution.[^214]

### Human rights and internal conflicts

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Armed_conflict_zones_in_Myanmar.png/250px-Armed_conflict_zones_in_Myanmar.png)

Map of conflict zones in Myanmar. States and regions affected by fighting during and after 1995 are highlighted in yellow.

There is consensus that the former military regime in Myanmar (1962–2010) was one of the world's most repressive and abusive regimes.[^215] [^216] In November 2012, [Samantha Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samantha_Power "Samantha Power"), Barack Obama's Special Assistant to the President on Human Rights, wrote on the White House blog that "Serious human rights abuses against civilians in several regions continue, including against women and children." [^111] Members of the United Nations and major international human rights organisations have issued repeated and consistent reports of widespread and systematic human rights violations in Myanmar. The United Nations General Assembly has repeatedly [^217] called on the Burmese military junta to respect human rights and in November 2009 the General Assembly adopted a resolution "strongly condemning the ongoing systematic violations of human rights and fundamental freedoms" and calling on the Burmese military regime "to take urgent measures to put an end to violations of international human rights and humanitarian law." [^218]

International human rights organisations including [Human Rights Watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch "Human Rights Watch") [^219] and [Amnesty International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amnesty_International "Amnesty International") [^220] have repeatedly documented and condemned widespread human rights violations in Myanmar. The *Freedom in the World 2011* report by [Freedom House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_House "Freedom House") notes, "The military junta has... suppressed nearly all basic rights; and committed human rights abuses with impunity." In July 2013, the [Assistance Association for Political Prisoners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assistance_Association_for_Political_Prisoners "Assistance Association for Political Prisoners") indicated that approximately 100 political prisoners were being held in Burmese prisons.[^221] [^222] [^223] [^224] Evidence gathered by a British researcher was published in 2005 regarding the extermination or "Burmisation" of certain ethnic minorities, such as the [Karen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karen_people "Karen people"), [Karenni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karenni_people "Karenni people") and [Shan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_people "Shan people").[^225]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Mae_La_refugee_camp2.jpg/250px-Mae_La_refugee_camp2.jpg)

Mae La camp, Tak, Thailand, one of the largest of nine UNHCR camps in Thailand 226

Based on the evidence gathered by Amnesty photographs and videos of the ongoing armed conflict between the Myanmar military and the [Arakan Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arakan_Army "Arakan Army") (AA), attacks escalated on civilians in Rakhine State. Ming Yu Hah, [Amnesty International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amnesty_International "Amnesty International") 's Deputy Regional Director for Campaigns said, the [UN Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UN_Security_Council "UN Security Council") must urgently refer the situation in Myanmar to the [International Criminal Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Criminal_Court "International Criminal Court").[^227] The military is notorious for rampant use of sexual violence.[^28]

In November 2024, the [ICC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Criminal_Court "International Criminal Court") prosecutor [Karim A.A. Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karim_Ahmad_Khan "Karim Ahmad Khan") KC filed an arrest warrant application of Senior General, Acting President and Commander-in-Chief of the Myanmar Defence Services [Min Aung Hlaing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Min_Aung_Hlaing "Min Aung Hlaing") for "criminal responsibility for the crimes against humanity of deportation and persecution of the Rohingya, committed in Myanmar, and in part in Bangladesh".[^228]

The military takeover in 2021 has further worsened poverty in the country.[^229]

The Myanmar government has been alleged to have committed [genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohingya_genocide "Rohingya genocide") against the Rohingya. The [Rohingya people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohingya_people "Rohingya people") face severe persecution, denied citizenship and basic rights since 1982. Many have been expelled, making them one of the world's most persecuted minorities.[^230]

#### Government reforms

According to the [Crisis Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crisis_Group "Crisis Group"),[^231] since Myanmar transitioned to a new government in August 2011, the country's human rights record has been improving. Previously giving Myanmar its lowest rating of 7, the 2012 *Freedom in the World* report also notes improvement, giving Myanmar a 6 for improvements in civil liberties and political rights, the release of political prisoners, and a loosening of restrictions.[^232] In 2013, Myanmar improved yet again, receiving a score of 5 in civil liberties and 6 in political freedoms.[^233]

The government has assembled a [National Human Rights Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_National_Human_Rights_Commission "Myanmar National Human Rights Commission") that consists of 15 members from various backgrounds.[^234] Several activists in exile, including Thee Lay Thee Anyeint members, have returned to Myanmar after President Thein Sein's invitation to expatriates to return home to work for national development.[^235] In an address to the United Nations Security Council on 22 September 2011, Myanmar's Foreign Minister [Wunna Maung Lwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wunna_Maung_Lwin "Wunna Maung Lwin") confirmed the government's intention to release prisoners in the near future.[^236]

The government has also relaxed reporting laws, but these remain highly restrictive.[^237] In September 2011, several banned websites, including YouTube, [Democratic Voice of Burma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Voice_of_Burma "Democratic Voice of Burma") and [Voice of America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voice_of_America "Voice of America"), were unblocked.[^238]

Homosexual acts are [illegal in Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_Myanmar "LGBT rights in Myanmar") and can be punishable by life imprisonment.[^239] [^240]

## Economy

Myanmar's [economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy "Economy") is one of the [fastest growing economies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fastest_growing_economies "Fastest growing economies") in the world with a nominal GDP of US$76.09 billion in 2019 and an estimated purchasing power adjusted GDP of US$327.629 billion in 2017 according to the World Bank.[^241] Foreigners are able to legally lease but not own property.[^242] In December 2014, Myanmar set up its first stock exchange, the [Yangon Stock Exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yangon_Stock_Exchange "Yangon Stock Exchange").[^243]

The informal economy's share in Myanmar is one of the biggest in the world and is closely linked to corruption, smuggling, and illegal trade activities.[^244] [^245] In addition, decades of civil war and unrest have contributed to Myanmar's current levels of poverty and lack of economic progress. Myanmar lacks adequate [infrastructure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrastructure "Infrastructure"). Goods travel primarily across the Thai border (where most illegal drugs are exported) and along the Irrawaddy River.[^246] Notably, opium production in Myanmar is the world's second-largest source of [opium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium "Opium") after [Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium_production_in_Afghanistan "Opium production in Afghanistan"), producing some 25% of the world's opium, forming part of the Golden Triangle. While opium poppy cultivation in Myanmar had declined year-on-year since 2015, cultivation area increased by 33% totalling 40,100 hectares alongside an 88% increase in yield potential to 790 tonnes in 2022 according to latest data from the [United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Office_on_Drugs_and_Crime "United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime") (UNODC) Myanmar Opium Survey 2022.[^247] With that said, the [United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Office_on_Drugs_and_Crime "United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime") (UNODC) has also warned that opium production in Myanmar may rise again if the economic crunch brought on by COVID-19 and the country's 1 February military coup persists, with significant public health and security consequences for much of Asia.[^248] At the same time, the Golden Triangle, and specifically [Shan State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_State "Shan State") in Myanmar, is believed to be the largest [methamphetamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methamphetamine "Methamphetamine") -producing area in the world. The growing signs of an intensification of methamphetamine manufacturing activity within and around the Golden Triangle, and a corresponding decrease in the number of production facilities dismantled in other parts of the region, suggests that methamphetamine manufacture in East and Southeast Asia is now consolidated into the lower Mekong region.[^249] Countries in East and Southeast Asia have collectively witnessed sustained increases in seizures of methamphetamine over the last decade, totalling over 171 tons and a record of over 1 billion methamphetamine tablets in 2021 according to the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime, more than any other part of the world.[^250] In April and May 2020, Myanmar authorities reported Asia's largest ever drug operation in Shan State, totalling what was believed to be 193 million methamphetamine tablets, hundreds of kilogrammes of crystal methamphetamine as well as some heroin, and over 162,000 litres and 35.5 tons of drug precursors as well as sophisticated production equipment and several staging and storage facilities.[^251]

Both China and India have attempted to strengthen ties with the government for economic benefit in the early 2010s. Many Western nations, including the United States and Canada, and the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union"), have historically imposed investment and trade sanctions on Myanmar. The United States and European Union eased most of their sanctions in 2012.[^252] From May 2012 to February 2013, the United States began to lift its economic sanctions on Myanmar "in response to the historic reforms that have been taking place in that country." [^253] Foreign investment comes primarily from China, Singapore, the Philippines, South Korea, India, and Thailand.[^254] The military has stakes in some major industrial corporations of the country (from oil production and consumer goods to transportation and tourism).[^255] [^256]

### Economic history

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Myingyan_Railway_Station_2.jpg/250px-Myingyan_Railway_Station_2.jpg)

The trains are relatively slow in Myanmar. The railway trip from Bagan to Mandalay takes about 7.5 hours (179 km or 111 mi).

Under the [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") administration, the people of Burma were at the bottom of the social hierarchy, with [Europeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europeans "Europeans") at the top, Indians, Chinese, and Christianised minorities in the middle, and Buddhist Burmese at the bottom.[^257] Forcefully integrated into the world economy, Burma's economy grew by involving itself with extractive industries and [cash crop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cash_crop "Cash crop") agriculture. However, much of the wealth was concentrated in the hands of Europeans. The country became the world's largest exporter of [rice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rice "Rice"), mainly to European markets, while other colonies like India suffered mass starvation.[^258] Being a follower of free market principles, the British opened up the country to large-scale immigration with Rangoon exceeding New York City as the greatest immigration port in the world in the 1920s. Historian [Thant Myint-U](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thant_Myint-U "Thant Myint-U") states, "This was out of a total population of only 13 million; it was equivalent to the United Kingdom today taking 2 million people a year." By then, in most of Burma's largest cities, [Rangoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rangoon "Rangoon"), [Akyab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akyab "Akyab"), [Bassein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathein "Pathein") and [Moulmein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moulmein "Moulmein"), the Indian immigrants formed a majority of the population. The Burmese under British rule felt helpless, and reacted with a "racism that combined feelings of superiority and fear".[^257]

Crude oil production, an indigenous industry of [Yenangyaung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yenangyaung "Yenangyaung"), was taken over by the British and put under [Burmah Oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmah_Oil "Burmah Oil") monopoly. British Burma began exporting crude oil in 1853.[^259] European firms produced 75% of the world's teak.[^36] The wealth was, however, mainly concentrated in the hands of Europeans. In the 1930s, agricultural production fell dramatically as international rice prices declined and did not recover for several decades.[^260] During the Japanese invasion of Burma in World War II, the British followed a [scorched earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scorched_earth "Scorched earth") policy. They destroyed major government buildings, oil wells and mines that developed for tungsten ([Mawchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mawchi "Mawchi")), tin, lead and silver to keep them from the Japanese. Ultimately, Myanmar was bombed extensively by both the Japanese and the [Allied powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II "Allies of World War II").[^261] [^262]

After independence, the country was in ruins, with its major infrastructure destroyed. With the loss of India, Burma lost relevance and obtained independence from the British. After a parliamentary government was formed in 1948, Prime Minister [U Nu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U_Nu "U Nu") embarked upon a policy of nationalisation, and the state was declared the owner of all of the land in Burma. The government tried to implement an eight-year plan partly financed by injecting money into the economy, but this caused inflation to rise.[^263] The [1962 coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1962_Burmese_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1962 Burmese coup d'état") was followed by an economic scheme called the [Burmese Way to Socialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_Way_to_Socialism "Burmese Way to Socialism"), a plan to nationalise all industries, except for [agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture"). While the economy continued to grow at a slower rate, the country eschewed a Western-oriented development model, and by the 1980s, was left behind capitalist powerhouses like [Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore "Singapore"), which were integrated with Western economies.[^264] [^91] Myanmar asked for admittance to a [least developed country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_developed_country "Least developed country") status in 1987 to receive debt relief.[^265]

### Agriculture

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Rijstvelden_Myanmar_2006.jpg/250px-Rijstvelden_Myanmar_2006.jpg)

Rice is Myanmar's largest agricultural product.

The major agricultural product is [rice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rice_production_in_Myanmar "Rice production in Myanmar"), which covers about 60% of the country's total cultivated land area. Rice accounts for 97% of total food grain production by weight. Through collaboration with the [International Rice Research Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Rice_Research_Institute "International Rice Research Institute"), 52 modern rice varieties were released in the country between 1966 and 1997, helping increase national rice production to 14 million tons in 1987 and to 19 million tons in 1996. By 1988, modern varieties were planted on half of the country's rice lands, including 98 percent of the irrigated areas.[^266] In 2008, rice production was estimated at 50 million tons.[^267]

### Extractive industries

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Corundum-271368.jpg/250px-Corundum-271368.jpg)

Large ruby ( corundum ) crystal from Mogok. Size: 5.5 × 3.2 × 3 cm. 268

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/MyoMa.jpg/250px-MyoMa.jpg)

Mogok City

Myanmar produces precious stones such as [rubies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubies "Rubies"), [sapphires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sapphire "Sapphire"), [pearls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearl "Pearl"), and [jade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jade "Jade"). [Rubies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubies "Rubies") are the biggest earner; 90% of the world's rubies come from the country, whose red stones are prized for their purity and hue. Thailand buys the majority of the country's gems. Myanmar's "Valley of Rubies", the mountainous [Mogok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mogok_Township "Mogok Township") area, 200 km (120 mi) north of [Mandalay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandalay "Mandalay"), is noted for its rare pigeon's blood rubies and blue sapphires.[^269]

Many [U.S.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S. "U.S.") and [European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") jewellery companies, including Bulgari, Tiffany, and Cartier, refuse to import these stones based on reports of deplorable working conditions in the mines. [Human Rights Watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch "Human Rights Watch") has encouraged a complete ban on the purchase of Burmese gems based on these reports and because nearly all profits go to the junta, as the majority of mining activity in the country is government-run.[^270] The government of Myanmar controls the gem trade by direct ownership or by joint ventures with private owners of mines.[^271]

[Rare-earth elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rare-earth_elements "Rare-earth elements") are also a significant export, as Myanmar supplies around 10% of the world's rare earths.[^272] Conflict in Kachin State has threatened the operations of its mines as of February 2021.[^273] [^274]

Other major industries include the production of agricultural goods, textiles, wood products, construction materials, gems, metals, oil, and natural gas. Myanmar Engineering Society has identified at least 39 locations capable of geothermal power production, and some of these hydrothermal reservoirs lie quite close to Yangon, which is a significant underutilised resource for electrical production.[^275]

### Tourism

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Tourists_in_Myanmar_%288397039153%29.jpg/250px-Tourists_in_Myanmar_%288397039153%29.jpg)

Tourists in Myanmar

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/U_Bein_Bridge_Mandalay_4.jpg/250px-U_Bein_Bridge_Mandalay_4.jpg)

U Bein Bridge in Mandalay

The government receives a significant percentage of the income of private-sector tourism services.[^276] The most popular available tourist destinations in Myanmar include big cities such as [Yangon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yangon "Yangon") and [Mandalay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandalay "Mandalay"); religious sites in [Mon State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_State "Mon State"), [Pindaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pindaya "Pindaya"), [Bago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bago,_Burma "Bago, Burma") and [Hpa-An](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hpa-An "Hpa-An"); nature trails in [Inle Lake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inle_Lake "Inle Lake"), [Kengtung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kengtung "Kengtung"), [Putao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Putao_District "Putao District"), [Pyin Oo Lwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyin_Oo_Lwin "Pyin Oo Lwin"); ancient cities such as [Bagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bagan "Bagan") and [Mrauk-U](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mrauk-U "Mrauk-U"); as well as beaches in Nabule,[^277] [Ngapali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ngapali_Beach "Ngapali Beach"), [Ngwe-Saung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ngwe_Saung_Beach "Ngwe Saung Beach"), and [Mergui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mergui "Mergui").[^278] Nevertheless, much of the country is off-limits to tourists, and interactions between foreigners and the people of Myanmar, particularly in the border regions, are subject to police scrutiny. They are not to discuss politics with foreigners, under penalty of imprisonment, and in 2001, the Myanmar Tourism Promotion Board issued an order for local officials to protect tourists and limit "unnecessary contact" between foreigners and ordinary Burmese people.[^279]

The most common way for travellers to enter the country is by air.[^280] According to the website *[Lonely Planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lonely_Planet "Lonely Planet")*, getting into Myanmar is problematic: "No bus or train service connects Myanmar with another country, nor can you travel by car or motorcycle across the border – you must walk across." They further state that "It is not possible for foreigners to go to/from Myanmar by sea or river." [^280] There are a few border crossings that allow the passage of private vehicles, such as the border between [Ruili](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruili "Ruili") (China) to [Mu-se](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu-se "Mu-se"), the border between [Htee Kee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Htee_Kee "Htee Kee") (Myanmar) and [Phu Nam Ron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phu_Nam_Ron "Phu Nam Ron") (Thailand)—the most direct border between [Dawei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dawei "Dawei") and [Kanchanaburi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanchanaburi "Kanchanaburi"), and the border between [Myawaddy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myawaddy "Myawaddy") and [Mae Sot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mae_Sot "Mae Sot"), Thailand. At least one tourist company has successfully run commercial overland routes through these borders since 2013.[^281]

Flights are available from most countries, though direct flights are limited mainly to Thai and other [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN") airlines. According to *[Eleven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weekly_Eleven "Weekly Eleven")* magazine, "In the past, there were only 15 international airlines and increasing numbers of airlines have begun launching direct flights from Japan, Qatar, Taiwan, South Korea, Germany and Singapore." [^282]

## Demographics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Downtownflatsyangon.jpg/250px-Downtownflatsyangon.jpg)

A block of apartments in downtown Yangon, facing Bogyoke Market. Much of Yangon's urban population resides in densely populated flats.

The provisional results of the [2014 Myanmar Census](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Myanmar_Census "2014 Myanmar Census") showed that the total population was 51,419,420.[^285] This figure includes an estimated 1,206,353 persons in parts of northern [Rakhine State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rakhine_State "Rakhine State"), [Kachin State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kachin_State "Kachin State") and [Kayin State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kayin_State "Kayin State") who were not counted.[^286] People who were out of the country at the time of the census are not included in these figures. There are over 600,000 registered [migrant workers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Migrant_worker "Migrant worker") from Myanmar in [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand"), and millions more work illegally. Burmese citizens account for 80% of all migrant workers in Thailand.[^287] At the beginning of the 20th century, Burma's [population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Census_in_Myanmar "Census in Myanmar") was approximately 10 million.[^288] The national population density is 76 per square kilometre (200/sq mi), among the lowest in Southeast Asia.

Myanmar's fertility rate in 2011 was 2.23, slightly above the [replacement level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-replacement_fertility "Sub-replacement fertility") [^289] and low compared to [Southeast Asian countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asian_countries "Southeast Asian countries") of similar economic standing.[^289] There has been a significant decline in fertility in the 2000s, from a rate of 4.7 children per woman in 1983, down to 2.4 in 2001, despite the absence of any national population policy.[^289] [^290] [^291] The fertility rate is much lower in urban areas.

The relatively rapid decline in fertility is attributed to several factors, including extreme delays in marriage (almost unparalleled among developing countries in the region), the prevalence of illegal abortions, and the high proportion of single, unmarried women of reproductive age, with 25.9% of women aged 30–34 and 33.1% of men and women aged 25–34 being single.[^291] [^292]

These patterns stem from economic dynamics, including high income inequality, which results in residents of reproductive age opting for delay of marriage and family-building in favour of attempting to find employment and establish some form of wealth;[^291] the average age of marriage in Myanmar is 27.5 for men, 26.4 for women.[^291] [^292]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ethnolinguistic_map_of_Burma_1972_en.svg/330px-Ethnolinguistic_map_of_Burma_1972_en.svg.png)

Ethnolinguistic groups of Burma/Myanmar

Myanmar is [ethnically diverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnically_diverse "Ethnically diverse"). The government recognises [135 distinct ethnic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ethnic_groups_in_Myanmar "List of ethnic groups in Myanmar"). There are at least 108 different ethnolinguistic groups in Myanmar, consisting mainly of distinct [Tibeto-Burman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibeto-Burman "Tibeto-Burman") peoples, but with sizeable populations of [Tai–Kadai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tai%E2%80%93Kadai-speaking_peoples "Tai–Kadai-speaking peoples"), [Hmong–Mien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hmong%E2%80%93Mien "Hmong–Mien"), and Austroasiatic (Mon–Khmer) peoples.[^293]

Ethnic identity in modern-day Myanmar has been significantly shaped by British colonial rule, Christian missionaries, and [decolonisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decolonization "Decolonization") in the post-independence era. To this day, the [Burmese language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_language "Burmese language") does not have precise terminology that distinguishes the European concepts of [race and ethnicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnicity "Ethnicity"); the term *lu-myo* (လူမျိုး, lit. 'type of person') can reference race, ethnicity, and religion.[^294] For instance, many [Bamar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamar_people "Bamar people") self-identify as members of the 'Buddhist *lu-myo'* or the ' [Myanmar *lu-myo*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_people "Burmese people"),' which has posed a significant challenge for census-takers.[^295]

The [Bamar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamar "Bamar") form an estimated 68% of the population.[^296] 10% of the population are [Shan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_people "Shan people").[^296] The Kayin make up 7% of the population.[^296] The [Rakhine people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rakhine_people "Rakhine people") constitute 4% of the population. [Overseas Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_Chinese "Burmese Chinese") form approximately 3% of the population.[^296] [^297] Myanmar's ethnic [minority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minority_group "Minority group") groups prefer the term "ethnic nationality" over "ethnic minority" as the term "minority" furthers their sense of insecurity in the face of what is often described as "Burmanisation"—the proliferation and domination of the dominant [Bamar culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamar_people "Bamar people") over minority cultures.

[Mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_people "Mon people"), who form 2% of the population, are ethno-linguistically related to the [Khmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_people "Khmer people").[^296] [Overseas Indians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_Indians "Burmese Indians") are 2%.[^296] The remainder are [Kachin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kachin_people "Kachin people"), [Chin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chin_people "Chin people"), [Rohingya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohingya "Rohingya"), [Anglo-Indians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Indian "Anglo-Indian"), [Gurkha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_Gurkha "Burmese Gurkha"), [Nepali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People_of_Nepal "People of Nepal") and other ethnic minorities. Included in this group are the [Anglo-Burmese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Burmese "Anglo-Burmese"). Once forming a large and influential community, the Anglo-Burmese left the country in steady streams from 1958 onwards, principally to Australia and the United Kingdom. It is estimated that 52,000 Anglo-Burmese remain in Myanmar. As of 2009, 110,000 Burmese [refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refugee "Refugee") were living in refugee camps in Thailand.[^298]

Refugee camps exist along the Indian, Bangladeshi, and Thai borders, while several thousand are in [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia"). Conservative estimates state that there are over 295,800 minority refugees from Myanmar, with the majority being [Rohingya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohingya "Rohingya"), [Karen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karen_people "Karen people"), and [Karenni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karenni_people "Karenni people"), who are principally located along the Thai-Myanmar border.[^299] There are nine permanent refugee camps along the Thai-Myanmar border, most of which were established in the mid-1980s. The refugee camps are under the care of the Thai-Burma Border Consortium (TBBC). Since 2006,[^300] over 55,000 Burmese [refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refugee "Refugee") have been resettled in the United States.[^301]

The persecution of [Burmese Indians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_Indians "Burmese Indians"), [Burmese Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_Chinese "Burmese Chinese"), and other ethnic groups after the military coup headed by General [Ne Win](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ne_Win "Ne Win") in 1962 led to the expulsion or emigration of 300,000 people.[^302] They migrated to escape [racial discrimination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Racial_Discrimination_against_Burmese_Indians "Racial Discrimination against Burmese Indians") and the wholesale nationalisation of private enterprise that took place in 1964.[^303] The Anglo-Burmese at this time either fled the country or changed their names and blended in with the broader Burmese society.

Many [Rohingya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohingya "Rohingya") Muslims have fled Myanmar. Many refugees headed to neighbouring Bangladesh, including 200,000 in 1978 as a result of the [King Dragon operation in Arakan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Dragon_operation_in_Arakan "King Dragon operation in Arakan").[^304] 250,000 more left in 1991.[^305] Since August 2017, an estimated 23,000–43,700 Rohingya have been killed [^306] [^307] in the ongoing [Rohingya genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohingya_genocide "Rohingya genocide"), and another 730,000 have fled to Bangladesh.[^308]

### Languages

Myanmar is home to four major language families: [Sino-Tibetan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Tibetan "Sino-Tibetan"), [Tai–Kadai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tai%E2%80%93Kadai "Tai–Kadai"), [Austroasiatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austroasiatic "Austroasiatic"), and [Indo-European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-European "Indo-European").[^293] Sino-Tibetan languages are most widely spoken. They include [Burmese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_language "Burmese language"), [Karen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karen_people "Karen people"), [Kachin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jingpo_people "Jingpo people"), [Chin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chin_people "Chin people"), and Chinese (mainly [Hokkien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hokkien "Hokkien")). The primary Tai–Kadai language is [Shan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_language "Shan language"). [Mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_language "Mon language"), [Palaung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palaung_language "Palaung language"), and [Wa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Va_people "Va people") are the major Austroasiatic languages spoken in Myanmar. The two major Indo-European languages are [Pali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pali "Pali"), the liturgical language of Theravada Buddhism, and English.[^309] More than a hundred languages are spoken in total. Since many of them are known only within small tribes around the country, they may have been lost (many, if not all) after a few generations.

[Burmese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_language "Burmese language"), the mother tongue of the Bamar and official language of Myanmar, is related to [Tibetan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetic_languages "Tibetic languages") and Chinese.[^309] It is written in a [script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_alphabet "Burmese alphabet") consisting of circular and semi-circular letters, which were adapted from the [Mon script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_language "Mon language"), which in turn was developed from a southern Indian script in the 5th century. The earliest known inscriptions in the Burmese script date from the 11th century. It is also used to write [Pali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pali "Pali"), the sacred language of Theravada Buddhism, as well as several ethnic minority languages, including Shan, several Karen dialects, and Kayah (Karenni), with the addition of specialised characters and [diacritics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diacritic "Diacritic") for each language.[^310]

Although the country is predominantly Buddhist, significant numbers of people practice other recognized religions in Myanmar. Religious edifices and orders have been in existence for many years. The Christian and Muslim populations do, however, face religious persecution, and it is hard, if not impossible, for non-Buddhists to join the army or get government jobs, which represent the main routes to success and social advancement in the country.[^311] Persecution and targeting of civilians, religious minorities are particularly notable in eastern Myanmar, where over 3,000 villages have been destroyed in the past ten years.[^312] [^313] [^314] More than 200,000 Muslims have fled to Bangladesh by 2007 to escape persecution.[^315] [^316]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/A_nun_and_group_of_monks_praying_before_idols_in_Myanmar.jpg/250px-A_nun_and_group_of_monks_praying_before_idols_in_Myanmar.jpg)

Praying Buddhist monks in Shwedagon Pagoda

A large majority of the population practices Buddhism; estimates range from 80% [^317] to 89%.[^318] [^319] According to the [2014 Myanmar Census](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Myanmar_Census "2014 Myanmar Census"), 87.9% of the population identifies as Buddhists.[^320] [Theravāda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Therav%C4%81da "Theravāda") Buddhism is the most widespread.[^318] There are some 500,000 Buddhist monks and 75,000 nuns in this country of 54 million.[^321] Other religions are practised largely without obstruction, with the notable exception of some religious minorities such as the Rohingya people, who have continued to have their citizenship status denied and treated as illegal immigrants instead,[^230] and Christians in Chin State.[^322] According to 2014 census, 6.2% of the population identifies as Christian; 4.3% as Muslim; 0.8% as followers of tribal religions; 0.5% as [Hindus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism"); 0.2% as followers of other religions; and 0.1% follow no religion.[^320] According to the 2010 estimates of the [Pew Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pew_Research_Center "Pew Research Center"), 7% of the population is Christian; 4% is Muslim; 1% follows traditional [animistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animistic "Animistic") beliefs; and 2% follow other religions, including [Mahayana Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahayana_Buddhism "Mahayana Buddhism"), [Hinduism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism"), and [East Asian religions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asian_religions "East Asian religions").[^322] [^296] Jehovah's Witnesses have been present since 1914 [^323] and have about 80 congregations around the country and a branch office in Yangon publishing in 16 languages.[^324] A tiny Jewish community in Yangon had a synagogue but no resident rabbi.[^325]

Although Hinduism is practised by 0.5% of the population, it was a major religion in Myanmar's past.[^326] [^327] [Burmese folk religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_folk_religion "Burmese folk religion") is practised by many [Bamars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamars "Bamars") alongside Buddhism.

### Health

The general state of [health care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_care "Health care") in Myanmar is poor. The government spends anywhere from 0.5% to 3% of the country's GDP on health care, consistently ranking among the lowest in the world.[^328] [^329] Although [health care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_care "Health care") is nominally free, in reality, patients have to pay for medicine and treatment, even in public clinics and hospitals. Public hospitals lack many of the basic facilities and equipment. The 2010 maternal mortality rate per 100,000 births for Myanmar is 240. This is compared with 219.3 in 2008 and 662 in 1990. The under-5 mortality rate, per 1,000 births, is 73, and the neonatal mortality as a percentage of under-5s mortality is 47. According to Doctors without Borders, 25,000 Burmese AIDS patients died in 2007, deaths that could largely have been prevented by [antiretroviral therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiretroviral_therapy "Antiretroviral therapy") drugs and proper treatment.[^330]

HIV/AIDS, recognised as a disease of concern by the [Myanmar Ministry of Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Health_\(Myanmar\) "Ministry of Health (Myanmar)"), is most prevalent among [sex workers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sex_worker "Sex worker") and [intravenous drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_drug "Intravenous drug") users. In 2005, the estimated adult [HIV prevalence rate in Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HIV/AIDS_in_Myanmar "HIV/AIDS in Myanmar") was 1.3% (200,000–570,000 people), according to [UNAIDS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNAIDS "UNAIDS"), and early indicators of any progress against the HIV epidemic are inconsistent.[^331] [^332] [^333] However, the National AIDS Programme Myanmar found that 32% of sex workers and 43% of intravenous drug users in Myanmar have HIV.[^333]

### Education

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Students%2C_Hakha%2C_Chin_State%2C_Myanmar.jpg/250px-Students%2C_Hakha%2C_Chin_State%2C_Myanmar.jpg)

Students on their way to school, Kalaymyo, Sagaing Region, Myanmar

According to the [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO") Institute of Statistics, Myanmar's official [literacy rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literacy_rate "Literacy rate") as of 2000 was 90%.[^334] Historically, Myanmar has had high literacy rates. The educational system of Myanmar is operated by the government agency, the [Ministry of Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Education_\(Myanmar\) "Ministry of Education (Myanmar)"). The education system is based on the United Kingdom's system after nearly a century of British and Christian presence in Myanmar. Nearly all schools are government-operated, but there has been an increase in privately funded English language schools in the early 21st century. Schooling is compulsory until the end of elementary school, approximately 9 years old, while the compulsory schooling age is 15 or 16 at the international level.

There are 101 universities, 12 institutes, 9 degree colleges, and 24 colleges in Myanmar, a total of 146 higher education institutions.[^335] There are 10 technical training schools, 23 nursing training schools, 1 sport academy, and 20 midwifery schools. There are four international schools acknowledged by WASC and College Board— [The International School Yangon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_International_School_Yangon "The International School Yangon"), [Myanmar International School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_International_School "Myanmar International School"), Yangon International School, and International School of Myanmar in Yangon. Myanmar was ranked 122nd in the [Global Innovation Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Innovation_Index "Global Innovation Index") in 2025.[^336] [^337]

### Crime

Myanmar had a murder rate of 15.2 per 100,000 population with a total of 8,044 murders in 2012.[^338] Factors influencing Myanmar's high murder rate include communal violence and armed conflict.[^339] Myanmar is one of the world's most corrupt nations according to the 2012 [Transparency International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transparency_International "Transparency International") [Corruption Perceptions Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corruption_Perceptions_Index "Corruption Perceptions Index"), which ranked the country at number 171, out of 176 countries in total.[^340] Myanmar is the world's second largest producer of [opium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium "Opium") after [Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium_production_in_Afghanistan "Opium production in Afghanistan"), producing some 25% of the world's opium, and forms part of the [Golden Triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Triangle_\(Southeast_Asia\) "Golden Triangle (Southeast Asia)"). The opium industry was a monopoly during colonial times and has since been illegally operated by corrupt officials in the Burmese military and rebel fighters,[^341] primarily as the basis for heroin manufacture. Myanmar is the largest producer of methamphetamines in the world, with the majority of *[Ya ba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ya_ba "Ya ba")* found in Thailand produced in Myanmar, particularly in the Golden Triangle and northeastern Shan State, which borders Thailand, Laos, and China.[^342] Burmese-produced *ya ba* is typically trafficked to Thailand via Laos, before being transported through the northeastern Thai region of [Isan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isan "Isan").[^343]

## Culture

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Burmese_Ramayana_dance.jpg/250px-Burmese_Ramayana_dance.jpg)

Burmese Kinnayi Kinnaya dance

A diverse range of indigenous cultures exists in Myanmar, with the majority culture primarily Buddhist and [Bamar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamar "Bamar"). Bamar culture has been influenced by the cultures of neighbouring countries, manifested in its language, cuisine, music, dance, and theatre. The arts, particularly literature, have historically been influenced by the local form of Theravada Buddhism. Considered the national epic of Myanmar, the *[Yama Zatdaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yama_Zatdaw "Yama Zatdaw")*, an adaptation of India's *[Ramayana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramayana "Ramayana")*, has been influenced greatly by Thai, Mon, and Indian versions of the play.[^344] Buddhism is practised along with [nat worship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nat_\(spirit\) "Nat (spirit)"), which involves elaborate rituals to propitiate one from a pantheon of 37 nats.[^345] [^346]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Myanmar_Traditional_novitiation_march.JPG/250px-Myanmar_Traditional_novitiation_march.JPG)

A Buddhist Shinbyu ceremony in Mandalay

In a traditional village, the monastery is the centre of cultural life. Monks are venerated and supported by the lay people. A novitiation ceremony called [shinbyu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shinbyu "Shinbyu") is the most important [coming of age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coming_of_age "Coming of age") event for a boy, during which he enters the monastery for a short time.[^347] All male children in Buddhist families are encouraged to be a novice (beginner for Buddhism) before the age of twenty and to be a monk after the age of twenty. Girls have ear-piercing ceremonies (နားသ) at the same time.[^347] Burmese culture is most evident in villages where local festivals are held throughout the year, the most important being the [pagoda festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pagoda_festival "Pagoda festival").[^348] [^349] Many villages have a guardian nat, and superstition and taboos are commonplace.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Rakhine_Thingyan_2011.jpeg/250px-Rakhine_Thingyan_2011.jpeg)

An Arakan (Rakhine) girl pours water at travellers during the Burmese New Year Thingyan Water Festival in Yangon.

British colonial rule introduced Western elements of culture to Myanmar. Myanmar's education system is modelled after that of the United Kingdom. Colonial architectural influences are most evident in major cities such as Yangon.[^350] Many ethnic minorities, particularly the Karen in the southeast and the Kachin and Chin, who populate the far western state of [Chin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chin_State "Chin State") and the north and northeast, practice Christianity.[^351] According to *[The World Factbook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_World_Factbook "The World Factbook")*, the Burman population is 68% while other ethnic minorities groups constitute 32%. In contrast, the exiled leaders and organizations claim the 40% of the population is composed of ethnic minorities.

### Cuisine

[Burmese cuisine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_cuisine "Burmese cuisine") is characterised by extensive use of fish products such as [fish sauce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fish_sauce "Fish sauce"), [ngapi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ngapi "Ngapi") (fermented seafood), and dried prawns. [Mohinga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohinga "Mohinga") is the traditional breakfast dish and is Myanmar's national dish. Seafood is a common ingredient in coastal cities, while meat and poultry are more commonly used in landlocked cities like Mandalay. Freshwater fish and shrimp have been incorporated into inland cooking as a primary source of protein and are used in a variety of ways, fresh, salted whole or filleted, salted and dried, made into a salty paste, or fermented sour, and pressed. Burmese cuisine also includes a variety of salads (*[a thoke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_thoke "A thoke")*), centred on one major ingredient, ranging from starches like rice, wheat and rice noodles, glass noodles and vermicelli, to potato, ginger, tomato, [kaffir lime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaffir_lime "Kaffir lime"), long bean, and [lahpet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lahpet "Lahpet") (pickled tea leaves).

### Sport

The [Lethwei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lethwei "Lethwei"), [Bando](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bando "Bando"), [Banshay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banshay "Banshay"), and [Pongyi thaing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pongyi_thaing "Pongyi thaing") martial arts and [chinlone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinlone "Chinlone") are traditional sports in Myanmar.[^352] Football is played all over the country, even in villages, and its [national team](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_national_football_team "Myanmar national football team") is ruled by the [Myanmar Football Federation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar_Football_Federation "Myanmar Football Federation"). The [2013 Southeast Asian Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2013_Southeast_Asian_Games "2013 Southeast Asian Games") took place in Naypyidaw, Yangon, Mandalay, and [Ngwesaung Beach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ngwesaung_Beach "Ngwesaung Beach") in December, representing the third occasion that the event has been staged in Myanmar. Myanmar previously hosted the games in [1961](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1961_Southeast_Asian_Peninsular_Games "1961 Southeast Asian Peninsular Games") and [1969](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1969_Southeast_Asian_Peninsular_Games "1969 Southeast Asian Peninsular Games").[^353]

### Art

Burmese traditional art concepts are popular and respected by the Burmese people and people from abroad. Burmese contemporary art has developed quite rapidly on its own terms. Artists born after the 1980s have had greater chances of art practice outside the country.

One of the first to study Western art was [Ba Nyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ba_Nyan "Ba Nyan"). Together with [Ngwe Gaing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ngwe_Gaing "Ngwe Gaing") and a handful of other artists, they were the pioneers of the Western painting style. Later on, most young children learned the concepts from them. Some well known contemporary artists are [Lun Gywe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lun_Gywe "Lun Gywe"), Aung Kyaw Htet, [MPP Yei Myint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MPP_Yei_Myint "MPP Yei Myint"), Myint Swe, Min Wai Aung, [Aung Myint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aung_Myint "Aung Myint"), [Kin Maung Yin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kin_Maung_Yin "Kin Maung Yin"), [Po Po](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Po_Po "Po Po") and [Zaw Zaw Aung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zaw_Zaw_Aung "Zaw Zaw Aung").

### Media and communications

Because of Myanmar's political climate, there are not many media companies in relation to the country's population. Some are privately owned. All programming must meet the approval of the censorship board. The Burmese government announced on 20 August 2012 that it would stop censoring media before publication. Following the announcement, newspapers and other outlets no longer required approval from state censors; however, journalists in the country can still face consequences for what they write and say.[^354] In April 2013, international media reports were published to relay the enactment of the media liberalisation reforms that we announced in August 2012. For the first time in numerous decades, the publication of privately owned newspapers commenced in the country.[^355]

#### Internet

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Kayan_women_Burma_1.jpg/250px-Kayan_women_Burma_1.jpg)

Kayan women in a village near Inle Lake, 2010

Internet use is estimated to be relatively low compared to other countries.[^356] [^357] Myanmar's internet used to be subject to censorship, and authorities viewed e-mails and posts on Internet blogs until 2012 when the government removed media censorship. During the strict censorship days, activity at internet cafes was regulated, and one blogger named [Zarganar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zarganar "Zarganar") was sentenced to prison for publishing a video of destruction caused by [Cyclone Nargis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclone_Nargis "Cyclone Nargis") in 2008; Zarganar was released in October 2011.

Regarding communications infrastructure, Myanmar ranks last among Asian countries in the World Economic Forum's [Networked Readiness Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Networked_Readiness_Index "Networked Readiness Index") (NRI) – an indicator of a country's information and communication technology development level. With 139 countries reported on, Myanmar ranked number 133 overall in the 2016 NRI ranking.[^358]

### Film

Myanmar's first film was a documentary of the funeral of Tun Shein—a leading politician of the 1910s, who campaigned for Burmese independence in London. The first Burmese [silent film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silent_film "Silent film") *Myitta Ne Thuya* (*[Love and Liquor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Love_and_Liquor "Love and Liquor")*) in 1920, which proved a major success, despite its poor quality. During the 1920s and 1930s, many Burmese-owned film companies made and produced several films. The first Burmese [sound film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_film "Sound film") was produced in 1932 in [Bombay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bombay "Bombay"), India, with the title Ngwe Pay Lo Ma Ya (Money Can't Buy It). After World War II, Burmese cinema continued to address political themes. Many of the films produced in the early Cold War era had a strong propaganda element.

In the era that followed the political events of 1988, the film industry has been increasingly controlled by the government. Film stars who had been involved in political activities were banned from appearing in films. The government issues strict rules on censorship and largely determines who produces films, as well as who gets Academy Awards.[^359]

Over the years, the movie industry has also shifted to producing many lower-budget [direct-to-video](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct-to-video "Direct-to-video") films. Most of the movies produced nowadays are [comedies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comedy_film "Comedy film").[^360] In 2008, only 12 films worthy of being considered for an [Academy Award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academy_Award "Academy Award") were made, although at least 800 VCDs were produced.[^361] Myanmar is the primary subject of a 2007 graphic novel titled *Chroniques Birmanes* by [Québécois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quebec_City "Quebec City") author and animator, [Guy Delisle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guy_Delisle "Guy Delisle"). The graphic novel was translated into English under the title *[Burma Chronicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burma_Chronicles "Burma Chronicles")* in 2008. In 2009, a documentary about Burmese [videojournalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Videojournalist "Videojournalist") called *[Burma VJ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burma_VJ "Burma VJ")* was released.[^362] This film was nominated for [Best Documentary Feature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Documentary_Feature "Academy Award for Best Documentary Feature") at the [2010 Academy Awards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/82nd_Academy_Awards "82nd Academy Awards").[^363] *[The Lady](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Lady_\(2011_film\) "The Lady (2011 film)")* had its world premiere on 12 September 2011 at the [36th Toronto International Film Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/36th_Toronto_International_Film_Festival "36th Toronto International Film Festival").[^364]
