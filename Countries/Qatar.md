**Qatar**,[^2] [^3] officially the **State of Qatar**,[^4] is a country in [West Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Asia "West Asia"). It occupies the [Qatar Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Qatar "Geography of Qatar") on the northeastern coast of the [Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula") in the [Middle East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_East "Middle East"); it shares [its sole land border](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar%E2%80%93Saudi_Arabia_border "Qatar–Saudi Arabia border") with [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia") to the south, with the rest of its territory surrounded by the [Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf"). The [Gulf of Bahrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Bahrain "Gulf of Bahrain"), an inlet of the Persian Gulf, separates Qatar from nearby [Bahrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bahrain "Bahrain"). The capital is [Doha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doha "Doha"), home to over 80% of the country's inhabitants. Most of the land area is made up of flat, low-lying [desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert "Desert").

Qatar has been ruled as a [hereditary monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hereditary_monarchy "Hereditary monarchy") by the [House of Thani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Thani "House of Thani") since [Mohammed bin Thani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_bin_Thani "Mohammed bin Thani") signed an agreement with Britain in 1868 that recognised its separate status. Following [Ottoman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") rule, Qatar became a [British protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_protectorate "British protectorate") in 1916 and gained independence in 1971. The current [emir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emir "Emir") is [Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamim_bin_Hamad_Al_Thani "Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani"), who, like previous emirs, holds nearly all executive, legislative, and judicial authority in an [autocratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocratic "Autocratic") manner under the [Constitution of Qatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Qatar "Constitution of Qatar").[^19] He appoints the [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_prime_ministers_of_Qatar "List of prime ministers of Qatar") and [cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Qatar "Cabinet of Qatar"). The [Consultative Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consultative_Assembly_of_Qatar "Consultative Assembly of Qatar") (also known as the "Shura Council") can block legislation and has a limited ability to dismiss ministers, but is fully appointed by the emir. While Qatar held a partial [Shura Council election in 2021](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Qatari_general_election "2021 Qatari general election"), with two thirds of seats elected, [in 2024 it moved to abolish those elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Qatari_constitutional_referendum "2024 Qatari constitutional referendum") altogether, and reverted to a fully appointed Assembly.[^20]

In early 2017, the population of Qatar was 2.6 million, although only 313,000 of them were Qatari citizens and 2.3 million were [expatriates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expatriate "Expatriate") and [migrant workers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Migrant_workers "Migrant workers").[^21] Its [official religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_religion "State religion") is [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam").[^22] The country has the fourth-highest [GDP (PPP) per capita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(PPP\)_per_capita "List of countries by GDP (PPP) per capita") in the world [^23] and the eleventh-highest [GNI per capita (Atlas method)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GNI_\(nominal\)_per_capita "List of countries by GNI (nominal) per capita").[^24] It ranks 42nd in the [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index"), the third-highest HDI in the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world").[^25] It is a [high-income economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank_high-income_economy "World Bank high-income economy"), backed by the world's third-largest [natural gas reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_natural_gas_proven_reserves "List of countries by natural gas proven reserves") and oil reserves.[^26] Qatar is one of the world's largest exporters of liquefied natural gas [^27] and the world's largest emitter of carbon dioxide per capita.[^28]

In the 21st century, Qatar emerged as both a [major non-NATO ally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_non-NATO_ally "Major non-NATO ally") of the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and a [middle power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_power "Middle power") in the Arab world. [Its economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Qatar "Economy of Qatar") has grown rapidly due to its resource-wealth,[^29] [^30] and its geopolitical power has risen through its media group, [Al Jazeera Media Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Jazeera_Media_Network "Al Jazeera Media Network"), and reported financial support for rebel groups during the [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring").[^31] [^32] [^33] Qatar also forms part of the [Gulf Cooperation Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Cooperation_Council "Gulf Cooperation Council").[^6]

## Name

[Pliny the Elder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder"), a Roman writer, documented the earliest account pertaining to the inhabitants of the peninsula around the mid-first century AD; he referred to them as the *Catharrei*, a designation that may have derived from the name of a prominent local settlement.[^34] [^35] A century later, [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy") produced the first known map to depict the peninsula, referring to it as *Catara*.[^35] [^36] The map also referenced a town named "Cadara" to the east of the peninsula.[^37] The term "Catara" (inhabitants, *Cataraei*) [^38] was exclusively used until the 18th century, after which "Katara" emerged as the most commonly recognised spelling.[^37] Eventually, after several variations—"Katr", "Kattar" and "Guttur"—the modern derivative *Qatar* was adopted as the country's name.[^39]

According to classical linguistic accounts, the term "Qatar" may derive from a type of informal transaction known as *muqāṭarah*, in which goods were purchased in sealed containers without measuring or weighing the contents, for a fixed price. This practice, also called *jazāf*, was reportedly widespread in Qatar's markets. Another explanation connects the name to the word *qiṭār*, meaning [camel train](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camel_train "Camel train"), possibly alluding to Qatar's role in historical trade routes.[^40]

In [Standard Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Arabic "Standard Arabic"), the name is pronounced [\[ˈqɑtˤɑr\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Arabic "Help:IPA/Arabic"), while in the local dialect, [Qatari Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatari_Arabic "Qatari Arabic"), it is [\[ˈɡɪtˤɑr\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Arabic "Help:IPA/Arabic").[^14] English speakers use different approximate pronunciations as the Arabic pronunciations use sounds not present in English.[^41]

## History

### Prehistory

Human habitation in Qatar dates back to 50,000 years ago.[^42] Settlements and tools dating back to the [Stone Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stone_Age "Stone Age") have been unearthed in the peninsula.[^42] [Mesopotamian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia") artefacts originating from the [Ubaid period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubaid_period "Ubaid period") (c. 6500–3800 BC) have been discovered in abandoned coastal settlements.[^43] [Al Da'asa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Da%27asa "Al Da'asa"), a settlement located on the western coast of Qatar, is the most important Ubaid site in the country and is believed to have accommodated a small seasonal encampment.[^44] [^45] Some historians have theorised that the Sumerians may have originated from this region.[^46] [^47] [^48]

### Antiquity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Excavated_site_on_Al_Khor_Island.jpg/250px-Excavated_site_on_Al_Khor_Island.jpg)

Excavation of a Kassite dye site on Al Khor Island

[Kassite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kassites "Kassites") [Babylonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonia "Babylonia") material dating back to the second millennium BC found on the [Al Khor Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Khor_Island "Al Khor Island") attests to trade relations between the inhabitants of Qatar and the Kassites in modern-day [Bahrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bahrain "Bahrain").[^49] Among the findings were crushed snail shells and Kassite potsherds.[^44] It has been suggested that Qatar is the earliest known site of shellfish dye production, owing to a Kassite [purple dye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrian_purple "Tyrian purple") industry which existed on the coast.[^43] [^50]

In AD 224, the [Sasanian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sasanian_Empire "Sasanian Empire") gained control over the territories surrounding the Persian Gulf.[^51] Qatar played a role in the commercial activity of the Sasanids, contributing at least two commodities: precious pearls and purple dye.[^52] Under the Sasanid reign, many of the inhabitants in eastern Arabia were introduced to [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity") following the eastward dispersal of the religion by Mesopotamian Christians [^53] (see also [Christianity in pre-Islamic Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_in_pre-Islamic_Arabia "Christianity in pre-Islamic Arabia")). Monasteries were constructed and further settlements were founded during this era.[^54] [^55] During the latter part of the Christian era, Qatar comprised a region known as 'Beth Qatraye' ([Syriac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syriac_language "Syriac language") for "house of the Qataris").[^56] The region was not limited to Qatar; it also included [Bahrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bahrain "Bahrain"), [Tarout Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarout_Island "Tarout Island"), Al-Khatt, and [Al-Hasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Ahsa_Oasis "Al-Ahsa Oasis").[^57]

In 628, the Islamic prophet [Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad "Muhammad") sent a Muslim envoy to a ruler in eastern Arabia named [Munzir ibn Sawa Al-Tamimi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munzir_ibn_Sawa_Al-Tamimi "Munzir ibn Sawa Al-Tamimi") and requested that he and his subjects accept Islam. Munzir obliged his request, and accordingly most of the Arab tribes in the region converted to Islam.[^58] In the middle of the century, the [Muslim conquest of Persia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_Persia "Muslim conquest of Persia") resulted in the fall of the Sasanian Empire.[^59]

### Early and late Islamic period (661–1783)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Abbasids850.png/250px-Abbasids850.png)

Abbasid Caliphate at its greatest extent, c. 850

Qatar was described as a famous horse and camel breeding centre during the [Umayyad period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate").[^60] In the 8th century, it started benefiting from its commercially strategic position in the Persian Gulf and went on to become a centre of pearl trading.[^61] [^62] Substantial development in the pearling industry around the Qatari Peninsula occurred during the [Abbasid period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate").[^60] Ships voyaging from [Basra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basra "Basra") to [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") and [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") would make stops in Qatar's ports during this period. [Chinese porcelain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_ceramics "Chinese ceramics"), [West African](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Africa "West Africa") coins, and artefacts from [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand") have been discovered in Qatar.[^59] Archaeological remains from the 9th century suggest that Qatar's inhabitants used greater wealth to construct higher quality homes and public buildings. Over 100 stone-built houses, two mosques, and an Abbasid fort were constructed in [Murwab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murwab "Murwab") during this period.[^63] [^64] As the [caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caliphate "Caliphate") 's prosperity waned in [Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq "Iraq"), Qatar experienced a corresponding decline.[^65]

Qatar is mentioned in 13th-century Muslim scholar [Yaqut al-Hamawi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yaqut_al-Hamawi "Yaqut al-Hamawi") 's book, *[Mu'jam Al-Buldan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu%27jam_Al-Buldan "Mu'jam Al-Buldan")*, which alludes to the Qataris' fine striped woven cloaks and their skills in improvement and finishing of spears.[^66]

Much of eastern Arabia was controlled by the [Usfurids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usfurids "Usfurids") in 1253, but control of the region was seised by the prince of [Ormus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ormus "Ormus") in 1320.[^67] Qatar's pearls provided the kingdom with one of its main sources of income.[^68]

### Portuguese era (1507–1650)

In 1515, [Manuel I of Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manuel_I_of_Portugal "Manuel I of Portugal") vassalised the Kingdom of Ormus. [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal "Kingdom of Portugal") went on to seize a significant portion of eastern Arabia in 1521.[^68] [^69]

After the fall of the [Jabrid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jabrids "Jabrids") Dynasty with the conquest of Bahrain by the Portuguese, the Arabian coast up to [Al Hassa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uqair "Uqair") came under the rule and influence of the Portuguese empire. Attempts by the Ottomans to dominate the region were eliminated with the Portuguese reconquest of the castle of [Tarout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarout_Castle "Tarout Castle") [^70] or [Al Qatif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Qatif_\(1551\) "Siege of Qatif (1551)") in 1551. In 1550, the inhabitants of [Al-Hasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Ahsa_Oasis "Al-Ahsa Oasis") had voluntarily submitted to the rule of the [Ottomans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"), preferring them to the Portuguese.[^71]

Archaeological finds are still being excavated from one of the Portuguese fortresses that served as a base to dominate the region as [Ruwayda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruwayda "Ruwayda").[^72] The first representation of Qatar appears on the Portuguese map by Luis Lázaro in 1563, showing the "city of Qatar" as a fortress, possibly referring to the fort of Ruwayda.[^73]

Having retained a negligible military presence in the area, the Ottomans were expelled by the [Bani Khalid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bani_Khalid_\(tribe\) "Bani Khalid (tribe)") tribe and [their emirate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bani_Khalid_Emirate "Bani Khalid Emirate") in 1670.[^74]

### Bahraini and Saudi rule (1783–1868)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Map_of_Catura_%28Qatar%29_1794_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Map_of_Catura_%28Qatar%29_1794_%28cropped%29.jpg)

A 1794 map by Samuel Dunn depicting Catura (at centre) in the historical region of Bahrain

In 1766, members of the [Al Khalifa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Khalifa "House of Khalifa") family of the [Utub](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utub "Utub") tribal confederation migrated from [Kuwait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuwait "Kuwait") to [Zubarah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zubarah "Zubarah") in Qatar.[^75] [^76] By the time of their arrival, the Bani Khalid exercised weak authority over the peninsula, notwithstanding the fact that the largest village was ruled by their distant kin.[^77] In 1783, Qatar-based [Bani Utbah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bani_Utbah "Bani Utbah") clans and allied Arab tribes [invaded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bani_Utbah_invasion_of_Bahrain "Bani Utbah invasion of Bahrain") and annexed [Bahrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bahrain "Bahrain") from the Persians. The Al Khalifa imposed their authority over Bahrain and retained their jurisdiction over Zubarah.[^75]

Following his swearing-in as crown prince of the [Emirate of Diriyah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Saudi_state "First Saudi state") in 1788, [Saud ibn Abd al-Aziz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saud_bin_Abdulaziz_Al_Saud_\(1748%E2%80%931814\) "Saud bin Abdulaziz Al Saud (1748–1814)") moved to expand [Wahhabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wahhabism "Wahhabism") territory eastward towards the Persian Gulf and Qatar. After defeating the Bani Khalid in 1795, the Wahhabi were attacked on two fronts. The Ottomans and [Egyptians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Egypt "Ottoman Egypt") assaulted the western front, while the Al Khalifa in Bahrain and the [Omanis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omani_Empire "Omani Empire") launched an attack against the eastern front.[^78] [^79] Upon being made aware of the Egyptian advance on the western frontier in 1811, the Wahhabi amir reduced his garrisons in Bahrain and Zubarah in order to redeploy his troops. [Said bin Sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Said_bin_Sultan "Said bin Sultan"), Sultan of [Muscat and Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscat_and_Oman "Muscat and Oman"), capitalised on this opportunity and raided the Wahhabi garrisons on the eastern coast, setting fire to the fort in Zubarah. The Al Khalifa was effectively returned to power thereafter.[^79]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Qatar%2C_Zubarah_%2810%29%2C_ruined_city.JPG/250px-Qatar%2C_Zubarah_%2810%29%2C_ruined_city.JPG)

A partially restored section of the ruined town of Zubarah

As punishment for piracy, an [East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_India_Company "East India Company") vessel bombarded [Doha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doha "Doha") in 1821, destroying the town and forcing hundreds of residents to flee. In 1825, the [House of Thani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Thani "House of Thani") was established with Sheikh [Mohammed bin Thani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_bin_Thani "Mohammed bin Thani") as the first leader.[^80]

Although Qatar was considered a [dependency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_territory "Dependent territory") of Bahrain, the Al Khalifa faced opposition from the local tribes. In 1867, the Al Khalifa, along with the ruler of [Abu Dhabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Abu_Dhabi "Emirate of Abu Dhabi"), sent a massive naval force to [Al Wakrah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Wakrah "Al Wakrah") in an effort to crush the Qatari rebels. This resulted in the maritime [Qatari–Bahraini War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatari%E2%80%93Bahraini_War "Qatari–Bahraini War") of 1867–68, in which Bahraini and Abu Dhabi forces sacked and looted Doha and Al Wakrah.[^81] The Bahraini hostilities were in violation of the [Perpetual Truce of Peace and Friendship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpetual_Truce_of_Peace_and_Friendship "Perpetual Truce of Peace and Friendship") of 1861. The joint incursion, in addition to the Qatari counter-attack, prompted British Political Resident, Colonel [Lewis Pelly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_Pelly "Lewis Pelly") to impose a settlement in 1868. His mission to Bahrain and Qatar and the resulting peace treaty were milestones because they implicitly recognised the distinctness of Qatar from Bahrain and explicitly acknowledged the position of Mohammed bin Thani. In addition to censuring Bahrain for its breach of agreement, Pelly negotiated with Qatari sheikhs who were represented by Mohammed bin Thani.[^82] The negotiations were the first stage in the development of Qatar as a sheikhdom.[^83]

### Ottoman period (1871–1915)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Qatar_in_Stielers_Handatlas_1891_59.JPG/250px-Qatar_in_Stielers_Handatlas_1891_59.JPG)

Qatar in an 1891 map by Adolf Stieler, with Al Bidda as the major settlement

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Olddoha2-771x410.jpg/250px-Olddoha2-771x410.jpg)

Old city of Doha, January 1904

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Barzan_towers_3.jpg/250px-Barzan_towers_3.jpg)

Barzan Towers are watchtowers that were built in the late 19th century and renovated in 1910 by Sheikh Mohammed bin Jassim Al Thani.

Under military and political pressure from the governor of the Ottoman [Vilayet of Baghdad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vilayet_of_Baghdad "Vilayet of Baghdad"), [Midhat Pasha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midhat_Pasha "Midhat Pasha"), the ruling Al Thani tribe submitted to Ottoman rule in 1871.[^84] The Ottoman government imposed reformist ([Tanzimat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanzimat "Tanzimat")) measures concerning taxation and land registration to fully integrate these areas into the empire.[^84] Despite the disapproval of local tribes, Al Thani continued supporting the Ottoman rule. Qatari-Ottoman relations stagnated, and in 1882 they suffered further setbacks when the Ottomans refused to aid Al Thani in his expedition of Abu Dhabi-occupied [Khor Al Adaid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khor_Al_Adaid "Khor Al Adaid") and offered only limited support in the [Qatari–Abu Dhabi War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatari%E2%80%93Abu_Dhabi_War "Qatari–Abu Dhabi War"), mainly due to fear of British intervention on Abu Dhabi's side.[^85] In addition, the Ottomans supported the Ottoman subject Mohammed bin Abdul Wahab who attempted to supplant Al Thani as [kaymakam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaymakam "Kaymakam") of Qatar in 1888.[^86] This eventually led Al Thani to rebel against the Ottomans, whom he believed were seeking to usurp control of the peninsula. He resigned as kaymakam and stopped paying taxes in August 1892.[^87]

In February 1893, Mehmed Hafiz Pasha arrived in Qatar in the interests of seeking unpaid taxes and accosting Jassim bin Mohammed's opposition to proposed Ottoman administrative reforms. Fearing that he would face death or imprisonment, Jassim retreated to Al Wajbah (16 km or 10 mi west of Doha), accompanied by several tribe members. Mehmed's demand that Jassim disband his troops and pledge his loyalty to the Ottomans was met with refusal. In March, Mehmed imprisoned Jassim's brother and 13 prominent Qatari tribal leaders on the Ottoman [corvette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corvette "Corvette") *Merrikh* as punishment for his insubordination. After Mehmed declined an offer to release the captives for a fee of 10,000 [liras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_lira "Turkish lira"), he ordered a column of approximately 200 troops to advance towards Jassim's [Al Wajbah Fort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Wajbah_Fort "Al Wajbah Fort") under the command of Yusuf Effendi, thus signalling the start of the [Battle of Al Wajbah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Al_Wajbah "Battle of Al Wajbah").[^59]

Effendi's troops came under heavy gunfire by a sizable troop of Qatari infantry and cavalry shortly after arriving at Al Wajbah. They retreated to Shebaka fortress where they were again forced to draw back from a Qatari incursion. After retreating from Shebaka to Al Bidda, the Ottomans were besieged by Jassim's forces, forcing them to surrender, release captives, and secure safe passage for Mehmed Pasha's cavalry to [Hofuf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hofuf "Hofuf").[^88] Although Qatar did not gain full independence from the Ottoman Empire, the result of the battle forced a treaty that would later form the basis of Qatar's emerging as an autonomous country within the empire.[^89]

### British period (1916–1971)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Zubarah_Fort1.jpg/250px-Zubarah_Fort1.jpg)

Zubarah Fort built in 1938

By the [Anglo-Ottoman Convention of 1913](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Ottoman_Convention_of_1913 "Anglo-Ottoman Convention of 1913"), the Ottomans agreed to renounce their claim to Qatar and withdraw their garrison from Doha. With the outbreak of [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") however, nothing was done to carry this out, and the garrison remained in the fort at Doha, although its numbers dwindled as men deserted. In 1915, with the presence of British gunboats in the harbour, Abdullah bin Jassim Al Thani (who was pro-British) persuaded the remainder to abandon the fort, and when British troops approached the following morning, they found it deserted.[^90] [^91]

Qatar became a British protectorate on 3 November 1916 [^92] when the United Kingdom signed a treaty with Sheikh Abdullah bin Jassim Al Thani to bring Qatar under its [Trucial System of Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trucial_States "Trucial States"). The treaty reserved foreign affairs and defence to the United Kingdom but allowed internal autonomy. While Abdullah agreed not to enter into any relations with any other power without the prior consent of the British government, the latter guaranteed the protection of Qatar from aggression by sea and provide its 'good offices' in the event of an attack by land. This latter undertaking was left deliberately vague.[^90] [^93]

On 5 May 1935, while agreeing an oil concession with the [Anglo-Persian Oil Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Persian_Oil_Company "Anglo-Persian Oil Company"), Abdullah signed another treaty with the British government which granted Qatar protection against internal and external threats.[^90] Oil reserves were first discovered in 1939. Exploitation and development were, however, delayed by [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II").[^94]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/British_stamp_overprinted_for_use_in_Qatar_1_April_1957.jpg/250px-British_stamp_overprinted_for_use_in_Qatar_1_April_1957.jpg)

A British Wilding series stamp, issued 1 April 1957, and overprinted for use in Qatar

The focus of British interests in Qatar changed after the Second World War with the independence of India, the creation of [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan") in 1947, and the development of oil in Qatar. In 1949, the appointment of the first British political officer in Doha, John Wilton, signified a strengthening of Anglo-Qatari relations.[^95] Oil exports began in 1949, and oil revenues became the country's main source of revenue; the pearl trade had gone into decline. These revenues were used to fund the expansion and modernisation of Qatar's infrastructure.

When Britain officially announced in 1968 that it would withdraw from the Persian Gulf in three years' time, Qatar joined talks with Bahrain and seven other Trucial States to create the [Federation of Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federation_of_Arab_Emirates "Federation of Arab Emirates"). Regional disputes, however, persuaded Qatar and Bahrain to withdraw from the talks and become independent states separate from the Trucial States, which went on to become the [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates").[^96]

### Independence and later (1971–2000)

Under an agreement with the United Kingdom,[^97] [^98] on 3 September 1971, the "special treaty arrangements" that were "inconsistent with full international responsibility as a sovereign and independent state" were terminated.[^98] [Ahmad bin Ali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmad_bin_Ali_Al_Thani "Ahmad bin Ali Al Thani") was the emir of Qatar. He was [deposed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1972_Qatari_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1972 Qatari coup d'état") on 22 February 1972 by [Khalifa bin Hamad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalifa_bin_Hamad_Al_Thani "Khalifa bin Hamad Al Thani").

In 1991, Qatar played a significant role in the [Gulf War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_War "Gulf War"), particularly during the [Battle of Khafji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Khafji "Battle of Khafji") in which Qatari tanks rolled through the streets of the town and provided fire support for the [Saudi Arabian National Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabian_National_Guard "Saudi Arabian National Guard") units that were engaging [Iraqi Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraqi_Army "Iraqi Army") troops. Qatar allowed coalition troops from Canada to use the country as an [airbase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada_Dry_One "Canada Dry One") to launch aircraft on [combat air patrol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combat_air_patrol "Combat air patrol") duty and also permitted air forces from the United States and France to operate in its territories.[^42]

In 1995, Emir [Hamad bin Khalifa Al Thani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamad_bin_Khalifa_Al_Thani "Hamad bin Khalifa Al Thani") [seised control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1995_Qatari_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1995 Qatari coup d'état") of the country from his father [Khalifa bin Hamad Al Thani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalifa_bin_Hamad_Al_Thani "Khalifa bin Hamad Al Thani"), with the support of the armed forces and cabinet, as well as neighbouring states and France.[^99] [^100] Under Emir Hamad, Qatar experienced a moderate degree of liberalisation, including the launch of the [Al Jazeera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Jazeera_Media_Network "Al Jazeera Media Network") television station in 1996, the endorsement of [women's suffrage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage "Women's suffrage") or right to vote in municipal elections in 1999, participation of women in society in 2003, drafting its first written [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Qatar "Constitution of Qatar") in 2005 and inauguration of a Roman Catholic church in 2008.[^101] An [unsuccessful counter-coup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1996_Qatari_coup_attempt "1996 Qatari coup attempt") was staged in 1996.

### 21st century

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Corniche_Doha_Qatar.jpg/250px-Corniche_Doha_Qatar.jpg)

Traditional dhows in front of the West Bay skyline as seen from the Doha Corniche

Qatar's economy and status as a [regional power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power") rapidly grew in the 2000s.[^102] According to the UN, the nation's economic growth, measured by GDP, was the fastest in the world during this decade.[^103] [^104] The basis of this growth lay in the exploitation of [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas") in the North Field during the 1990s.[^105] At the same time, the population tripled between 2001 and 2011, mostly from an influx of foreigners.[^106]

In 2003, Qatar served as the [United States Central Command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Central_Command "United States Central Command") headquarters and one of the main launching sites of the [invasion of Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq "2003 invasion of Iraq") to topple the government of [Saddam Hussein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saddam_Hussein "Saddam Hussein").[^107] Shortly before the invasion, Qatari government offered sanctuary to Saddam.[^108] In March 2005, a [suicide bombing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suicide_attack "Suicide attack") killed a British teacher [^109] at the Doha Players Theatre, shocking the country, which had not previously experienced acts of terrorism. The bombing was carried out by Omar Ahmed Abdullah Ali, an Egyptian resident in Qatar who had suspected ties to [Al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Qaeda_in_the_Arabian_Peninsula "Al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula").[^110] [^111]

The increased influence of Qatar and its role during the [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring"), especially during the [2011 uprisings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bahraini_protests_of_2011%E2%80%932014 "Bahraini protests of 2011–2014") in Bahrain against King [Hamad bin Isa Al Khalifa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamad_bin_Isa_Al_Khalifa "Hamad bin Isa Al Khalifa"), [worsened longstanding tensions with Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar%E2%80%93Saudi_Arabia_diplomatic_conflict "Qatar–Saudi Arabia diplomatic conflict"), the neighbouring [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates"), and Bahrain.[^112] In 2011, Qatar joined [NATO operations in Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_military_intervention_in_Libya "2011 military intervention in Libya") and reportedly armed Libyan opposition groups against [Muammar Gaddafi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi "Muammar Gaddafi").[^113] It was also a major funder of weapons for [rebel groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belligerents_in_the_Syrian_civil_war#Syrian_opposition_and_allies "Belligerents in the Syrian civil war") in the [Syrian civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syrian_civil_war "Syrian civil war") against [Bashar al-Assad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bashar_al-Assad "Bashar al-Assad").[^114] Qatar participated in the [Saudi Arabian-led intervention in Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi-led_intervention_in_the_Yemeni_civil_war "Saudi-led intervention in the Yemeni civil war") against the [Houthis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houthis "Houthis") and forces loyal to former President [Ali Abdullah Saleh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ali_Abdullah_Saleh "Ali Abdullah Saleh").[^115]

In June 2017, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Bahrain, Egypt and Yemen [broke diplomatic ties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar_diplomatic_crisis "Qatar diplomatic crisis") with Qatar, accusing Qatar of [supporting terrorism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar_and_state-sponsored_terrorism "Qatar and state-sponsored terrorism").[^116] The crisis escalated a dispute over Qatar's support of the [Muslim Brotherhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_Brotherhood "Muslim Brotherhood"), which is considered a terrorist organisation by some Arab nations,[^117] as well as over its ties with [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran "Iran"). Qatar was expelled from the anti-Houthi coalition. The crisis lead to a strengthening of Qatar's relationship with Iran, which provided significant [material and political support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Qatar_relations#2017_Iranian_support_for_Qatar "Iran–Qatar relations"), rather than its weakening.[^118] [^119] [^120] [^121] The diplomatic crisis ended in January 2021 with the signing of the [al-Ula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Ula "Al-Ula") declaration.[^122] Qatar did not fulfil any of the original 13 demands, with analysts saying that the Gulf states agreed instead to a vague "joint security declaration".[^123]

On 2 December 2010, Qatar won the right to host the [2022 FIFA World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_FIFA_World_Cup "2022 FIFA World Cup") from 21 November to 18 December 2022, making it the first Arab and Muslim-majority country to do so, and the third Asian country to host it following the [2002 FIFA World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2002_FIFA_World_Cup "2002 FIFA World Cup") in Japan and South Korea.[^124]. The awarding increased further investment and developments within the nation during the 2010s.[^125] In June 2013, Sheikh [Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamim_bin_Hamad_Al_Thani "Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani") became the emir of Qatar after his father handed over power.[^126] Sheikh Tamim has prioritised improving the domestic welfare of citizens, which includes establishing advanced healthcare and education systems, and expanding the country's infrastructure in preparation for the hosting of the 2022 World Cup.[^127]
