**Jordan**,[^1] officially the **Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan**,[^2] is a country in the [Southern Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Levant "Southern Levant") region of [West Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Asia "West Asia"). Jordan is bordered by [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria") to the north, [Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq "Iraq") to the east, [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia") to the south, and both [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel") and [Palestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestine "Palestine") ([West Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Bank "West Bank")) to the west. The [Jordan River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_River "Jordan River"), flowing into the [Dead Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_Sea "Dead Sea"), is located along the country's western border within the [Jordan Rift Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_Rift_Valley "Jordan Rift Valley"). Jordan has a small coastline along the [Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea") in its southwest, separated by the [Gulf of Aqaba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Aqaba "Gulf of Aqaba") from [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt"). [Amman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amman "Amman") is the country's capital and [largest city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Jordan "List of cities in Jordan"), as well as the [most populous city in the Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_largest_cities_in_the_Levant_region_by_population "List of largest cities in the Levant region by population").

Inhabited by humans since the [Paleolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleolithic "Paleolithic") period, three kingdoms developed in [Transjordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transjordan_\(region\) "Transjordan (region)") during the [Iron Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age"): [Ammon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammon "Ammon"), [Moab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moab "Moab") and [Edom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edom "Edom"). In the third century BC, the Arab [Nabataeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nabataeans "Nabataeans") established [their kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nabataean_Kingdom "Nabataean Kingdom") centered in [Petra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petra "Petra"). The [Greco-Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Roman_world "Greco-Roman world") period saw the establishment of several cities in Transjordan that comprised the [Decapolis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decapolis "Decapolis"). After the end of [Byzantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire") rule, the region became part of the Islamic caliphates of the [Rashidun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashidun_Caliphate "Rashidun Caliphate"), [Umayyad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate"), [Abbasid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate"), and the [Ottoman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"). Following the 1916 [Great Arab Revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Arab_Revolt "Great Arab Revolt") during [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), former [Ottoman Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria_vilayet "Syria vilayet") was [partitioned](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_of_the_Ottoman_Empire#British_Mandates "Partition of the Ottoman Empire"), leading to the [establishment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Establishment_of_the_Emirate_of_Transjordan "Establishment of the Emirate of Transjordan") of the [Emirate of Transjordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Transjordan "Emirate of Transjordan") in 1921, which became a British protectorate. In 1946, Jordan gained independence and became officially known as the Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan.[^3] Jordan [captured and annexed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordanian_annexation_of_the_West_Bank "Jordanian annexation of the West Bank") the [West Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Bank "West Bank") during the [1948 Palestine war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1948_Palestine_war "1948 Palestine war") until it was occupied by Israel [in 1967](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six-Day_War "Six-Day War"). Jordan [renounced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordanian_disengagement_from_the_West_Bank "Jordanian disengagement from the West Bank") its claim to the territory to the [Palestinians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestine_Liberation_Organization "Palestine Liberation Organization") in 1988 and signed a [peace treaty with Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel%E2%80%93Jordan_peace_treaty "Israel–Jordan peace treaty") in 1994.

Jordan is a [semi-arid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-arid_climate "Semi-arid climate") country, covering an area of 89,342 km <sup>2</sup> (34,495 sq mi) with a population of 11.5 million, making it the [eleventh-most populous Arab country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Arab_League_countries_by_population "List of Arab League countries by population"). The dominant majority, or around 95% of the country's population, is [Sunni Muslim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunni_Islam "Sunni Islam"), with the rest being mostly [Arab Christian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_in_Jordan "Christianity in Jordan"). Jordan was mostly unscathed by the violence that swept the region following the [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring") in 2010. From as early as 1948, Jordan has accepted refugees from multiple neighbouring countries in conflict. An estimated 2.1 million [Palestinian refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_refugees "Palestinian refugees"), most of whom hold Jordanian citizenship, as well as 1.4 million [Syrian refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syrian_refugees "Syrian refugees"), were residing in Jordan in 2015.[^7] The kingdom is also a refuge for thousands of [Christian Iraqis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_in_Iraq "Christianity in Iraq") fleeing persecution.[^11] [^12] While Jordan continues to accept refugees, the large Syrian influx during the 2010s has placed substantial strain on national resources and infrastructure.[^13]

The sovereign state is a [semi-constitutional monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-constitutional_monarchy "Semi-constitutional monarchy"), but the king holds wide executive and legislative powers. Jordan is a founding member of the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League") and the [Organisation of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation"). Jordan has a high [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index"), ranking 100th, and is considered a lower middle income economy. The [Jordanian economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Jordan "Economy of Jordan"), one of the smallest economies in the region, is attractive to foreign investors based upon a skilled workforce.[^14] Jordan is a major tourist destination, also attracting medical tourism with [its well-developed health sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_in_Jordan "Health in Jordan").[^15] Nonetheless, a lack of natural resources, large flow of refugees, and regional turmoil have hampered economic growth.[^16]

## Etymology

Jordan takes its name from the [Jordan River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_River "Jordan River"), which forms much of Jordan's northwestern border.[^17] While several theories for the origin of the river's name have been proposed (such as relation to the [Egyptian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_language "Egyptian language") loanword 'yǝʾor'; 'big river', the [Nile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile "Nile")),[^18] it is most plausible that it derives from the [Semitic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semitic_languages "Semitic languages") word 'Yarad' ( ירד; 'flow down' or 'descend"), referring to the river's declivity [^19] and with cognates found in [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"), [Aramaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aramaic_language "Aramaic language"), [Hebrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_language "Hebrew language"), [Mandaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandaic_language "Mandaic language"), etc.[^19]

The earliest use of the name is found in the ancient Egyptian [Papyrus Anastasi I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papyrus_Anastasi_I "Papyrus Anastasi I") as *Yārdon*, probably dating to the reign of [Rameses II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rameses_II "Rameses II").[^20] Much of the area that makes up modern Jordan was historically called *[Transjordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transjordan_\(region\) "Transjordan (region)")*, meaning "across the Jordan"; the term is used to denote the lands east of the river.[^19] The [Hebrew Bible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_Bible "Hebrew Bible") uses the term עבר הירדן *Ever ha'Yarden* (lit. 'the other side of the Jordan') for the area.[^19] Early Arab chronicles call the river *Al-Urdunn* (a term cognate to the Hebrew *Yarden*).[^21] *[Jund Al-Urdunn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jund_Al-Urdunn "Jund Al-Urdunn")* was a military district around the river in the early Islamic era.[^21] Later, during the [Crusades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Jerusalem "Kingdom of Jerusalem") in the beginning of the second millennium, a lordship was established in the area under the name of *[Oultrejordain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oultrejordain "Oultrejordain")*.[^22]

## History

### Ancient period

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/20100923_amman37.JPG/250px-20100923_amman37.JPG)

The 'Ain Ghazal Statues, c. 7250 BC, uncovered in Amman, are some of the oldest human statues ever found.

The oldest known evidence of [hominid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hominid "Hominid") habitation in Jordan dates back at least 200,000 years.[^23] Jordan is a rich source of [Paleolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleolithic "Paleolithic") human remains (up to 20,000 years old) due to its location within the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant"), where [migrations out of Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recent_African_origin_of_modern_humans "Recent African origin of modern humans") converged,[^24] and its more humid climate during the [Late Pleistocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Pleistocene "Late Pleistocene"), which resulted in the formation of numerous remains-preserving wetlands in the region.[^25] Past lakeshore environments attracted different groups of hominids, and several remains of tools dating from the Late Pleistocene have been found there.[^24] Scientists have found the world's oldest known evidence of bread-making at a 14,500-year-old [Natufian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natufian_culture "Natufian culture") site in Jordan's northeastern desert.[^26]

During the [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") period (10,000–4,500 BC), there was a transition there from a [hunter-gatherer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hunter-gatherer "Hunter-gatherer") culture to a culture with established populous agricultural villages.[^27] ['Ain Ghazal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayn_Ghazal_\(archaeological_site\) "Ayn Ghazal (archaeological site)"), one such village located at a site in the eastern part of present-day [Amman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amman "Amman"), is one of the largest known prehistoric settlements in the [Near East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_East "Near East").[^28] [Dozens of plaster statues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%27Ain_Ghazal_Statues "'Ain Ghazal Statues") of the human form, dating to 7250 BC or earlier, have been uncovered there. They are one of the oldest large-scale representations of humans ever found.[^29] During the [Chalcolithic period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalcolithic "Chalcolithic") (4500–3600 BC), several villages emerged in Transjordan including [Tulaylet Ghassul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teleilat_el_Ghassul "Teleilat el Ghassul") in the [Jordan Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_Valley "Jordan Valley");[^30] a series of circular stone enclosures in the eastern basalt desert from the same period have long baffled archaeologists.[^31]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Mesha_Stele_%28511142469%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Mesha_Stele_%28511142469%29_%28cropped%29.jpg)

The Mesha Stele (c. 840 BC) records the glory of Mesha, King of Moab, displayed at the Louvre Museum.

Fortified towns and urban centres first emerged in the southern Levant early in the [Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") (3600–1200 BC).[^32] [Wadi Feynan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wadi_Feynan "Wadi Feynan") became a regional centre for copper extraction: the metal was exploited on a large scale to produce bronze.[^33] Trade and movement of people in the Middle East peaked, causing cultural innovations and whole civilisations to spread.[^34] Villages in Transjordan expanded rapidly in areas with reliable water-resources and arable land.[^34] [Ancient Egyptian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") populations expanded towards the Levant and came to control both banks of the Jordan River.[^35]

During the [Iron Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age") (1200–332 BC), after the withdrawal of the Egyptians, Transjordan was home to the kingdoms of [Ammon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammon "Ammon"), [Edom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edom "Edom") and [Moab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moab "Moab").[^36] These peoples spoke [Semitic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semitic_languages "Semitic languages") of the [Canaanite group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canaanite_languages "Canaanite languages"). Archaeologists have concluded that their polities were tribal kingdoms rather than states.[^36] Ammon was located in the Amman plateau; Moab in the highlands east of the Dead Sea; and Edom in the area around [Wadi Araba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabah "Arabah") in the south.[^36] The northwestern region of the Transjordan, known then as [Gilead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilead "Gilead"), was settled by the [Israelites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israelites "Israelites").[^37]

The three kingdoms continually clashed with the neighbouring Hebrew kingdoms of [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Israel_\(Samaria\) "Kingdom of Israel (Samaria)") and [Judah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Judah "Kingdom of Judah"), centred west of the Jordan River.[^38] One record of this is the [Mesha Stele](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesha_Stele "Mesha Stele"), erected by the Moabite king [Mesha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesha "Mesha") in 840 BC. In an inscription on it, he lauds himself for the building projects that he initiated in Moab and commemorates his glory and his victory against the Israelites.[^39] The stele constitutes one of the most important archeological parallels to [accounts recorded in the Bible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historicity_of_the_Bible "Historicity of the Bible").[^40] At the same time, Israel and the Kingdom of [Aram-Damascus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aram-Damascus "Aram-Damascus") competed for control of the Gilead.[^41] [^42]

Around 740–720 BC, Israel and Aram-Damascus were conquered by the [Neo-Assyrian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire "Neo-Assyrian Empire"). The kingdoms of Ammon, Edom and Moab were subjugated but were allowed to maintain some degree of independence.[^43] Then in 627 BC, following after the disintegration of the Assyrians' empire, [Babylonians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-Babylonian_Empire "Neo-Babylonian Empire") took control of the area.[^43] Although the kingdoms supported the Babylonians against Judah in the 597 BC [sack of Jerusalem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_\(597_BC\) "Siege of Jerusalem (597 BC)"), they rebelled against Babylon a decade later.[^43] The kingdoms were reduced to [vassals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vassal "Vassal"), a status they retained under the [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") and [Hellenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macedonia_\(ancient_kingdom\) "Macedonia (ancient kingdom)") empires.[^43] By the beginning of [Roman rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") around 63 BC, the kingdoms of Ammon, Edom and Moab had lost their distinct identities and were assimilated into the Roman culture.[^36] Some Edomites survived longer – driven by the Nabataeans, they had migrated to southern [Judea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judea "Judea"), which became known as [Idumaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idumaea "Idumaea"). They were later converted to [Judaism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judaism "Judaism") by the [Hasmoneans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasmonean_dynasty "Hasmonean dynasty").[^44]

### Classical period

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Al_Khazneh_Petra_edit_2_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Al_Khazneh_Petra_edit_2_%28cropped%29.jpg)

Petra, one of the New 7 Wonders of the World, contains Al-Khazneh, believed to be the mausoleum of the Nabataean King Aretas IV, 1st century AD.

[Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") 's [conquest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wars_of_Alexander_the_Great "Wars of Alexander the Great") of the Persian Empire in 332 BC introduced [Hellenistic culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_period "Hellenistic period") to the Middle East.[^45] After Alexander's death in 323 BC, the [empire split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_of_Babylon "Partition of Babylon") among his generals, and in the end much of Transjordan was disputed between the [Ptolemies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemaic_Kingdom "Ptolemaic Kingdom") based in Egypt and the [Seleucids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seleucid_Empire "Seleucid Empire") based in Syria.[^45] The [Nabataeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nabataeans "Nabataeans"), nomadic Arabs based south of Edom, managed to establish an independent kingdom in 169 BC by exploiting the struggle between the two Greek powers.[^45] The [Nabataean Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nabataean_Kingdom "Nabataean Kingdom") controlled much of the trade routes of the region, and it stretched south along the [Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea") coast into the [Hejaz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hejaz "Hejaz") desert, up to as far north as [Damascus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damascus "Damascus"), which it controlled for a short period (85–71 BC).[^46] The Nabataeans massed a fortune from their control of the trade routes, often drawing the envy of their neighbours.[^47] Petra, Nabataea's capital, flourished in the 1st century AD, driven by its extensive water irrigation systems and agriculture.[^48] The Nabataeans were talented [stone carvers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stone_carving "Stone carving"), building their most elaborate structure, [Al-Khazneh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Khazneh "Al-Khazneh"), in the first century AD.[^49] It is believed to be the [mausoleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mausoleum "Mausoleum") of the Arab Nabataean King [Aretas IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aretas_IV_Philopatris "Aretas IV Philopatris").[^49]

Roman legions under [Pompey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pompey "Pompey") conquered much of the Levant in 63 BC, inaugurating a period of Roman rule that lasted four centuries.[^50] In 106 AD, Emperor [Trajan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajan "Trajan") annexed Nabataea unopposed and rebuilt the [King's Highway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King%27s_Highway_\(ancient\) "King's Highway (ancient)") which became known as the [Via Traiana Nova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Via_Traiana_Nova "Via Traiana Nova") road.[^50] The Romans gave the Greek cities of Transjordan— [Philadelphia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philadelphia_\(Amman\) "Philadelphia (Amman)") (Amman), [Gerasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerash "Jerash") (Jerash), [Gedara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umm_Qays "Umm Qays") (Umm Quays), [Pella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pella,_Jordan "Pella, Jordan") (Tabaqat Fahl) and [Arbila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irbid "Irbid") (Irbid)—and other Hellenistic cities in Palestine and southern Syria, a level of autonomy by forming the *[Decapolis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decapolis "Decapolis")*, a ten-city league.[^51] Jerash is one of the best preserved Roman cities in the East; it was even visited by Emperor [Hadrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadrian "Hadrian") during his journey to Palestine.[^52]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Jerash_01_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Jerash_01_%28cropped%29.jpg)

The Oval Forum of Jerash (c. 1st century AD), then member of the ten-city Greco-Roman league, the Decapolis, seven of which are present in modern-day Jordan

In 324 AD, the Roman Empire split and the Eastern Roman Empire, later known as the [Byzantine Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire"), continued to control or influence the region until 636.[^53] Christianity had [become legal within the empire in 313](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edict_of_Milan "Edict of Milan") after co-emperors [Constantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantine_the_Great "Constantine the Great") and [Licinius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Licinius "Licinius") signed an edict of toleration.[^53] In 380, the [Edict of Thessalonica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edict_of_Thessalonica "Edict of Thessalonica") made Christianity the official state religion. Transjordan prospered during the Byzantine era, and Christian churches were built throughout the region.[^54]

The [Aqaba Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqaba_Church "Aqaba Church") in [Ayla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqaba "Aqaba") was built during this era. It is considered to be the [world's first purpose built Christian church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_oldest_church_buildings "List of oldest church buildings").[^55] [Umm ar-Rasas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umm_ar-Rasas "Umm ar-Rasas") in southern Amman contains at least 16 Byzantine churches.[^56] Meanwhile, Petra's importance declined as sea trade routes emerged, and after a [363 earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilee_earthquake_of_363 "Galilee earthquake of 363") destroyed many structures it declined further, eventually being abandoned.[^49] The [Sasanian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sasanian_Empire "Sasanian Empire") in the east became the Byzantines' rivals, and [frequent confrontations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine%E2%80%93Sasanian_wars "Byzantine–Sasanian wars") sometimes led to the Sasanids controlling some parts of the region, including Transjordan.[^57]

### Islamic era

In 629, during the [Battle of Mu'tah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Mu%27tah "Battle of Mu'tah") in what is today [Karak Governorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karak_Governorate "Karak Governorate"), the Byzantines and their [Arab Christian clients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patronage_in_ancient_Rome "Patronage in ancient Rome"), the [Ghassanids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghassanids "Ghassanids"), staved off an attack by a Muslim [Rashidun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashidun "Rashidun") force that marched northwards towards the Levant from the Hejaz.[^58] The Byzantines however were defeated by the Muslims in 636 at the decisive [Battle of the Yarmuk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Yarmuk "Battle of the Yarmuk") just north of Transjordan.[^58] Transjordan was an essential territory for the conquest of Damascus.[^59] The Rashidun caliphate was followed by that of the [Umayyads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") (661–750).[^59]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/The_Madaba_Map%2C_part_of_a_floor_mosaic_in_the_early_Byzantine_church_of_Saint_George_depicting_the_Holy_Land_in_the_6th_century_AD%2C_Madaba%2C_Jordan_%2834477807231%29.jpg/250px-thumbnail.jpg)

Byzantine Madaba Map showing the Jordan River and the Dead Sea. Dating to the 6th century AD, it is the oldest surviving depiction of the Holy Land.

Under the Umayyad Caliphate, several [desert castles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert_castles "Desert castles") were constructed in Transjordan, including [Qasr Al-Mshatta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qasr_Al-Mshatta "Qasr Al-Mshatta") and [Qasr Al-Hallabat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qasr_Al-Hallabat "Qasr Al-Hallabat").[^59] The [Abbasid Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate") 's campaign to take over the Umayyad's began in a village in Transjordan known as [Humayma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humayma "Humayma").[^60] The powerful [749 earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/749_Galilee_earthquake "749 Galilee earthquake") is thought to have contributed to the Umayyads' [defeat by the Abbasids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_revolution "Abbasid revolution"), who moved the caliphate's capital from Damascus to [Baghdad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baghdad "Baghdad").[^60] During Abbasid rule (750–969), several Arab tribes moved northwards and settled in the Levant.[^59] As had happened during the Roman era, growth of maritime trade diminished Transjordan's central position, and the area became increasingly impoverished.[^61] After the decline of the Abbasids, Transjordan was ruled by the [Fatimid Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimid_Caliphate "Fatimid Caliphate") (969–1070), then by the [Crusader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusades "Crusades") [Kingdom of Jerusalem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Jerusalem "Kingdom of Jerusalem") (1115–1187).[^62]

The Crusaders constructed several castles as part of the Lordship of Oultrejordain, including [Montreal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montreal_\(castle\) "Montreal (castle)") and Al-Karak.[^63] During the [Battle of Hattin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Hattin "Battle of Hattin") (1187) near [Lake Tiberias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_of_Galilee "Sea of Galilee") just north of Transjordan, the Crusaders lost to [Saladin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saladin "Saladin"), the founder of the [Ayyubid dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayyubid_dynasty "Ayyubid dynasty") (1187–1260).[^64] The Ayyubids built the [Ajloun Castle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajloun_Castle "Ajloun Castle") and rebuilt older castles to be used as military outposts against the Crusaders.[^64] Villages in Transjordan under the Ayyubids became important stops for Muslim pilgrims going to [Mecca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mecca "Mecca") who travelled along the route that connected Syria to the Hejaz.[^65] Several of the Ayyubid castles were used and expanded by the [Mamluks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mamluk_Sultanate "Mamluk Sultanate") (1260–1516), who divided Transjordan between the provinces of Karak and Damascus.[^66] During the next century Transjordan experienced [Mongol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire") attacks, but the Mongols were ultimately repelled by the Mamluks at the [Battle of Ain Jalut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Ain_Jalut "Battle of Ain Jalut") (1260).[^67]
