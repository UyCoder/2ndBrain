Chinese infrastructure investment and development in Nepal are critical to the territorial integrity of the People’s Republic of China (PRC) and strategically extend the power of the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) into sensitive spaces of South Asia. While trade flows and investment patterns across the China-Nepal borderlands reflect asymmetrical power relations between Beijing and Kathmandu, a grounded, geographic review of the region reveals three key observations: a historical linkage between border resolutions and Chinese-facilitated infrastructure development in Nepal, an ongoing “corridorization” of Nepal that is both real and imagined, and a persistent oscillation of border openings and closings that challenges the mobility practices of local populations and yet also escapes the PRC’s enhanced controls. Attention to the Himalayan region, much like borderlands elsewhere in Asia, reinforces the adage to look to the margins to see the state in new and often overlooked ways, as “borders offer unique vantage points to produce decentered accounts of the state and denaturalized narratives of nationalist projects.” [^1]

This essay progresses geographically and historically as follows.[^2] A brief review of China-Nepal border exchanges beginning in 1955 through the early 2000s provides essential historical context. Importantly, this period includes episodes of conflict between Nepal and China and also establishes the diplomatic terms, and cartographic delimitations, on which the China-Nepal border and its map function today. Next, attention to a two-decade period in Sino-Nepali relations (from 2000 to 2020) illustrates the rapid scaling up of transborder infrastructure development, foreign direct investment, and humanitarian aid between the two countries. Characterized as a period of “infrastructural relations” between Beijing and Kathmandu, this is a time of Chinese activity in Nepal under the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) when Chinese FDI, as well as humanitarian and post-disaster activities, reached new levels, eclipsing precedents linked to India. Finally, the closure of the China-Nepal border induced by the Covid-19 pandemic led to the subsequent gradual but often unpredictable reopening of a select number of border posts. Despite varying levels of functionality at most Himalayan border crossings, commercial and social activities throughout these spaces reflect not just asymmetries of geopolitical power but also shared geoeconomic interests between Nepal and China as well as crucial agency exercised by the state of Nepal and its borderland citizens.

## Histories of Development and Security: The Making of the China-Nepal Border

Nepal’s diplomatic relations with China were established in 1955, soon after the CCP annexed Tibet. The two countries subsequently demarcated a 1,414-kilometer Himalayan border, and a series of boundary treaties and protocols were drafted for international cooperation, many of which also established a formal link between border security, international trade, and infrastructure development. During this Cold War era, the Nepali government’s National Planning Commission set up regular five-year development plans, based largely on models established by the Soviet and CCP governments.

Between 1961 and 1979, and again through the 1980s and into the early 2000s, Chinese and Nepali authorities drafted several reiterations of their 1961 Boundary Treaty along with a series of maps designating the border. These documents did not change the border so much as revisit agreements on territorial designation and maintain demarcations that run along Himalayan ridgelines. Periodic reviews in 1979, 1988, and 2005 included physical visits to border sites to inspect, assess, and repair border markers; as well, more recent efforts have been made to collect GPS coordinates at each site. With the important exception of the Lipu Lekh Pass and Kalapani territory disputed between Kathmandu and New Delhi, delimitations of the Nepal-China border remain predominantly unchanged since 1961.[^3]

In the early 1960s, new terms of engagement also pointed to Beijing’s desire to create a more stable relationship with Kathmandu. As an early form of “infrastructural relations,” [^4] this evolution of border resolutions was inextricably tied to unprecedented levels of Chinese aid and investment in infrastructural development across Nepal. For example, in an explicit expression of “friendship” focused on technical expertise and infrastructure development, the Protocol on Economic Aid led directly to the China-Nepal Highway Construction Agreement of 1961 and subsequently to the development of the China-Nepal Arniko-Friendship Highway in the 1960s.

## Infrastructure Investment and Humanitarian Aid: Disasters and Interventions

Infrastructure development and shared visions of expanding trade and transportation corridors between China and South Asia have long characterized operations on the China-Nepal border and Sino-Nepali relations more broadly. The scale of Chinese financial investment and material development that emerged in Nepal a decade ago is telling. For example, in 2014 Chinese FDI to Nepal surpassed Indian FDI for the first time, and China has remained Nepal’s largest investor since then. In March 2015, marking 60 years of diplomatic relations, Beijing committed a fivefold increase to its grant assistance to Kathmandu. The pledged annual amount of 800 million renminbi ($130 million) was earmarked largely for infrastructure development, technical assistance, and professional training.[^5] In borderland contexts, Kathmandu and Beijing also signed a memorandum of understanding in November 2014 committing 10 million renminbi ($1.63 million) annually over a period of five years for the development of Nepal’s northern border districts. As many of these borderland regions are relatively remote and lack advanced medical facilities or other road-accessible infrastructure, such as large bazaars or modern shopping malls, Chinese investment and aid packages for road construction enable local borderland populations to access markets and hospitals in the neighboring Tibet Autonomous Region (TAR).[^6]

Following major earthquakes that struck Nepal in April and May 2015, relations between China and Nepal became even closer, especially in the Himalayan borderlands. Beijing responded to the disaster with its largest ever humanitarian effort and rapidly mobilized earthmoving equipment assigned to hydropower development projects in order to reopen cross-border roads and other infrastructural systems. In financial terms, Beijing’s reaction to the earthquakes was also unprecedented. At the International Conference on Nepal’s Reconstruction in June 2015, China committed 4.7 billion renminbi ($480 million) for infrastructural repair and development. As such, the 2015 earthquakes reshaped Nepal’s geopolitical and financial relationship with China as well as with other foreign donors and developers, especially India.[^7]

During the 2015–16 post-earthquake winter, political relations between Kathmandu and New Delhi deteriorated as an Indian-backed blockade at Nepal’s southern border precipitated a nationwide fuel crisis.[^8] But again Beijing came to the rescue. To alleviate Nepal’s energy emergency, the Chinese embassy in Kathmandu coordinated a highly publicized delivery of fuel to Nepal, sending one dozen tankers to the Timure trade port at the newly opened northern border crossing that links the large Tibetan market town of Kyirong to Nepal’s Rasuwa District. Opened in late 2014, this cross-border road quickly became a key vector in ever closer Sino-Nepali international—and indeed, infrastructural—relations. Even if the emergency energy provision was more a symbolic gesture than a substantive intervention, the fuel relief marked a major turning point in broader geopolitical alignments between Nepal, India, and China.

Long neglected by the government of Nepal because of geographic remoteness and the historical marginalization of Indigenous communities, the borderland populations of Nepal’s northern districts rely on critically important Chinese aid. From eastern to western Nepal, elected leaders of rural municipalities in Himalayan districts are regularly invited to China’s TAR to discuss ongoing adjustments to trade relations as well as the distribution of aid packages. Negotiating on behalf of their local constituents, but also representative of the Nepali state, these leaders routinely leverage their positions in strategic and sensitive borderland spaces to extract support from the Chinese government. Provided in part to extend a geopolitical hand into areas that are culturally Tibetan, such material and in-kind support from China frequently includes the provision of excavators and road construction materials as well as solar light panels, batteries, and other equipment directed for rural development. So long as Tibet, and control over its restive environment, remains a central concern to the CCP, neighboring regions of Nepal and their ethnically Tibetan populations will continue to be under the close gaze and extraterritorial reach of Chinese authorities.

Across the Himalayan borderlands, humanitarian aid and infrastructure investment serve as key vectors for what is widely identified as the grounded presence of a global China.[^9] That is, China’s strategic actions following a series of interrelated emergencies that began in 2015 helped relieve Nepal’s post-earthquake crises, took advantage of growing anti-Indian sentiments in Kathmandu, and enabled China to fill a political and economic vacuum in Nepal through aid and development interventions. Through this sequence of post-disaster interventions in Nepal, Chinese activities in the Himalayan borderlands also fit within wider contexts of China’s global development and investment portfolio, now known as transborder BRI projects.

## Trade, Transportation, and Disconnections: From the Belt and Road to Covid-19

Formal dialogue about Nepal’s inclusion in BRI began in 2015, and Kathmandu officially signed onto the program in May 2017. At the 2nd Belt and Road Forum for International Cooperation in April 2019, Chinese and Nepali political leaders announced a new regional infrastructural objective: the Nepal-China Trans-Himalayan Multi-Dimensional Connectivity Network. Combining several proposed BRI projects into one transborder mega-project, the Connectivity Network prioritizes the operationalization of six economic corridors between Nepal and China with enhanced border facilities and advanced transport infrastructure. Emphasizing the high expectations and future promise of this infrastructural assemblage, the president of Nepal, Bidhya Devi Bhandari, stated at the 2nd Belt and Road Forum: “For a landlocked country like Nepal, connectivity is of paramount importance in its socio-economic development. With enhanced connectivity, vast opportunities for trade, investment, tourism and people-to-people relations will open up.” [^10]

During subsequent diplomatic meetings later in 2019, Nepali and Chinese officials signed eighteen new memoranda of understanding and two letters of exchange related to transborder infrastructure investment and development. This further cemented the two countries’ “strategic partnership,” buttressed upon a base of infrastructural relations.[^11]

At the 2nd Belt and Road Forum, President Bhandari also signed with her Chinese counterparts the Protocol on Implementing the Agreement on Transit and Transportation to legalize and further facilitate cross-border commerce along north-south corridors between China and Nepal. This agreement had major geopolitical and geoeconomic implications, ending Nepal’s sole dependence on India for sea-bound export and enabling business enterprises to access four Chinese sea ports and three land ports for trading with third countries.[^12]

Ultimately, visions behind Chinese infrastructure development in Nepal are also oriented toward the Indian marketplace and the potential for Nepal to be a transit zone for the export of Chinese goods to the massive consumer base of South Asia. Tellingly, when the Agreement on Transit and Transportation came into effect in February 2020, it immediately set off negotiations with shipping lines and importers to rewrite modes of transportation, logistical schedules, and administrative procedures for Nepal’s import-export sector. At this time, however, while China-India transit trade through Nepal is part of a bigger dream, the functional operation of the Connectivity Network has yet to be realized, largely due to geological instability combined with other political challenges in the region.

Since early 2020, progress on the development of BRI programs across Nepal has been lackluster. In January 2020, China closed all fourteen of its traditional and official border points with Nepal. Later that spring, as the Covid-19 pandemic began to further paralyze Nepal and its Himalayan borderlands, the first Chinese cargo train carrying construction material and medical equipment bound for Nepal arrived in the Tibetan city of Shigatse, only to then be stopped in its proverbial tracks.[^13] From then until today, Chinese exports into Nepal have flowed more like a trickle than a flood. The scaling up of trans-Himalayan trade as widely imagined is systematically stymied because of roads broken by landslides, borders closed by pandemics and politics, and diplomatic disagreements on how best to move ahead with previously achieved agreements and treaties. That is, while failure to make progress on BRI projects is partly attributable to the closure of the border crossings during the Covid-19 pandemic, it is equally a consequence of political turmoil in Nepal and the logistical and geographic challenges of getting development work done in the country, whether related to BRI or other initiatives.

## Openings and Closures: Differentiated Mobilities across the Borderlands

Reopening the fourteen China-Nepal border sites is significant and long awaited, but functionality remains largely partial, and operability is complicated by environmental and geological hazards. Although four major points were reopened in 2023, ten other crossings, all with more modest transportation and infrastructural facilities, were reopened only in May 2024.[^14] Today, the size, scale, and accessibility of these sites are far from uniform.

The first sites along the China-Nepal border to reopen in May 2023—Zhangmu-Kodari and Kyirong-Rasuwa—are the largest crossings and have major infrastructure, including formal customs houses, dry ports, and quarantine facilities. While motorable bridges are also a key component of both borders, such infrastructure is a major source of vulnerability. Zhangmu-Kodari—the primary and only road-accessible border crossing between Nepal and China for over 50 years—was significantly damaged in the 2015 earthquakes, rendering its Friendship Bridge inoperable, in addition to its upstream and downstream routes along the Arniko Highway. Similarly, while the Kyirong-Rasuwa border post and cross-border road opened to great fanfare in 2014 and immediately offered an alternative to the singular (and precarious) Arniko Highway and Friendship Bridge, it too remains plagued by problems.[^15] Although the route presents great potential for scaling up China-Nepal trade, landslides routinely force closure of the road. More recently, catastrophic flash flooding of the Lende River along the China-Nepal border at the northern edge of Rasuwa District caused severe devastation to all border facilities, adjacent towns and villages, and untold numbers of community members. The key bridge at Rasuwagadhi and dry port of Timure were both swept away, downstream hydropower projects were severely damaged, and at least eleven people were killed, with many more missing following the floods.[^16]

Beyond the ongoing oscillations of border openings and closures at the two major road-accessible sites, the reopening in spring and fall of 2023 of two secondary borders points in Nepal’s more western districts of Mustang and Humla reflects another dimension of China-Nepal infrastructural relations. Both of these border crossings, Likse-Korala and Purang-Hilsa, have experienced remarkably rapid development of local infrastructural facilities, particularly with respect to transport accessibility and the installation of advanced surveillance systems. While both roads and cameras are significantly more advanced on the Chinese side of the border, the impacts of these infrastructural systems are perhaps even more noteworthy and transformative on the Nepali side.

Secondary borders and movement across them function as mechanisms of diplomacy and barometers of security between China and Nepal. Chinese security interests at the Purang-Hilsa border garnered widespread attention via a geospatially sophisticated *New York Times* profile last year.[^17] Control over mobility for Loba Indigenous communities in Mustang with long-standing ties to Tibetan populations in the PRC also has attracted scrutiny from a variety of observers.[^18] Although these highland Himalayan border crossings have now been officially reopened for over two years,[^19] the movement of people and the trade of goods across both borders remain highly regulated by Chinese authorities and subject to decisions that local community members say are hard to predict and seemingly made on the whim of PRC officials stationed in the TAR. As numerous informants explained to me during a series of field trips to border areas in Mustang District, as well as Rasuwa and the Friendship Bridge, even though the land is Nepali, China controls it.[^20]

Control over minority groups has long been a central concern of the CCP. Foreign investment and development in Nepal’s borderlands enable Chinese authorities to implement new surveillance mechanisms and leverage extraterritorial power over what are viewed from Beijing as potentially restive ethnically Tibetan populations outside the PRC’s borders. In the interest of maintaining a stable periphery, Sino-Nepali dialogue connecting development and security across their shared border is continuously renewed. For instance, the “List of Instruments Signed and Exchanged between Nepal and China” in October 2019 established critical links between BRI development programs and bilateral security regimes.[^21] In local contexts, such infrastructural security cooperation is especially prominent in border districts like Mustang and Humla as well as in areas such as Tsum Nubri and Lapchi, given that these regions are also home to culturally Tibetan-Himalayan Indigenous peoples and Tibetan communities in exile.

At some of the smaller China-Nepal border crossings, access to marketplaces in Tibet are of massive importance to communities living in Nepal, despite the modest size of local border infrastructure. This sort of inverse relationship between the significance of cross-border mobility and the scale of border infrastructure is especially true for communities in Nepal’s northernmost regions of Lapchi and Tsum Nubri. Here, connections across the border, as well as goods available in Tibet, are of paramount importance; yet transport linkages to central Nepal remain limited, and in some cases barely exist at all.[^22] As such, during the Covid-19 pandemic it was profoundly difficult for many members of these communities to endure nearly five years of border closure that they have long relied on crossing to access everyday commodities, ranging from foodstuffs and household goods to medicines, fuels, and locally utilized vehicles. Despite these challenges, however, residents in both Lapchi and Tsum Nubri continue to exercise creative ingenuity and indeed agency, devising ways to access goods from Tibet as well as initiating new mechanisms of mobility, with or without infrastructural support, to downstream markets and facilities elsewhere in Nepal. Some tactics to overcome border closures include mobilizing kinship networks in Tibet to help move goods into Nepal as well as rerouting trade flows via less traveled pathways and border crossings outside the gaze of state surveillance.

## Conclusion

A close analysis of asymmetries in power and connectivity across Himalayan borderlands reveals a critical contemporary conjuncture. First, China’s presence in Nepal both deepened and accelerated with earthquake and energy relief in 2015. This point of inflection also marks a key pivot in wider international relations across the Asian region that continues today. Second, local populations across the Himalayan borderlands experienced economic and political impacts of the Covid-19 pandemic as unpredictable border controls. But in response, communities in Nepal generated strategic methods to circumvent border closures and gain access to goods and services required from Tibet and China. Third, infrastructure investment and development serve as key levers that China utilizes to expand its influence and power in Nepal and more widely across South Asia. That is, Chinese power operates politically and spatially through control of the roads, fences, and gates that connect and separate borderland people and places.

Management and control of the China-Nepal borderlands is of great importance to leadership in the PRC. From limiting access to marketplaces in the TAR for citizens of Nepal because of political sensitivities or concerns about Covid-19 to the distribution of aid packages for Himalayan Indigenous communities with ethnic and cultural ties to Tibet, the Sino-Nepali borderlands are critical spaces where Chinese commitments to development, security, and civilization converge. While transborder mobility and exchange across these regions was renewed only recently, and largely by the dictate of Chinese officials alone, residents of Nepal’s northern border areas will continue to function as key actors and everyday agents in the broader geopolitical relationship between Kathmandu and Beijing. As is so often the case, one can look to the borderlands, at the so-called margins of the state, to gain a better sense of what continues to occupy concerns in the political center.
