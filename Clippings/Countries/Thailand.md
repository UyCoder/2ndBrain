**Thailand**,[^3] officially the **Kingdom of Thailand**,[^4] and formerly known as **Siam** until 1939,[^5] is a country located in [Mainland Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland_Southeast_Asia "Mainland Southeast Asia"). It shares land borders with [Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar "Myanmar") to the west and northwest, [Laos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laos "Laos") to the east and northeast, [Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia "Cambodia") to the southeast, and [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia") to the south. Its maritime boundaries include the [Gulf of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Thailand "Gulf of Thailand") and the [Andaman Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andaman_Sea "Andaman Sea"), as well as maritime borders with [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam"), [Indonesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesia "Indonesia"), and [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"). Thailand has a population of nearly 66 million people [^19] and covers an area of approximately 513,115 km <sup>2</sup> (198,115 sq mi).[^20] The country's [capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_city "Capital city") and [largest city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_municipalities_in_Thailand#Largest_cities_by_urban_population "List of municipalities in Thailand") is [Bangkok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangkok "Bangkok").[^21]

Archaeological evidence indicates that humans have inhabited the area of present-day Thailand for at least 40,000 years. Indigenous ethnic groups include the [Mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_people "Mon people"), [Khmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_people "Khmer people"), and [Malay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malay_people "Malay people") people. The [Tai people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tai_people "Tai people") are believed to have originated from the [Điện Biên Phủ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%90i%E1%BB%87n_Bi%C3%AAn_Ph%E1%BB%A7 "Điện Biên Phủ") region since the 5th century and began migrating into the territory of modern Thailand between the 8th and 10th centuries. During the classical historical period, major kingdoms such as [Sukhothai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sukhothai_Kingdom "Sukhothai Kingdom"), [Lan Na](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lan_Na "Lan Na"), and [Ayutthaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayutthaya_Kingdom "Ayutthaya Kingdom") were established. The Sukhothai Kingdom is regarded as the beginning of [Thai history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_history "Thai history"), while the Ayutthaya Kingdom, founded in 1350 CE, became a [regional power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power") replacing the [Khmer Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Empire "Khmer Empire"). European contact began in 1511 CE when [Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire") envoys arrived in Ayutthaya. The Ayutthaya Kingdom flourished until its complete destruction during the [1765–1767 Burmese–Siamese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese%E2%80%93Siamese_War_\(1765%E2%80%931767\) "Burmese–Siamese War (1765–1767)") by the [Burmese forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Burmese_Armed_Forces "Royal Burmese Armed Forces") under the [Konbaung dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konbaung_dynasty "Konbaung dynasty") in 1767.

After the fall of Ayutthaya, [King Taksin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Taksin "King Taksin") reunified the kingdom and established the [Thonburi Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thonburi_Kingdom "Thonburi Kingdom"), which lasted only 15 years before he was overthrown by [Phutthayotfa Chulalok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rama_I "Rama I") (Rama I), the founder of the [Chakri dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chakri_dynasty "Chakri dynasty"). King Rama I established the [Rattanakosin Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rattanakosin_Kingdom "Rattanakosin Kingdom") and moved the capital to Bangkok in 1782. During the era of [Western imperialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_imperialism_in_Asia "Western imperialism in Asia"), Siam remained the only country in Southeast Asia to avoid colonisation by European powers, although it ceded territory, trade rights, and legal privileges through several [unequal treaties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unequal_treaties "Unequal treaties").[^22] The governance system evolved into an [absolute monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy "Absolute monarchy") centralised under the rule of [King Chulalongkorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Chulalongkorn "King Chulalongkorn") (Rama V). Siam [adapted to international relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chulalongkorn%27s_first_visit_to_Europe "Chulalongkorn's first visit to Europe") during the imperialist era and joined [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") on the side of the [Allies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I "Allies of World War I"), a political decision aimed at revising the effects of unequal treaties and enhancing Siam's international status.

Following the [Siamese revolution of 1932](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siamese_revolution_of_1932 "Siamese revolution of 1932") by the [Khana Ratsadon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khana_Ratsadon "Khana Ratsadon") (lit. 'People's Party'), Siam transitioned from an absolute monarchy to a [parliamentary constitutional monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_constitutional_monarchy "Parliamentary constitutional monarchy") and was officially renamed Thailand. During [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II_in_Thailand "World War II in Thailand"), the country was under the [military dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_dictatorship "Military dictatorship") of [Plaek Phibunsongkhram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plaek_Phibunsongkhram "Plaek Phibunsongkhram") and allied with the [Empire of Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_Japan "Empire of Japan") as part of the [Axis powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_powers "Axis powers"), but Thailand did not become a defeated nation due to the underground [Free Thai Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_Thai_Movement "Free Thai Movement") 's "Declaration of Peace", which was recognised by the [Allies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_in_World_War_II "Allies in World War II"). During the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), Thailand became a key [major non-NATO ally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_non-NATO_ally "Major non-NATO ally") of the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and played a major role in [countering communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-communism "Anti-communism") in the [Korean War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_War "Korean War"), the [Vietnam War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_War "Vietnam War"), and other [proxy wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxy_wars "Proxy wars"). Thailand also joined the [Southeast Asia Treaty Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia_Treaty_Organization "Southeast Asia Treaty Organization") (SEATO). Despite brief periods of [liberal democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_democracy "Liberal democracy") in the 1970s and 1990s, Thailand alternated between [liberal democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_democracy "Liberal democracy") and military dictatorship.

Since the 2000s, Thailand has experienced political conflict between supporters and opponents of Prime Minister [Thaksin Shinawatra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thaksin_Shinawatra "Thaksin Shinawatra") (in office [2001–2006](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premiership_of_Thaksin_Shinawatra "Premiership of Thaksin Shinawatra")), leading to the [2006](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_Thai_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "2006 Thai coup d'état") and [2014](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Thai_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "2014 Thai coup d'état") coup d'états. Today, Thailand operates under the [2017 Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Thailand "Constitution of Thailand") and a [coalition government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalition_government "Coalition government") following the [2019 Thai general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_Thai_general_election "2019 Thai general election"), as well as [political demonstrations demanding democracy and monarchy reform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020%E2%80%932021_Thai_protests "2020–2021 Thai protests"). The current constitutional structure still allows *de facto* political influence by the [Royal Thai Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Thai_Armed_Forces "Royal Thai Armed Forces").[^23] Thailand has also faced border tensions from the [2008–2011 Cambodian–Thai border crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008%E2%80%932011_Cambodian%E2%80%93Thai_border_crisis "2008–2011 Cambodian–Thai border crisis") extending to the [2025 Cambodia–Thailand border crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Cambodia%E2%80%93Thailand_border_crisis "2025 Cambodia–Thailand border crisis") involving territorial disputes and military clashes.

Currently, Thailand is recognised as a [developing country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country") and holds significant geopolitical importance in Southeast Asia. It is a [unitary state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_state "Unitary state") governed under a [parliamentary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system") with a [bicameral legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Thailand "National Assembly of Thailand") comprising the elected [House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Representatives_of_Thailand "House of Representatives of Thailand") and the appointed [Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senate_of_Thailand "Senate of Thailand"). Thailand is a member of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), a major non- [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") ally of the United States, and a founding member of the [Association of Southeast Asian Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_of_Southeast_Asian_Nations "Association of Southeast Asian Nations") (ASEAN), as well as a [regional power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power"). The Royal Thai Armed Forces are among the largest military forces in Southeast Asia. Thailand's economy is the second-largest in the region and the 23rd globally by [purchasing power parity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity "Purchasing power parity"), ranking 29th by [gross domestic product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_domestic_product "Gross domestic product"). Thailand is classified as a [newly industrialised country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newly_industrialized_country "Newly industrialized country"), with manufacturing, agriculture, and [tourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourism_in_Thailand "Tourism in Thailand") being the main economic sectors.[^24] [^25]

## Etymology

Thailand [^3] was known by outsiders before 1939 as *Siam*.[^5] According to [George Cœdès](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_C%C5%93d%C3%A8s "George Cœdès"), the word *Thai* ([ไทย](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%B9%84%E0%B8%97%E0%B8%A2 "wikt:ไทย")) means 'free man' in the Thai language, "differentiating the Thai from the natives encompassed in Thai society as serfs".[^26]<sup><span title="Page / location: 197">: 197</span> </sup> According to [Chit Phumisak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chit_Phumisak "Chit Phumisak"), Thai (ไท) simply means 'people' or 'human being'; his investigation shows that some rural areas used the word "Thai" instead of the usual Thai word *khon* ([คน](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%B8%84%E0%B8%99 "wikt:คน")) for people.[^27] According to [Michel Ferlus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Ferlus "Michel Ferlus"), the ethnonyms Thai-Tai (or Thay-Tay) would have evolved from the etymon *\*k(ə)ri:* 'human being'.[^6] [^29]

Thais often refer to their country using the polite form *prathet Thai* ([Thai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_language "Thai language"): ประเทศไทย). They also use the more colloquial term *mueang Thai* ([Thai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_language "Thai language"): เมืองไทย) or simply *Thai;* the word *[mueang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mueang "Mueang")*, archaically referring to a [city-state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandala_\(political_model\) "Mandala (political model)"), is commonly used to refer to a city or town as the centre of a region. *Ratcha Anachak Thai* ([Thai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_language "Thai language"): ราชอาณาจักรไทย) means 'kingdom of Thailand' or 'kingdom of Thai'.

Etymologically, its components are: *ratcha* ([Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit_language "Sanskrit language"): राजन्, *[rājan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%C4%81jan "Rājan")*, 'king, royal, realm'), *ana-* ([Pali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pali "Pali") *āṇā* 'authority, command, power', itself from the Sanskrit आज्ञा, *ājñā*, of the same meaning), and *\-chak* (from Sanskrit [चक्र](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%A4%9A%E0%A4%95%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%B0 "wikt:चक्र") *cakra-* 'wheel', a symbol of power and rule).

The [Thai National Anthem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_National_Anthem "Thai National Anthem") ([Thai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_language "Thai language"): เพลงชาติ), written by [Luang Saranupraphan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luang_Saranupraphan "Luang Saranupraphan") during the patriotic 1930s, refers to the Thai nation as *prathet Thai* ([Thai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_language "Thai language"): ประเทศไทย). The first line of the national anthem is: *prathet thai ruam lueat nuea chat chuea thai* ([Thai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_language "Thai language"): ประเทศไทยรวมเลือดเนื้อชาติเชื้อไทย), 'Thailand is founded on blood and flesh'.[^30]

The former name *Siam* may have originated from [Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit") श्याम (*śyāma*, 'dark') [^27] or [Mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_language "Mon language") ရာမည (*rhmañña*, 'stranger'), probably the same root as *[Shan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_people "Shan people")* and *[Assam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assam "Assam")*.[^31] The word *Śyâma* is possibly not the true origin, but a pre-designed deviation from its proper, original meaning.[^32] [^33] Another theory is the name derives from the Chinese calling this region 'Xian'.[^7] [^34]<sup><span title="Page / location: 8">: 8</span></sup>

The ancient Khmers used the word *Siam* to refer to people settled in the west [Chao Phraya River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chao_Phraya_River "Chao Phraya River") valley surrounding the ancient city of [Nakhon Pathom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nakhon_Pathom "Nakhon Pathom") in the present-day [central Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Thailand "Central Thailand"); it may probably originate from the name of Lord [Krishna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krishna "Krishna"), which also called *Shyam*, as in the [Wat Sri Chum Inscription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wat_Sri_Chum_Inscription "Wat Sri Chum Inscription"), dated 13th century CE, mentions [Phra Maha Thera Sri Sattha](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phra_Maha_Thera_Sri_Sattha&action=edit&redlink=1 "Phra Maha Thera Sri Sattha (page does not exist)") came to restore [Phra Pathommachedi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phra_Pathommachedi "Phra Pathommachedi") at the city of Lord Krishna (Nakhon Pathom) in the early era of the [Sukhothai Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sukhothai_Kingdom "Sukhothai Kingdom").[^35]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Kingdom_%26_People_of_Siam_%28Vol_1%2C_1857%2C_15%29.svg/250px-Kingdom_%26_People_of_Siam_%28Vol_1%2C_1857%2C_15%29.svg.png)

SPPM Mongkut Rex Siamensium, King Mongkut 's signature

The signature of King [Mongkut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongkut "Mongkut") (r. 1851–1868) reads *SPPM* (*Somdet Phra Poramenthra Maha*) *Mongkut Rex Siamensium* (Mongkut, King of the Siamese). This usage of the name in [the country's first international treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bowring_Treaty "Bowring Treaty") gave the name *Siam* official status, until 24 June 1939 when it was changed to *Thailand*.[^36]

## History

### Prehistory and origins

There is evidence of continuous human habitation in present-day Thailand from 20,000 years ago to the present day.[^37]<sup><span title="Page / location: 4">: 4</span> </sup> The earliest evidence of rice growing is dated at 2,000 BCE.[^38]<sup><span title="Page / location: 4">: 4</span> </sup> Areas comprising what is now Thailand participated in the Maritime Jade Road, as ascertained by archeological research. The trading network existed for 3,000 years, between 2000 BCE to 1000 CE.[^39] [^40] [^41] [^42] Bronze appeared c. 1,250–1,000 BCE.[^38]<sup><span title="Page / location: 4">: 4</span> </sup> The site of [Ban Chiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ban_Chiang "Ban Chiang") in northeast Thailand currently ranks as the earliest known centre of copper and bronze production in Southeast Asia.[^43] Iron appeared around 500 BCE.[^38]<sup><span title="Page / location: 5">: 5</span></sup>

The [Kingdom of Funan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Funan "Kingdom of Funan") was the first and most powerful Southeast Asian kingdom at the time (2nd century BCE).[^37]<sup><span title="Page / location: 5">: 5</span> </sup> The [Mon people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_people "Mon people") established the principalities of [Dvaravati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dvaravati "Dvaravati") and Kingdom of [Hariphunchai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hariphunchai "Hariphunchai") in the 6th century. The [Khmer people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_people "Khmer people") established the [Khmer empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_empire "Khmer empire"), centred in [Angkor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angkor "Angkor"), in the 9th century.[^37]<sup><span title="Page / location: 7">: 7</span> </sup> [Tambralinga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tambralinga "Tambralinga"), a Malay state controlling trade through the [Malacca Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca "Strait of Malacca"), rose in the 10th century.[^37]<sup><span title="Page / location: 5">: 5</span> </sup> The Indochina peninsula was heavily influenced by the [culture and religions of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indianized_kingdom "Indianized kingdom") from the time of the Kingdom of Funan to that of the Khmer Empire.[^44]

The [Thai people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_people "Thai people") are of the [Tai ethnic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tai_peoples "Tai peoples"), characterised by common linguistic roots.[^45]<sup><span title="Page / location: 2">: 2</span> </sup> Chinese chronicles first mention the Tai peoples in the 6th century BCE. While there are many assumptions regarding the origin of Tai peoples, [David K. Wyatt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_K._Wyatt "David K. Wyatt"), a historian of Thailand, argued that their ancestors who at present inhabit Laos, Thailand, Myanmar, India, and China came from the [Điện Biên Phủ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%90i%E1%BB%87n_Bi%C3%AAn_Ph%E1%BB%A7 "Điện Biên Phủ") area between the 5th and the 8th century.[^45]<sup><span title="Page / location: 6">: 6</span></sup>

Thai people began migrating into present-day Thailand gradually from the 6th to 11th century, which Mon and Khmer people occupied at the time.[^46] Thus Thai culture was influenced by Indian, Mon, and Khmer cultures.[^47]<sup><span title="Page / location: 203">: 203</span> </sup> Tai people intermixed with various ethnic and cultural groups in the region, resulting in many groups of present-day Thai people.[^8] Genetic evidences suggested that ethnolinguistics could not accurately predict the origins of the Thais.[^48] [^49] [^50] [Sujit Wongthes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sujit_Wongthes "Sujit Wongthes") argued that Thai is not a race or ethnicity but a culture group.[^51]

According to French historian [George Cœdès](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_C%C5%93d%C3%A8s "George Cœdès"), "The Thai first enter history of [Farther India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Farther_India "Farther India") in the eleventh century with the mention of *Syam* slaves or prisoners of war in [Champa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Champa "Champa") epigraphy", and "in the twelfth century, the [bas-reliefs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bas-relief "Bas-relief") of [Angkor Wat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angkor_Wat "Angkor Wat") " where "a group of warriors" are described as *Syam*,[^26]<sup><span title="Page / location: 190–191, 194–195">: 190–191, 194–195</span> </sup> though Cham accounts do not indicate the origins of *Syam* or what ethnic group they belonged to.[^52] The origins and ethnicity of the *Syam* remain unclear, with some literature suggesting that *Syam* refers to the [Shan people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_people "Shan people"), the [Bru people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bru_people "Bru people"), or the [Brau people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brau_people "Brau people").[^52] [^53] However, [Mainland Southeast Asian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland_Southeast_Asia "Mainland Southeast Asia") sources from before the fourteenth century primarily used the word *Syam* as an [ethnonym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnonym "Ethnonym"), referring to those who belonged to a separate cultural category different from the Khmer, Cham, Bagan, or Mon. This contrasts with the Chinese sources, where *Xian* was used as a [toponym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toponym "Toponym").[^52]

### Early Tai confederate cities (691 BCE – 13th century CE)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Tai_Migration.svg/250px-Tai_Migration.svg.png)

Map showing geographic distribution of Kra–Dai linguistic family. Arrows represent general pattern of the migration of Tai-speaking tribes along the rivers and over the lower passes. 38: 27

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Location_Lanna_%28under_King_Tilok%29.png/250px-Location_Lanna_%28under_King_Tilok%29.png)

Lan Na kingdom during the reign of Tilokaraj ( r. 1441–1487)

Theoretically, [Tai-Kadai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tai-Kadai "Tai-Kadai") -speaking people formed as early as the 12th century BCE in the middle of the [Yangtze basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yangtze_basin "Yangtze basin"). Some groups later migrated south to [Guangxi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangxi "Guangxi").[^54] However, after several bloody centuries against [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") influence in [Guangxi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangxi "Guangxi") between 333 BCE and the 11th century, hundreds of thousands of Tais were killed,[^55]<sup><span title="Page / location: 5">: 5</span> </sup> [^56]<sup><span title="Page / location: 193, 239–249">: 193, 239–249</span> </sup> thus, [Tai people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tai_people "Tai people") began to move southwestward along the rivers and over the lower passes into the mountain north of [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia") and river valleys in present-day [Assam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assam "Assam") of [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India").[^57] [^58] Some evidence indicates that the ancestors of Tai people migrated en masse southwestwards out of Yunnan only after the 1253 [Mongol invasion of Dali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uriyangkhadai#Invasion_of_Yunnan_and_Tibet "Uriyangkhadai"); however, it is not generally accepted.[^59]<sup><span title="Page / location: 38">: 38</span></sup>

Tais defeated [indigenous tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples "Indigenous peoples") and emerged as the new power in the new region. As a result, several Tai city-states were established, scattered from [Điện Biên Phủ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%90i%E1%BB%87n_Bi%C3%AAn_Ph%E1%BB%A7 "Điện Biên Phủ") in present-day northwestern [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam") and highland [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia") to [northeastern India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northeastern_India "Northeastern India").[^60] [^61] [^62]

According to the *Simhanavati legend* given in several chronicles, the first Tai city-state in northern Thailand, [Singhanavati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singhanavati "Singhanavati"), was founded around the 7th century.[^63]<sup><span title="Page / location: 5, 9">: 5, 9</span> </sup> However, several modern [geology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology "Geology") and [archaeology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeology "Archaeology") studies found that its centre, Yonok Nahaphan, dates from 691 BCE–545 CE;[^63]<sup><span title="Page / location: 7">: 7</span> </sup> [^64] [^65] [^66] this roughly coincides with the establishment of [Shan States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shan_people#History "Shan people"), another Tai's federated principalities in the present-day northeast [Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar "Myanmar").[^67] [^68] [^69] [^70] as well as [Muang Sua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muang_Sua "Muang Sua") ([Luang Prabang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luang_Prabang "Luang Prabang")) in the east.[^71] [^72]

After Singhanavati was submerged below [Chiang Saen Lake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Saen_Lake "Chiang Saen Lake") due to an [earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthquake "Earthquake") in 545,[^64] [^66] [^73] the survivors then founded a new seat at [Wieng–Prueksha](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wieng%E2%80%93Prueksha&action=edit&redlink=1 "Wieng–Prueksha (page does not exist)"). The kingdom lasted for another 93 years.[^74]

In addition to [Singhanavati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singhanavati "Singhanavati"), another northern principality probably related to the Tai people, [Ngoenyang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ngoenyang "Ngoenyang"), was established as the successor of Singhanavati in 638 by [Lavachakkaraj](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lavachakkaraj&action=edit&redlink=1 "Lavachakkaraj (page does not exist)"), also centred in [Wieng–Prueksha](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wieng%E2%80%93Prueksha&action=edit&redlink=1 "Wieng–Prueksha (page does not exist)") (present-day [Mae Sai District](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mae_Sai_District "Mae Sai District"), [Chiang Rai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Rai_province "Chiang Rai province")).[^34]<sup><span title="Page / location: 8">: 8</span> </sup> Its seat was moved to [Chiang Mai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Mai "Chiang Mai") in 1262 by King [Mangrai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mangrai "Mangrai"), which considered the foundation of the [Lan Na](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lan_Na "Lan Na") kingdom. Mangrai unified the surrounding area and also created a network of states through political alliances to the east and north of the [Mekong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mekong "Mekong"). His dynasty would rule the kingdom continuously for the next two centuries.[^34]<sup><span title="Page / location: 8">: 8</span> </sup> [Lan Na](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lan_Na "Lan Na") expanded its territory southward and annexed the [Mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_people "Mon people") [Hariphunchai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hariphunchai "Hariphunchai") of [Dvaravati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dvaravati "Dvaravati") in 1292.[^75]<sup><span title="Page / location: 208">: 208</span></sup>

In the late tenth century, Tai people began to migrate further south to the present-day upper [central Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Thailand "Central Thailand").[^76]<sup><span title="Page / location: 46–9, 83–6">: 46–9, 83–6</span> </sup> Around the 1100s period, several cities in this area, such as Songkwae, Sawankhalok, and Chakangrao, were ruled by the Tai people, eventually launching several battles against the pre-existing [Mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_people "Mon people") of [Lavo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lavo_Kingdom "Lavo Kingdom"), who had been falling under [Chenla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chenla "Chenla") and [Khmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Empire "Khmer Empire") influences since the 7th century, thus bringing the establishment of the Tai people's independent state, [Sukhothai Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sukhothai_Kingdom "Sukhothai Kingdom"), in the upper [Chao Phraya River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chao_Phraya_River "Chao Phraya River") valley in 1238.[^45]<sup><span title="Page / location: 52–3">: 52–3</span></sup>

The earliest conflict between [Tai people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tai_people "Tai people") and the preexisting ethnics was recorded in the mid-4th century when the ruler of [Singhanavati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singhanavati "Singhanavati"), [Pangkharat](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pangkharat&action=edit&redlink=1 "Pangkharat (page does not exist)"), forcibly lost the seat at Yonok to King Khom from Umongasela (present-day [Fang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fang_district "Fang district")). He then fled to Vieng Si Tuang (เวียงศรีทวง; present-day Wiang Phang Kham, [Mae Sai district](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mae_Sai_district "Mae Sai district")) but had to send tributes to Yonok annually until his son, [Phrom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrom "Phrom"), took back Yonok and expelled King Khom from Umongasela.[^77] [^78] [^79] [Phrom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrom "Phrom") also marched the troops south to occupy [Chakangrao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kamphaeng_Phet_province "Kamphaeng Phet province") from the enemy as well as founding the city of [Songkwae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phitsanulok "Phitsanulok").[^77] Some historians suggest that [Lavo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lavo_Kingdom "Lavo Kingdom") 's capital, [Lopburi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lopburi "Lopburi"), was once seized by [Phrom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrom "Phrom").[^77] In contrast, [Tai people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tai_people "Tai people") instead established relationships with Mon via [royal intermarriages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_intermarriage "Royal intermarriage").[^76]<sup><span title="Page / location: 46–9, 83–6">: 46–9, 83–6</span></sup>

### Mon and Lavo Kingdoms (5th century CE – 1388 CE)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Mon_political_entities.png/250px-Mon_political_entities.png)

Political entities of the Mon people around the 6th–7th centuries

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/DvaravatiMapThailand.png/250px-DvaravatiMapThailand.png)

Map of settlements of Dvaravati culture from the 6th to 9th centuries

The [Chao Phraya River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chao_Phraya_River "Chao Phraya River") in what is now [central Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Thailand "Central Thailand") had once been the home of the [Mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_people "Mon people") [Dvaravati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dvaravati "Dvaravati") culture, which prevailed from the seventh century to the tenth century.[^80] Samuel Beal discovered the polity among the Chinese writings on Southeast Asia as "Duoluobodi". During the early 20th century, archaeological excavations led by [George Coedès](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Coed%C3%A8s "George Coedès") found [Nakhon Pathom Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nakhon_Pathom_Province "Nakhon Pathom Province") to be a centre of Dvaravati culture. The two most important sites were Nakorn Pathom and U Thong (in modern [Suphan Buri Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suphan_Buri_Province "Suphan Buri Province")). The inscriptions of Dvaravati were in [Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit") and [Mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_language "Mon language") using the script derived from the [Pallava alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pallava_alphabet "Pallava alphabet") of the [South Indian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_India "South India") Pallava dynasty.

It is believed that the Dvaravati borrowed [Theravada Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theravada "Theravada") through its contacts with Sri Lanka, while the ruling class participated in [Hindu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu "Hindu") rites. [Dvaravati art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dvaravati_art "Dvaravati art"), including the Buddha sculptures and [stupas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stupa "Stupa"), showed strong similarities to those of the [Gupta Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gupta_Empire "Gupta Empire") of India. The eastern parts of the Chao Phraya valley were subjected to a more Khmer and Hindu influence as the inscriptions are found in Khmer and Sanskrit.[^81]

Dvaravati was a network of city-states paying tribute to more powerful ones according to the [mandala political model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandala_\(Southeast_Asian_political_model\) "Mandala (Southeast Asian political model)"). Dvaravati culture expanded into [Isan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isan "Isan") as well as south as far as the [Kra Isthmus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kra_Isthmus "Kra Isthmus"). Dvaravati culture lost its influence around the tenth century when they submitted to the more unified Lavo-Khmer polity.

Around the tenth century, the city-states of Dvaravati merged into the mandalas of: [Lavo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lavo "Lavo") (modern [Lopburi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lopburi "Lopburi")) and [Suvarnabhumi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suvarnabhumi "Suvarnabhumi") (modern [Suphan Buri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suphan_Buri "Suphan Buri")). According to a legend in the Northern Chronicles, in 903, a king of [Tambralinga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tambralinga "Tambralinga") invaded and took Lavo and installed a Malay prince on the Lavo throne. The Malay prince was married to a Khmer princess who had fled an Angkorian dynastic bloodbath. The son of the couple contested the Khmer throne and became [Suryavarman I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suryavarman_I "Suryavarman I") (1006–1050), thus bringing Lavo under Khmer domination through marital union. Suryavarman I also expanded into the [Khorat Plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khorat_Plateau "Khorat Plateau") (later styled "Isan"), constructing many temples.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/%E0%B8%9B%E0%B8%A3%E0%B8%B2%E0%B8%AA%E0%B8%B2%E0%B8%97%E0%B8%9B%E0%B8%A3%E0%B8%B2%E0%B8%87%E0%B8%84%E0%B9%8C%E0%B8%AA%E0%B8%B2%E0%B8%A1%E0%B8%A2%E0%B8%AD%E0%B8%942.jpg/250px-%E0%B8%9B%E0%B8%A3%E0%B8%B2%E0%B8%AA%E0%B8%B2%E0%B8%97%E0%B8%9B%E0%B8%A3%E0%B8%B2%E0%B8%87%E0%B8%84%E0%B9%8C%E0%B8%AA%E0%B8%B2%E0%B8%A1%E0%B8%A2%E0%B8%AD%E0%B8%942.jpg)

Wat Phra Prang Sam Yod, a 13th-century Angkorian temple in Lopburi.

After the [Angkor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angkor "Angkor") lost Lavo to [Suphannabhum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suphannabhum "Suphannabhum") princes in 1052, the younger prince [Chandrachota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandrachota "Chandrachota") claimed the throne. His son, [Narai I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narai_I "Narai I"), moved Lavo's seat to [Ayodhya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phra_Nakhon_Si_Ayutthaya_\(city\) "Phra Nakhon Si Ayutthaya (city)") in the 1080s. This resulted in the throne of Lavo's Lavaburi being vacant from 1087 to 1106 (or ruled by unknown kings), [Si Satchanalai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Si_Satchanalai "Si Satchanalai") king, [Kesariraja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kesariraja "Kesariraja") who is of [Mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_people "Mon people") 's [Chaliang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mueang_Chaliang "Mueang Chaliang") and [Tai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tai_people "Tai people") 's [Chiang Saen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Saen "Chiang Saen") lineages, took over the seat. An attempt to re-expand influence to Lavo by the Angkor occurred in 1181 when [Jayavarman VII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jayavarman_VII "Jayavarman VII") appointed his lineage Narupatidnavarman to govern Lavapura.[^82] Due to this political pressure, a Tai ruler Sri Thammasokkarat (ศรีธรรมโศกราช), who was also from Si Satchanalai, fled to [Nakhon Si Thammarat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nakhon_Si_Thammarat_Kingdom "Nakhon Si Thammarat Kingdom").[^83]<sup><span title="Page / location: 38–39">: 38–39</span> </sup> Sri Thammasokkarat married to princess of Dhanyapura (Dong Mae Nang Mueang in the present-day [Nakhon Sawan province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nakhon_Sawan_province "Nakhon Sawan province")), and their descendants ruled [Tambralinga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tambralinga "Tambralinga") until the late 13th century.[^84]<sup><span title="Page / location: 290–2">: 290–2</span> </sup> [The Customs of Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Customs_of_Cambodia "The Customs of Cambodia") of [Zhou Daguan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Daguan "Zhou Daguan"), as an official delegation sent by the [Yuan dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_dynasty "Yuan dynasty") to [Angkor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angkor "Angkor") from 1296 to 1297, says the Siamese people exerted significant influence over Lavo's [Lavapura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lopburi "Lopburi") and appeared in huge numbers in the Angkorian capital of [Yaśodharapura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ya%C5%9Bodharapura "Yaśodharapura").[^85] [^86]

[Prang Sam Yot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phra_Prang_Sam_Yot "Phra Prang Sam Yot") was built during this period.[^87]<sup><span title="Page / location: 170">: 170</span> </sup> However, Lavapura was probably taken back by a Tai royal from Phraek Si Racha (แพรกศรีราชา; present-day [Sankhaburi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sankhaburi_district "Sankhaburi district")) in the 13th century.[^76]<sup><span title="Page / location: 109">: 109</span> </sup> During this era, Angkor's power waned due to the weak rule and feuding began in the Angkor.[^88]<sup><span title="Page / location: 120">: 120</span></sup>

All of the turmoil, as mentioned earlier, also led to the independence declaration of the [Sukhothai Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sukhothai_Kingdom "Sukhothai Kingdom") in 1238.[^89]

#### The earliest evidence mentioning the Siamese

The earliest evidence to mention the Siam people are stone inscriptions found in [Angkor Borei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angkor_Borei "Angkor Borei") of [Funan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funan "Funan") (K.557 and K.600), dated 661 CE, the slave's name is mentioned as "Ku Sayam" meaning "Sayam female slaves" (Ku is a prefix used to refer to female slaves in the pre-Angkorian era), and the Takéo inscriptions (K.79) written in 682 during the reign of [Bhavavarman II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhavavarman_II "Bhavavarman II") of [Chenla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chenla "Chenla") also mention Siam Nobel: *Sāraṇnoya Poña Sayam*, which was transcribed into English as: *the rice field that was given to the poña (noble rank) who was called Sayam (Siam)*.[^90]

The [Song Huiyao Jigao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_Huiyao_Jigao "Song Huiyao Jigao") (960–1279) indicate Siamese people settled in the west [central Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Thailand "Central Thailand") and their state was called *Xiān guó* ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"): 暹國), while the eastern plain belonged to the Mon of [Lavo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lavo_Kingdom "Lavo Kingdom") ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"): 羅渦國),[^91] who later fell under the [Chenla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chenla "Chenla") and [Khmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Empire "Khmer Empire") hegemony around the 7th–9th centuries.[^92] Those Mon political entities, which also included [Haripuñjaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haripu%C3%B1jaya "Haripuñjaya") in the north and several city-states in the [northeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northeastern_Thailand "Northeastern Thailand"), are collectively called [Dvaravati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dvaravati "Dvaravati").

However, the states of Siamese and Lavo were later merged via the [royal intermarriage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_intermarriage "Royal intermarriage") and became [Ayutthaya Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayutthaya_Kingdom "Ayutthaya Kingdom") in the mid-14th century,[^91] while the southwestern [Isan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isan "Isan") principalities, centred in [Phanom Rung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phanom_Rung_Historical_Park "Phanom Rung Historical Park") and [Phimai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phimai "Phimai"), later pledged allegiance to Siamese's Ayutthaya during the reign of [Borommarachathirat II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borommarachathirat_II "Borommarachathirat II") (r. 1424–1448).[^93] The remaining principal city-states in [Isan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isan "Isan") region became [Lan Xang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lan_Xang "Lan Xang") around 1353 after the twin cities of [Muang Sua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muang_Sua "Muang Sua") ([Luang Prabang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luang_Prabang "Luang Prabang")) and *Vieng Chan Vieng Kham* ([Vientiane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vientiane "Vientiane")) became independent following the death of the [Sukhothai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sukhothai_Kingdom "Sukhothai Kingdom") king [Ram Khamhaeng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ram_Khamhaeng "Ram Khamhaeng").[^94]<sup><span title="Page / location: 51">: 51</span></sup>

According to the Wat Kud Tae inscription (K.1105), dated c. 7th century, during the period that the eastern Mon entity, [Lavo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lavo_Kingdom "Lavo Kingdom"), was strongly influenced by the [Chenla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chenla "Chenla"), the Siamese in the west also established a [royal intermarriage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_intermarriage "Royal intermarriage") with [Chenla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chenla "Chenla") as Sri Chakatham, prince of Sambhuka (ศามภูกะ, in the present-day [Ratchaburi province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratchaburi_province "Ratchaburi province")), married to a princess of [Isanavarman I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isanavarman_I "Isanavarman I"), and two [mandalas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandala_\(political_model\) "Mandala (political model)") then became an ally.[^95] After [Chenla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chenla "Chenla") sieged [Funan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funan "Funan") and moved the centre to [Angkor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angkor "Angkor"), both Siamese and the Angkorian eventually marched the troops to attack [Vijaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vijaya_\(Champa\) "Vijaya (Champa)") of [Champa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Champa "Champa") in 1201 during the reign of [Jayavarman VII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jayavarman_VII "Jayavarman VII"), as recorded in the Cho-Dinh inscription (C.3).[^96]
