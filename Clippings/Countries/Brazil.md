**Brazil**,[^3] officially the **Federative Republic of Brazil**,[^4] is the largest country in [South America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_America "South America"). It is also the world's [fifth-largest country by area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_area "List of countries and dependencies by area") and the [seventh-largest by population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_population "List of countries and dependencies by population"), with over 213 million people. Brazil is a [federation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federation "Federation") composed of 26 [states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federative_units_of_Brazil "Federative units of Brazil") and a [Federal District](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_District_\(Brazil\) "Federal District (Brazil)"), which hosts the capital, [Brasília](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bras%C3%ADlia "Brasília"). Its [most populous city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_municipalities_in_Brazil_by_population "List of municipalities in Brazil by population") is [São Paulo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo "São Paulo"), followed by [Rio de Janeiro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro "Rio de Janeiro"). Brazil has the most [Portuguese speakers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_of_Portuguese_Language_Countries "Community of Portuguese Language Countries") in the world and is the only country in the [Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americas "Americas") where [Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_language "Portuguese language") is an [official language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese-speaking_world "Portuguese-speaking world").[^18] [^19]

Bounded by the [Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") on the east, Brazil has a [coastline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastline_of_Brazil "Coastline of Brazil") of 7,491 kilometers (4,655 mi),[^20] among [the longest in the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_length_of_coastline "List of countries and dependencies by length of coastline"). Covering roughly half of South America's land area, it [borders all other countries and territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borders_of_Brazil "Borders of Brazil") on the continent except [Ecuador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecuador "Ecuador") and [Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile "Chile").[^21] Brazil encompasses a wide range of tropical and [subtropical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtropics "Subtropics") landscapes, as well as [wetlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pantanal "Pantanal"), [savannas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerrado "Cerrado"), [plateaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_Highlands "Brazilian Highlands"), and low mountains. It contains most of the [Amazon basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon_basin "Amazon basin"), including the [world's largest river system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon_River "Amazon River") and most extensive [virgin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old-growth_forest "Old-growth forest") [tropical forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon_rainforest "Amazon rainforest"). Brazil has diverse [wildlife](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildlife_of_Brazil "Wildlife of Brazil"), a variety of [ecological systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environment_of_Brazil "Environment of Brazil"), and extensive natural resources spanning numerous [protected habitats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protected_areas_of_Brazil "Protected areas of Brazil").[^20] It ranks first among 17 [megadiverse countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megadiverse_countries "Megadiverse countries"), with its natural heritage being the subject of significant global interest, as [environmental degradation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_degradation "Environmental degradation") (through processes such as [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation_in_Brazil "Deforestation in Brazil")) directly affect global issues such as [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Brazil "Climate change in Brazil") and [biodiversity loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_loss "Biodiversity loss").

Brazil was inhabited by [various indigenous peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Cabraline_history_of_Brazil "Pre-Cabraline history of Brazil") prior to the [landing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discovery_of_Brazil "Discovery of Brazil") of Portuguese explorer [Pedro Álvares Cabral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral "Pedro Álvares Cabral") in 1500. The territory was subsequently claimed and settled by [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire"), which founded the country's first city, [São Vicente](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Vicente,_S%C3%A3o_Paulo "São Vicente, São Paulo"), in 1532 and imported [enslaved Africans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery_in_Brazil "Slavery in Brazil") to work on [plantations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_sugar_cycle "Brazilian sugar cycle").[^22] Brazil remained a [colony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_Brazil "Colonial Brazil") until 1815, when it was elevated to a [united kingdom with Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Portugal,_Brazil_and_the_Algarves "United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves") after the [transfer of the Portuguese court to Rio de Janeiro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transfer_of_the_Portuguese_court_to_Brazil "Transfer of the Portuguese court to Brazil"). Prince [Pedro of Braganza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_I_of_Brazil "Pedro I of Brazil") declared Brazilian [independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_of_Brazil "Independence of Brazil") in 1822 and, after waging a [war against Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_War_of_Independence "Brazilian War of Independence"), established the [Empire of Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_Brazil "Empire of Brazil"). The country's [first constitution in 1824](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_Constitution_of_1824 "Brazilian Constitution of 1824") established a [bicameral legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Assembly_\(Brazil\) "General Assembly (Brazil)") and enshrined principles such as freedom of religion and the press; slavery was [gradually abolished](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abolitionism_in_Brazil "Abolitionism in Brazil") until its [final abolition in 1888](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lei_%C3%81urea "Lei Áurea"). Brazil became a [presidential republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Brazilian_Republic "First Brazilian Republic") following a [military coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation_of_the_Republic_\(Brazil\) "Proclamation of the Republic (Brazil)") in 1889. An [armed revolution in 1930](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolution_of_1930 "Revolution of 1930") ended the First Republic and brought [Getúlio Vargas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Get%C3%BAlio_Vargas "Getúlio Vargas") to power. Vargas' [self-coup in 1937](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1937_Brazilian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1937 Brazilian coup d'état") ushered the authoritarian [Estado Novo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estado_Novo_\(Brazil\) "Estado Novo (Brazil)"), in which he oversaw [Brazil's involvement in World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil_in_World_War_II "Brazil in World War II"). Democracy was restored after [Vargas' ousting in 1945](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1945_Brazilian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1945 Brazilian coup d'état"). An authoritarian [military dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_dictatorship_in_Brazil "Military dictatorship in Brazil") emerged [in 1964](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1964_Brazilian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1964 Brazilian coup d'état") with support from the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and ruled until 1985, after which civilian governance resumed. Brazil's current [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Brazil "Constitution of Brazil"), enacted in 1988, defines it as a [democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") [federal republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_republic "Federal republic").[^23]

Brazil is a [regional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power") and [middle power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_power "Middle power"),[^24] [^25] [^26] and has been described as a [rising global power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emerging_power "Emerging power").[^27] [^28] [^29] [^30] It is an [emerging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emerging_market "Emerging market"),[^31] [^32] [upper-middle income](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country") economy and [newly industrialized country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newly_industrialized_country "Newly industrialized country"),[^33] with one of the 10 [largest economies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of countries by GDP (nominal)") in the world in both nominal and [PPP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(PPP\) "List of countries by GDP (PPP)") terms,[^14] [^34] the largest economy in [Latin America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_America "Latin America") and the [Southern Hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere "Southern Hemisphere"), and the [largest share of wealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_total_wealth "List of countries by total wealth") in South America. With a [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_Complexity_Index "Economic Complexity Index") and highly [diversified economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Brazil "Economy of Brazil"), Brazil is one of the world's major or primary exporters of various [agricultural goods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture_in_Brazil "Agriculture in Brazil"), [mineral resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mining_in_Brazil "Mining in Brazil"), and [manufactured products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industry_in_Brazil "Industry in Brazil").[^35] The country ranks [thirteenth in the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_Brazil "List of World Heritage Sites in Brazil") by number of [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO") [World Heritage Sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Site "World Heritage Site").[^36] Brazil is a founding member of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), the [G20](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G20 "G20"), [BRICS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BRICS "BRICS"), [G4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G4_nations "G4 nations"), [Mercosur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercosur "Mercosur"), [Organization of American States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_of_American_States "Organization of American States"), [Organization of Ibero-American States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_of_Ibero-American_States "Organization of Ibero-American States"), and the [Community of Portuguese Language Countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_of_Portuguese_Language_Countries "Community of Portuguese Language Countries"); it is also an observer state of the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League") and a [major non-NATO ally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_non-NATO_ally "Major non-NATO ally") of the United States.[^37] [^38]

## Etymology

The word *Brazil* probably comes from the Portuguese word for [brazilwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paubrasilia "Paubrasilia"), a tree that once grew plentifully along the Brazilian coast.[^39] In Portuguese, brazilwood is called *pau-brasil*, with the word *brasil* commonly given the etymology 'red like an [ember](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ember "Ember") ', formed from *brasa* ('ember') and the suffix *\-il* (from *\-iculum* or *\-ilium*).[^40] It has alternatively been suggested that this is a [folk etymology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folk_etymology "Folk etymology") for a word for the plant related to an Arabic or Asian word for a red plant.[^41] As brazilwood produces a deep red dye, it was highly valued by the European textile industry and was the earliest commercially exploited product from Brazil.[^42] Throughout the 16th century, massive amounts of brazilwood were harvested by [indigenous peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil "Indigenous peoples in Brazil") (mostly [Tupi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tupi_people "Tupi people")) along the Brazilian coast, who sold the timber to European traders in return for assorted European consumer goods.[^43]

The official Portuguese name of the land, in original Portuguese records, was the 'Land of the Holy Cross' (*Terra da Santa Cruz*),[^44] but European sailors and merchants commonly called it the 'Land of Brazil' (*Terra do Brasil*) because of the brazilwood trade.[^45] Popular usage eclipsed and eventually supplanted the official Portuguese name. Some early sailors called it the 'Land of Parrots'.[^46] In the [Guarani language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guarani_language "Guarani language"), Brazil is called *Pindorama*, meaning 'land of the palm trees'.[^47]

## History

### Pre-Cabraline era

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Pinturas_Rupestres_-_Serra_da_Capivara_I.jpg/250px-Pinturas_Rupestres_-_Serra_da_Capivara_I.jpg)

Rock art at Serra da Capivara National Park, one of the largest and oldest concentrations of prehistoric sites in the Americas 48

Some of the earliest human remains found in the Americas, [Luzia Woman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luzia_Woman "Luzia Woman"), were found in the area of [Pedro Leopoldo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_Leopoldo "Pedro Leopoldo"), [Minas Gerais](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minas_Gerais "Minas Gerais"), and provide evidence of human habitation going back at least 11,000 years.[^49] The earliest pottery ever found in the Western Hemisphere was excavated in the Amazon basin of Brazil and [radiocarbon dated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating "Radiocarbon dating") to over 8,000 years ago (6000 BC). The pottery was found near [Santarém](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santar%C3%A9m,_Par%C3%A1 "Santarém, Pará") and provides evidence that the region supported a complex prehistoric culture.[^50] The [Marajoara culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marajoara_culture "Marajoara culture") flourished on [Marajó](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maraj%C3%B3 "Marajó") in the Amazon delta from AD 400 to 1400, developing sophisticated pottery, [social stratification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_stratification "Social stratification"), large populations, [mound building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mound_building "Mound building"), and complex social formations such as [chiefdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiefdom "Chiefdom").[^51]

Around the time of the Portuguese arrival, the territory of present day Brazil had an estimated Indigenous population of 7 million people,[^52] mostly semi-nomadic, who subsisted on hunting, fishing, gathering, and migrant agriculture. The population comprised several large Indigenous ethnic groups (e.g., the Tupis, [Guaranis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guaran%C3%AD_people "Guaraní people"), and [Gês](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%AA_peoples "Gê peoples"). The Tupi people were subdivided into the [Tupiniquins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tupiniquim "Tupiniquim") and [Tupinambás](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tupinamb%C3%A1_people "Tupinambá people").[^53]

Before the arrival of the Europeans, the boundaries between these groups and their subgroups were marked by wars that arose from differences in culture, language and moral beliefs.[^54] These wars also involved large-scale military actions on land and water, with [cannibalistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_cannibalism "Human cannibalism") rituals on prisoners of war.[^55] [^56] While heredity had some weight, leadership was a status more won over time than assigned in succession ceremonies and conventions.[^54] Slavery among the Indigenous groups had a different meaning than it had for Europeans, since it originated from a diverse socioeconomic organization, in which asymmetries were translated into [kinship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinship "Kinship") relations.[^57]

### European colonization and Colonial Brazil

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Desembarque_de_Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral_em_Porto_Seguro_em_1500_by_Oscar_Pereira_da_Silva_%281865%E2%80%931939%29.jpg/250px-Desembarque_de_Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral_em_Porto_Seguro_em_1500_by_Oscar_Pereira_da_Silva_%281865%E2%80%931939%29.jpg)

Pedro Álvares Cabral landing in Porto Seguro in 1500

Following the 1494 [Treaty of Tordesillas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Tordesillas "Treaty of Tordesillas"), the land now called Brazil was claimed for the [Portuguese Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire") on 22 April 1500, with [the arrival of the Portuguese fleet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discovery_of_Brazil "Discovery of Brazil") commanded by [Pedro Álvares Cabral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral "Pedro Álvares Cabral").[^58] Along [the coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastline_of_Brazil "Coastline of Brazil"), the Portuguese encountered [various Indigenous communities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil#Distribution "Indigenous peoples in Brazil"), most of whom spoke languages of the [Tupi–Guarani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tupi%E2%80%93Guaran%C3%AD "Tupi–Guaraní") family.[^59] Though the first settlement, [São Vicente](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Vicente,_S%C3%A3o_Paulo "São Vicente, São Paulo"), was founded in 1532, colonization effectively began in 1534, when King [John III of Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_III_of_Portugal "John III of Portugal") divided the territory into the fifteen private and autonomous [captaincies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captaincies_of_Brazil "Captaincies of Brazil").[^60] [^61]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Benedito_Calixto_de_Jesus_-_Funda%C3%A7%C3%A3o_de_S%C3%A3o_Vicente%2C_Acervo_do_Museu_Paulista_da_USP.jpg/250px-Benedito_Calixto_de_Jesus_-_Funda%C3%A7%C3%A3o_de_S%C3%A3o_Vicente%2C_Acervo_do_Museu_Paulista_da_USP.jpg)

The founding of São Vicente, in what is now the state of São Paulo, marks the effective beginning of colonization in Brazil, in 1532.

However, the decentralized and unorganized captaincy system proved unsuccessful, and in 1549 the Portuguese king restructured them into the [Governorate General of Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governorate_General_of_Brazil "Governorate General of Brazil") in the city of [Salvador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvador,_Bahia "Salvador, Bahia"), which became the capital of a single and centralized Portuguese colony in South America.[^61] [^62] In the first two centuries of colonization, Indigenous and European groups lived in constant war, establishing opportunistic alliances in order to gain advantages against each other.[^63] [^64] [^65] [^66]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/L%27Ex%C3%A9cution_de_la_Punition_de_Fouet_by_Jean-Baptiste_Debret.jpg/250px-L%27Ex%C3%A9cution_de_la_Punition_de_Fouet_by_Jean-Baptiste_Debret.jpg)

Execution of the Punishment of the Whip by Jean-Baptiste Debret. Nearly 5 million enslaved Africans were imported to Brazil during the Atlantic slave trade, more than any other country. 67

By the mid-16th century, [cane sugar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar#Sugarcane "Sugar") had become [Brazil's most important export](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_sugar_cycle "Brazilian sugar cycle"),[^59] [^68] while slaves purchased in Sub-Saharan Africa in the [slave market of Western Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery_in_Africa#Atlantic_slave_trade "Slavery in Africa") [^69] (not only those from Portuguese allies of their colonies in [Angola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Angola "Portuguese Angola") and [Mozambique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Mozambique "Portuguese Mozambique")), had become its largest import,[^70] [^71] to cope with [sugarcane plantations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engenho "Engenho"), due to increasing international demand for Brazilian sugar.[^72] [^73] Brazil received more than 2.8 million slaves from Africa between the years 1500 and 1800.[^74]

By the end of the 17th century, sugar exports began to decline [^75] and the discovery of gold by [bandeirantes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bandeirantes "Bandeirantes") in the 1690s would become the new backbone of the colony's economy, fostering a [gold rush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_Gold_Rush "Brazilian Gold Rush") [^76] which attracted thousands of new settlers to Brazil from Portugal and all Portuguese colonies around the world.[^77] This increased level of immigration in turn caused [some conflicts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Emboabas "War of the Emboabas") between newcomers and old settlers.[^78]

Portuguese expeditions known as *bandeiras* gradually [expanded Brazil's original colonial frontiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uti_possidetis#The_Brazilian_frontier_movement_into_Spanish-claimed_lands "Uti possidetis") in South America to its approximately current borders.[^79] [^80] In this era, other European powers tried to colonize parts of Brazil, in incursions that the Portuguese had to fight, notably the French [in Rio during the 1560s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France_Antarctique "France Antarctique"), [in Maranhão during the 1610s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equinoctial_France "Equinoctial France"), and the [Dutch in Bahia and Pernambuco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_invasions_of_Brazil "Dutch invasions of Brazil"), during the [Dutch–Portuguese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch%E2%80%93Portuguese_War "Dutch–Portuguese War"), after the end of [Iberian Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberian_Union "Iberian Union").[^81]

The Portuguese colonial administration in Brazil had two objectives that would ensure colonial order and the monopoly of Portugal's wealthiest and largest colony: to keep under control and eradicate all forms of slave rebellion and resistance, such as the [Quilombo of Palmares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palmares_\(quilombo\) "Palmares (quilombo)"),[^82] and to repress all movements for autonomy or independence, such as the *[Inconfidência Mineira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inconfid%C3%AAncia_Mineira "Inconfidência Mineira")* (1789).[^83]

### Kingdom of Brazil

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Aclama%C3%A7%C3%A3o_do_rei_Dom_Jo%C3%A3o_VI_no_Rio_de_Janeiro.jpg/250px-Aclama%C3%A7%C3%A3o_do_rei_Dom_Jo%C3%A3o_VI_no_Rio_de_Janeiro.jpg)

The Acclamation of King João VI of the United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves in Rio de Janeiro, 6 February 1818

In late 1807, Spanish and Napoleonic forces threatened the security of [continental Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_Portugal "Continental Portugal"), causing [Prince Regent John](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_VI_of_Portugal "John VI of Portugal"), in the name of [Queen Maria I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maria_I_of_Portugal "Maria I of Portugal"), to [move the royal court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transfer_of_the_Portuguese_court_to_Brazil "Transfer of the Portuguese court to Brazil") from [Lisbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisbon "Lisbon") to [Rio de Janeiro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro "Rio de Janeiro").[^84] There they established some of Brazil's first financial institutions, such as its local [stock exchanges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stock_exchange "Stock exchange") [^85] and its [National Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banco_do_Brasil "Banco do Brasil"), additionally ending the Portuguese monopoly on Brazilian trade and opening Brazil's ports to other nations. In 1809, in retaliation for being forced into exile, the Prince Regent ordered the [conquest of French Guiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_conquest_of_French_Guiana "Portuguese conquest of French Guiana").[^86]

With the end of the [Peninsular War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peninsular_War "Peninsular War") in 1814, the courts of Europe demanded that Queen Maria I and Prince Regent John return to Portugal, deeming it unfit for the head of an ancient European monarchy to reside in a colony. In 1815, to justify continuing to live in Brazil, where the royal court had thrived for six years, the Crown established the [United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Portugal,_Brazil_and_the_Algarves "United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves"), thus creating a [pluricontinental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluricontinental "Pluricontinental") transatlantic monarchic state.[^87] However, the leadership in Portugal, resentful of the new status of its larger colony, continued to demand the return of the court to Lisbon (see [Liberal Revolution of 1820](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_Revolution_of_1820 "Liberal Revolution of 1820")). In 1821, acceding to the demands of revolutionaries who had taken the city of [Porto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porto "Porto"),[^88] John VI departed for Lisbon. There he swore an oath to the new constitution, leaving his son, [Prince Pedro de Alcântara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_I_of_Brazil "Pedro I of Brazil"), as Regent of the [Kingdom of Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Brazil "Kingdom of Brazil").[^89]

### Empire of Brazil

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Pedro_Am%C3%A9rico_-_Independ%C3%AAncia_ou_Morte_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/250px-Pedro_Am%C3%A9rico_-_Independ%C3%AAncia_ou_Morte_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg)

Declaration of the Brazilian independence by Pedro I on 7 September 1822

Tensions between Portuguese and Brazilians increased and the [Portuguese Cortes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Cortes "Portuguese Cortes"), guided by the new political regime imposed by the Liberal Revolution, tried to re-establish Brazil as a colony.[^90] The Brazilians refused to yield, and Prince Pedro decided to side with them, [declaring the country's independence from Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_Declaration_of_Independence "Brazilian Declaration of Independence") on 7 September 1822.[^91] A month later, Prince Pedro was proclaimed the first [Emperor of Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_of_Brazil "Emperor of Brazil"), with the royal title of Dom [Pedro I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_I_of_Brazil "Pedro I of Brazil"), resulting in the founding of the [Empire of Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_Brazil "Empire of Brazil").[^92]

The [Brazilian War of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_War_of_Independence "Brazilian War of Independence"), which had already begun along this process, spread through the northern, northeastern regions and in the [Cisplatina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cisplatina "Cisplatina") province.[^93] The last Portuguese soldiers surrendered on 8 March 1824;[^94] Portugal [officially recognized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Rio_de_Janeiro_\(1825\) "Treaty of Rio de Janeiro (1825)") Brazilian independence on 29 August 1825.[^95]

On 7 April 1831, worn down by years of administrative turmoil and political dissent with both liberals and conservatives, including an attempt of [republican secession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confederation_of_the_Equator "Confederation of the Equator") [^96] and unreconciled to the way that absolutists in Portugal had given in the succession of King John VI, Pedro I departed for Portugal to [reclaim his daughter's crown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_Wars "Liberal Wars") after [abdicating the Brazilian throne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdication_of_Pedro_I_of_Brazil "Abdication of Pedro I of Brazil") in favor of his five-year-old son and heir (Dom [Pedro II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_II_of_Brazil "Pedro II of Brazil")).[^97]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Pedro_Am%C3%A9rico_-_D._Pedro_II_na_abertura_da_Assembl%C3%A9ia_Geral.jpg/250px-Pedro_Am%C3%A9rico_-_D._Pedro_II_na_abertura_da_Assembl%C3%A9ia_Geral.jpg)

Pedro II, Emperor of Brazil between 1831 and 1889

As the new Emperor could not exert his constitutional powers until he came of age, a [regency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regency_period_\(Empire_of_Brazil\) "Regency period (Empire of Brazil)") was set up by the [General Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Assembly_\(Brazil\) "General Assembly (Brazil)").[^98] In the absence of a charismatic figure who could represent a moderate face of power, a series of localized rebellions took place, such as the [Cabanagem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabanagem "Cabanagem") in [Grão-Pará](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gr%C3%A3o-Par%C3%A1_Province "Grão-Pará Province"), the [Malê Revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mal%C3%AA_Revolt "Malê Revolt") in Salvador, the [Balaiada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balaiada "Balaiada") ([Maranhão](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maranh%C3%A3o "Maranhão")), the [Sabinada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabinada "Sabinada") ([Bahia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bahia "Bahia")), and the [Ragamuffin War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ragamuffin_War "Ragamuffin War"), which began in [Rio Grande do Sul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Sul "Rio Grande do Sul") and was supported by [Giuseppe Garibaldi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppe_Garibaldi "Giuseppe Garibaldi"). These emerged from the provinces' dissatisfaction with the central power, coupled with old and latent social tensions peculiar to a vast, slaveholding and newly independent nation state.[^99] This period of internal political and social upheaval, which included the [Praieira revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praieira_revolt "Praieira revolt") in [Pernambuco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pernambuco "Pernambuco"), was overcome only at the end of the 1840s, years after the end of the regency, which occurred with the [premature coronation of Pedro II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dom_Pedro_II#Early_coronation "Dom Pedro II") in 1841.[^100]

During the last phase of the monarchy, internal political debate centered on the issue of slavery. The [Atlantic slave trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade_to_Brazil "Atlantic slave trade to Brazil") was outlawed in 1850,[^101] as a result of the British [Aberdeen Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aberdeen_Act "Aberdeen Act") and the [Eusébio de Queirós Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eus%C3%A9bio_de_Queir%C3%B3s_Law "Eusébio de Queirós Law"), but only in May 1888, after a long process of [internal mobilization and debate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abolitionism_in_Brazil "Abolitionism in Brazil") for an ethical and legal dismantling of [slavery in the country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery_in_Brazil "Slavery in Brazil"), was the institution formally abolished with the approval of the [Golden Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lei_%C3%81urea "Lei Áurea").[^102]

The foreign-affairs policies of the monarchy dealt with issues pertaining Brazil's neighboring countries in the [Southern Cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Cone "Southern Cone"). Long after the [Cisplatine War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cisplatine_War "Cisplatine War") that resulted in the independence of [Uruguay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uruguay "Uruguay"),[^103] Brazil won three international wars during the 58-year reign of Pedro II: the [Platine War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platine_War "Platine War"), the [Uruguayan War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uruguayan_War "Uruguayan War") and the devastating [Paraguayan War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraguayan_War "Paraguayan War"), the largest war effort in Brazilian history.[^104] [^105]

Although there was no desire among the majority of Brazilians to change the country's form of government,[^106] on 15 November 1889, in disagreement with the majority of the [Imperial Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Brazilian_Army "Imperial Brazilian Army") officers, as well as with rural and financial elites, the monarchy was overthrown by a [military coup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation_of_the_Republic_\(Brazil\) "Proclamation of the Republic (Brazil)").[^107] A few days later, the [national flag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_Brazil "Flag of Brazil") was replaced with a new design that included the national motto " *Ordem e Progresso* ", influenced by [positivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positivism "Positivism"). 15 November is now [Republic Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_Day "Republic Day"), a national holiday.[^108]

### Early Republic

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Proclama%C3%A7%C3%A3o_da_Rep%C3%BAblica_by_Benedito_Calixto_1893.jpg/250px-Proclama%C3%A7%C3%A3o_da_Rep%C3%BAblica_by_Benedito_Calixto_1893.jpg)

Proclamation of the Republic, 1893, oil on canvas by Benedito Calixto

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Constru%C3%A7%C3%A3o_do_Congresso_Nacional_Esplanada_dos_Minist%C3%A9rios_1959-10_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Constru%C3%A7%C3%A3o_do_Congresso_Nacional_Esplanada_dos_Minist%C3%A9rios_1959-10_%28cropped%29.jpg)

Construction of the National Congress building in Brasília, 1959, during the JK administration

The [early republican government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_the_Sword "Republic of the Sword") was a military dictatorship, with the army dominating affairs both in Rio de Janeiro and in the states. Freedom of the press disappeared and elections were controlled by those in power.[^109] Not until 1894, following an [economic crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encilhamento "Encilhamento") and [a military one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolta_da_Armada "Revolta da Armada"), did civilians take power, remaining there until October 1930.[^110] [^111] [^112]

In this first republican period, Brazil maintained a relative balance characterized by a success in resolving border disputes with neighboring countries,[^113] only broken by the [Acre War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acre_War "Acre War") (1899–1902) and [its involvement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil_during_World_War_I "Brazil during World War I") in [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") (1914–1918),[^114] [^115] [^116] followed by a [failed attempt to exert a prominent role](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidency_of_Artur_Bernardes#Withdrawal_from_the_League_of_Nations "Presidency of Artur Bernardes") in the [League of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/League_of_Nations "League of Nations");[^117] Internally, from the crisis of *Encilhamento* [^118] [^119] [^120] and the [Navy Revolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolta_da_Armada "Revolta da Armada"),[^121] a prolonged cycle of financial, political and social instability began until the 1920s, keeping the country besieged by various rebellions, both civilian [^122] [^123] [^124] and military.[^125] [^126] [^127]

Little by little, [a cycle of general instability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rebellions_and_revolutions_in_Brazil#1st_Republican_period_\(1889%E2%80%931930\) "Rebellions and revolutions in Brazil") sparked by these crises undermined the regime to such an extent that in the wake of the murder of his running mate, the defeated opposition presidential candidate [Getúlio Vargas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Get%C3%BAlio_Vargas "Getúlio Vargas"), supported by most of the military, successfully led the [Revolution of 1930](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolution_of_1930 "Revolution of 1930").[^128] [^129] Vargas and the military were supposed to assume power temporarily, but instead closed down Congress, extinguished the Constitution, ruled with emergency powers and replaced the states' governors with his own supporters.[^130] [^131]

In the 1930s, three attempts to remove Vargas and his supporters from power failed. The first was the [Constitutionalist Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutionalist_Revolution "Constitutionalist Revolution") in 1932, led by São Paulo's oligarchy. The second was a [Communist uprising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_uprising_of_1935 "Brazilian uprising of 1935") in November 1935, and the last one a *[putsch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integralist_Uprising "Integralist Uprising")* attempt by [local fascists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_Integralist_Action "Brazilian Integralist Action") in May 1938.[^132] [^133] [^134] The 1935 uprising created a security crisis in which Congress transferred more power to the executive branch. The [1937 *coup d'état*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1937_Brazilian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1937 Brazilian coup d'état") resulted in the cancellation of the 1938 election and formalized Vargas as dictator, beginning the [Estado Novo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estado_Novo_\(Brazil\) "Estado Novo (Brazil)") era. During this period, government brutality and censorship of the press increased.[^135]

During [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), Brazil remained neutral until August 1942, when the country suffered [retaliation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submarine_warfare#Atlantic_ocean "Submarine warfare") by [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") and [Fascist Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy_under_fascism "Italy under fascism") in a strategic dispute over the South Atlantic, and, therefore, [entered the war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil_in_World_War_II "Brazil in World War II") on the [allied side](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II "Allies of World War II").[^136] [^137] [^138] In addition to [its participation in the battle of the Atlantic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Atlantic#South_Atlantic_\(May_1942_%E2%80%93_September_1943\) "Battle of the Atlantic"), Brazil also sent an [expeditionary force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_Expeditionary_Force "Brazilian Expeditionary Force") to fight in the [Italian campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Campaign_\(World_War_II\) "Italian Campaign (World War II)").[^139]

With the Allied victory in 1945 and the end of the fascist regimes in Europe, Vargas' position became untenable, and he was [swiftly overthrown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1945_Brazilian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1945 Brazilian coup d'état") in another military coup, with democracy reinstated by the same army that had ended it 15 years earlier.[^140] Vargas committed suicide in August 1954 amid a political crisis, after having returned to power by election in 1950.[^141] [^142]

Several brief interim governments followed Vargas' suicide.[^143] [Juscelino Kubitschek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juscelino_Kubitschek "Juscelino Kubitschek") became president in 1956 and assumed a conciliatory stance towards the political opposition that allowed him to govern without major crises.[^144] The economy and industrial sector grew remarkably,[^145] but his greatest achievement was the construction of the new capital city of [Brasília](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bras%C3%ADlia "Brasília"), inaugurated in 1960.[^146] Kubitschek's successor, [Jânio Quadros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%A2nio_Quadros "Jânio Quadros"), resigned in 1961 less than a year after taking office.[^147] His vice-president, [João Goulart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Goulart "João Goulart"), assumed the presidency, but aroused strong political opposition [^148] and was [deposed in April 1964](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1964_Brazilian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1964 Brazilian coup d'état") by a coup that resulted in a [military dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_dictatorship_in_Brazil "Military dictatorship in Brazil").[^149]

### Military dictatorship

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Tanques_ocupam_a_Avenida_Presidente_Vargas%2C_1968-04-04.jpg/250px-Tanques_ocupam_a_Avenida_Presidente_Vargas%2C_1968-04-04.jpg)

M41s along the Avenida Presidente Vargas, Rio de Janeiro, in April 1968, during the military dictatorship

The new regime was intended to be transitory,[^150] but gradually closed in on itself and became a full dictatorship with the promulgation of the [Fifth Institutional Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AI-5 "AI-5") in 1968.[^151] Oppression was not limited to those who resorted to [guerrilla tactics to fight the regime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armed_struggle_against_the_Brazilian_military_dictatorship "Armed struggle against the Brazilian military dictatorship"), but also reached institutional opponents, artists, journalists and other members of civil society,[^152] [^153] inside and outside the country through " [Operation Condor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Condor "Operation Condor") ".[^154] [^155] Like other [authoritarian regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism"), due to an economic boom, known as the " [economic miracle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_Miracle "Brazilian Miracle") ", the Brazilian military dictatorship reached a peak in popularity in the early 1970s.[^156]

Slowly, however, the wear and tear of years of dictatorial power had not slowed the repression, even after the defeat of the leftist guerrillas.[^157] The inability to deal with the economic crises of the period and popular pressure made a redemocratization policy inevitable, which from the regime side was led by Generals [Ernesto Geisel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernesto_Geisel "Ernesto Geisel") and [Golbery do Couto e Silva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golbery_do_Couto_e_Silva "Golbery do Couto e Silva").[^158] With the enactment of the [Amnesty Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amnesty_law#Brazil "Amnesty law") in 1979, Brazil began a slow [return to democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redemocratization_in_Brazil "Redemocratization in Brazil"), which was completed during the 1980s.[^100]

### Contemporary era

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Ulyssesguimaraesconstituicao_restored.jpg/250px-Ulyssesguimaraesconstituicao_restored.jpg)

Ulysses Guimarães holding the Constitution of 1988

Civilians returned to power in 1985 when [José Sarney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Sarney "José Sarney") assumed the presidency. He became unpopular during his tenure through failure to control the economic crisis and [hyperinflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperinflation "Hyperinflation") he inherited from the military regime.[^159] Sarney's unsuccessful government led to the [election in 1989](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_presidential_election,_1989 "Brazilian presidential election, 1989") of the almost-unknown [Fernando Collor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fernando_Collor_de_Mello "Fernando Collor de Mello"), who was subsequently [impeached by the National Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_of_Fernando_Collor_de_Mello "Impeachment of Fernando Collor de Mello") in 1992.[^160] Collor was succeeded by his vice-president, [Itamar Franco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Itamar_Franco "Itamar Franco"), who appointed [Fernando Henrique Cardoso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fernando_Henrique_Cardoso "Fernando Henrique Cardoso") as Minister of Finance. In 1994, Cardoso devised a highly successful [Plano Real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plano_Real "Plano Real") that,[^161] after decades of [failed economic plans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_Brazilian_economic_stabilization_plans "Timeline of Brazilian economic stabilization plans") made by previous governments attempting to curb hyperinflation, finally stabilized the Brazilian economy.[^162] [^163] Cardoso won the [1994 election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1994_Brazilian_general_election "1994 Brazilian general election"), and [again in 1998](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1998_Brazilian_general_election "1998 Brazilian general election").[^164]

The [peaceful transition of power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peaceful_transition_of_power "Peaceful transition of power") from Cardoso to his main opposition leader, [Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luiz_In%C3%A1cio_Lula_da_Silva "Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva") ([elected in 2002](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_presidential_election,_2002 "Brazilian presidential election, 2002") and [re-elected in 2006](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_presidential_election,_2006 "Brazilian presidential election, 2006")), was seen as proof that Brazil had achieved a long-sought political stability.[^165] [^166] However, sparked by discontent accumulated over decades from corruption, [police brutality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Police_brutality_by_country#Brazil "Police brutality by country"), inefficiencies of the political [establishment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Establishment "The Establishment") and [public service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_service "Public service"), [numerous peaceful protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2013_protests_in_Brazil "2013 protests in Brazil") erupted in Brazil in the middle of the first term of [Dilma Rousseff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dilma_Rousseff "Dilma Rousseff"), who had succeeded Lula after winning election [in 2010](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_presidential_election,_2010 "Brazilian presidential election, 2010") and again [in 2014](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_presidential_election,_2014 "Brazilian presidential election, 2014") by narrow margins.[^167] [^168]

Rousseff [was impeached](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_of_Dilma_Rousseff "Impeachment of Dilma Rousseff") by the [Brazilian Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_Congress "Brazilian Congress") in 2016, halfway into her second term,[^169] [^170] and replaced by her vice-president [Michel Temer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Temer "Michel Temer"), who assumed full presidential powers after Rousseff's impeachment was accepted on 31 August. Large street [protests for and against her](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015%E2%80%9316_protests_in_Brazil "2015–16 protests in Brazil") took place during the impeachment process.[^171] The charges against her were fueled by political and economic crises along with evidence of involvement with politicians from all the primary political parties. In 2017, the Supreme Court requested the investigation of 71 Brazilian lawmakers and nine ministers of President Michel Temer's cabinet who were allegedly linked to the [Petrobras corruption scandal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Car_Wash "Operation Car Wash").[^172] President Temer himself was also accused of [corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corruption_in_Brazil "Corruption in Brazil").[^173]

In the fiercely contested [2018 elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2018_Brazilian_general_election "2018 Brazilian general election"), the controversial conservative candidate [Jair Bolsonaro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jair_Bolsonaro "Jair Bolsonaro") of the [Social Liberal Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_Liberal_Party_\(Brazil\) "Social Liberal Party (Brazil)") (PSL) was elected president, winning in the second round against [Fernando Haddad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fernando_Haddad "Fernando Haddad"), of the [Workers Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Workers%27_Party_\(Brazil\) "Workers' Party (Brazil)") (PT), with the support of 55.13% of the valid votes.[^174] In the early 2020s, Brazil became [one of the hardest hit countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_Brazil "COVID-19 pandemic in Brazil") during the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic"), receiving the second-highest death toll worldwide after the United States.[^175] In May 2021, [Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luiz_In%C3%A1cio_Lula_da_Silva "Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva") stated that he would run for a third term in the [2022 Brazilian general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Brazilian_general_election "2022 Brazilian general election") against Bolsonaro.[^176] In October 2022, Lula was in first place in the first round, with 48.43% of the support from the electorate, and received 50.90% of the votes in the second round.[^177] [^178] On 8 January 2023, a week after Lula's inauguration, a mob of Bolsonaro's supporters [attacked Brazil's federal government buildings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Brazilian_Congress_attack "2023 Brazilian Congress attack") in the capital, [Brasília](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bras%C3%ADlia "Brasília"), after several weeks of unrest.[^179] [^180]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Brazil_topo.jpg/250px-Brazil_topo.jpg)

A topographic map of Brazil

Brazil occupies nearly half of the land area of South America (47.7%), including a large area along its eastern coast and much of the continent's interior.[^181] It borders [Uruguay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uruguay "Uruguay") to the south; [Argentina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argentina "Argentina") and [Paraguay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraguay "Paraguay") to the southwest; [Bolivia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolivia "Bolivia") and [Peru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peru "Peru") to the west; [Colombia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colombia "Colombia") to the northwest; and [Venezuela](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venezuela "Venezuela"), [Guyana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guyana "Guyana"), [Suriname](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suriname "Suriname"), and France (French overseas region of [French Guiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Guiana "French Guiana")) to the north. It shares a border with every South American country except [Ecuador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecuador "Ecuador") and [Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile "Chile").[^20]

The Brazilian territory also encompasses a number of oceanic archipelagos, such as [Fernando de Noronha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fernando_de_Noronha "Fernando de Noronha"), [Rocas Atoll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocas_Atoll "Rocas Atoll"), [Saint Peter and Saint Paul Archipelago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Peter_and_Saint_Paul_Archipelago "Saint Peter and Saint Paul Archipelago"), and the islands of [Trindade and Martim Vaz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trindade_and_Martim_Vaz "Trindade and Martim Vaz").[^20] Its size, relief, climate, and natural resources make Brazil geographically diverse.[^181] Including its Atlantic islands, Brazil lies between latitudes [6°N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/6th_parallel_north "6th parallel north") and [34°S](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/34th_parallel_south "34th parallel south"), and longitudes [28°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/28th_meridian_west "28th meridian west") and [74°W](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/74th_meridian_west "74th meridian west").[^20]

Brazil is the [fifth largest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_outlying_territories_by_total_area "List of countries and outlying territories by total area") country in the world, and third largest in the Americas, with a total area of 8,515,767.049 km <sup>2</sup> (3,287,956 sq mi),[^182] including 55,455 km <sup>2</sup> (21,411 sq mi) of water. Brazil is the only country in the world that has the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator") and the [Tropic of Capricorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn "Tropic of Capricorn") running through it.[^20] It spans four [time zones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_zone "Time zone"); from [UTC−5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTC-05 "UTC-05") comprising the state of [Acre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acre_\(state\) "Acre (state)") and the westernmost portion of [Amazonas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazonas_\(Brazilian_state\) "Amazonas (Brazilian state)"), to [UTC−4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTC%E2%88%9204 "UTC−04") in the western states, to [UTC−3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTC%E2%88%9203 "UTC−03") in the eastern states (the [national time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_in_Brazil "Time in Brazil")) and [UTC−2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTC%E2%88%9202 "UTC−02") in the [Atlantic islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_islands_of_Brazil "List of islands of Brazil").[^183]

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_types_of_Brazil.svg/250px-K%C3%B6ppen_climate_types_of_Brazil.svg.png)

Brazil map of Köppen climate classification zones

The climate of Brazil comprises a wide range of weather conditions across a large area and varied topography, but most of the country is tropical.[^20] According to the [Köppen system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification "Köppen climate classification"), Brazil hosts six major climatic subtypes: [desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert_climate "Desert climate"), [equatorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_rainforest_climate "Tropical rainforest climate"), [tropical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_climate "Tropical climate"), [semiarid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-arid_climate "Semi-arid climate"), [oceanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceanic_climate "Oceanic climate") and [subtropical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate "Humid subtropical climate"). The different climatic conditions produce environments ranging from [equatorial rainforests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_moist_broadleaf_forests "Tropical and subtropical moist broadleaf forests") in the north and semiarid deserts in the northeast, to [temperate coniferous forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperate_coniferous_forest "Temperate coniferous forest") in the south and [tropical savannas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_grasslands,_savannas,_and_shrublands "Tropical and subtropical grasslands, savannas, and shrublands") in central Brazil.[^184]

In Brazil, [forest cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_cover "Forest cover") is around 59% of the total land area, equivalent to 496,619,600 hectares (ha) of forest in 2020, down from 588,898,000 hectares (ha) in 1990. In 2020, naturally regenerating forest covered 485,396,000 hectares (ha) and planted forest covered 11,223,600 hectares (ha). Of the naturally regenerating forest, 44% was reported to be [primary forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_forest "Primary forest") (consisting of native tree species with no clearly visible indications of human activity) and around 30% of the forest area was found within protected areas. For 2015, 56% of the forest area was reported to be under [public ownership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_ownership "State ownership") and 44% [private ownership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_property "Private property").[^185] [^186]

Many regions have starkly different [microclimates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microclimate "Microclimate").[^187] [^188] An equatorial climate characterizes much of northern Brazil. There is no real [dry season](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry_season "Dry season"), but there are some variations in the period of the year when most rain falls.[^184] Temperatures average 25 °C (77 °F),[^188] with more significant temperature variation between night and day than between seasons.[^187] Over central Brazil, rainfall is more seasonal, characteristic of a savanna climate.[^187] This region is as extensive as the Amazon basin but has a very different climate as it lies farther south at a higher altitude.[^184] In the interior northeast, seasonal rainfall is even more extreme.[^189] South of Bahia, near the coasts, and more southerly most of the state of São Paulo, the distribution of rainfall changes, with rain falling throughout the year.[^184] The south enjoys subtropical conditions, with cool winters and average annual temperatures not exceeding 18 °C (64.4 °F);[^188] winter frosts and [snowfall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snow_in_Brazil "Snow in Brazil") are not rare in the highest areas.[^184] [^187]

The semiarid climatic region generally receives less than 800 millimeters (31.5 in) of rain,[^189] most of which generally falls in a period of three to five months of the year [^190] and occasionally less than this, creating long periods of drought.[^187] Brazil's 1877–78 *[Grande Seca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grande_Seca "Grande Seca")* (Great Drought), the worst in Brazil's history,[^191] caused approximately half a million deaths.[^192] A similarly devastating drought occurred in 1915.[^193] In 2024, for the first time, "a drought has covered all the way from the North to the country's Southeast". It is the strongest drought in Brazil since the beginning of measurement in the 1950s, covering almost 60% of the country's territory. The drought is linked to deforestation and climate change.[^194] [^195] [^196]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/20211026_Cumulative_carbon_dioxide_CO2_emissions_by_country_-_bar_chart.svg/250px-20211026_Cumulative_carbon_dioxide_CO2_emissions_by_country_-_bar_chart.svg.png)

Since 1850, Brazil has cumulatively contributed an amount of CO 2 placing it among the top greenhouse gas emitters in the world, mainly due to its land use and forestry practices. 197

[Climate change in Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Brazil "Climate change in Brazil") is causing higher temperatures and longer-lasting heatwaves, changing precipitation patterns, more intense wildfires and heightened fire risk.[^198] Brazil's hydropower, agriculture and urban water supplies will be affected.[^199] Brazil's rainforests, and the Amazon, are particularly at risk to climate change. At worst, large areas of the Amazon River basin could turn into savannah, with severe consequences for global climate and local livelihoods.[^200] [Extreme weather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_weather "Extreme weather") events like droughts and flash floods are causing annual losses of around R$13 billion (US$2.6 billion), equivalent to 0.1% of the country's 2022 GDP. Climate impacts could exacerbate poverty.[^199]

Brazil's [greenhouse gas emissions per person are higher than the global average](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_greenhouse_gas_emissions_per_capita "List of countries by greenhouse gas emissions per capita"), and Brazil is among the top 10 [highest emitting countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_greenhouse_gas_emissions "List of countries by greenhouse gas emissions"). [Greenhouse gas emissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions "Greenhouse gas emissions") by Brazil are over 4% of the annual world total.[^201] In 2026 the Brazilian government has launched a National Climate Change Plan which should cut greenhouse gas emissions by 59% - 67% by 2035, and make the country carbon neutral by 2050.[^202] Brazil hosted the [2025 United Nations Climate Change Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference "2025 United Nations Climate Change Conference"),[^203] in which it launched the [Global carbon market coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_Coalition_\(COP30_proposal\) "Climate Coalition (COP30 proposal)") immediately joined by 11 countries including Brazil, [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China"), [the European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union"), [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom").[^204] According to some calculations, a global carbon market can speed up emission reduction seven-fold.[^205]

### Topography and hydrography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Amazon_River_ESA387332.jpg/250px-Amazon_River_ESA387332.jpg)

Satellite image of the Amazon Delta

Brazilian topography is also diverse and includes hills, mountains, plains, highlands, and scrublands. Much of the terrain lies between 200 meters (660 ft) and 800 meters (2,600 ft) in elevation.[^206] The main upland area occupies most of the southern half of the country.[^206] The northwestern parts of the plateau consist of broad, rolling terrain broken by low, rounded hills.[^206]

The southeastern section is more rugged, with a complex mass of ridges and mountain ranges reaching elevations of up to 1,200 meters (3,900 ft).[^206] These ranges include the [Mantiqueira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mantiqueira_Mountains "Mantiqueira Mountains") and [Espinhaço](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Espinha%C3%A7o_Mountains "Espinhaço Mountains") mountains and the [Serra do Mar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serra_do_Mar "Serra do Mar").[^206] In the north, the [Guiana Highlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guiana_Shield "Guiana Shield") form a major drainage divide, separating rivers that flow south into the Amazon Basin from rivers that empty into the [Orinoco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orinoco "Orinoco") River system, in Venezuela, to the north. The highest point in Brazil is the [Pico da Neblina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pico_da_Neblina "Pico da Neblina") at 2,994 meters (9,823 ft), and the lowest is the Atlantic Ocean.[^20]

Brazil has a dense and complex system of rivers, one of the world's most extensive, with eight major drainage basins, all of which drain into the Atlantic.[^207] Major rivers include the [Amazon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon_River "Amazon River") (the world's largest in terms of water volume), the [Paraná](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paran%C3%A1_River "Paraná River") and its major tributary the [Iguaçu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iguazu_River "Iguazu River") (which includes the [Iguazu Falls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iguazu_Falls "Iguazu Falls")), the [Negro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rio_Negro_\(Amazon\) "Rio Negro (Amazon)"), [São Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Francisco_River "São Francisco River"), [Xingu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xingu_River "Xingu River"), [Madeira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madeira_River "Madeira River") and [Tapajós](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tapaj%C3%B3s "Tapajós") rivers.[^207]

### Biodiversity and conservation

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Toco_Toucan_%28Ramphastos_toco%29_-_48153967707.jpg/250px-Toco_Toucan_%28Ramphastos_toco%29_-_48153967707.jpg)

The toco toucan is an animal typical of the Brazilian savannas.

The [wildlife of Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildlife_of_Brazil "Wildlife of Brazil") comprises all naturally occurring animals, plants, and fungi in the South American country. Home to 60% of the Amazon rainforest, which accounts for approximately one-tenth of all species in the world,[^208] Brazil is considered to have the greatest [biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity "Biodiversity") of any country on the planet, containing over 70% of all animal and plant species catalogued.[^209] Brazil has the most known species of plants (55,000), freshwater fish (3,000) and mammals (over 689).[^210] It also ranks third on the list of countries with the most bird species (1,832) and second with the most reptile species (744).[^210] The number of fungal species is unknown but is large.[^211] Brazil is second only to [Indonesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesia "Indonesia") as the country with the most [endemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endemic "Endemic") species.[^212]

Brazil's large territory comprises different ecosystems, such as the Amazon rainforest, recognized as having the greatest biological diversity in the world,[^213] with the [Atlantic Forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Forest "Atlantic Forest") and the [Cerrado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerrado "Cerrado") sustaining the greatest biodiversity.[^214] In the south, the [Araucaria moist forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Araucaria_moist_forests "Araucaria moist forests") grow under temperate conditions.[^214] The rich wildlife of Brazil reflects the variety of natural habitats. Scientists estimate that the total number of plant and animal species in Brazil could approach four million, mostly invertebrates.[^214] Larger mammals include carnivores [pumas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cougar "Cougar"), [jaguars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaguar "Jaguar"), [ocelots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocelot "Ocelot"), rare [bush dogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bush_dog "Bush dog"), and [foxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fox "Fox"), and herbivores [peccaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peccary "Peccary"), [tapirs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tapir "Tapir"), [anteaters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anteater "Anteater"), [sloths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloth "Sloth"), [opossums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opossum "Opossum") and [armadillos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armadillo "Armadillo"). [Deer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deer "Deer") are plentiful in the south, and many species of [New World monkeys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_World_monkey "New World monkey") are found in the northern rainforests.[^214] [^215]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/20220910_Amazon_deforestation_and_degradation%2C_by_country_-_Amazon_Watch.svg/250px-20220910_Amazon_deforestation_and_degradation%2C_by_country_-_Amazon_Watch.svg.png)

Cumulatively, Brazil has the highest percentage of deforested and highly degraded rainforest of any Amazonia nation. 216

More than one-fifth of the Amazon rainforest in Brazil has been completely destroyed, and more than 70 mammals are endangered.[^210] The threat of extinction comes from several sources, including [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation_in_Brazil "Deforestation in Brazil") and [poaching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poaching "Poaching"). Extinction is even more problematic in the Atlantic Forest, where nearly 93% of the forest has been cleared.[^217] Of the 202 endangered animals in Brazil, 171 are in the Atlantic Forest.[^218] The Amazon rainforest has been under direct threat of deforestation since the 1970s because of rapid economic and demographic expansion. Extensive legal and illegal logging destroy forests the size of a small country per year, and with it a diverse series of species through [habitat destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_destruction "Habitat destruction") and [habitat fragmentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation "Habitat fragmentation").[^219] Since 1970, over 600,000 square kilometers (230,000 sq mi) of the Amazon rainforest have been cleared by logging.[^220]

In 2017, preserved native vegetation occupied 61% of the Brazilian territory. Agriculture occupied only 8% of the national territory and pastures 19.7%.[^221] For comparison, in 2019, although 43% of the entire European continent has forests, only 3% of the total forest area in Europe is of native forest.[^222] Brazil has a strong interest in conservation, as its agriculture sector directly depends on its forests.[^223]
