**France**,[^8] officially the **French Republic**,[^9] is a country primarily located in [Western Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Europe "Western Europe").[^10] [Its overseas regions and territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_France "Overseas France") include [French Guiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Guiana "French Guiana") in [South America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_America "South America"), [Saint Pierre and Miquelon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Pierre_and_Miquelon "Saint Pierre and Miquelon") in the [North Atlantic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Atlantic "North Atlantic"), the [French West Indies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_West_Indies "French West Indies"), and [many islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_islands_of_France "List of islands of France") in [Oceania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceania "Oceania") and the [Indian Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean"). [Metropolitan France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_France "Metropolitan France") shares borders with [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium "Belgium") and [Luxembourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luxembourg "Luxembourg") to the north; [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany") to the northeast; [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland") to the east; [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy") and [Monaco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monaco "Monaco") to the southeast; [Andorra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andorra "Andorra") and [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain") to the south; and a maritime border with the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") to the northwest. Its metropolitan area extends from the [Rhine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhine "Rhine") to the [Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") and from the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") to the [English Channel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Channel "English Channel") and the [North Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Sea "North Sea"). Its 18 [integral regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regions_of_France#List_of_administrative_regions "Regions of France") —five of which are overseas—span a combined area of 632,702 km <sup>2</sup> (244,288 sq mi), with [a total population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_France "Demographics of France") estimated at over 69.1 million in 2026. Its capital, [largest city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_communes_in_France_with_over_20,000_inhabitants "List of communes in France with over 20,000 inhabitants") and main cultural and economic centre is [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris "Paris").

Metropolitan France was settled during the [Iron Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age") by [Celtic tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ancient_Celtic_peoples_and_tribes "List of ancient Celtic peoples and tribes") known as [Gauls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauls "Gauls") before [Rome annexed the area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Gaul "Roman Gaul") in 51 BC, leading to a distinct [Gallo-Roman culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallo-Roman_culture "Gallo-Roman culture"). In the [Early Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages "Early Middle Ages"), the [Franks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franks "Franks") formed the kingdom of [Francia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francia "Francia"), which became the heartland of the [Carolingian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_Empire "Carolingian Empire"). The [Treaty of Verdun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun "Treaty of Verdun") of 843 partitioned the empire, with [West Francia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Francia "West Francia") evolving into the [Kingdom of France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_France "Kingdom of France"). In the [High Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Middle_Ages "High Middle Ages"), France was a powerful but decentralised [feudal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism "Feudalism") kingdom, but from the mid-14th to the mid-15th centuries, France was plunged into a dynastic conflict with [England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_England "Kingdom of England") known as the [Hundred Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War "Hundred Years' War"). In the 16th century, [French culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_France "Culture of France") flourished during the [French Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Renaissance "French Renaissance"), and a [French colonial empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_colonial_empire "French colonial empire") emerged. Internally, France was dominated by the conflict with the [House of Habsburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Habsburg "House of Habsburg") and the [French Wars of Religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Wars_of_Religion "French Wars of Religion") between [Catholics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholics "Catholics") and [Huguenots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huguenots "Huguenots"). France was successful in the [Thirty Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War "Thirty Years' War") and further increased its influence during the reign of [Louis XIV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_XIV "Louis XIV").

The [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution") of 1789 overthrew the [Ancien Régime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime "Ancien Régime") and produced the [Declaration of the Rights of Man](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Rights_of_Man_and_of_the_Citizen "Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen"), which expresses the nation's ideals to this day. France reached its political and military zenith in the early 19th century under [Napoleon Bonaparte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon "Napoleon"), subjugating much of continental Europe and establishing the [First French Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_French_Empire "First French Empire"). Its collapse initiated a period of relative decline during which France endured the [Bourbon Restoration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bourbon_Restoration_in_France "Bourbon Restoration in France") until the founding of the [French Second Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Second_Republic "French Second Republic"), which was succeeded by the [Second French Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_French_Empire "Second French Empire") upon [Napoleon III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_III "Napoleon III") 's takeover. His empire collapsed during the [Franco-Prussian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War "Franco-Prussian War") in 1870. This led to the establishment of the [French Third Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Third_Republic "French Third Republic"), and a period of economic prosperity and cultural and scientific flourishing known as the [Belle Époque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belle_%C3%89poque "Belle Époque"). France was one of the [major participants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_Entente "Triple Entente") of [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), from which [it emerged victorious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles "Treaty of Versailles") at great human and economic cost. It was among the [Allies of World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II "Allies of World War II"), but it surrendered and [was occupied](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_military_administration_in_occupied_France_during_World_War_II "German military administration in occupied France during World War II") by [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") in 1940. Following [its liberation in 1944](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberation_of_France "Liberation of France"), the short-lived [Fourth Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Fourth_Republic "French Fourth Republic") was established and later dissolved over the course of the [Algerian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_War "Algerian War"). The current [Fifth Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Fifth_Republic "French Fifth Republic"), a [semi-presidential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-presidential_system "Semi-presidential system") system, was formed in 1958 by [Charles de Gaulle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle "Charles de Gaulle"). [Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria "Algeria") and most French colonies became independent in the 1960s, with the majority retaining [close economic and military ties with France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7afrique "Françafrique").

France retains its centuries-long status as a global centre [of art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_art "French art"), [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_and_technology_in_France "Science and technology in France"), [cuisine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_cuisine "French cuisine") and [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_philosophy "French philosophy"). It hosts the [fourth-largest number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites_by_country "World Heritage Sites by country") of UNESCO [World Heritage Sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites "World Heritage Sites"), with [54 in total](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_France "List of World Heritage Sites in France"), and is the [world's leading tourist destination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Tourism_rankings "World Tourism rankings"), having received 102 million foreign [visitors in 2025](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourism_in_France "Tourism in France"). A [developed country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country"), France has a [high nominal per capita income globally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\)_per_capita "List of countries by GDP (nominal) per capita"), and [its economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_France "Economy of France") ranks among the largest in the world [by both nominal GDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of countries by GDP (nominal)") and [PPP-adjusted GDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(PPP\) "List of countries by GDP (PPP)"). It is a [great power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_power "Great power"), being one of the five [permanent members of the United Nations Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permanent_members_of_the_United_Nations_Security_Council "Permanent members of the United Nations Security Council") and an official [nuclear-weapon state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_states_with_nuclear_weapons "List of states with nuclear weapons"). The country is part of [multiple international organisations and forums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outline_of_France#International_organisation_membership "Outline of France").

## Etymology

Originally applied to the whole [Frankish Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francia "Francia"), the name *France* comes from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") *[Francia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francia "Francia")*, or 'realm of the [Franks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franks "Franks") '.[^21] The [name of the Franks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Name_of_the_Franks "Name of the Franks") is related to the English word *frank* ('free'): the latter stems from the [Old French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_French "Old French") *franc* ('free, noble, sincere'), and ultimately from the [Medieval Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_Latin "Medieval Latin") word *francus* ('free, exempt from service; a freeman, a Frank'), a generalisation of the tribal name that emerged as a [Late Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Latin "Late Latin") borrowing of the reconstructed [Frankish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankish_language "Frankish language") [endonym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endonym_and_exonym "Endonym and exonym") \* *Frank*.[^22] [^23] It has been suggested that the meaning 'free' was adopted because after the conquest of [Gaul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaul "Gaul"), only Franks were free of taxation,[^24] or more generally because they had the status of freemen in contrast to servants or slaves.[^23] The etymology of *\*Frank* is uncertain. It is traditionally derived from the [Proto-Germanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language "Proto-Germanic language") word \* *frankōn*, which translates as 'javelin' or 'lance' (the throwing axe of the Franks was known as the *[francisca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francisca "Francisca")*),[^25] although these weapons may have been named because of their use by the Franks, not the other way around.[^23]

In English, 'France' is pronounced [/ f r æ n s /](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English") [*FRANSS*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key") in American English and [/ f r ɑː n s /](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English") [*FRAHNSS*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key") or [/ f r æ n s /](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English") [*FRANSS*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key") in British English. The pronunciation with [/ ɑː /](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English") is mostly confined to accents with the [trap–bath split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trap%E2%80%93bath_split "Trap–bath split") such as [Received Pronunciation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Received_Pronunciation "Received Pronunciation"), though it can be also heard in some other dialects such as [Cardiff English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiff_English "Cardiff English").[^26]

## History

### Prehistory

The oldest traces of archaic humans in what is now France date from approximately 1.8 million years ago.[^27] [Neanderthals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neanderthal "Neanderthal") occupied the region into the [Upper Paleolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic "Upper Paleolithic") era but were slowly replaced by *[Homo sapiens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human "Human")* around 35,000 BC.[^28] This period witnessed the emergence of [cave painting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cave_painting "Cave painting") in the [Dordogne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dordogne "Dordogne") and [Pyrenees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrenees "Pyrenees"), including at [Lascaux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lascaux "Lascaux"), dated to c. 18,000 BC.[^27] At the end of the [Last Glacial Period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_Glacial_Period "Last Glacial Period") (10,000 BC), the climate became milder;[^27] from approximately 7,000 BC, this part of Western Europe entered the [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") era, and its inhabitants became [sedentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedentism "Sedentism").

After demographic and agricultural development between the 4th and 3rd millennia BC, [metallurgy appeared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-age_system "Three-age system"), initially working gold, [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalcolithic "Chalcolithic") and [bronze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age"), then later [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age").[^29] France has numerous [megalithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megalith "Megalith") sites from the Neolithic, including the [Carnac stones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnac_stones "Carnac stones") site (approximately 3,300 BC).

### Antiquity (6th century BC – 5th century AD)

In 600 BC, [Ionian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionia "Ionia") [Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greeks_in_pre-Roman_Gaul "Greeks in pre-Roman Gaul") from [Phocaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phocaea "Phocaea") founded the colony of [Massalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massalia "Massalia") (present-day [Marseille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marseille "Marseille")).[^30] [Celtic tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celtic_tribes "Celtic tribes") penetrated parts of eastern and northern France, spreading through the rest of the country between the 5th and 3rd century BC.[^31] Around 390 BC, the Gallic chieftain [Brennus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brennus_\(leader_of_the_Senones\) "Brennus (leader of the Senones)") and his troops made their way to [Roman Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Italy "Roman Italy"), defeated the Romans in the [Battle of the Allia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Allia "Battle of the Allia"), and besieged and ransomed Rome.[^32] This left Rome weakened, and the Gauls continued to harass the region until 345 BC when they entered into a peace treaty.[^33] But the Romans and the Gauls remained adversaries for centuries.[^34]

![Maison Carrée temple in Nemausus Corinthian columns and portico](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/France-002364_-_Square_House_%2815867600545%29.jpg/250px-France-002364_-_Square_House_%2815867600545%29.jpg)

The Maison carrée was a temple of the Gallo-Roman city of Nemausus (present-day Nîmes ) and is one of the best-preserved Roman temples anywhere.

Around 125 BC, the south of Gaul was conquered by the Romans, who called this region *[Provincia Nostra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallia_Narbonensis "Gallia Narbonensis")* ("Our Province"), which evolved into [Provence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provence "Provence") in French.[^35] [Julius Caesar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Caesar "Julius Caesar") conquered the remainder of Gaul and overcame a revolt by Gallic chieftain [Vercingetorix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vercingetorix "Vercingetorix") in 52 BC.[^36] Gaul was divided by [Augustus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustus "Augustus") into provinces,[^37] and many cities were founded during the [Gallo-Roman period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Gaul "Roman Gaul"), including [Lugdunum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lugdunum "Lugdunum") (present-day [Lyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyon "Lyon")), the capital of the Gauls.[^37] In 250–290 AD, Roman Gaul suffered a crisis with its [fortified borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limes_\(Roman_Empire\) "Limes (Roman Empire)") attacked by barbarians.[^38] The situation improved in the first half of the 4th century, a period of revival and prosperity.[^39] In 312, Emperor [Constantine I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantine_the_Great "Constantine the Great") [converted to Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantine_the_Great_and_Christianity "Constantine the Great and Christianity"). Christians, who had been persecuted, increased.[^40] But from the 5th century, the [barbarian invasions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Migration_Period "Migration Period") resumed.[^41] [Teutonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teutons "Teutons") tribes invaded the region, the [Visigoths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visigoths "Visigoths") settling in the southwest, the [Burgundians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burgundians "Burgundians") along the [Rhine Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhine_Valley "Rhine Valley"), and the Franks in the north.[^42]

### Early Middle Ages (5th–10th century)

In [late antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_antiquity "Late antiquity"), Gaul was divided into Germanic kingdoms and a remaining Gallo-Roman territory. [Celtic Britons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celtic_Britons "Celtic Britons"), fleeing the [Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement_of_Britain "Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain"), settled in west [Armorica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armorica "Armorica"); the Armorican peninsula was renamed [Brittany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brittany "Brittany"), and [Celtic culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celts "Celts") was revived.

The first leader to unite all Franks was [Clovis I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clovis_I "Clovis I"), who began his reign as king of the [Salian Franks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salian_Franks "Salian Franks") in 481, routing the last forces of the Roman governors in 486. Clovis said he would be baptised a Christian in the event of victory against the [Visigothic Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom "Visigothic Kingdom"), which was said to have guaranteed the battle. Clovis [regained the southwest from the Visigoths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco-Visigothic_Wars "Franco-Visigothic Wars") and was baptised in 508. Clovis was the first [Germanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanic_peoples "Germanic peoples") conqueror after the [Fall of the Western Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire "Fall of the Western Roman Empire") to convert to Catholic Christianity; thus France was given the title "Eldest daughter of the Church" by the papacy,[^43] and French kings were called "the Most Christian Kings of France".

![painting of Clovis I conversion to Catholicism in 498, a king being baptised in a tub in a cathedral surrounded by bishop and monks](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Chlodwigs_taufe.jpg/250px-Chlodwigs_taufe.jpg)

With Clovis 's conversion to Catholicism in 498, the Frankish monarchy, elective and secular until then, became hereditary and of divine right.

The Franks embraced the Christian [Gallo-Roman culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallo-Roman_culture "Gallo-Roman culture"), and Gaul was renamed *[Francia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francia "Francia")* ("Land of the Franks"). The Germanic Franks adopted [Romanic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romance_languages "Romance languages"). Clovis made [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris "Paris") his capital and established the [Merovingian dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merovingian_dynasty "Merovingian dynasty"), but his kingdom would not survive his death. The Franks treated land as a private possession and divided it among their heirs, so four kingdoms emerged from that of Clovis: Paris, [Orléans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orl%C3%A9ans "Orléans"), [Soissons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soissons "Soissons"), and [Rheims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reims "Reims"). The [last Merovingian kings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roi_fain%C3%A9ant "Roi fainéant") [lost power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_behind_the_throne "Power behind the throne") to their [mayors of the palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mayor_of_the_palace "Mayor of the palace") (head of household). One mayor of the palace, [Charles Martel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Martel "Charles Martel"), defeated an [Umayyad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") invasion at the [Battle of Tours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Tours "Battle of Tours") in 732.[^44] His son, [Pepin the Short](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pepin_the_Short "Pepin the Short"), seized the crown of Francia from the weakened Merovingians and founded the [Carolingian dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_dynasty "Carolingian dynasty"). Pepin's son [Charlemagne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlemagne "Charlemagne") reunited the Frankish kingdoms and built an empire across Western and [Central Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Europe "Central Europe").

Proclaimed [Holy Roman Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor "Holy Roman Emperor") by [Pope Leo III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Leo_III "Pope Leo III") and thus establishing the French government's longtime [historical association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Catholic_Church_in_France "History of the Catholic Church in France") with the [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church"),[^45] Charlemagne tried to revive the [Western Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire "Western Roman Empire") and its cultural grandeur. Charlemagne's son [Louis I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_the_Pious "Louis the Pious") kept the empire united, however in 843 it was divided between Louis' three sons into [East Francia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Francia "East Francia"), [Middle Francia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Francia "Middle Francia") and [West Francia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Francia "West Francia"). West Francia approximated the area occupied by modern France and was its precursor.[^46]

Throughout the 9th and 10th centuries, incessant [Viking raids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking_expansion "Viking expansion") severely weakened the Carolingian monarchy, accelerating the fragmentation of West Francia into a decentralised, [feudal society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism "Feudalism"). As central authority faltered, local nobility secured hereditary rights to their titles and lands, creating a system where the king's authority became more religious and symbolic than secular, frequently challenged by powerful territorial princes. After the [Battle of Hastings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings "Battle of Hastings") in 1066, [William the Conqueror](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_the_Conqueror "William the Conqueror") added "King of England" to his titles, becoming vassal and the equal of the king of France, creating recurring tensions.

### High and Late Middle Ages (10th–15th century)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Joan_of_Arc_miniature_graded.jpg/250px-Joan_of_Arc_miniature_graded.jpg)

Joan of Arc led the French Army to several important victories during the Hundred Years' War (1337–1453), which paved the way for the final victory.

The Carolingian dynasty ruled France until 987, when [Hugh Capet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hugh_Capet "Hugh Capet") was crowned [king of the Franks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs "List of French monarchs").[^47] His descendants unified the country through wars and inheritance. From 1190, the [Capetian rulers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capetian_dynasty "Capetian dynasty") began to be referred as "kings of France" rather than "kings of the Franks".[^48] Later kings expanded their directly possessed [*domaine royal*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crown_lands_of_France "Crown lands of France") to cover over half of modern France by the 15th century. Royal authority became more assertive, centred on a [hierarchically conceived society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm "Estates of the realm") distinguishing [nobility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_nobility "French nobility"), clergy, and [commoners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estates_General_\(France\) "Estates General (France)").

The nobility played a prominent role in [Crusades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusades "Crusades") to restore Christian access to the [Holy Land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Land "Holy Land"). French knights made up most reinforcements in the 200 years of the Crusades, in such a fashion that the Arabs referred to crusaders as *Franj*.[^49] French Crusaders imported French into the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant"), making [Old French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_French "Old French") the base of the *[lingua franca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lingua_franca "Lingua franca")* ("Frankish language") of the [Crusader states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusader_states "Crusader states").[^49] The [Albigensian Crusade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albigensian_Crusade "Albigensian Crusade") was launched in 1209 to eliminate the heretical [Cathars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catharism "Catharism") in the southwest of modern-day France.[^50]

From the 11th century, the [House of Plantagenet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Plantagenet "House of Plantagenet"), rulers of the [County of Anjou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/County_of_Anjou "County of Anjou"), established its dominion over the surrounding provinces of [Maine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maine_\(province\) "Maine (province)") and [Touraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Touraine "Touraine"), then built an "empire" from England to the [Pyrenees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrenees "Pyrenees"), covering half of modern France. Tensions between France and the [Plantagenet empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angevin_Empire "Angevin Empire") would last a hundred years, until [Philip II of France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_II_of_France "Philip II of France") conquered, between 1202 and 1214, most continental possessions of the empire, leaving England and [Aquitaine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquitaine "Aquitaine") to the Plantagenets.

[Charles IV the Fair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_IV_of_France "Charles IV of France") died without an heir in 1328.[^51] The crown passed to [Philip of Valois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_VI_of_France "Philip VI of France") rather than Edward Plantagenet, who had become [Edward III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_III "Edward III") of England the previous year. During the reign of Philip, the monarchy reached the height of its medieval power.[^51] Edward contested Philip's throne in 1337, beginning the intermittent [Hundred Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War "Hundred Years' War") between England and France.[^52] Boundaries changed, but landholdings inside France by English kings remained extensive for decades. With charismatic leaders such as [Joan of Arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joan_of_Arc "Joan of Arc"), French counterattacks won back most English continental territories. France was struck by the [Black Death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Death "Black Death"), from which half of the 17 million population died.[^53]

### Early modern period (15th century–1789)

The [French Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Renaissance "French Renaissance") saw cultural development and standardisation of French, which became the [official language of France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinance_of_Villers-Cotter%C3%AAts "Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts") and Europe's aristocracy. France became rivals of the [House of Habsburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Habsburg "House of Habsburg") during the [Italian Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Wars "Italian Wars"), which would dictate much of their later foreign policy until the mid-18th century. French explorers claimed lands in the Americas, paving expansion of the [French colonial empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_colonial_empire "French colonial empire"). The rise of Protestantism led France to a civil war known as the [French Wars of Religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Wars_of_Religion "French Wars of Religion").[^54] This forced [Huguenots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huguenots "Huguenots") to flee to Protestant regions such as the [British Isles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Isles "British Isles") and [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland"). The wars were ended by [Henri IV's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France "Henry IV of France") [Edict of Nantes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edict_of_Nantes "Edict of Nantes"), which granted some freedom of religion to the Huguenots. [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habsburg_Spain "Habsburg Spain") troops [^55] assisted the Catholics from 1589 to 1594 and invaded France in 1597. Spain and France returned to all-out war between 1635 and 1659. The [Franco-Spanish War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco-Spanish_War_\(1635%E2%80%931659\) "Franco-Spanish War (1635–1659)") cost France 300,000 casualties; total deaths on both sides were 200,000.[^56]

Under [Louis XIII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_XIII "Louis XIII"), [Cardinal Richelieu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_Richelieu "Cardinal Richelieu") promoted centralisation of the state and reinforced royal power. He destroyed castles of defiant lords and denounced the use of private armies. By the end of the 1620s, Richelieu established "the royal monopoly of force".[^57] France fought in the [Thirty Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War "Thirty Years' War"), supporting the Protestant side against the Habsburgs. From the 16th to the 19th century, France was responsible for about 10% of the [transatlantic slave trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade "Atlantic slave trade").[^58]

![Louis XIV of France standing in plate armour and blue sash facing left holding baton](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg/250px-Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg)

Louis XIV, the "Sun King", was the absolute monarch of France who made the country the leading power in Europe.

During [Louis XIV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_XIV "Louis XIV") 's minority, trouble known as [The Fronde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Fronde "The Fronde") occurred. This rebellion was driven by feudal lords and [sovereign courts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament "Parliament") as a reaction to the [royal absolute power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolutism_\(European_history\) "Absolutism (European history)"). The monarchy reached its peak during the 17th century and reign of Louis XIV, during which France further increased its influence.[^59] By turning lords into [courtiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Courtier "Courtier") at the [Palace of Versailles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palace_of_Versailles "Palace of Versailles"), his command of the military went unchallenged. The "Sun King" made France the leading European power. France became the [most populous European country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_France "Demographics of France") and had tremendous influence over European politics, economy, and culture. French became the most-used language in diplomacy, [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Languages_of_science "Languages of science"), and literature until the 20th century.[^60] France took control of territories in the Americas, Africa and Asia. In 1685, Louis XIV [revoked the Edict of Nantes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edict_of_Fontainebleau "Edict of Fontainebleau"), forcing thousands of Huguenots into exile and published the *[Code Noir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_Noir "Code Noir")* providing the legal framework for slavery and expelling Jews from French colonies.[^61]

Under the wars of [Louis XV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_XV "Louis XV"), France lost [New France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_France "New France") and most [Indian possessions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_India "French India") after its defeat in the [Seven Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War "Seven Years' War") (1756–1763). [Its European territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_France "Metropolitan France") kept growing, however, with acquisitions such as [Lorraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorraine "Lorraine") and [Corsica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corsica "Corsica"). Louis XV's weak rule, including the decadence of his court, discredited the monarchy, which in part paved the way for the [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution").[^62]

[Louis XVI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_XVI "Louis XVI") [supported America with money, fleets and armies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France_in_the_American_Revolutionary_War "France in the American Revolutionary War"), helping them win [independence from Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War "American Revolutionary War"). France gained revenge but verged on bankruptcy—a factor that contributed to the Revolution. Some of the [Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment") occurred in French intellectual circles, and scientific breakthroughs, such as the [naming of oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier "Antoine Lavoisier") and the first [hot air balloon carrying passengers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montgolfier_brothers "Montgolfier brothers"), were achieved by French scientists. French explorers took part in the [voyages of scientific exploration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_and_American_voyages_of_scientific_exploration "European and American voyages of scientific exploration") through maritime expeditions. Enlightenment philosophy, in which [reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalism "Rationalism") is advocated as the primary source of [legitimacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_legitimacy "Political legitimacy"), undermined the power of and support for the monarchy and was a factor in the Revolution.

### Revolutionary France (1789–1799)

![drawing of the Storming of the Bastille on 14 July 1789, smoke of gunfire enveloping stone castle](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Prise_de_la_Bastille.jpg/250px-Prise_de_la_Bastille.jpg)

The Storming of the Bastille on 14 July 1789 was the most emblematic event of the French Revolution.

The French Revolution was a period of political and societal change that began with the [Estates General of 1789](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estates_General_of_1789 "Estates General of 1789"), and ended with the [coup of 18 Brumaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_of_18_Brumaire "Coup of 18 Brumaire") in 1799 and the formation of the [French Consulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Consulate "French Consulate"). Many of its ideas are fundamental principles of [liberal democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_democracy "Liberal democracy"),[^63] while its values and institutions remain central to modern political discourse.[^64]

[Its causes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causes_of_the_French_Revolution "Causes of the French Revolution") were a combination of social, political and economic factors, which the *[Ancien Régime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime "Ancien Régime")* proved unable to manage. A financial crisis and social distress led in May 1789 to the [convocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convocation "Convocation") of the [Estates General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estates_General_of_1789 "Estates General of 1789"), which was converted into a [National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_\(French_Revolution\) "National Assembly (French Revolution)") in June. The [Storming of the Bastille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Storming_of_the_Bastille "Storming of the Bastille") on 14 July led to a series of radical measures by the Assembly, among them the [abolition of feudalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abolition_of_feudalism_in_France "Abolition of feudalism in France"), state control over the [Catholic Church in France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_France "Catholic Church in France"), and a [declaration of rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Rights_of_Man_and_of_the_Citizen "Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen").[^65]

The next three years were dominated by a struggle for political control, exacerbated by [economic depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_depression "Economic depression"). Military defeats following the outbreak of the [French Revolutionary Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars "French Revolutionary Wars") in April 1792 resulted in the [insurrection of 10 August 1792](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurrection_of_10_August_1792 "Insurrection of 10 August 1792"). The [monarchy was abolished](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation_of_the_abolition_of_the_monarchy "Proclamation of the abolition of the monarchy") and replaced by the [French First Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_First_Republic "French First Republic") in September, while [Louis XVI was executed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Execution_of_Louis_XVI "Execution of Louis XVI") in January 1793.[^66]

After another [revolt in June 1793](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurrection_of_31_May_%E2%80%93_2_June_1793 "Insurrection of 31 May – 2 June 1793"), the constitution was suspended and power passed from the [National Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Convention "National Convention") to the [Committee of Public Safety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Committee_of_Public_Safety "Committee of Public Safety"). About 16,000 people were executed in a [Reign of Terror](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reign_of_Terror "Reign of Terror"), which [ended in July 1794](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermidorian_Reaction "Thermidorian Reaction"). Weakened by external threats and internal opposition, the Republic was replaced in 1795 by the [Directory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Directory "French Directory"). Four years later in 1799, the [Consulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Consulate "French Consulate") seized power in [a coup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_of_18_Brumaire "Coup of 18 Brumaire") led by [Napoleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon "Napoleon").[^67]

### Napoleonic France (1799–1815)

![painting of Napoleon in 1806 standing with hand in vest attended by staff and Imperial guard regiment](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Jacques-Louis_David_-_The_Emperor_Napoleon_in_His_Study_at_the_Tuileries_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/250px-Jacques-Louis_David_-_The_Emperor_Napoleon_in_His_Study_at_the_Tuileries_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg)

Napoleon, Emperor of the French, built a vast empire across Europe. 68

[Napoleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon "Napoleon") became [First Consul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Consulate "French Consulate") in 1799 and later [Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Year_XII "Constitution of the Year XII") of the [French Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_French_Empire "First French Empire"). Changing sets of [European coalitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolutionary_and_Napoleonic_Wars "French Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars") declared [wars on Napoleon's empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars "Napoleonic Wars"). His armies conquered most of continental Europe with swift victories such as the [battles of Jena-Auerstadt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Jena%E2%80%93Auerstedt "Battle of Jena–Auerstedt") and [Austerlitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Austerlitz "Battle of Austerlitz"). Members of the [Bonaparte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Bonaparte "House of Bonaparte") family were appointed monarchs in some of the newly established kingdoms.[^69]

These victories led to the worldwide expansion of French revolutionary ideals and reforms, such as the [metric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_system "Metric system"), [Napoleonic Code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Code "Napoleonic Code") and [Declaration of the Rights of Man](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Rights_of_Man_and_of_the_Citizen "Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen"). In 1812 Napoleon [attacked Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_invasion_of_Russia "French invasion of Russia"), reaching [Moscow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow "Moscow"). Thereafter his army disintegrated through supply problems, disease, Russian attacks, and finally winter. After this catastrophic campaign and the ensuing [uprising of European monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Sixth_Coalition "War of the Sixth Coalition") against his rule, Napoleon was defeated. About a million Frenchmen [died during the Napoleonic Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars_casualties "Napoleonic Wars casualties"), including 306,000 killed.[^56] [^69] After his [brief return](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hundred_Days "Hundred Days") from exile, Napoleon was finally defeated in 1815 at the [Battle of Waterloo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo "Battle of Waterloo"), and the [Bourbon monarchy was restored](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bourbon_Restoration_in_France "Bourbon Restoration in France") with new constitutional limitations.

### Colonial empire

The discredited Bourbon dynasty was overthrown by the [July Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/July_Revolution "July Revolution") of 1830, which established the constitutional [July Monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/July_Monarchy "July Monarchy"); French troops began the [conquest of Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_conquest_of_Algeria "French conquest of Algeria"). Unrest led to the [French Revolution of 1848](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution_of_1848 "French Revolution of 1848") and the end of the July Monarchy. The abolition of slavery and the introduction of male universal suffrage was re-enacted in 1848. In 1852, president of the French Republic, [Louis-Napoléon Bonaparte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_III "Napoleon III"), Napoleon I's nephew, was proclaimed emperor of the [Second Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_French_Empire "Second French Empire"), as Napoleon III. He multiplied French interventions abroad, especially in [Crimea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crimean_War "Crimean War"), [Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_French_intervention_in_Mexico "Second French intervention in Mexico") and [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Italian_War_of_Independence "Second Italian War of Independence"). Napoleon III was unseated following defeat in the [Franco-Prussian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War "Franco-Prussian War") of 1870, and his regime was replaced by the [Third Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Third_Republic "French Third Republic"). By 1875, the French conquest of Algeria was complete, with approximately 825,000 Algerians killed from famine, disease, and violence.[^70] France suffered an estimated 10,000 killed and 35,000 wounded across all colonial campaigns.[^56] A few thousand died in Mexico or [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_conquest_of_Vietnam "French conquest of Vietnam"), but the vast majority perished in Algeria. Disease claimed an even heavier toll; one estimate puts total French and [Foreign Legion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Foreign_Legion "French Foreign Legion") deaths from battle and disease for the entire 19th century at 110,000.[^56]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/EmpireFrench.png/250px-EmpireFrench.png)

French colonial empires: First Second

France had [colonial possessions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_colonial_empire "French colonial empire") since the beginning of the 17th century, but in the 19th and 20th centuries its empire extended significantly and became [the second-largest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_largest_empires "List of largest empires") behind the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire").[^71] Including metropolitan France, the total area reached almost 13 million square kilometres in the 1920s and 1930s, 9% of the world's land. Known as the *[Belle Époque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belle_%C3%89poque "Belle Époque")*, the turn of the century was characterised by optimism, regional peace, economic prosperity and technological, scientific and cultural innovations. In 1905, [state secularism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secular_state "Secular state") was [officially established](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1905_French_law_on_the_Separation_of_the_Churches_and_the_State "1905 French law on the Separation of the Churches and the State").

### Early to mid-20th century (1914–1946)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/El_114_de_infanter%C3%ADa%2C_en_Par%C3%ADs%2C_el_14_de_julio_de_1917%2C_Le%C3%B3n_Gimpel.jpg/250px-El_114_de_infanter%C3%ADa%2C_en_Par%C3%ADs%2C_el_14_de_julio_de_1917%2C_Le%C3%B3n_Gimpel.jpg)

French Poilus posing with their war-torn flag in 1917, during World War I

France was [invaded by Germany and defended by Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_entry_into_World_War_I "French entry into World War I") at the start of [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") in August 1914. A rich industrial area in the north was occupied. France and the [Allies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I "Allies of World War I") emerged victorious against the [Central Powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Powers "Central Powers") at tremendous human cost. It left 1.4 million French soldiers dead, 4% of its population.[^72] [^73] In addition, France suffered 4,266,000 wounded.[^56] Interwar was marked by [intense international tensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Events_preceding_World_War_II_in_Europe "Events preceding World War II in Europe") and social reforms introduced by the [Popular Front government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_Front_\(France\) "Popular Front (France)") (e.g., [annual leave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annual_leave "Annual leave"), [eight-hour workdays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eight-hour_day "Eight-hour day"), [women in government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_government "Women in government")).

In 1940, France was [invaded and quickly defeated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_France "Battle of France") by [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany"). France was divided into a [German occupation zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_military_administration_in_occupied_France_during_World_War_II "German military administration in occupied France during World War II") in the north, an [Italian occupation zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_occupation_of_France "Italian occupation of France") and an unoccupied territory, the rest of France, which consisted of southern France and the French empire. The [Vichy government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vichy_France "Vichy France"), an authoritarian regime collaborating with Germany, ruled the unoccupied territory. [Free France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_France "Free France"), the government-in-exile led by [Charles de Gaulle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle "Charles de Gaulle"), was set up in London.[^74]

From 1942 to 1944, about 160,000 French citizens, including around [75,000 Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Holocaust_in_France "The Holocaust in France"),[^75] were deported to [death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extermination_camp "Extermination camp") and [concentration camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internment "Internment").[^76] On 6 June 1944, the Allies [invaded Normandy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Overlord "Operation Overlord"), and in August they [invaded Provence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Dragoon "Operation Dragoon"). The Allies and [French Resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Resistance "French Resistance") emerged victorious, and French sovereignty was restored with the [Provisional Government of the French Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_the_French_Republic "Provisional Government of the French Republic") (GPRF). This interim government, established by de Gaulle, continued to [wage war against Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Allied_invasion_of_Germany "Western Allied invasion of Germany") and to [purge collaborators from office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89puration_l%C3%A9gale "Épuration légale"). It made important reforms e.g. suffrage extended to women and the creation of a [social security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_security_in_France "Social security in France") system.

### 1946–present

![Charles de Gaulle seated in uniform looking left with folded arms](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/De_Gaulle-OWI.jpg/250px-De_Gaulle-OWI.jpg)

Charles de Gaulle, a hero of World War I, leader of the Free French during World War II, and President of France

A new constitution resulted in the [Fourth Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Fourth_Republic "French Fourth Republic") (1946–1958), which saw strong economic growth (*les [Trente Glorieuses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trente_Glorieuses "Trente Glorieuses")*). France was a founding member of [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") and attempted to [regain control of French Indochina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Indochina_War "First Indochina War"), but was defeated by the [Viet Minh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viet_Minh "Viet Minh") in 1954. France faced another [anti-colonialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-colonialist "Anti-colonialist") [conflict in Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algerian_War "Algerian War"), then part of France and home to over one million European settlers ([Pied-Noir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pied-Noir "Pied-Noir")). The French systematically used torture and repression, including extrajudicial killings to keep control.[^77] This conflict nearly led to a coup and civil war.[^78]

During the [May 1958 crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/May_1958_crisis_in_France "May 1958 crisis in France"), the weak Fourth Republic gave way to the [Fifth Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Fifth_Republic "French Fifth Republic"), which included a strengthened presidency.[^79] The war concluded with the [Évian Accords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89vian_Accords "Évian Accords") in 1962 which led to [Algerian independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1962_Algerian_independence_referendum "1962 Algerian independence referendum"), at a high price: between half a million and one million deaths and over 2 million internally-displaced Algerians.[^80] Around one million Pied-Noirs and [Harkis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harki "Harki") fled from Algeria to France.[^81] A vestige of the empire is the [French overseas departments and territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_France "Overseas France").

During the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), de Gaulle pursued a policy of "national independence" towards the [Western](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Bloc "Western Bloc") and [Eastern blocs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Bloc "Eastern Bloc"). He withdrew from NATO's military-integrated command (while remaining within the alliance), launched a [nuclear development programme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_de_dissuasion "Force de dissuasion") and made France the [fourth nuclear power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction "France and weapons of mass destruction"). He [restored](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89lys%C3%A9e_Treaty "Élysée Treaty") cordial [Franco-German relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France%E2%80%93Germany_relations "France–Germany relations") to create a European counterweight between American and Soviet spheres of influence. However, he opposed any development of a [supranational Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supranational_union "Supranational union"), favouring [sovereign nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state "Sovereign state"). The revolt of [May 1968](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/May_68 "May 68") had an enormous social impact; it was a watershed moment when a conservative moral ideal (religion, patriotism, respect for authority) shifted to a more liberal moral ideal (secularism, individualism, sexual revolution). Although the revolt was a political failure (the [Gaullist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaullism "Gaullism") party emerged stronger than before) it announced a split between the French and de Gaulle, who resigned.[^82]

In the post-Gaullist era, France remained one of the most developed [economies in the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of countries by GDP (nominal)") but faced crises that resulted in high unemployment rates and increasing public debt. In the late 20th and early 21st centuries, France has been at the forefront of the development of a supranational [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union"), notably by signing the [Maastricht Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maastricht_Treaty "Maastricht Treaty") in 1992, establishing the [eurozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurozone "Eurozone") in 1999 [^83] and signing the [Treaty of Lisbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon "Treaty of Lisbon") in 2007.[^84] France has fully reintegrated into NATO and since participated in most NATO-sponsored wars.[^85]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Overview_of_Calais_Jungle.jpg/250px-Overview_of_Calais_Jungle.jpg)

Calais Jungle

Since the 19th century, France has [received many immigrants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immigration_to_France "Immigration to France"), often male [foreign workers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_worker "Foreign worker") from European Catholic countries who generally returned home when not employed.[^86] During the 1970s France faced an economic crisis and allowed new immigrants (mostly from the [Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maghreb "Maghreb"), in northwest Africa) [^86] to permanently [settle in France with their families](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family_reunification "Family reunification") and acquire citizenship. It resulted in hundreds of thousands of Muslims living in subsidised public housing and suffering from high unemployment rates.[^87] The government had a policy of [assimilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_assimilation "Cultural assimilation") of immigrants, where they were expected to adhere to French values and norms.[^88] From the 2000s, France experienced continued immigration from Africa, the Middle East, and Asia. In addition, [Calais](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Migrants_around_Calais "Migrants around Calais") became a transit area for migrants.

Since the [1995 public transport bombings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1995_France_bombings "1995 France bombings"), France has been targeted by Islamist organisations, notably the [*Charlie Hebdo* attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/January_2015_%C3%8Ele-de-France_attacks "January 2015 Île-de-France attacks") in 2015 which provoked the [largest public rallies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republican_marches "Republican marches") in French history, gathering 4.4 million people,[^89] the [November 2015 Paris attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/November_2015_Paris_attacks "November 2015 Paris attacks") which resulted in 130 deaths, the deadliest attack on French soil since World War II [^90] and the deadliest in the European Union since the [Madrid train bombings in 2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2004_Madrid_train_bombings "2004 Madrid train bombings").[^91] [Opération Chammal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Op%C3%A9ration_Chammal "Opération Chammal"), France's military efforts to contain [ISIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq_and_the_Levant "Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant"), killed over 1,000 ISIS troops between 2014 and 2015.[^92]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Chamonix_valley_from_la_Fl%C3%A9g%C3%A8re%2C2010_07.JPG/250px-Chamonix_valley_from_la_Fl%C3%A9g%C3%A8re%2C2010_07.JPG)

Chamonix valley with the Mont Blanc at background, the highest mountain in the Alps and Western Europe on the border with Italy

The vast majority of France's territory and population is situated in Western Europe and is called [Metropolitan France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_France "Metropolitan France"). It is bordered by the [North Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Sea "North Sea") in the north, the [English Channel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Channel "English Channel") in the northwest, the [Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") in the west and the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") in the southeast. Its land borders consist of [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium "Belgium") and [Luxembourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luxembourg "Luxembourg") in the northeast, [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany") and [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland") in the east, [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy") and [Monaco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monaco "Monaco") in the southeast, and [Andorra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andorra "Andorra") and [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain") in the south and southwest. Except for the northeast, most of France's land borders are roughly delineated by natural boundaries and geographic features: to the south, the [Pyrenees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrenees "Pyrenees") and the [Alps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alps "Alps"); to the southeast, [Jura Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jura_Mountains "Jura Mountains"); and to the east, the [Rhine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhine "Rhine") river. Including [Overseas France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_France "Overseas France"), it also borders [Suriname](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suriname "Suriname") and [Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil "Brazil") through [French Guiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Guiana "French Guiana") in [South America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_America "South America"), and the [Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands "Kingdom of the Netherlands") through [Saint Martin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Martin_\(island\) "Saint Martin (island)") in the [Caribbean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caribbean "Caribbean").[^93] Metropolitan France includes various coastal islands, of which the largest is [Corsica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corsica "Corsica"). Metropolitan France is situated mostly between latitudes [41°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/41st_parallel_north "41st parallel north") and [51° N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/51st_parallel_north "51st parallel north"), and longitudes [6° W](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/6th_meridian_west "6th meridian west") and [10° E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10th_meridian_east "10th meridian east"), on the western edge of Europe, and thus lies within the northern [temperate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperate_climate "Temperate climate") zone. Its continental part covers about 1000 km from north to south and from east to west.

Metropolitan France covers 551,500 square kilometres (212,935 sq mi),[^94] the largest among [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") members.[^83] France's total land area, with its overseas departments and territories (excluding [Adélie Land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ad%C3%A9lie_Land "Adélie Land")), is 643,801 km <sup>2</sup> (248,573 sq mi),[^95] 0.45% of the total land area on Earth. France possesses a wide variety of landscapes, from coastal plains in the north and west to the mountain ranges of the Alps in the southeast, the [Massif Central](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massif_Central "Massif Central") in the south-central and [Pyrenees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrenees "Pyrenees") in the southwest. With numerous [overseas departments and territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_France "Overseas France") scattered across the planet, France possesses the second-largest [exclusive economic zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone "Exclusive economic zone") (EEZ) in the world, covering 11,035,000 km <sup>2</sup> (4,261,000 sq mi). Its EEZ covers approximately 8% of the total surface of all the EEZs of the world.

### Geology, topography and hydrography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/2013.07.05_roussillon_-_roque_anth%C3%A9ron_172.JPG/250px-2013.07.05_roussillon_-_roque_anth%C3%A9ron_172.JPG)

Geological formations near Roussillon, Vaucluse

Metropolitan France has a wide variety of topographic sets and natural landscapes. During the [Hercynian uplift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hercynian_uplift "Hercynian uplift") in the [Paleozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleozoic "Paleozoic") Era, the [Armorican Massif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armorican_Massif "Armorican Massif"), the Massif Central, the [Morvan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morvan "Morvan"), the [Vosges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vosges "Vosges") and [Ardennes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ardennes "Ardennes") ranges and the island of Corsica were formed. These massifs delineate several sedimentary basins such as the [Aquitaine Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquitaine_Basin "Aquitaine Basin") in the southwest and the [Paris Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Basin "Paris Basin") in the north. Various routes of natural passage, such as the [Rhône Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne_Valley "Rhône Valley"), allow easy communication. The Alpine, Pyrenean and Jura Mountains are much younger and have less eroded forms. At 4,810.45 metres (15,782 ft) [^96] above sea level, [Mont Blanc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mont_Blanc "Mont Blanc") in the Alps is the highest point in Western Europe. 60% of municipalities are classified as having moderate seismic risk.

The coastlines offer contrasting landscapes: mountain ranges along the [French Riviera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Riviera "French Riviera"), coastal cliffs such as the [Côte d'Albâtre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Alb%C3%A2tre "Côte d'Albâtre"), and wide sandy plains in the [Languedoc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Languedoc "Languedoc"). Corsica lies off the Mediterranean coast. France has an extensive river system consisting of the four major rivers: the [Seine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seine "Seine"), the [Loire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loire "Loire"), the [Garonne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garonne "Garonne"), the [Rhône](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne "Rhône") and their tributaries, whose combined catchment includes over 62% of the metropolitan territory. The Rhône divides the Massif Central from the Alps and flows into the Mediterranean Sea at the [Camargue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camargue "Camargue"). The Garonne meets the [Dordogne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dordogne_\(river\) "Dordogne (river)") just after [Bordeaux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bordeaux "Bordeaux"), forming the [Gironde estuary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gironde_estuary "Gironde estuary"), the largest estuary in Western Europe which after approximately 100 kilometres (62 mi) empties into the Atlantic Ocean.[^97] Other water courses drain towards the [Meuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meuse "Meuse") and Rhine along the northeastern borders. France has 11,000,000 km <sup>2</sup> (4,200,000 sq mi) of marine waters within three oceans under its jurisdiction, of which 97% are overseas.

### Environment

On June 28, 2019, in [Vérargues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V%C3%A9rargues "Vérargues"), the temperature reached 46 °C, setting a temperature record in Metropolitan France since records began.[^98] [Climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_France "Climate change in France") means that this kind of heatwave in France can be expected to occur 10 times more frequently than it would have done a century ago.[^99] [^100] France was one of the first countries to create an environment ministry, in 1971.[^101] France is ranked [19th by carbon dioxide emissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions "List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions") due to the country's heavy investment in [nuclear power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_France "Nuclear power in France") following the [1973 oil crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_oil_crisis "1973 oil crisis"),[^102] which now accounts for 75 per cent of its electricity production [^103] and results in less pollution.[^104] [^105] According to the 2020 [Environmental Performance Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_Performance_Index "Environmental Performance Index") conducted by [Yale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yale "Yale") and [Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbia_University "Columbia University"), France was the fifth most environmentally conscious country in the world.[^106] [^107] Like all European Union state members, France agreed to cut carbon emissions by at least 20% of 1990 levels by 2020.[^108] As of 2009, carbon dioxide emissions per capita are lower than that of China.[^109] The country was set to impose a [carbon tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_tax "Carbon tax") in 2009;[^110] however, the plan was abandoned due to fears of it burdening French businesses.[^111]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Roseli%C3%A8re_de_Pauillac_et_carrelets_sur_l%27estuaire_de_la_Gironde%2C_France.jpg/250px-Roseli%C3%A8re_de_Pauillac_et_carrelets_sur_l%27estuaire_de_la_Gironde%2C_France.jpg)

The Gironde estuary is classified as a marine nature park and is part of the Natura 2000 network. The left bank is part of the Médoc regional nature park. With a surface area of 635 km 2 (245 sq mi), it is the largest estuary in Europe.

Forests account for 31 per cent of the land area—the fourth-highest proportion in Europe—representing an increase of 7 per cent since 1990.[^112] [^113] [^114] The forests are some of the most diverse in Europe, comprising more than 140 species of trees.[^115] France had a 2018 [Forest Landscape Integrity Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_Landscape_Integrity_Index "Forest Landscape Integrity Index") mean score of 4.52/10, ranking it 123rd globally.[^116] There are nine [national parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_park "National park") [^117] and 46 [natural parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protected_area "Protected area").[^118] A regional nature park [^119] (French: *parc naturel régional* or PNR) is a public establishment in France between local authorities and the national government covering an inhabited rural area of outstanding beauty, to protect the scenery and heritage as well as setting up sustainable economic development in the area.[^120] [^121] As of 2019 there are 54 PNRs in France.[^122]

## Politics

France is a [representative democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_democracy "Representative democracy") organised as a unitary [semi-presidential republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-presidential_republic "Semi-presidential republic").[^123] Democratic traditions and values are deeply rooted in French culture, identity and politics.[^124] The [Constitution of the Fifth Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_France "Constitution of France") was approved by [referendum in 1958](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1958_French_constitutional_referendum "1958 French constitutional referendum"), establishing a framework consisting of executive, legislative and judicial branches.[^125] It sought to address the instability of the Third and Fourth Republics by combining elements of both the parliamentary and presidential systems, while greatly strengthening the authority of the executive relative to the legislature.[^124]
