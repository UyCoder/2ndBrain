The **United Arab Emirates** (**UAE**),[^2] also known simply as the **Emirates**,[^3] is a country in [West Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Asia "West Asia"), situated at the eastern end of the [Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula"). It is a [federal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_monarchy "Federal monarchy") semi-constitutional [monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy "Monarchy") made up of [seven emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirates_of_the_United_Arab_Emirates "Emirates of the United Arab Emirates"), with [Abu Dhabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Dhabi "Abu Dhabi") serving as its national capital. The UAE borders [Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman "Oman") to the east and northeast, and [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia") to the southwest. It shares [maritime borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maritime_boundary "Maritime boundary") with [Qatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar "Qatar") and [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran "Iran") in the [Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf"), and with Oman in the [Gulf of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman "Gulf of Oman"). In 2024, the UAE had an estimated population of over 11 million. [Dubai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dubai "Dubai") is the country's [largest city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_cities_in_the_United_Arab_Emirates "List of cities in the United Arab Emirates"). [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") is the [state religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_religion "State religion"). [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic") is the official language while [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language") remains the most spoken language and the language of business.[^6] [^18]

The present-day United Arab Emirates is located within the historical region of [Eastern Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Arabia "Eastern Arabia"), which was oriented to maritime trade and [seafaring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seafaring "Seafaring"). The [Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire") arrived in the region around 1500 and set up bases on the territory while waging [wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese%E2%80%93Safavid_wars "Portuguese–Safavid wars") against the [Persians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safavid_Iran "Safavid Iran"). After their expulsion in 1622 the [Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company "Dutch East India Company") controlled the straits and established global maritime dominance.[^19] By the 19th century, with [pearling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearling_industry "Pearling industry") becoming a major economic activity, [piracy became rampant in the gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piracy_in_the_Persian_Gulf "Piracy in the Persian Gulf"), prompting [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") intervention.[^20] [^21] Local [sheikhdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheikhdoms "Sheikhdoms") formed a pact with the United Kingdom to create the [Trucial States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trucial_States "Trucial States"), a [British protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_protectorate "British protectorate") that was effectively shielded from attempted [Saudi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Saudi_state "First Saudi state") and [Omani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omani_Empire "Omani Empire") [suzerainty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suzerainty "Suzerainty").[^22] The Trucial States remained under British influence until full independence as the United Arab Emirates in 1971. [Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zayed_bin_Sultan_Al_Nahyan "Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan"), ruler of Abu Dhabi and the country's first president (1971–2004), oversaw rapid development of the Emirates by investing revenues from newly found [oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_reserve "Oil reserve") into healthcare, education, and infrastructure.[^23]

The United Arab Emirates is considered a [middle power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_power "Middle power") in global affairs;[^24] Dubai serves as an international hub of finance, tourism, and commerce.[^25] [^26] Only 11% of the population are native [Emiratis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emiratis "Emiratis"); the vast majority of inhabitants are [expatriates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expatriates "Expatriates") and [migrant workers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Migrant_workers "Migrant workers"), most of whom are from [South Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Asia "South Asia").[^27] The UAE has the world's [seventh-largest oil reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_proven_oil_reserves "List of countries by proven oil reserves") and [seventh-largest natural gas reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_natural_gas_proven_reserves "List of countries by natural gas proven reserves").[^28] [^29] The UAE has the most diversified economy among the members of the [Gulf Cooperation Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Cooperation_Council "Gulf Cooperation Council") (GCC), having become less reliant on natural resources in the 21st century and increasingly focusing on tourism and business.[^30] The UAE is a member of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League"), [Organisation of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation"), [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement"), [World Trade Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Trade_Organization "World Trade Organization"), and [BRICS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BRICS "BRICS"); it is also a dialogue partner of the [Shanghai Cooperation Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Cooperation_Organisation "Shanghai Cooperation Organisation").

The [Federal Supreme Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Supreme_Council "Federal Supreme Council"), made up of the seven ruling [emirs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirs "Emirs"), is the highest state authority. It jointly appoints one member as [federal president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_the_United_Arab_Emirates "President of the United Arab Emirates"), who appoints a [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_prime_ministers_of_the_United_Arab_Emirates "List of prime ministers of the United Arab Emirates"), who forms and leads [the cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_Arab_Emirates "Cabinet of the United Arab Emirates").[^31] The UAE is an [authoritarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") state but generally liberal by regional standards.[^21] It ranks highly in several social indicators such as housing, [healthcare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Healthcare_in_the_United_Arab_Emirates "Healthcare in the United Arab Emirates"), [education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Education_in_the_United_Arab_Emirates "Education in the United Arab Emirates") and personal safety, as well as the highest regionally in the [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index").[^32] Human rights organisations consider the UAE substandard on human rights, ranking low in the [human freedom index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Freedom_Index "Human Freedom Index") due to reports of government critics being imprisoned and tortured, families harassed by the state security apparatus, and cases of [forced disappearances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forced_disappearance "Forced disappearance").[^33] Individual rights such as the freedoms of [assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_assembly "Freedom of assembly"), [association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_association "Freedom of association"), [expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_expression "Freedom of expression"), and the [freedom of the press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Press_Freedom_Index "Press Freedom Index") are severely repressed.[^34]

## Etymology

The United Arab Emirates is named after the seven emirates that formed a federation: the Emirate of [Abu Dhabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Dhabi "Abu Dhabi"), the Emirate of [Dubai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dubai "Dubai"), the Emirate of [Ajman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajman "Ajman"), the Emirate of [Sharjah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharjah "Sharjah"), the Emirate of [Ras Al Khaimah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ras_Al_Khaimah "Ras Al Khaimah"), the Emirate of [Umm Al Quwain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umm_Al_Quwain "Umm Al Quwain"), and the Emirate of [Fujairah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fujairah "Fujairah").[^35]

## History

### Antiquity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/C%C3%A1ntaro_%2826738115330%29.jpg/250px-C%C3%A1ntaro_%2826738115330%29.jpg)

2nd century BCE era jar found in Mleiha Archaeological site in Sharjah

Stone tools recovered reveal a settlement of people from Africa some 127,000 years ago and a stone tool used for butchering animals discovered on the Arabian coast suggests an even older habitation from 130,000 years ago.[^36] In time, lively trading links developed with civilisations in [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia"), Iran, and the [Harappan culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilisation "Indus Valley civilisation") of the Indus Valley. This contact persisted and became wider, probably motivated by the trade in copper from the [Hajar Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hajar_Mountains "Hajar Mountains"), which commenced around 3,000 BCE.[^37] Sumerian sources talk of the [Magan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magan_\(civilization\) "Magan (civilization)") civilisation, which has been identified as encompassing the modern UAE and Oman.[^38]

There are six periods of human settlement with distinctive behaviours in the region before Islam, which include the [Hafit period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hafit_period "Hafit period") from 3,200 to 2,600 BCE, the [Umm Al Nar culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umm_Al_Nar_culture "Umm Al Nar culture") from 2,600 to 2,000 BCE, and the [Wadi Suq culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wadi_Suq_culture "Wadi Suq culture") from 2,000 to 1,300 BCE. From 1,200 BCE to the advent of Islam in Eastern Arabia, through three distinctive [Iron Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age_in_the_United_Arab_Emirates "Iron Age in the United Arab Emirates") and the [Mleiha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mleiha "Mleiha") period, the area was variously occupied by the [Achaemenids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") and other forces, and saw the construction of fortified settlements and extensive husbandry thanks to the development of the [*falaj*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qanat "Qanat") irrigation system.

### Islam

The spread of [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") to the northeastern tip of the [Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula") is thought to have followed directly from a letter sent by the [Islamic prophet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_prophet "Islamic prophet") [Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad "Muhammad") to the rulers of [Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman "Oman") in 630 CE. This led to a group of rulers travelling to [Medina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medina "Medina"), converting to Islam, and subsequently driving a successful uprising against the unpopular [Sassanids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sasanian_Empire "Sasanian Empire"), who dominated the coast at the time.[^39] Following the death of Muhammad, the new Islamic communities south of the [Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf") threatened to disintegrate, with insurrections against the [Muslim leaders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_leaders "Muslim leaders"). Caliph [Abu Bakr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Bakr "Abu Bakr") sent an army from the capital Medina which completed its reconquest of the territory ([the Ridda Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ridda_wars "Ridda wars")) with the [Battle of Dibba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Dibba "Battle of Dibba") in which 10,000 lives are thought to have been lost.[^40] This assured the integrity of the [Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caliphate "Caliphate") and the unification of the Arabian Peninsula under the newly emerging [Rashidun Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashidun_Caliphate "Rashidun Caliphate").

In 637, Julfar—situated within the geographic boundaries of modern-day [Ras Al Khaimah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ras_Al_Khaimah "Ras Al Khaimah") —was an important port that was used as a staging post for the Islamic invasion of the [Sasanian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sasanian_Empire "Sasanian Empire").[^41] The area of the [Al Ain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Ain "Al Ain") / [Buraimi Oasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buraimi_Oasis "Buraimi Oasis") was known as Tu'am and was an important trading post for camel routes between the coast and the Arabian interior.[^42]

The earliest [Christian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity") site in the UAE was first discovered in the 1990s, an extensive monastic complex on what is now known as [Sir Bani Yas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sir_Bani_Yas "Sir Bani Yas") Island and which dates back to the seventh century. Thought to be [Nestorian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nestorianism "Nestorianism") and built in 600 CE, the church appears to have been abandoned peacefully in 750 CE.[^43] It forms a rare physical link to a legacy of Christianity, which is thought to have spread across the peninsula from 50 to 350 CE following trade routes. Certainly, by the fifth century, Oman had a bishop named John – the last bishop of Oman being Etienne, in 676 CE.[^44]

### Portuguese era

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Forte_de_doba.jpg/250px-Forte_de_doba.jpg)

A painting of the Portuguese Empire Doba Fortress in Dibba Al-Hisn in 1620

The harsh desert environment led to the emergence of the "versatile tribesman", nomadic groups who subsisted due to a variety of economic activities, including animal husbandry, agriculture, and hunting. The seasonal movements of these groups led not only to frequent clashes between groups but also to the establishment of seasonal and semi-seasonal settlements and centres. These formed tribal groupings whose names are still carried by modern Emiratis, including the [Bani Yas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bani_Yas "Bani Yas") and [Al Bu Falah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Nahyan_family "Al Nahyan family") of [Abu Dhabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Dhabi "Abu Dhabi"), Al Ain, [Liwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liwa_Oasis "Liwa Oasis"), and the west coast; the [Dhawahir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhawahir "Dhawahir"), Awamir, [Al Ali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Ali_\(tribe\) "Al Ali (tribe)"), and [Manasir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manasir_\(tribe\) "Manasir (tribe)") of the interior; the [Sharqiyin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharqiyin "Sharqiyin") of the east coast; and the [Qawasim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Qasimi "Al-Qasimi") to the north.[^45]

With the expansion of European [colonial empires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_empire "Colonial empire"), [Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire"), [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire"), and [Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_Empire "Dutch Empire") forces appeared in the Persian Gulf region. By the 18th century, the Bani Yas confederation was the dominant force in most of the area now known as Abu Dhabi,[^46] [^47] [^48] while the Northern [Al Qawasim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Qasimi "Al-Qasimi") (Al Qasimi) dominated maritime commerce. The Portuguese maintained an influence over the coastal settlements, building [forts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fortification "Fortification") in the wake of the bloody 16th-century conquests of coastal communities by [Albuquerque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque "Afonso de Albuquerque") and the Portuguese commanders who followed him – particularly on the east coast at [Muscat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscat "Muscat"), [Sohar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sohar "Sohar"), and [Khor Fakkan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khor_Fakkan "Khor Fakkan").[^49]

The southern coast of the Persian Gulf was known to the British as the " [Pirate Coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_United_Arab_Emirates "History of the United Arab Emirates") ",[^50] [^51] as boats of the Al Qawasim federation harassed British-flagged shipping from the 17th century into the 19th.[^52] The charge of piracy is disputed by modern Emirati historians, including the current ruler of Sharjah, [Sheikh Sultan Al Qasimi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan_bin_Muhammad_Al-Qasimi "Sultan bin Muhammad Al-Qasimi"), in his 1986 book *The Myth of Arab Piracy in the Gulf*.[^53]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Persian_Gulf_1507-1750.gif/250px-Persian_Gulf_1507-1750.gif)

Purple – Portuguese in the Persian Gulf in the 16th and 17th century. Main cities, ports, and routes.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Ras_Al_Khaimah_under_attack%2C_1809_01.jpg/250px-Ras_Al_Khaimah_under_attack%2C_1809_01.jpg)

A painting depicting the burning of the coastal town and port of Emirate of Ras Al Khaimah during the Persian Gulf campaign of 1809

British expeditions to protect their Indian trade routes led to campaigns against Ras Al Khaimah and other harbours along the coast, including the [Persian Gulf campaign of 1809](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf_campaign_of_1809 "Persian Gulf campaign of 1809") and the more successful [campaign of 1819](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf_campaign_of_1819 "Persian Gulf campaign of 1819"). The following year, Britain and a number of local rulers signed a [maritime truce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Maritime_Treaty_of_1820 "General Maritime Treaty of 1820"),[^54] giving rise to the term [Trucial States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trucial_States "Trucial States"), which came to define the status of the coastal emirates. A further treaty was signed in 1843 and in 1853, the [Perpetual Maritime Truce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpetual_Maritime_Truce "Perpetual Maritime Truce") was agreed. To this was added the 'Exclusive Agreements', signed in 1892, which made the Trucial States a British protectorate.[^55]

Under the 1892 treaty, the trucial sheikhs agreed not to dispose of any territory except to the British and not to enter into relationships with any foreign government other than the British without their consent. In return, the British promised to protect the Trucial Coast from all aggression by sea and to help in case of land attack. British maritime policing meant that pearling fleets could operate in relative security. However, the British prohibition of the [slave trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_slavery "History of slavery") meant an important source of income was lost to some sheikhs and merchants.[^56]

In 1869, the Qubaisat tribe settled at [Khor Al Adaid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khor_Al_Adaid "Khor Al Adaid") and tried to enlist the support of the Ottomans. Khor Al Adaid was claimed by Abu Dhabi at that time, a claim supported by the British. In 1906, the British Political Resident, [Percy Cox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percy_Cox "Percy Cox"), confirmed in writing to the ruler of Abu Dhabi, [Zayed bin Khalifa Al Nahyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zayed_bin_Khalifa_Al_Nahyan "Zayed bin Khalifa Al Nahyan") ('Zayed the Great'), that Khor Al Adaid belonged to his sheikhdom.[^57]

### British era and discovery of oil

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dhayah_Fort_showing_hilltop_location.jpg/250px-Dhayah_Fort_showing_hilltop_location.jpg)

Dhayah Fort at the hill top. In 1819 it was the last Al-Qasimi stronghold to fall in the Persian Gulf campaign of 1819. The fall of Dhayah was to pave the way for the signing of the General Maritime Treaty of 1820.

During the 19th and early 20th centuries, the [pearling industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearl_hunting "Pearl hunting") thrived, providing both income and employment to the people of the Persian Gulf.[^58] The [First World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") had a severe impact on the industry, but it was the [economic depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_depression "Economic depression") of the late 1920s and early 1930s, coupled with the invention of the [cultured pearl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultured_pearl "Cultured pearl"), that wiped out the trade. The remnants of the trade eventually faded away shortly after the [Second World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), when the newly independent [Government of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_India "Government of India") imposed heavy taxation on imported pearls. The decline of pearling resulted in extreme economic hardship in the Trucial States.[^59]

In 1922, the British government secured undertakings from the rulers of the Trucial States not to sign concessions with foreign companies without their consent. Aware of the potential for the development of natural resources such as oil, following finds in Persia (from 1908) and Mesopotamia (from 1927), a British-led oil company, the [Iraq Petroleum Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq_Petroleum_Company "Iraq Petroleum Company") (IPC), showed an interest in the region. The [Anglo-Persian Oil Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Persian_Oil_Company "Anglo-Persian Oil Company") (APOC, later to become [British Petroleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BP "BP"), or BP) had a 23.75% share in IPC. From 1935, onshore concessions to explore for oil were granted by local rulers, with APOC signing the first one on behalf of Petroleum Concessions Ltd (PCL), an associate company of IPC.[^60]

APOC was prevented from developing the region alone because of the restrictions of the [Red Line Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Line_Agreement "Red Line Agreement"), which required it to operate through IPC. A number of options between PCL and the trucial rulers were signed, providing useful revenue for communities experiencing poverty following the collapse of the pearl trade. However, the wealth of oil which the rulers could see from the revenues accruing to surrounding countries remained elusive. The first bore holes in Abu Dhabi were drilled by IPC's operating company, Petroleum Development (Trucial Coast) Ltd (PDTC) at Ras Sadr in 1950, with a 13,000-foot-deep (4,000-metre) bore hole taking a year to drill and turning out dry, at the tremendous cost at the time of £1 million.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Mid-20th_century_Dubai.JPG/250px-Mid-20th_century_Dubai.JPG)

Dubai in 1950: the area in this photo shows Bur Dubai in the foreground (centred on Al-Fahidi Fort ), Deira in middle-right on the other side of the creek, and Al Shindagha (left) and Al Ras (right) in the background across the creek, from Deira.

The British set up a development office that helped in some small developments in the emirates. The seven [sheikhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheikh "Sheikh") of the [emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirates_of_the_United_Arab_Emirates "Emirates of the United Arab Emirates") then decided to form a council to coordinate matters between them and took over the development office. In 1952, they formed the Trucial States Council,[^61] and appointed [Adi Al Bitar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adi_Bitar "Adi Bitar"), Dubai's [Sheikh Rashid bin Saeed Al Maktoum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashid_bin_Saeed_Al_Maktoum "Rashid bin Saeed Al Maktoum") 's legal advisor, as secretary general and legal advisor to the council. The council was terminated once the United Arab Emirates was formed.[^62] The tribal nature of society and the lack of definition of borders between emirates frequently led to disputes, settled either through mediation or, more rarely, force. The [Trucial Oman Scouts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trucial_Oman_Scouts "Trucial Oman Scouts") was a small military force used by the British to keep the peace.

In 1953, a subsidiary of [BP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BP "BP"), D'Arcy Exploration Ltd, obtained an offshore concession from the ruler of Abu Dhabi. BP joined with [Compagnie Française des Pétroles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_SE "Total SE") (later Total) to form operating companies, Abu Dhabi Marine Areas Ltd (ADMA) and Dubai Marine Areas Ltd (DUMA). A number of undersea oil surveys were carried out, including one led by the famous marine explorer [Jacques Cousteau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Cousteau "Jacques Cousteau").[^63] [^64]

In 1958, a floating platform rig was towed from [Hamburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamburg "Hamburg"), [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany"), and positioned over the [Umm Shaif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umm_Shaif_oil_field "Umm Shaif oil field") pearl bed, in Abu Dhabi waters, where drilling began. In March, it struck oil in the Upper Thamama rock formation. This was the first commercial discovery of the Trucial Coast, leading to the first exports of oil in 1962. ADMA made further offshore discoveries at Zakum and elsewhere, and other companies made commercial finds such as the [Fateh oilfield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fateh_Oil_Field "Fateh Oil Field") off Dubai and the Mubarak field off Sharjah (shared with Iran).[^65]

Meanwhile, onshore exploration was hindered by territorial disputes. In 1955, the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") represented Abu Dhabi and Oman in their dispute with Saudi Arabia over the [Buraimi Oasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Buraimi_Governorate "Al Buraimi Governorate").[^66] A 1974 agreement between Abu Dhabi and Saudi Arabia seemed to have settled the Abu Dhabi-Saudi [border dispute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_dispute "Territorial dispute"), but this has not been ratified.[^67] The UAE's border with Oman was ratified in 2008.[^68]

PDTC continued its onshore exploration away from the disputed area, drilling five more bore holes that were also dry. However, on 27 October 1960, the company discovered oil in commercial quantities at the Murban No. 3 well on the coast near Tarif.[^69] In 1962, PDTC became the [Abu Dhabi Petroleum Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Dhabi_Petroleum_Company "Abu Dhabi Petroleum Company"). As oil revenues increased, the ruler of Abu Dhabi, [Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zayed_bin_Sultan_Al_Nahyan "Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan"), undertook a massive construction program, building schools, housing, hospitals, and roads. When Dubai's oil exports commenced in 1969, Sheikh [Rashid bin Saeed Al Maktoum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashid_bin_Saeed_Al_Maktoum "Rashid bin Saeed Al Maktoum"), the ruler of Dubai, was able to invest the revenues from the limited reserves found to spark the diversification drive that would create the modern [global city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_city "Global city") of Dubai.[^23]

### Independence

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Flag-hoisting_at_the_Union_Declaration.jpg/250px-Flag-hoisting_at_the_Union_Declaration.jpg)

Historic photo depicting the first hoisting of the United Arab Emirates flag by the rulers of the emirates at the Union House in Dubai on 2 December 1971

By 1966, it had become clear that the British government could no longer afford to administer and protect the [Trucial States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trucial_States "Trucial States"), what is now the United Arab Emirates. British [Members of Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_of_Parliament_\(United_Kingdom\) "Member of Parliament (United Kingdom)") (MPs) debated the preparedness of the [Royal Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Navy "Royal Navy") to defend the [sheikhdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheikhdom "Sheikhdom"). On 24 January 1968, British Prime Minister [Harold Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Wilson "Harold Wilson") announced the government's decision, reaffirmed in March 1971 by Prime Minister [Edward Heath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Heath "Edward Heath"), to end the treaty relationships with the seven trucial sheikhdoms. Days after the announcement, the ruler of Abu Dhabi, Sheikh [Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zayed_bin_Sultan_Al_Nahyan "Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan"), fearing vulnerability, tried to persuade the British to honour the protection treaties by offering to pay the full costs of keeping the [British Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Armed_Forces "British Armed Forces") in the Emirates. The British [Conservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_Party_\(UK\) "Conservative Party (UK)") government rejected the offer.[^70] After [Labour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labour_Party_\(UK\) "Labour Party (UK)") MP [Goronwy Roberts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goronwy_Roberts,_Baron_Goronwy-Roberts "Goronwy Roberts, Baron Goronwy-Roberts") informed Sheikh Zayed of the news of British withdrawal, the nine Persian Gulf sheikhdoms attempted to form a union of Arab emirates, but by mid-1971 they were still unable to agree on terms of union even though the British treaty relationship was to expire in December of that year.[^71]

Fears of vulnerability were realised the day before independence. An Iranian destroyer group broke formation from an exercise in the lower Gulf, sailing to the [Tunb islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_and_Lesser_Tunbs "Greater and Lesser Tunbs"). On 30 November 1971 the islands were [taken by force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seizure_of_Abu_Musa_and_the_Greater_and_Lesser_Tunbs "Seizure of Abu Musa and the Greater and Lesser Tunbs"), civilians and Arab defenders alike allowed to flee. Two British warships, the aircraft carrier [HMS Eagle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HMS_Eagle_\(R05\) "HMS Eagle (R05)") and the [HMS Albion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HMS_Albion_\(R07\) "HMS Albion (R07)"), stood idle during the course of the invasion.[^72] A destroyer group approached the island of [Abu Musa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Musa "Abu Musa") as well. But there, [Sheikh Khalid bin Muhammad Al Qasimi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalid_bin_Muhammad_Al_Qasimi "Khalid bin Muhammad Al Qasimi") had already negotiated with the Iranian shah, and the island was quickly leased to Iran for $3 million a year. Meanwhile, Saudi Arabia laid claim to swathes of Abu Dhabi.[^73] It was not until 1974 that a [border agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Jeddah_\(1974\) "Treaty of Jeddah (1974)") was signed with Saudi Arabia, formally demarcating the frontiers between the UAE and Saudi Arabia. The UAE's sense of threat from Iran influenced its financial support for [Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ba%27athist_Iraq "Ba'athist Iraq") during the [Iran–Iraq War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War "Iran–Iraq War").[^74]

Originally intended to be part of the proposed Federation of Arab Emirates, Bahrain became independent in August, and Qatar in September 1971. When the British-Trucial Sheikhdoms treaty expired on 1 December 1971, both emirates became fully independent.[^75] On 2 December 1971, six of the emirates (Abu Dhabi, Ajman, Dubai, Fujairah, Sharjah, and Umm Al Quwain) agreed to enter into a union named the United Arab Emirates. [Ras al-Khaimah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Ras_Al_Khaimah "Emirate of Ras Al Khaimah") joined later, on 10 January 1972.[^76] [^77] In February 1972, the [Federal National Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_National_Council "Federal National Council") (FNC) was created; it was a 40-member consultative body appointed by the seven rulers. The UAE joined the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League") on 6 December 1971 and the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") on 9 December.[^78] It was a founding member of the [Gulf Cooperation Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Cooperation_Council "Gulf Cooperation Council") in May 1981, with Abu Dhabi hosting the first [GCC summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Cooperation_Council#History_and_founding "Gulf Cooperation Council").

### Post-independence period

The founding fathers of the United Arab Emirates were: [Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zayed_bin_Sultan_Al_Nahyan "Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan") of [Abu Dhabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Dhabi "Abu Dhabi"); [Rashid bin Saeed Al Maktoum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashid_bin_Saeed_Al_Maktoum "Rashid bin Saeed Al Maktoum") of [Dubai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dubai "Dubai"); [Khalid bin Muhammad Al Qasimi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalid_bin_Muhammad_Al_Qasimi "Khalid bin Muhammad Al Qasimi") of [Sharjah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharjah "Sharjah"); [Rashid bin Humaid Al Nuaimi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashid_bin_Humaid_Al_Nuaimi_III "Rashid bin Humaid Al Nuaimi III") of [Ajman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajman "Ajman"); [Ahmad bin Rashid Al Mualla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmad_bin_Rashid_Al_Mualla "Ahmad bin Rashid Al Mualla") of [Umm Al Quwain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umm_Al_Quwain "Umm Al Quwain"); [Saqr bin Mohammed Al Qasimi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saqr_bin_Mohammed_Al_Qasimi "Saqr bin Mohammed Al Qasimi") of [Ras Al Khaimah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ras_Al_Khaimah "Ras Al Khaimah") and [Mohammed bin Hamad Al Sharqi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_bin_Hamad_Al_Sharqi "Mohammed bin Hamad Al Sharqi") of [Fujairah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fujairah "Fujairah").

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Burj_Khalifa_%28worlds_tallest_building%29_and_the_Dubai_skyline_%2825781049892%29.jpg/250px-Burj_Khalifa_%28worlds_tallest_building%29_and_the_Dubai_skyline_%2825781049892%29.jpg)

Skyline of Dubai

The UAE supported military operations by the US and other [coalition states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalition_of_the_Gulf_War "Coalition of the Gulf War") engaged in the [Gulf War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_War "Gulf War") against [Saddam Hussein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saddam_Hussein "Saddam Hussein") in [Ba'athist Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ba%27athist_Iraq "Ba'athist Iraq") (1991), as well as operations supporting the global [war on terror](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_on_terror "War on terror") for the [Horn of Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horn_of_Africa "Horn of Africa") at [Al Dhafra Air Base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Dhafra_Air_Base "Al Dhafra Air Base") located outside of Abu Dhabi. The air base also supported Allied operations during the 1991 Persian Gulf War and [Operation Northern Watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Northern_Watch "Operation Northern Watch"). The country had already signed a [military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military "Military") defence agreement with the U.S. in 1994 and one with France in 1995.[^79] [^80] In January 2008, France and the UAE signed a deal allowing France to set up a permanent military base in the emirate of Abu Dhabi.[^81] The UAE joined international military operations in Libya in March 2011.

On 2 November 2004, the UAE's first president, Sheikh [Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zayed_bin_Sultan_Al_Nahyan "Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan"), died. Sheikh [Khalifa bin Zayed Al Nahyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalifa_bin_Zayed_Al_Nahyan "Khalifa bin Zayed Al Nahyan") was elected as the [president of the UAE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_the_United_Arab_Emirates "President of the United Arab Emirates"). Sheikh [Mohammed bin Zayed Al Nahyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_bin_Zayed_Al_Nahyan "Mohammed bin Zayed Al Nahyan") succeeded Sheikh Khalifa as crown prince of Abu Dhabi.[^82] In January 2006, Sheikh [Maktoum bin Rashid Al Maktoum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maktoum_bin_Rashid_Al_Maktoum "Maktoum bin Rashid Al Maktoum"), the prime minister of the UAE and the ruler of Dubai, died, and Sheikh [Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammed_bin_Rashid_Al_Maktoum "Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum") assumed both roles.

The first ever national elections were held on 16 December 2006. A number of voters chose half of the members of the [Federal National Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_National_Council "Federal National Council"). The UAE has largely escaped the [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring"), which other countries have experienced; however, 60 Emirati activists from [Al Islah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Islah_\(United_Arab_Emirates\) "Al Islah (United Arab Emirates)") were apprehended for an alleged coup attempt and the attempt of the establishment of an [Islamist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamism "Islamism") state in the UAE.[^83] [^84] [^85] Mindful of the protests in nearby Bahrain, in November 2012 the UAE outlawed online mockery of its government or attempts to organise public protests through social media.[^23]

On 29 January 2020, the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic") was confirmed to have [reached the UAE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_the_United_Arab_Emirates "COVID-19 pandemic in the United Arab Emirates").[^86] Two months later, in March, the government announced the closure of shopping malls, schools, and places of worship, in addition to imposing a 24-hour curfew, and suspending all [Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirates_\(airline\) "Emirates (airline)") passenger flights.[^87] [^88] This resulted in a major economic downturn, which eventually led to the merger of more than 50% of the UAE's [federal agencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category:United_Arab_Emirates_federal_entities "Category:United Arab Emirates federal entities").[^89]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/President_Joe_Biden_stands_with_leaders_of_the_GCC_countries%2C_Egypt%2C_Iraq%2C_and_Jordan.jpg/250px-President_Joe_Biden_stands_with_leaders_of_the_GCC_countries%2C_Egypt%2C_Iraq%2C_and_Jordan.jpg)

UAE's President Mohamed bin Zayed Al Nahyan and other leaders at the GCC +3 summit in Jeddah, 16 July 2022

On 29 August 2020, the UAE established normal diplomatic relations with [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel") and with the help of the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), they signed the [Abraham Accords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Accords "Abraham Accords") with [Bahrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bahrain "Bahrain").[^90]

On 9 February 2021, the UAE achieved a historic milestone when its probe, named *[Hope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirates_Mars_Mission "Emirates Mars Mission")*, successfully reached [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars") 's orbit. The UAE became the first country in the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world") to reach Mars, the fifth country to successfully reach Mars, and the second country, after an [Indian probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_Orbiter_Mission "Mars Orbiter Mission"), to orbit Mars on its maiden attempt.

On 14 May 2022, Sheikh [Mohamed bin Zayed Al Nahyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohamed_bin_Zayed_Al_Nahyan "Mohamed bin Zayed Al Nahyan") was elected as the UAE's new president after the death of [Sheikh Khalifa bin Zayed Al Nahyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalifa_bin_Zayed_Al_Nahyan "Khalifa bin Zayed Al Nahyan").[^91]

Following the beginning of coordinated [U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Iran_war "2026 Iran war") on 28 February 2026, the UAE faced a series of retaliatory [drone and missile attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Iranian_strikes_on_the_United_Arab_Emirates "2026 Iranian strikes on the United Arab Emirates") from Iran, whose [Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Revolutionary_Guard_Corps "Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps") targeted both U.S. military assets and critical Emirati economic infrastructure.[^92] The UAE is strengthening security cooperation with Israel.[^93] At the start of the Iran war, Israel sent its [Iron Dome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Dome "Iron Dome") missile-defence system and military personnel to operate it to the UAE, the first time the technology has been sent to another country.[^94] *[The Wall Street Journal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal "The Wall Street Journal")* reported citing people familiar with the matter that the UAE had been discreetly striking Iran throughout the war, including a strike on [Lavan Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lavan_Island "Lavan Island") in early April, thus making it an active belligerent.[^95]

The UAE announced its decision to quit OPEC on 28 April 2026 effective 1 May 2026, partially to allow increased oil production.[^96] [^93] On 2 May, the UAE and [Qatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar "Qatar") signed a new free trade zone agreement with [South Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Korea "South Korea").[^97]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Satellite_image_of_United_Arab_Emirates_in_October.jpg/250px-Satellite_image_of_United_Arab_Emirates_in_October.jpg)

Satellite image of United Arab Emirates

The United Arab Emirates is situated in the [Middle East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_East "Middle East"), bordering the [Gulf of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman "Gulf of Oman") and the [Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf"), between [Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman "Oman") and [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia"); it is in a strategic location slightly south of the [Strait of Hormuz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Hormuz "Strait of Hormuz"), a vital transit point for world [crude oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum").[^98]

The UAE lies between 22°30' and 26°10' north latitude and between 51° and 56°25′ east longitude. It shares a 530-kilometre (330 mi) border with Saudi Arabia on the west, south, and southeast, and a 450-kilometre (280 mi) border with Oman on the southeast and northeast. The land border with Qatar in the [Khor Al Adaid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khor_Al_Adaid "Khor Al Adaid") area is about nineteen kilometres (12 miles) in the northwest; however, it is a source of [ongoing dispute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia_%E2%80%93_United_Arab_Emirates_border_dispute "Saudi Arabia – United Arab Emirates border dispute").[^99] Following Britain's military departure from the UAE in 1971, and its establishment as a new state, the UAE laid claim to Iranian-occupied islands of Abu Musa and the Greater and the Lesser Tunbs, when Iran captured them during the British rule, resulting in disputes with Iran that remain unresolved.[^100] The UAE also disputes claim on other islands against the neighbouring state of Qatar.[^101] The largest emirate, [Abu Dhabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Dhabi_\(emirate\) "Abu Dhabi (emirate)"), accounts for 87% of the UAE's total area,[^102] 67,340 square kilometres (26,000 sq mi).[^103] The smallest emirate, [Ajman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Ajman "Emirate of Ajman"), encompasses only 259 km <sup>2</sup> (100 sq mi).[^104]

The UAE coast stretches for nearly 650 km (404 mi) along the southern shore of the [Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf"), briefly interrupted by an isolated outcrop of the Sultanate of Oman. Six of the emirates are situated along the Persian Gulf, and the seventh, Fujairah, is on the eastern coast of the peninsula with direct access to the Gulf of Oman.[^105] Most of the coast consists of [salt pans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_pan_\(geology\) "Salt pan (geology)") that extend 8–10 km (5.0–6.2 mi) inland.[^106] The largest [natural harbour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harbor "Harbor") is at Dubai, although other ports have been dredged at Abu Dhabi, Sharjah, and elsewhere.[^107] Numerous islands are found in the Persian Gulf, and the ownership of some of them has been the subject of international disputes with both [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran "Iran") and [Qatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar "Qatar"). The smaller islands, as well as many [coral reefs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coral_reef "Coral reef") and shifting sandbars, are a menace to navigation. Strong tides and occasional windstorms further complicate ship movements near the shore. The UAE also has a stretch of the [Al Bāţinah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Batinah_Region "Al Batinah Region") coast of the Gulf of Oman. The [Musandam Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musandam_Governorate "Musandam Governorate"), the very tip of Arabia by the Strait of Hormuz, and [Madha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madha "Madha") are [exclaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enclave_and_exclave "Enclave and exclave") of Oman separated by the UAE.[^108]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/View_from_Jebel_Jais_-_panoramio.jpg/250px-View_from_Jebel_Jais_-_panoramio.jpg)

Roads leading to Jebel Jais, the highest mountain in the UAE (1,892 m), in Ras Al Khaimah

South and west of Abu Dhabi, vast, rolling [sand dunes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dune "Dune") merge into the [Rub al-Khali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rub_al-Khali "Rub al-Khali") (Empty Quarter) of Saudi Arabia.[^109] The desert area of Abu Dhabi includes two important oases with adequate underground water for permanent settlements and cultivation. The extensive [Liwa Oasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liwa_Oasis "Liwa Oasis") is in the south near the undefined border with Saudi Arabia. About 100 km (62 mi) to the northeast of Liwa is the [Al-Buraimi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Buraimi_Governorate "Al Buraimi Governorate") oasis, which extends on both sides of the Abu Dhabi-Oman border. [Lake Zakher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Zakher "Lake Zakher") in Al Ain is a [human-made lake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man-made_lake "Man-made lake") near the border with Oman that was created from treated waste water.[^110]

Prior to withdrawing from the area in 1971, Britain delineated the internal borders among the seven emirates in order to preempt territorial disputes that might hamper formation of the federation. In general, the rulers of the emirates accepted the British interventions, but in the case of boundary disputes between Abu Dhabi and Dubai, and also between Dubai and Sharjah, conflicting claims were not resolved until after the UAE became independent. The most complicated borders were in the [Western Hajar Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Hajar_Mountains "Western Hajar Mountains"), where five of the emirates contested jurisdiction over more than a dozen enclaves.

### Biodiversity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/1501200713074_Acacia_tortilis.jpg/250px-1501200713074_Acacia_tortilis.jpg)

Acacia trees growing in desert suburbs near Fujairah

The UAE contains the following terrestrial ecoregions: [Al Hajar montane woodlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Hajar_montane_woodlands "Al Hajar montane woodlands") and shrublands, [Gulf of Oman desert and semi-desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman_desert_and_semi-desert "Gulf of Oman desert and semi-desert"), and Al-Hajar foothill [xeric woodlands and shrublands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deserts_and_xeric_shrublands "Deserts and xeric shrublands").[^111]

The oases grow [date palms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenix_dactylifera "Phoenix dactylifera"), [acacia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acacia "Acacia"), and [eucalyptus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eucalyptus "Eucalyptus") trees. In the desert, the flora is very sparse and consists of [grasses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grasses "Grasses") and thorn bushes. The indigenous fauna had come close to extinction because of intensive hunting, which has led to a conservation program on [Sir Bani Yas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sir_Bani_Yas "Sir Bani Yas") Island initiated by Sheikh Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan in the 1970s, resulting in the survival of, for example, [Arabian Oryx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Oryx "Arabian Oryx"), [Arabian camel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_camel "Arabian camel"), and [leopards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leopard "Leopard"). [Coastal fish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastal_fish "Coastal fish") and [mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mammals "Mammals") consist mainly of [mackerel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mackerel "Mackerel"), [perch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estuary_perch "Estuary perch"), and [tuna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuna "Tuna"), as well as [sharks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shark "Shark") and whales.

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Water_stress%2C_top_countries_%282022%29.svg/250px-Water_stress%2C_top_countries_%282022%29.svg.png)

United Arab Emirates was the second most water stressed country in the world in 2022. 112

The climate of the UAE is subtropical-arid with hot summers and warm winters. The climate is categorised as desert climate. The hottest months are July and August, when average maximum temperatures reach above 45 °C (113 °F) on the [coastal plain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastal_plain "Coastal plain"). In the Hajar Mountains, temperatures are considerably lower, a result of increased elevation.[^113] Average minimum temperatures in January and February are between 10 and 14 °C (50 and 57 °F).[^114] During the late summer months, a humid southeastern wind known as Sharqi (i.e. "Easterner") makes the coastal region especially unpleasant. The average annual rainfall in the coastal area is less than 120 mm (4.7 in), but in some mountainous areas annual rainfall often reaches 350 mm (13.8 in). Rain in the coastal region falls in short, torrential bursts during the winter months, sometimes resulting in floods in ordinarily dry [wadi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wadi "Wadi") beds.[^115] The region is prone to occasional, violent [dust storms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dust_storm "Dust storm"), which can severely reduce visibility.

On 28 December 2004, snow was recorded in the UAE for the first time, in the [Jebel Jais](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jebel_Jais "Jebel Jais") mountain cluster in Ras al-Khaimah.[^116] A few years later, there were more sightings of snow and hail.[^117] [^118] The Jebel Jais mountain cluster has experienced snow only twice since records began.[^119]
