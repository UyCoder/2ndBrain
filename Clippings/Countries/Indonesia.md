**Indonesia**,[^2] officially the **Republic of Indonesia**,[^3] is a country in [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia") and [Oceania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceania "Oceania"), between the [Indian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean") and [Pacific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Ocean "Pacific Ocean") oceans. Comprising over [17,000 islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_islands_of_Indonesia "List of islands of Indonesia"), including [Sumatra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumatra "Sumatra"), [Java](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Java "Java"), [Sulawesi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulawesi "Sulawesi"), and parts of [Borneo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borneo "Borneo") and [New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Guinea "New Guinea"), Indonesia is the world's largest [archipelagic state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archipelagic_state "Archipelagic state") and the [14th-largest country by area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_area "List of countries and dependencies by area"), at 1,904,569 square kilometres (735,358 square miles). Indonesia has significant areas of wilderness that support one of the [world's highest levels of biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megadiverse_countries "Megadiverse countries"). It shares [land borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Template:Borders_of_Indonesia "Template:Borders of Indonesia") with [Papua New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea "Papua New Guinea"), [Timor-Leste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timor-Leste "Timor-Leste"), and [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia"), as well as [maritime borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maritime_border "Maritime border") with seven other countries, including [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia"), [Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore "Singapore"), and the [Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines "Philippines").

The [Indonesian archipelago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nusantara_\(term\) "Nusantara (term)") has been inhabited since prehistoric times, with early human presence evidenced by fossils of *[Homo erectus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_erectus "Homo erectus")* and *[Homo sapiens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_sapiens "Homo sapiens")*, and megalithic sites. By the early second millennium, it had become a crossroads for international [trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade "Trade") linking East and South Asia. Over the centuries, external influences—including [Hinduism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism_in_Indonesia "Hinduism in Indonesia"), [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism_in_Indonesia "Buddhism in Indonesia") and later [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Indonesia "Islam in Indonesia") —were absorbed into local societies, which introduced lasting cultural and religious influences. European powers later competed to monopolise trade in the Spice Islands of [Maluku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maluku_Islands "Maluku Islands") during the [Age of Discovery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Discovery "Age of Discovery"), followed by three and a half centuries of [Dutch colonial rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies#History "Dutch East Indies"), before Indonesia [proclaimed its independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation_of_Indonesian_Independence "Proclamation of Indonesian Independence") in the [aftermath of World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aftermath_of_World_War_II "Aftermath of World War II").

Since independence, Indonesia has experienced [separatist conflicts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separatism "Separatism"), corruption, political upheaval and natural disasters, alongside [democratisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-Suharto_era_in_Indonesia "Post-Suharto era in Indonesia") and rapid economic growth. The country today is a [presidential republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_system "Presidential system") with an elected [legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Consultative_Assembly "People's Consultative Assembly") and consists of [38 provinces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provinces_of_Indonesia "Provinces of Indonesia"), some of which enjoy [greater autonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomous_administrative_division "Autonomous administrative division") than others. Home to over 280 million people, Indonesia [ranks fourth in the world by population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_population "List of countries and dependencies by population") and has the largest [Muslim population of any country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_by_country "Islam by country"). More than half of Indonesians live on [Java](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Java "Java"), the [most heavily populated island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_islands_by_population "List of islands by population") in the world, while the capital [Jakarta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakarta "Jakarta") is the world's [most populous city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_largest_cities "List of largest cities").

Indonesian society comprises hundreds of [ethnic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Indonesia "Ethnic groups in Indonesia") and [linguistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Languages_of_Indonesia "Languages of Indonesia") groups, with [Javanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Javanese_people "Javanese people") forming the largest. National identity is unified under the motto *[Bhinneka Tunggal Ika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhinneka_Tunggal_Ika "Bhinneka Tunggal Ika")*, reflected by a [national language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesian_language "Indonesian language") alongside cultural and religious pluralism. A [newly industrialised country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newly_industrialised_country "Newly industrialised country"), Indonesia has the largest [national economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Indonesia "Economy of Indonesia") in Southeast Asia by GDP. The country is active in regional and global affairs as a [middle power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_power "Middle power") and is a member of major multilateral organisations, including the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), [G20](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G20 "G20"), the [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement"), [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN"), and the [Organisation of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation").

## Etymology

The name *Indonesia* is usually explained as a compound of the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") words *[Indos](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Indo- "wikt:Indo-")* (Ἰνδός) and [nesos](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BD%E1%BF%86%CF%83%CE%BF%CF%82 "wikt:νῆσος") (νῆσος), together meaning 'Indian islands'.[^26] The term dates to the 19th century, well before the formation of independent Indonesia. In 1850, [George Windsor Earl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Windsor_Earl "George Windsor Earl"), an English [ethnologist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnology "Ethnology"), proposed the terms *Indunesians* —and his preferred term, *Malayunesians* —for the inhabitants of the "Indian Archipelago or [Malay Archipelago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malay_Archipelago "Malay Archipelago") ".[^27] [^28] In the same publication, [James Richardson Logan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Richardson_Logan "James Richardson Logan") used *Indonesia* as a geographical term for the *Indian Archipelago*.[^29] [^30] Dutch academics writing in East Indies publications were reluctant to adopt *Indonesia*. They preferred *Malay Archipelago* ([Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_language "Dutch language"): *Maleische Archipel*); the *[Netherlands East Indies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies "Dutch East Indies")* (*Nederlandsch Oost Indië*), popularly *Indië*; *the East* (*de Oost*); and *Insulinde*.[^31]

After 1900, *Indonesia* became more common in academic circles outside the Netherlands, and native nationalist groups adopted it for political expression.[^31] [Adolf Bastian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Bastian "Adolf Bastian") of the University of Berlin popularised the name through his book *Indonesien oder die Inseln des Malayischen Archipels, 1884–1894*. Among indigenous figures, [Soewardi Soerjaningrat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ki_Hajar_Dewantara "Ki Hajar Dewantara") was an early promoter of the name; in November 1918, he established the *Indonesisch Persbureau* in The Hague, a press bureau that used *Indonesia* in its name.[^28]

## History

### Prehistory, early states, and Islamisation

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Lubang_Jeriji_Sal%C3%A9h_cave_painting_of_Bull.jpg/250px-Lubang_Jeriji_Sal%C3%A9h_cave_painting_of_Bull.jpg)

One of the oldest known figurative paintings, a depiction of a bull, was discovered in the Lubang Jeriji Saléh cave and dated to between 40,000 and 44,000 years ago.

The Indonesian archipelago has been inhabited since prehistoric times. Fossils of *[Homo erectus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_erectus "Homo erectus")* (" [Java Man](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Java_Man "Java Man") ") date back between 2 million and 500,000 BCE,[^32] [^33] [^34] [^35] while *[Homo sapiens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_sapiens "Homo sapiens")* arrived around 50,000 BCE.[^36] [^37] Archaeological discoveries include [cave paintings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cave_painting "Cave painting") in Sulawesi, where narrative rock art has been dated to at least 51,200 years ago.[^38] [^39] Later megalithic traditions appeared in several regions, including Java, Sumatra, Sulawesi, Sumba, Flores, and Nias, where stone monuments were used for burial, ancestor veneration, commemoration, and ritual life.[^40]

Beginning several millennia BCE, peoples of the [Austronesian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austronesian_peoples "Austronesian peoples") language group migrated from [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") into island Southeast Asia,[^41] [^42] and related languages spread across much of the archipelago.[^43] Rice cultivation and village life developed unevenly across the islands, while early trade in the last centuries BCE and the early centuries CE connected parts of the archipelago with South and East Asia.[^44] [^45] Foreign merchants, goods, technologies, writing systems, and religious ideas entered local societies through these routes, helping some coastal settlements grow into ports and centres of local rule.[^46] [^47]

From the 7th century, maritime kingdoms such as the Sumatra-based [Srivijaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Srivijaya "Srivijaya") grew through trade and Buddhist religious networks.[^48] [^49] Hindu and Buddhist influences also appeared in Javanese court culture, religious life, and temple architecture.[^49] Between the 8th and 10th centuries, the [Sailendra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sailendra "Sailendra") and [Mataram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mataram_Kingdom "Mataram Kingdom") dynasties created major architectural works, including [Borobudur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borobudur "Borobudur") and [Prambanan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prambanan "Prambanan").[^50] Following a failed [Mongol invasion of Java](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_invasion_of_Java "Mongol invasion of Java"),[^51] the [Majapahit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majapahit "Majapahit") empire emerged in the late 13th century and came to dominate much of the archipelago's maritime commerce.[^46] These kingdoms and empires linked courts, ports, and religious communities across parts of the archipelago, although their authority varied by region and period.[^46] [^52]

Along the trade routes of the [Indian Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean") and the [Strait of Malacca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca "Strait of Malacca"), Islam [was established](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spread_of_Islam_in_Indonesia "Spread of Islam in Indonesia") in northern Sumatra by the late 13th century,[^53] and spread further through Java and Sumatra over the following centuries. Historians have linked its expansion to maritime trade,[^54] the rise of coastal sultanates,[^55] and the work of Islamic teachers and missionaries, including the *[Wali Sanga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wali_Sanga "Wali Sanga")*.[^56] In Java, older Javanese cultural forms entered Islamic practice, giving Javanese Islam a style unlike those found in Malaya and Sumatra.[^57]

### European contact, colonial rule, and independence

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Nicolaas_Pieneman_-_The_Submission_of_Prince_Dipo_Negoro_to_General_De_Kock.jpg/250px-Nicolaas_Pieneman_-_The_Submission_of_Prince_Dipo_Negoro_to_General_De_Kock.jpg)

An 1835 painting illustrating the submission of Prince Diponegoro to General De Kock at the end of the Java War in 1830

European entry into the archipelago began in the early 16th century, when [Portuguese expeditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Empire_in_the_Indonesian_Archipelago "Portuguese Empire in the Indonesian Archipelago") sought the eastern sources of the Asian spice trade.[^58] Dutch voyages followed later in the century, and in 1602 competing Dutch trading companies were merged into the [Dutch East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company "Dutch East India Company") (*Verenigde Oostindische Compagnie*, VOC).[^59] The VOC began as a commercial company but acquired military, diplomatic, and territorial functions before it was dissolved in 1800, after which its possessions passed to the [Dutch state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batavian_Republic "Batavian Republic") as the [Dutch East Indies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies "Dutch East Indies").[^60]

Dutch rule expanded in stages and met repeated resistance, including in [Java](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Java_War "Java War"), [Sumatra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Padri_Wars "Padri Wars"), [Bali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Template:Campaignbox_Dutch_interventions_in_Bali "Template:Campaignbox Dutch interventions in Bali"), and [Aceh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aceh_War "Aceh War").[^61] [^62] During the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Dutch authority spread across several outer-island regions, bringing most of present-day Indonesia under colonial rule.[^61] [^63] In [western New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_New_Guinea "Western New Guinea"), an early Dutch outpost was abandoned in the 1830s, and permanent occupation came only after 1898.[^64] The resulting colony was governed from Batavia through centralised authority, with both direct rule and indirect arrangements across many local societies.[^65]

The [Japanese invasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies_campaign "Dutch East Indies campaign") and [occupation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_the_Dutch_East_Indies "Japanese occupation of the Dutch East Indies") during [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") ended Dutch colonial rule; by 1945, Japan's impending defeat and surrender left nationalist leaders room to act.[^66] [^67] On 17 August 1945, shortly after [Japan's surrender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surrender_of_Japan "Surrender of Japan"), [Sukarno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sukarno "Sukarno") and [Mohammad Hatta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammad_Hatta "Mohammad Hatta") issued the [Proclamation of Indonesian Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation_of_Indonesian_Independence "Proclamation of Indonesian Independence"); Sukarno later became the country's first president and Hatta its first vice-president.[^68] [^67] The Netherlands then attempted to restore colonial rule, prompting the [Indonesian National Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesian_National_Revolution "Indonesian National Revolution").[^69] [^70] The conflict ended in 1949, when Indonesian resistance and pressure from the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") and the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") led the Netherlands to agree to a [transfer of sovereignty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch%E2%80%93Indonesian_Round_Table_Conference "Dutch–Indonesian Round Table Conference").[^71] [^72]

### Independent Indonesia

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Presiden_Sukarno_%28retouched%29.jpg/250px-Presiden_Sukarno_%28retouched%29.jpg) ![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Mohammad_Hatta%2C_Pekan_Buku_Indonesia_1954%2C_p242.jpg/250px-Mohammad_Hatta%2C_Pekan_Buku_Indonesia_1954%2C_p242.jpg)

[Sukarno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sukarno "Sukarno") (*left*) and [Mohammad Hatta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohammad_Hatta "Mohammad Hatta") (*right*), Indonesia's founding fathers and the first [president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Indonesia "President of Indonesia") and [vice president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vice_President_of_Indonesia "Vice President of Indonesia") respectively

Sukarno replaced [parliamentary democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_democracy_period_in_Indonesia "Liberal democracy period in Indonesia") with " [Guided Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guided_Democracy_in_Indonesia "Guided Democracy in Indonesia") ", concentrating authority around the presidency while balancing political Islam, [the military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesian_National_Armed_Forces "Indonesian National Armed Forces"), and the [Communist Party of Indonesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Indonesia "Communist Party of Indonesia") (PKI).[^73] After the [attempted coup in 1965](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/30_September_Movement "30 September Movement"), the military blamed the PKI and, with allied civilian groups, carried out a widespread and violent [anti-communist campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesian_mass_killings_of_1965%E2%80%9366 "Indonesian mass killings of 1965–66").[^4] The PKI was destroyed, Sukarno lost effective power, and Major General [Suharto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suharto "Suharto") assumed the presidency in 1968, establishing the authoritarian [New Order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Order_\(Indonesia\) "New Order (Indonesia)") regime.[^78] [^79] The new administration was supported by Western governments during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"),[^80] reopened Indonesia to foreign investment,[^81] [^82] and governed during three decades of rapid economic growth and industrialisation.[^83]

Indonesia's [invasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesian_invasion_of_East_Timor "Indonesian invasion of East Timor") of [East Timor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_East_Timor_\(1975\) "Democratic Republic of East Timor (1975)") in 1975 and the [occupation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesian_occupation_of_East_Timor "Indonesian occupation of East Timor") that followed prompted international criticism,[^84] [^85] and the [Santa Cruz massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Cruz_massacre "Santa Cruz massacre") in 1991 put Indonesia's human-rights record under greater international scrutiny.[^86] [^87] The [Asian financial crisis in 1997–98](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1997_Asian_financial_crisis "1997 Asian financial crisis") turned into a political crisis, causing [unrest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/May_1998_riots_of_Indonesia "May 1998 riots of Indonesia") and [Suharto's resignation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_Suharto "Fall of Suharto") in May 1998.[^88] [^89] In 1999, East Timor [voted to secede](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1999_East_Timorese_independence_referendum "1999 East Timorese independence referendum") after nearly a quarter-century under Indonesian rule,[^90] a period discussed in scholarship in relation to genocide and occupation.[^91]

In the [post-Suharto era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-Suharto_era_in_Indonesia "Post-Suharto era in Indonesia"), Indonesia introduced democratic reforms, including regional autonomy and the [first direct presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2004_Indonesian_presidential_election "2004 Indonesian presidential election").[^92] [^93] The early years of reform also saw political instability,[^94] [terrorism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrorism_in_Indonesia "Terrorism in Indonesia"),[^95] and ethnic and religious conflict in several regions.[^96] [^97] A 2005 peace agreement ended [large-scale fighting in Aceh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurgency_in_Aceh "Insurgency in Aceh") after the previous year's [Indian Ocean tsunami](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake_and_tsunami "2004 Indian Ocean earthquake and tsunami") brought more international attention to the province.[^98] Since the mid-2000s, Indonesia's economy has generally grown steadily,[^99] while its electoral democracy has endured,[^100] although corruption and criticism of authoritarian practices have persisted.[^101] [^102]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Bromo-Semeru-Batok-Widodaren.jpg/250px-Bromo-Semeru-Batok-Widodaren.jpg)

Mount Semeru and Mount Bromo in East Java. Indonesia has frequent earthquakes and volcanic eruptions.

Indonesia is an equatorial archipelago of coastal plains, mountains, and volcanic islands. It lies between latitudes [11°S](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/11th_parallel_south "11th parallel south") and [6°N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/6th_parallel_north "6th parallel north") and longitudes [95°E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/95th_meridian_east "95th meridian east") and [141°E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/141st_meridian_east "141st meridian east"),[^103] and is the world's largest [archipelagic state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archipelagic_state "Archipelagic state"), extending 5,120 kilometres (3,181 mi) from east to west and 1,760 kilometres (1,094 mi) from north to south.[^104] Several straits, including [Malacca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malacca_Strait "Malacca Strait"), [Sunda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunda_Strait "Sunda Strait"), and [Lombok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lombok_Strait "Lombok Strait"), carry international traffic between the [Indian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean") and [Pacific Oceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Ocean "Pacific Ocean"), including ships transporting oil, gas, and industrial goods.[^105] [^106]

The number of Indonesian islands varies by counting method, with published figures ranging from 13,466 named islands to more than 17,500; about 922 are permanently inhabited.[^107] [^17] Its five main islands are [Sumatra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumatra "Sumatra"), [Java](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Java "Java"), [Borneo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borneo "Borneo") (shared with [Brunei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brunei "Brunei") and [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia")), [Sulawesi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulawesi "Sulawesi"), and [New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Guinea "New Guinea") (shared with [Papua New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea "Papua New Guinea")).[^108] Java accounts for less than 7% of Indonesia's land area but is its most densely settled island, with intensive farming and extensive urban growth.[^109] Forest cover also differs widely, with larger forested areas in Papua and Maluku than in Java and Bali.[^110]

Indonesia's terrain includes high peaks, volcanic lakes, major rivers, and coastal plains. At 4,884 metres (16,024 ft), [Puncak Jaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puncak_Jaya "Puncak Jaya") in New Guinea is Indonesia's highest peak,[^111] while [Lake Toba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Toba "Lake Toba") in [Sumatra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumatra "Sumatra") is its largest lake.[^112] In [Kalimantan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalimantan "Kalimantan"), the [Kapuas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kapuas_River "Kapuas River"), [Barito](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barito_River "Barito River"), and [Mahakam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahakam_River "Mahakam River") form river networks that have long linked coastal and inland settlements.[^113] Oceans, islands, monsoons, and topography affect rainfall;[^114] plate boundaries make earthquakes and volcanic eruptions frequent;[^115] and past changes in island connections have affected species distributions.[^116]

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Koppen-Geiger_Map_IDN_present.svg/330px-Koppen-Geiger_Map_IDN_present.svg.png)

Köppen-Geiger climate classification map of Indonesia 117

Indonesia's equatorial position and monsoon winds give it a tropical climate. Most lowland areas are warm and humid throughout the year, while higher terrain is cooler; seasonal temperature differences are small.[^118] Much of the country has a [tropical rainforest climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_rainforest_climate "Tropical rainforest climate"), with tropical monsoon and savanna climates across parts of the islands.[^103] The wet season usually runs from November to April and the dry season from May to October, although the timing differs by region.[^118] Seasonal rainfall helps set rice-planting calendars and influences droughts, floods, and fires.[^119] [^120] [^121]

Rainfall varies across the archipelago. Western Sumatra, Java, and the interiors of Kalimantan and Papua are among the wetter areas, while regions nearer Australia, including Nusa Tenggara, are generally drier.[^122] The surrounding oceans, island geography, monsoon winds, and topography all affect where and when rain falls.[^123] In drier parts of the country, El Niño events can reduce rainfall and lengthen dry periods, causing water shortages and crop losses.[^120] [^119]

Indonesia is expected to face rising temperatures, changes in rainfall, higher sea levels, and greater exposure to heat and flooding.[^118] [^124] These changes affect agriculture, water supply, public health, coastal settlements, and wildfire management.[^118] [^125] [Sea-level rise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_level_rise "Sea level rise") is especially relevant to coastal areas, where much of Indonesia's population and infrastructure is located.[^126] [^118] Poorer households and communities with weaker infrastructure are likely to have less capacity to adapt.[^127]

### Geology

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Lake_Toba_and_the_surrounding_hills.jpg/250px-Lake_Toba_and_the_surrounding_hills.jpg)

Lake Toba in North Sumatra, the world's largest known Cenozoic caldera. 128

Indonesia lies on the Pacific [Ring of Fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_of_Fire "Ring of Fire"), where several major tectonic plates meet along subduction zones and active faults.[^5] [^115] It is one of the world's most active tectonic regions, with frequent earthquakes and volcanic eruptions.[^129] [^115] Nearby communities face hazards from eruptions, lahars, ash fall, earthquakes, tsunamis, and other geological events.[^130] [^115]

About 130 volcanoes are classified as active,[^129] with volcanism concentrated along the [Sunda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunda_Arc "Sunda Arc"), [Banda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banda_Arc "Banda Arc"), and [Halmahera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halmahera_Arc "Halmahera Arc") arcs.[^131] [^132] Fresh volcanic ash can damage crops, settlements, transport, and health, while weathered ash helps form fertile soils in volcanic regions, including parts of Java and Bali.[^130] Large eruptions can also produce pyroclastic flows, lahars, volcanic tsunamis, and, in rare cases, affect climate and air travel beyond Indonesia.[^129] [^133]

Several eruptions in the archipelago have had effects far from their source. A [super-eruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Youngest_Toba_eruption#Toba_catastrophe_theory "Youngest Toba eruption") at present-day [Lake Toba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Toba "Lake Toba") occurred around 74,000 years ago and has been examined for possible effects on climate and ancient human populations.[^134] The [eruption of Mount Tambora in 1815](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1815_eruption_of_Mount_Tambora "1815 eruption of Mount Tambora") had global climatic effects and contributed to the [Year Without a Summer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Year_Without_a_Summer "Year Without a Summer") in 1816 across parts of the Northern Hemisphere.[^135] The [eruption of Krakatoa in 1883](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1883_eruption_of_Krakatoa "1883 eruption of Krakatoa") caused severe regional destruction and drew worldwide scientific attention.[^136]

Earthquakes are frequent, especially along offshore subduction zones and active faults that cross the archipelago.[^137] [^138] Offshore earthquakes can generate destructive tsunamis, while shallow inland and near-coastal earthquakes can heavily damage populated areas.[^138] [^139] Major recent events include the [2004 earthquake and tsunami near northern Sumatra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake_and_tsunami "2004 Indian Ocean earthquake and tsunami"), the [2006 Yogyakarta earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_Yogyakarta_earthquake "2006 Yogyakarta earthquake"), and the [2018 Sulawesi earthquake and tsunami](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2018_Sulawesi_earthquake_and_tsunami "2018 Sulawesi earthquake and tsunami").[^138] [^139] [^140]

### Biodiversity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/202306_Varanus_komodoensis.jpg/250px-202306_Varanus_komodoensis.jpg)

The Komodo dragon ( Varanus komodoensis ) is one of Indonesia's officially designated national animals under a presidential decree in 1993 141

Indonesia is one of 17 [megadiverse countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megadiverse_countries "Megadiverse countries") recognised by [Conservation International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_International "Conservation International").[^142] Its many islands, habitats, and geological history have produced a large number of plant and animal species, many found nowhere else.[^143] Deep-water channels between islands have limited the movement of many species, leaving separate island lineages and high levels of endemism.[^144] [^145]

Indonesia's flora and fauna include Asian and [Australasian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australasian_realm "Australasian realm") species.[^146] The [Sunda Shelf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunda_Shelf "Sunda Shelf") islands have closer links to Asian fauna because lower sea levels once connected parts of western Indonesia with mainland Southeast Asia.[^147] [^148] Farther east, [Wallacea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wallacea "Wallacea") —including Sulawesi, Maluku, and the Lesser Sunda Islands—lies between the Sunda and Sahul shelves and has high endemism.[^149] [^150] In [western New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_New_Guinea "Western New Guinea"), plate movement, changing island distances, and former land connections helped isolate microhylid frog lineages and diversify them.[^151]

Indonesia has 54,716 kilometres (33,999 miles) of coastline,[^17] with coastal and marine ecosystems that include coral reefs, mangroves, and seagrass beds.[^152] Its reefs form part of the [Coral Triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coral_Triangle "Coral Triangle") and contain many coral and reef fish species.[^153] Indonesia also contains most of Southeast Asia's old-growth forest.[^154] Forest clearing and fragmentation continue to affect terrestrial habitats, while land-based pollution and destructive fishing have reduced coral diversity on some reefs.[^155] [^156]

### Environment and conservation

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Bunaken_Marine_Park.JPG/250px-Bunaken_Marine_Park.JPG)

Bunaken National Park in the Coral Triangle, one of Indonesia's over 100 marine protected areas

Indonesia's major environmental pressures include peatland degradation, deforestation,[^155] [^110] [^6] logging, plantation expansion, and mining.[^157] [^155] Peat swamp forests store large amounts of carbon and support distinct plant communities and threatened fauna, but logging, fire, drainage, and land conversion have reduced them.[^159]

For many threatened species, including the critically endangered [Bali myna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bali_myna "Bali myna"),[^160] [Sumatran orangutan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumatran_orangutan "Sumatran orangutan"),[^161] and [Javan rhinoceros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Javan_rhinoceros "Javan rhinoceros"),[^162] the main pressures include habitat loss, habitat degradation, and illegal exploitation. Forest fragmentation and land-use change break habitats into smaller blocks, isolate wildlife populations, and reduce biodiversity.[^163] Conservation work includes protected areas, species and genetic conservation, forest and land rehabilitation, and community-based biodiversity management.[^164] [^165]

As of 2024, Indonesia has designated 554 protected areas covering 27 million hectares, or 14% of its land area,[^164] along with marine reserves [^166] and 54 national parks.[^167] Poaching, illegal logging, settlement expansion, tourism pressure, and non-timber cultivation still affect protected areas;[^164] implementation is also limited by weak local capacity, coordination, monitoring, and stakeholder participation.[^165] Conservation measures can affect local rights and livelihoods,[^143] [^168] [^169] especially where stricter rules limit access to natural resources used for income.[^170]

## Government and politics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Ruang_MPR.jpg/250px-Ruang_MPR.jpg)

A presidential inauguration by the MPR in the Parliament Complex Jakarta, 2014

Indonesia is a unitary republic with a presidential system under the [1945 Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Indonesia "Constitution of Indonesia").[^25] [^171] The five principles of *[Pancasila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pancasila_\(politics\) "Pancasila (politics)")* appear in the preamble and are taught in civic education as the basis of Indonesian citizenship values.[^25] [^172] [^173] After the [fall of the New Order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_Suharto "Fall of Suharto") in 1998, four [constitutional amendments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amendments_to_the_Constitution_of_Indonesia "Amendments to the Constitution of Indonesia") altered the political system by introducing direct presidential elections, creating new state institutions, reducing the MPR's role, and giving regional autonomy a constitutional basis.[^174] Indonesia remained a [unitary state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_state "Unitary state"), while decentralisation laws gave substantial powers to provinces, districts, and cities.[^175]

The [president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Indonesia "President of Indonesia") is [head of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_state "Head of state"), [head of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_government "Head of government"), and commander-in-chief of the [Indonesian National Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesian_National_Armed_Forces "Indonesian National Armed Forces"), and may serve no more than two five-year terms.[^25] The [People's Consultative Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Consultative_Assembly "People's Consultative Assembly") (*Majelis Permusyawaratan Rakyat*, MPR) consists of elected members of the [People's Representative Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Representative_Council "People's Representative Council") (*Dewan Perwakilan Rakyat*, DPR) and the [Regional Representative Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_Representative_Council "Regional Representative Council") (*Dewan Perwakilan Daerah*, DPD).[^25] The MPR may amend the constitution, inaugurates the president and vice president, and may dismiss them under constitutional procedures.[^25] The DPR makes laws with the president, approves the state budget, and oversees the government. The DPD may propose, review, and advise on bills concerning regional government, but has less authority than the DPR.[^171] [^176]

[Judicial power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judiciary_of_Indonesia "Judiciary of Indonesia") is held by the [Supreme Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Indonesia "Supreme Court of Indonesia") (*Mahkamah Agung*), the courts beneath it, and the [Constitutional Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Indonesia "Constitutional Court of Indonesia") (*Mahkamah Konstitusi*).[^25] [^171] The Supreme Court is the final court for most civil and criminal cases and reviews regulations below the level of statutes.[^171] The Constitutional Court reviews statutes against the constitution, settles conflicts between state institutions, rules on electoral disputes and petitions to dissolve political parties, and gives impeachment-related opinions.[^171] [^174] The Religious Courts (*Pengadilan Agama*) hear Islamic family, inheritance, and certain Islamic economic cases for Muslims.[^177] The [Judicial Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judicial_Commission_of_Indonesia "Judicial Commission of Indonesia") (*Komisi Yudisial*) proposes candidates for Supreme Court justices and has duties concerning judges' conduct.[^25] [^178]
