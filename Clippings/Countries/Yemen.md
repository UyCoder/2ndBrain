**Yemen**,[^6] officially the **Republic of Yemen**,[^7] is a country in [West Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Asia "West Asia").[^19] Including the [Socotra Archipelago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socotra_Archipelago "Socotra Archipelago"), mainland Yemen is located in [southern Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Arabia "South Arabia"); bordering [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia") to [the north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia%E2%80%93Yemen_border "Saudi Arabia–Yemen border"), [Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman "Oman") to [the northeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman%E2%80%93Yemen_border "Oman–Yemen border"), the south-eastern part of the [Arabian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Sea "Arabian Sea") to the east, the [Gulf of Aden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden "Gulf of Aden") to the south, and the [Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea") to the west, sharing [maritime borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maritime_boundary "Maritime boundary") with [Djibouti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djibouti "Djibouti"), [Eritrea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eritrea "Eritrea"), [Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somaliland "Somaliland") and [Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalia "Somalia") across the [Horn of Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horn_of_Africa "Horn of Africa"). Covering roughly 455,503 square kilometres (175,871 square miles),[^13] with a coastline of approximately 2,000 kilometres (1,200 miles), Yemen is the second largest country on the Arabian Peninsula by area,[^20] and the largest by population.

[Sanaa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanaa "Sanaa") is its constitutional capital and largest city. Yemen's estimated population is 34.7 million, mostly [Arab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabs "Arabs") [Muslims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslims "Muslims"), which is estimated to be greater than that of Saudi Arabia.[^21] It is a member of the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League"), the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), the [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement") and the [Organisation of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation").

Owing to its geographic location, Yemen has been at the crossroads of many civilisations for over 7,000 years. The [Sabaeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabaeans "Sabaeans") formed a thriving commercial kingdom that [influenced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabaeans_in_the_Horn_of_Africa "Sabaeans in the Horn of Africa") parts of modern [Ethiopia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopia "Ethiopia") and [Eritrea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eritrea "Eritrea").[^22] [^23] [^24] In 275 AD, it was succeeded by the [Himyarite Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himyarite_Kingdom "Himyarite Kingdom"), which spanned much of Yemen's present-day territory and was heavily influenced by Judaism.[^25] [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_in_Yemen "Christianity in Yemen") arrived in the fourth century, followed by the rapid spread of [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") in the seventh century. From its conversion to Islam, Yemen became a center of Islamic learning, and Yemenite troops played a crucial role in early Islamic conquests.[^26] Much of [Yemen's architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architecture_of_Yemen "Architecture of Yemen") survived until modern times. For centuries, it was a primary producer of [coffee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee "Coffee"), exported through the port of [Mocha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mocha,_Yemen "Mocha, Yemen"). Various dynasties emerged between the 9th and 16th centuries.[^27] During the 19th century, the country was divided between the [Ottoman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemen_Eyalet "Yemen Eyalet") and [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") empires. After [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), the [Kingdom of Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Yemen "Kingdom of Yemen") was established, which in 1962 became the [Yemen Arab Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemen_Arab_Republic "Yemen Arab Republic") (North Yemen) following [a civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Yemen_civil_war "North Yemen civil war"). In 1967, the [People's Democratic Republic of Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Yemen "South Yemen") (South Yemen) gained its independence from the British [Aden Protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aden_Protectorate "Aden Protectorate"), becoming the first and only [communist state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_state "Communist state") in the [Middle East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_East "Middle East") and the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world"). In 1990, the two Yemeni states [united](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemeni_unification "Yemeni unification") to form the modern Republic of Yemen, with [Ali Abdullah Saleh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ali_Abdullah_Saleh "Ali Abdullah Saleh") serving as the first president until his resignation in 2012 in the wake of the [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring").[^28] [^29]

Since 2011, Yemen has been enduring [a political crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemeni_crisis "Yemeni crisis"), marked by [street protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemeni_Revolution "Yemeni Revolution") against poverty, unemployment, corruption, and President Saleh's plan to amend [Yemen's constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Yemen "Constitution of Yemen") and eliminate the presidential term limit.[^30] By 2015, the country became engulfed by [an ongoing civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemeni_civil_war_\(2014%E2%80%93present\) "Yemeni civil war (2014–present)") with multiple entities vying for governance, including the [Presidential Leadership Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_Leadership_Council "Presidential Leadership Council") of the internationally recognized government, and the [Houthi movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houthi_movement "Houthi movement") 's [Supreme Political Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Political_Council "Supreme Political Council"). This conflict, which has escalated to involve various foreign powers, has led to a [severe humanitarian crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_the_Yemeni_humanitarian_crisis "Timeline of the Yemeni humanitarian crisis").[^31] [^32] [^33] [^34] [^35] [^36]

Yemen is one of the [least developed countries in the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_developed_countries "Least developed countries"),[^37] facing significant obstacles to [sustainable development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_development "Sustainable development"),[^38] and is one of the poorest countries in the [Middle East and North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_East_and_North_Africa "Middle East and North Africa").[^39] In 2019, the United Nations reported that Yemen had the highest number of people in need of humanitarian aid, amounting to about 24 million individuals, or nearly 75% of its population.[^40] As of 2024, Yemen ranked the sixth-highest in the [Fragile States Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fragile_States_Index "Fragile States Index") [^41], and as of 2020, the second-worst on the [Global Hunger Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Hunger_Index "Global Hunger Index"), surpassed only by the [Central African Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_African_Republic "Central African Republic").[^41] As of 2024, Yemen is regarded as the world's least peaceful country by the [Global Peace Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Peace_Index "Global Peace Index").[^42] Additionally, it is ranked 184th on the [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index") as of 2025. Yemen is one of the world's most vulnerable countries to [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") and among the least prepared to handle its effects.

## Etymology

The term *Yamnat* was first mentioned in the [Old South Arabian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_South_Arabian "Old South Arabian") inscriptions on the title of one of the kings of the second [Himyarite Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himyarite_Kingdom "Himyarite Kingdom") known as [Shammar Yahri'sh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shammar_Yahri%27sh "Shammar Yahri'sh"). The term probably referred to the southwestern coastline of the [Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula") and the southern coastline between [Aden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aden "Aden") and [Hadhramaut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadhramaut "Hadhramaut").[^43] [^44] [Historical Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Yemen "Greater Yemen") included much greater territory than the current nation, stretching from northern ['Asir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%27Asir_Province "'Asir Province") in southwestern [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia") to [Dhofar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhofar_Governorate "Dhofar Governorate") in southern [Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman "Oman").[^45] [^46]

One etymology derives Yemen from *ymnt*, meaning literally "the [South](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Arabia "South Arabia") \[of the [Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula")\]", as [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic") derives cardinal directions based on [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map#Orientation "Map") towards the east, hence the underlying [linguistic root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_\(linguistics\) "Root (linguistics)") is *ymn* ([𐩺𐩣𐩬](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Semitic/yam%C4%ABn- "wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Semitic/yamīn-") in the [South Arabian alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_South_Arabian_script "Ancient South Arabian script")) meaning "right".[^47] [^48] Other sources claim that Yemen is related to *yamn* or *yumn*, meaning "felicity" or "blessed", as much of the country is fertile, in contrast to the barren land of most of Arabia.[^49] [^50] These etymologies may be connected, as [Semitic culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semitic_people "Semitic people") awards the right-hand side greater auspice and vitality.[^51] The Romans thus called it *[Arabia Felix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabia_Felix "Arabia Felix")* ("happy" or "fortunate Arabia"), as opposed to *[Arabia Deserta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabia_Deserta "Arabia Deserta")* ("deserted Arabia").

Latin and [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greeks "Greeks") writers referred to ancient Yemen as "India", which arose from the Persians calling the [Abyssinians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abyssinian_people "Abyssinian people") whom they came into contact with in South Arabia by the name of the black-skinned people who lived next to them.[^52] [^53]

## History

### Ancient history

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Jemen1988-022_hg.jpg/250px-Jemen1988-022_hg.jpg)

Ruins of the Great Dam of Marib

With its long sea border between eastern and western civilizations, Yemen has long existed at a crossroads of cultures with a strategic location in terms of trade on the west of the Arabian Peninsula. Large settlements for their era existed in the mountains of northern Yemen as early as 5000 BC.[^54] The [Sabaean Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabaeans "Sabaeans") existed in [Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemen_\(region\) "Yemen (region)") from 1000 BC to 275 AD.[^24] [^55] Its inhabitants were the Sabaeans,[^8] The four major kingdoms or tribal confederations in [South Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Arabia "South Arabia") were Saba, [Hadhramaut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadhramaut "Hadhramaut"), [Qataban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qataban "Qataban"), and [Ma'in](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minaeans "Minaeans").

*[Sabaʾ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabaeans "Sabaeans")* ([Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"): سَـبَـأ) [^56] [^57] is thought to be biblical Sheba and was the most prominent federation.[^58] The Sabaean rulers adopted the title [Mukarrib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mukarrib "Mukarrib") generally thought to mean *unifier*,[^59] or a *priest-king*,[^60] or the head of the confederation of South Arabian kingdoms, the "king of the kings".[^61] The role of the Mukarrib was to bring the various tribes under the kingdom and preside over them all.[^62] The Sabaeans built the [Great Dam of Marib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marib_Dam "Marib Dam") around 940 BC.[^63] The dam was built to withstand the seasonal flash floods surging down the valley.

By the third century BC, Qataban, Hadhramaut, and Ma'in became independent from Saba and established themselves in the Yemeni arena. Minaean rule stretched as far as [Dedan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dedanites "Dedanites"),[^64] with their capital at [Baraqish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baraqish "Baraqish"). The Sabaeans regained their control over Ma'in after the collapse of Qataban in 50 BC. By the time of the Roman expedition to Arabia Felix in 25 BC, the Sabaeans were once again the dominating power in Southern Arabia.[^65] [Aelius Gallus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aelius_Gallus "Aelius Gallus") was ordered to lead a military campaign to establish Roman dominance over the Sabaeans.[^66]

The [Romans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") had a vague and contradictory geographical knowledge about Arabia Felix. A Roman army of 10,000 men was defeated before reaching [Marib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marib "Marib").[^67] [Strabo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strabo "Strabo") 's close relationship with Aelius Gallus led him to attempt to justify his friend's defeat in his writings. It took the Romans six months to reach Marib and 60 days to return to [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt"). The Romans blamed their [Nabataean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nabataeans "Nabataeans") guide and executed him for treachery.[^68] No direct mention in Sabaean inscriptions of the Roman expedition has yet been found.

After the Roman expedition (perhaps earlier) the country fell into chaos, and two clans, namely [Hamdan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banu_Hamdan "Banu Hamdan") and [Himyar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himyar "Himyar"), claimed kingship, assuming the title King of Sheba and Dhu Raydan.[^69] Dhu Raydan, *i.e.*, Himyarites, allied themselves with [Aksum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aksum "Aksum") in Ethiopia against the Sabaeans.[^70] The chief of [Bakil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bakil "Bakil") and king of Saba and Dhu Raydan, [El Sharih Yahdhib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ilasaros "Ilasaros"), launched successful campaigns against the Himyarites and Habashat, *i.e.*, Aksum. El Sharih took pride in his campaigns and added the title Yahdhib to his name, which means "suppressor"; he used to kill his enemies by cutting them to pieces.[^71] Sanaa came into prominence during his reign, as he built the [Ghumdan Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghumdan_Palace "Ghumdan Palace") as his place of residence.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Dhamar_Ali_Yahbur_%28bust%29.jpg/250px-Dhamar_Ali_Yahbur_%28bust%29.jpg)

A bronze statue of Dhamar Ali Yahbur I, a Himyarite king who probably reigned in the late 3rd or early 4th century AD. Displayed in the National Museum of Yemen.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/South_Arabian_-_Stele_with_a_Female_Bust_-_Walters_2173.jpg/250px-South_Arabian_-_Stele_with_a_Female_Bust_-_Walters_2173.jpg)

A Sabaean gravestone of a woman holding a stylized sheaf of wheat, a symbol of fertility in ancient Yemen

The Himyarites annexed Sanaa from Hamdan around 100 AD.[^72] [Hashdi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hashid "Hashid") tribesmen rebelled against them and regained Sanaa around 180.[^73] [Shammar Yahri'sh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shammar_Yahri%27sh "Shammar Yahri'sh") had conquered Hadhramaut, [Najran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Najran "Najran"), and [Tihamah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tihamah "Tihamah") by 275, thus unifying Yemen and consolidating Himyarite rule.[^74] [^75] The Himyarites rejected [polytheism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytheism "Polytheism") and adhered to a consensual form of [monotheism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotheism "Monotheism") called [Rahmanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rahmanism "Rahmanism").[^76]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/British_Museum_Yemen_04.jpg/250px-British_Museum_Yemen_04.jpg)

Bronze hand with an inscribed prayer in Himyaritic to god Ta'lab

In 354, Roman Emperor [Constantius II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantius_II "Constantius II") sent an embassy headed by [Theophilos the Indian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theophilos_the_Indian "Theophilos the Indian") to convert the Himyarites to Christianity.[^77] According to [Philostorgius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philostorgius "Philostorgius"), the mission was resisted by local Jews.[^78] Several inscriptions have been found in [Hebrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_language "Hebrew language") and [Sabaean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabaean_language "Sabaean language") praising the ruling house in Jewish terms for "...helping and empowering the People of Israel." [^79]

According to Islamic traditions, King [As'ad the Perfect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tub%27a_Abu_Kariba_As%27ad "Tub'a Abu Kariba As'ad") mounted a military expedition to support the Jews of [Yathrib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yathrib "Yathrib").[^80] Abu Kariba As'ad, as known from the inscriptions, led a military campaign to central Arabia or [Najd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Najd "Najd") to support the vassal [Kingdom of Kinda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kinda "Kingdom of Kinda") against the [Lakhmids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lakhmids "Lakhmids").[^81] However, no direct reference to Judaism or Yathrib was discovered from his lengthy reign. Abu Kariba died in 445, having reigned for almost 50 years.[^82] By 515, Himyar became increasingly divided along religious lines and a bitter conflict between different factions paved the way for an [Aksumite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aksum "Aksum") intervention. The last Himyarite king Ma'adikarib Ya'fur was supported by Aksum against his Jewish rivals. Ma'adikarib was Christian and launched a campaign against the Lakhmids in southern [Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq "Iraq"), with the support of other Arab allies of [Byzantium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium "Byzantium").[^83] The Lakhmids were a bulwark of [Persia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persia "Persia"), which was intolerant to a proselytizing religion like Christianity.[^84]

After the death of Ma'adikarib Ya'fur around 521, a Himyarite Jewish warlord called [Dhu Nuwas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhu_Nuwas "Dhu Nuwas") rose to power. Emperor [Justinian I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justinian_I "Justinian I") sent an embassy to Yemen. He wanted the officially Christian Himyarites to use their influence on the tribes in inner Arabia to launch military operations against Persia. Justinian I bestowed the "dignity of king" upon the Arab [sheikhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheikh "Sheikh") of Kindah and [Ghassan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghassanids "Ghassanids") in central and northern Arabia.[^85] From early on, Roman and Byzantine policy was to develop close links with the powers of the coast of the [Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea"). They were successful in converting [^86] Aksum and influencing their culture. The results concerning to Yemen were rather disappointing.[^85]

A Kendite prince called Yazid bin Kabshat rebelled against Abraha and his Arab Christian allies. A truce was reached once the Great Dam of Marib had suffered a breach.[^87] Abraha died around 570. The [Sasanid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sasanid_Empire "Sasanid Empire") annexed Aden around 570. Under their rule, most of Yemen enjoyed great autonomy except for Aden and Sanaa. This era marked the collapse of ancient South Arabian civilization, since the greater part of the country was under several independent clans until the arrival of Islam in 630.[^88]

### Middle Ages

#### Advent of Islam and the three dynasties

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Great_Mosque_of_Sana%27a1.jpg/250px-Great_Mosque_of_Sana%27a1.jpg)

The interior of the Great Mosque of Sanaa, the oldest mosque in Yemen

[Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad "Muhammad") sent his cousin [Ali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ali "Ali") to Sanaa and its surroundings around 630. At the time, Yemen was the most advanced region in Arabia.[^89] The [Banu Hamdan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banu_Hamdan "Banu Hamdan") confederation was among the first to accept Islam. Muhammad sent [Muadh ibn Jabal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muadh_ibn_Jabal "Muadh ibn Jabal"), as well to Al-Janad, in present-day [Taiz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiz "Taiz"), and dispatched letters to various tribal leaders.[^90] Major tribes, including Himyar, sent delegations to [Medina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medina "Medina") during the "year of delegations" around 630–631. Several Yemenis accepted Islam before 630, such as [Ammar ibn Yasir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammar_ibn_Yasir "Ammar ibn Yasir"), [Al-Ala'a Al-Hadrami](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Ala%27a_Al-Hadrami "Al-Ala'a Al-Hadrami"), [Miqdad ibn Aswad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miqdad_ibn_Aswad "Miqdad ibn Aswad"), [Abu Musa Ashaari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Musa_Ashaari "Abu Musa Ashaari"), and [Sharhabeel ibn Hasana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharhabeel_ibn_Hasana "Sharhabeel ibn Hasana"). A man named ['Abhala ibn Ka'ab Al-Ansi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aswad_Ansi "Aswad Ansi") expelled the remaining Persians and claimed he was a prophet of [Rahman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rahman_\(Islamic_term\) "Rahman (Islamic term)"). He was assassinated by a Yemeni of Persian origin called [Fayruz al-Daylami](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fayruz_al-Daylami "Fayruz al-Daylami"). Christians, who were mainly staying in [Najran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Najran "Najran") along with Jews, agreed to pay *[jizyah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jizya "Jizya")* ([Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"): جِـزْيَـة), although some Jews converted to Islam, such as [Wahb ibn Munabbih](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wahb_ibn_Munabbih "Wahb ibn Munabbih") and [Ka'ab al-Ahbar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ka%27ab_al-Ahbar "Ka'ab al-Ahbar").

Yemen was stable during the [Rashidun Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashidun_Caliphate "Rashidun Caliphate"). Yemeni tribes played a pivotal role in the Islamic expansion into Egypt, Iraq, Persia, the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant"), [Anatolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolia "Anatolia"), [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa"), [Sicily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Islam_in_Southern_Italy "History of Islam in Southern Italy"), and [Andalusia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andalusia "Andalusia").[^91] [^92] [^93] Yemeni tribes who settled in [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria") contributed significantly to the solidification of [Umayyad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") rule, especially during the reign of [Marwan I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marwan_I "Marwan I"). Powerful Yemenite tribes such as Kinda were on his side during the [Battle of Marj Rahit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Marj_Rahit_\(684\) "Battle of Marj Rahit (684)").[^94] [^95]

Muhammad ibn Abdullah ibn Ziyad founded the Ziyadid dynasty in [Tihamah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tihamah "Tihamah") around 818. The state stretched from [Haly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Qunfudhah "Al Qunfudhah") (in present-day Saudi Arabia) to Aden. They nominally recognized the [Abbasid Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate") but ruled independently from [Zabid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zabid "Zabid").[^96] By virtue of its location, they developed a special relationship with [Abyssinia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopia "Ethiopia"). The chief of the [Dahlak islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dahlak_Archipelago "Dahlak Archipelago") exported slaves, as well as amber and leopard hides, to the ruler of Yemen.[^97] They controlled only a small portion of the coastal strip in Tihamah along the Red Sea, and never exercised control over the highlands and Hadhramaut.[^98] A Himyarite clan called the [Yufirids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yufirids "Yufirids") established their rule over the highlands from [Saada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saada "Saada") to [Taiz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiz "Taiz"), while Hadhramaut was an Ibadi stronghold and rejected all allegiance to the Abbasids in [Baghdad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baghdad "Baghdad").[^96]

The first [Zaidi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zaidiyyah "Zaidiyyah") imam, [Yahya ibn al-Husayn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Hadi_ila%27l-Haqq_Yahya "Al-Hadi ila'l-Haqq Yahya"), arrived in Yemen in 893. He was a religious cleric and judge who was invited to come to Saada from Medina to arbitrate tribal disputes.[^99] Yahya persuaded local tribesmen to follow his teachings. The sect slowly spread across the highlands, as the tribes of [Hashid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hashid "Hashid") and [Bakil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bakil "Bakil"), later known as "the twin wings of the imamate", accepted his authority.[^100] He founded the [Zaidi imamate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rassids "Rassids") in 897. Yahya established his influence in Saada and Najran. He also tried to capture Sanaa from the Yufirids in 901 but failed miserably.
