**Canada** [^1] is a country in [North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America "North America"). Its [ten provinces and three territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada "Provinces and territories of Canada") extend from the Atlantic Ocean to the Pacific Ocean and northward into the Arctic Ocean, making it the [second-largest country by total area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_area "List of countries and dependencies by area"), with the [longest coastline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_length_of_coastline "List of countries by length of coastline") of any country. Its [border with the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_border "Canada–United States border") is the longest international land border. The country is characterized by a wide range of both [meteorologic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature_in_Canada "Temperature in Canada") and [geological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Canada "Geography of Canada") regions. With [a population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_of_Canada "Population of Canada") of over 41 million, it has widely varying population densities, with the majority residing in [its urban areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_largest_population_centres_in_Canada "List of the largest population centres in Canada") and large areas being sparsely populated. Its capital is [Ottawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottawa "Ottawa") and [its three largest metropolitan areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_census_metropolitan_areas_and_agglomerations_in_Canada "List of census metropolitan areas and agglomerations in Canada") are [Toronto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toronto "Toronto"), [Montreal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montreal "Montreal"), and [Vancouver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vancouver "Vancouver").

[Indigenous peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Canada "Indigenous peoples in Canada") have continuously inhabited what is now Canada for thousands of years. Beginning in the 16th century, [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_colonization_of_the_Americas "British colonization of the Americas") and [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_colonization_of_the_Americas "French colonization of the Americas") expeditions explored and later settled along the Atlantic coast. As a consequence of [various armed conflicts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_history_of_Canada "Military history of Canada"), France [ceded nearly all](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_\(1763\) "Treaty of Paris (1763)") of [its colonies in North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_France "New France") in 1763. In 1867, with the union of three [British North American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_North_America "British North America") colonies through [Confederation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Confederation "Canadian Confederation"), Canada was formed as a [federal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalism "Federalism") [dominion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominion "Dominion") of four provinces. This began an [accretion of provinces and territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_evolution_of_Canada "Territorial evolution of Canada") resulting in the [displacement of Indigenous populations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numbered_Treaties "Numbered Treaties"), and a process of increasing autonomy from the United Kingdom. This increased sovereignty was highlighted by the *[Statute of Westminster, 1931](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931 "Statute of Westminster 1931")*, and culminated in the *[Canada Act 1982](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada_Act_1982 "Canada Act 1982")*, which severed the vestiges of legal dependence on the [Parliament of the United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom "Parliament of the United Kingdom").

Canada is a [parliamentary democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system") and a [constitutional monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy") in the [Westminster tradition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westminster_system "Westminster system"). The country's [head of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_government "Head of government") is the [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Canada "Prime Minister of Canada"), who holds office by virtue of their ability to [command the confidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidence_and_supply "Confidence and supply") of the elected [House of Commons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada "House of Commons of Canada") and is appointed by the [governor general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada "Governor General of Canada"), representing the [monarch of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_of_Canada "Monarchy of Canada"), the ceremonial [head of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_state "Head of state"). The country is a [Commonwealth realm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_realm "Commonwealth realm") and is [officially bilingual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Official_bilingualism_in_Canada "Official bilingualism in Canada") (English and French) in the federal jurisdiction. It is [very highly ranked in international measurements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_rankings_of_Canada "International rankings of Canada") of government transparency, quality of life, economic competitiveness, innovation, education and human rights. It is one of the world's most [ethnically diverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_origins_of_people_in_Canada "Ethnic origins of people in Canada") and [multicultural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiculturalism_in_Canada "Multiculturalism in Canada") nations, the product of [large-scale immigration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immigration_to_Canada "Immigration to Canada"). Canada's long and complex [relationship with the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_relations "Canada–United States relations") has had a significant impact on [its history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Canada "History of Canada"), [economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Canada "Economy of Canada"), and [culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Canada "Culture of Canada").

A [developed country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country"), Canada has a [high nominal per capita income globally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\)_per_capita "List of countries by GDP (nominal) per capita") and its advanced economy ranks among the [largest in the world by nominal GDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of countries by GDP (nominal)"), relying chiefly upon [its abundant natural resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Canada#Natural_resources "Geography of Canada") and well-developed [international trade networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_largest_trading_partners_of_Canada "List of the largest trading partners of Canada"). Canada is recognized as a [middle power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_power "Middle power"); its support for [multilateralism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilateralism "Multilateralism") and [internationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internationalism_\(politics\) "Internationalism (politics)") has been closely related to [its foreign policies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_Canada "Foreign relations of Canada") of [peacekeeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_peacekeeping "Canadian peacekeeping") and [aid for developing countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_foreign_aid "Canadian foreign aid"). Canada promotes [its domestically shared values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_values "Canadian values") through participation in [multiple international organizations and forums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_organisation_membership_of_Canada "International organisation membership of Canada").

## Etymology

While a variety of theories have been postulated for the etymological origins of *Canada*, the name is now accepted as coming from the [St. Lawrence Iroquoian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Lawrence_Iroquoian "St. Lawrence Iroquoian") word *kanata*, meaning 'village' or 'settlement'.[^15] In 1535, Indigenous inhabitants of the present-day [Quebec City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quebec_City "Quebec City") region used the word to direct French explorer [Jacques Cartier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Cartier "Jacques Cartier") to the village of [Stadacona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stadacona "Stadacona").[^16] Cartier later used the word *Canada* to refer not only to that particular village but to the entire area subject to [Donnacona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donnacona "Donnacona") (the chief at Stadacona);[^16] by 1545, European books and maps had begun referring to this small region along the [Saint Lawrence River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River "Saint Lawrence River") as *Canada*.[^16]

From the 16th to the early 18th century, *[Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada_\(New_France\) "Canada (New France)")* referred to the part of [New France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_France "New France") that lay along the Saint Lawrence River.[^17] Following the British [conquest of New France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conquest_of_New_France "Conquest of New France"), this area was known as the British [Province of Quebec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Province_of_Quebec_\(1763%E2%80%931791\) "Province of Quebec (1763–1791)") from 1763 to 1791.[^18] In 1791, the area became two British colonies called [Upper Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_Canada "Upper Canada") and [Lower Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_Canada "Lower Canada"). These two colonies were collectively referred to as [the Canadas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Canadas "The Canadas") until their union as the [Province of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Province_of_Canada "Province of Canada") in 1841.[^19]

Upon [Confederation in 1867](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Confederation "Canadian Confederation"), *Canada* was adopted as the legal name for the new country at the [London Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Conference_of_1866 "London Conference of 1866") and the word *[dominion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominion "Dominion")* was conferred as the country's title, reflecting its status as a [self-governing nation within the British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire#Changing_status_of_the_white_colonies "British Empire").[^20] Following the [London Declaration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Declaration "London Declaration") of 1949, where it was agreed that India could be a full member of the [Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations") as a republic, not a dominion, the leaders of the Commonwealth countries agreed that the other members of the Commonwealth should not continue to be referred to as *dominions*. In 1951, Prime Minister [Louis St. Laurent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_St._Laurent "Louis St. Laurent") announced in the [Canadian House of Commons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_House_of_Commons "Canadian House of Commons") that in light of the *[Statute of Westminster, 1931](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931 "Statute of Westminster 1931")*, and the changes to the Commonwealth, *dominion* was no longer an accurate term to use to refer to Canada and would no longer be used by the federal government.[^21] Similarly, the United Kingdom no longer used the term *dominions* to refer to Canada or other members of the Commonwealth, instead beginning to use the term a *[realm of the Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_realm "Commonwealth realm")*.[^22]

The *[Canada Act 1982](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada_Act_1982 "Canada Act 1982")*, which brought the [Constitution of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Canada "Constitution of Canada") fully under Canadian control, referred only to *Canada*. Later that year, the name of the national holiday was changed from Dominion Day to [Canada Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada_Day "Canada Day").[^23]

## History

### Indigenous peoples

The [first inhabitants of North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Settlement_of_the_Americas "Settlement of the Americas") are generally hypothesized to have migrated from [Siberia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberia "Siberia") by way of the [Bering land bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beringia "Beringia") and arrived at least 14,000 years ago.[^24] The [Paleo-Indian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleo-Indian "Paleo-Indian") archeological sites at [Old Crow Flats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Crow_Flats "Old Crow Flats") and [Bluefish Caves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bluefish_Caves "Bluefish Caves") are two of the oldest sites of human habitation in Canada.[^25] The [characteristics of Indigenous societies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technological_and_industrial_history_of_Canada#The_Stone_Age:_Fire_\(14,000_BC_%E2%80%93_AD_1600\) "Technological and industrial history of Canada") included permanent settlements, agriculture, complex societal hierarchies, and trading networks.[^26] Some of these cultures had collapsed by the time European explorers arrived in the late 15th and early 16th centuries and have only been discovered through archeological investigations.[^27] [Indigenous peoples in present-day Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Canada "Indigenous peoples in Canada") include the [First Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Nations_in_Canada "First Nations in Canada"), [Inuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inuit "Inuit"), and [Métis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_people_in_Canada "Métis people in Canada"),[^28] the last being of [mixed descent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed-blood "Mixed-blood") who originated in the mid-17th century when First Nations people married European settlers and their offspring subsequently developed their own identity.[^28]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Indigenous_population_by_census_division.svg/330px-Indigenous_population_by_census_division.svg.png)

A map of Canada showing the percent of self-reported indigenous identity (First Nations, Inuit, Métis) by census division, according to the 2021 Canadian census 29

The [Indigenous population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_history_of_indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas "Population history of indigenous peoples of the Americas") at the time of the first European settlements is estimated to have been between 200,000 [^30] and two million,[^31] with a figure of 500,000 accepted by Canada's [Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_Aboriginal_Peoples "Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples").[^32] As a consequence of European colonization, the Indigenous population declined by forty to eighty percent.[^33] The decline is attributed to several causes, including the [transfer of European diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbian_exchange "Columbian exchange"), to which they had no natural immunity,[^34] conflicts over the fur trade, conflicts with the colonial authorities and settlers, and the loss of Indigenous lands to settlers and the subsequent collapse of several nations' self-sufficiency.[^35]

Although not without conflict, [European Canadians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Canadians "European Canadians") ' early interactions with First Nations and Inuit populations were relatively peaceful.[^36] First Nations and Métis peoples played a critical part in the development of [European colonies in Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Former_colonies_and_territories_in_Canada "Former colonies and territories in Canada"), particularly for their role in assisting European [coureurs des bois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coureur_des_bois "Coureur des bois") and [voyageurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyageurs "Voyageurs") in their explorations of the continent during the [North American fur trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_fur_trade "North American fur trade").[^37] These early European interactions with First Nations would change from [friendship and peace treaties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peace_and_Friendship_Treaties "Peace and Friendship Treaties") to the [dispossession of Indigenous lands through treaties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numbered_Treaties "Numbered Treaties").[^38] From the late 18th century, European Canadians forced Indigenous peoples to assimilate into a western Canadian society.[^39] [Settler colonialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Settler_colonialism_in_Canada "Settler colonialism in Canada") reached a climax in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.[^40] A period of redress began with the formation of a [reconciliation commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_and_Reconciliation_Commission_of_Canada "Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada") by the [Government of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Canada "Government of Canada") in 2008.[^41] This included acknowledgment of [cultural genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_genocide_of_Indigenous_peoples "Canadian genocide of Indigenous peoples"),[^42] [settlement agreements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Residential_Schools_Settlement_Agreement "Indian Residential Schools Settlement Agreement"),[^41] and betterment of racial discrimination issues, such as addressing the plight of [missing and murdered Indigenous women](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Missing_and_murdered_Indigenous_women "Missing and murdered Indigenous women").[^43]

### European colonization

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Nouvelle-France_map-en.svg/330px-Nouvelle-France_map-en.svg.png)

Map of territorial claims in North America by 1750. Possessions of British America (pink), New France (blue), and New Spain (orange); California, Pacific Northwest, and Great Basin not indicated. 44

It is believed that the first documented European to explore the east coast of Canada was [Norse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vikings "Vikings") explorer [Leif Erikson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leif_Erikson "Leif Erikson").[^45] In approximately 1000 AD, the Norse built a small short-lived encampment that was occupied sporadically for perhaps 20 years at [L'Anse aux Meadows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%27Anse_aux_Meadows "L'Anse aux Meadows") on the northern tip of [Newfoundland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newfoundland "Newfoundland").[^46] No further European exploration occurred until 1497, when seafarer [John Cabot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Cabot "John Cabot") explored and claimed Canada's [Atlantic coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Canada "Atlantic Canada") in the name of [Henry VII of England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England "Henry VII of England").[^47] In 1534, French explorer [Jacques Cartier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Cartier "Jacques Cartier") explored the [Gulf of Saint Lawrence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Saint_Lawrence "Gulf of Saint Lawrence") where, on July 24, he planted a 10-metre (33 ft) cross bearing the words, "long live the King of France", and took possession of the territory New France in the name of [King Francis I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_I_of_France "Francis I of France").[^48] The early 16th century saw European mariners with navigational techniques pioneered by the [Basque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basques "Basques") and [Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_discoveries "Portuguese discoveries") establish seasonal whaling and fishing outposts along the Atlantic coast.[^49] In general, early settlements during the [Age of Discovery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Discovery "Age of Discovery") appear to have been [short-lived](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_of_Canada#Ephemeral_European_settlements "Population of Canada") due to a combination of the harsh climate, problems with navigating trade routes and competing outputs in Scandinavia.[^50]

In 1583, Sir [Humphrey Gilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humphrey_Gilbert "Humphrey Gilbert"), by the [royal prerogative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_prerogative "Royal prerogative") of Queen [Elizabeth I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elizabeth_I "Elizabeth I"), founded [St John's, Newfoundland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._John%27s,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador "St. John's, Newfoundland and Labrador"), as the first North American [English seasonal camp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_overseas_possessions "English overseas possessions").[^51] In 1600, the French established their first seasonal trading post at [Tadoussac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tadoussac "Tadoussac") along the Saint Lawrence.[^52] French explorer [Samuel de Champlain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_de_Champlain "Samuel de Champlain") arrived in 1603 and established the first permanent year-round European settlements at [Port Royal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port-Royal_\(Acadia\) "Port-Royal (Acadia)") (in 1605) and Quebec City (in 1608).[^53] Among the [colonists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_colonization_of_the_Americas "French colonization of the Americas") of New France, *[Canadiens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Canadians "French Canadians")* extensively settled the Saint Lawrence River valley and [Acadians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acadians "Acadians") settled the present-day [Maritimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Maritimes "The Maritimes"), while fur traders and [Catholic missionaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church_and_the_Age_of_Discovery "Catholic Church and the Age of Discovery") explored the [Great Lakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Lakes "Great Lakes"), [Hudson Bay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hudson_Bay "Hudson Bay"), and the [Mississippi watershed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mississippi_watershed "Mississippi watershed") to [Louisiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana_\(New_France\) "Louisiana (New France)").[^54] The [Beaver Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beaver_Wars "Beaver Wars") broke out in the mid-17th century over control of the North American fur trade.[^55]

The English established additional settlements in [Newfoundland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newfoundland_\(island\) "Newfoundland (island)") in 1610 along with settlements in the [Thirteen Colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies "Thirteen Colonies") to the south.[^56] A series of [four wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_and_Indian_Wars "French and Indian Wars") erupted in colonial North America between 1689 and 1763; the later wars of the period constituted the North American theatre of the [Seven Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War "Seven Years' War").[^57] Mainland [Nova Scotia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova_Scotia "Nova Scotia") came under British rule with the 1713 [Treaty of Utrecht](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Utrecht "Treaty of Utrecht") and Canada and most of New France came under British rule in 1763 after the Seven Years' War.[^58]

### British North America

![Painting of General Wolfe dying in front of the British flag while attended by officers and native allies](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Benjamin_West_005.jpg/250px-Benjamin_West_005.jpg)

Benjamin West 's The Death of General Wolfe (1771) dramatizes James Wolfe 's death during the Battle of the Plains of Abraham at Quebec City. 59

The [Royal Proclamation of 1763](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Proclamation_of_1763 "Royal Proclamation of 1763") established First Nation treaty rights, created the [Province of Quebec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Province_of_Quebec_\(1763%E2%80%931791\) "Province of Quebec (1763–1791)") out of New France, and annexed [Cape Breton Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_Breton_Island "Cape Breton Island") to Nova Scotia.[^23] St John's Island (now [Prince Edward Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island "Prince Edward Island")) became a separate colony in 1769.[^60] To avert conflict in [Quebec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quebec "Quebec"), the British Parliament passed the [Quebec Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quebec_Act "Quebec Act") 1774, expanding Quebec's territory to the Great Lakes and [Ohio Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohio_River "Ohio River").[^61] More importantly, the Quebec Act afforded Quebec special autonomy and rights of self-administration at a time when the Thirteen Colonies were increasingly agitating against British rule.[^62] It re-established the French language, Catholic faith, and [French civil law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_France "Law of France") there, staving off the growth of an independence movement in contrast to the Thirteen Colonies.[^63] The Proclamation and the Quebec Act in turn angered many residents of the Thirteen Colonies, further fuelling anti-British sentiment in the years prior to the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution "American Revolution").[^23]

After the successful American War of Independence, the [1783 Treaty of Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_\(1783\) "Treaty of Paris (1783)") recognized the independence of the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and set the terms of peace, ceding [British North American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_North_America "British North America") territories south of the Great Lakes and east of the Mississippi River to the new country.[^64] The American war of independence also caused a large out-migration of [Loyalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loyalists "Loyalists"), the settlers who had fought against American independence. Many moved to Canada, particularly Atlantic Canada, where their arrival changed the demographic distribution of the existing territories. [New Brunswick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Brunswick "New Brunswick") was in turn split from Nova Scotia as part of a reorganization of Loyalist settlements in the Maritimes, which led to the incorporation of [Saint John, New Brunswick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_John,_New_Brunswick "Saint John, New Brunswick"), as Canada's first city.[^65] To accommodate the influx of English-speaking Loyalists in Central Canada, the [Constitutional Act of 1791](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_Act_1791 "Constitutional Act 1791") divided the province of Canada into French-speaking Lower Canada (later [Quebec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quebec "Quebec")) and English-speaking Upper Canada (later [Ontario](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontario#Canada_West_\(1841%E2%80%931867\) "Ontario")), granting each its own elected legislative assembly.[^66]

![Painting of Laura Secord warning British commander James FitzGibbon of an impending American attack at Beaver Dams](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Laura_Secord_warns_Fitzgibbons%2C_1813.jpg/250px-Laura_Secord_warns_Fitzgibbons%2C_1813.jpg)

War of 1812 heroine Laura Secord warning British commander James FitzGibbon of an impending American attack at Beaver Dams 67

The Canadas were the main front in the [War of 1812](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_1812 "War of 1812") between the United States and the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland "United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland"). Peace came in 1815; no boundaries were changed.[^68] Immigration resumed at a higher level, with over 960,000 arrivals from Britain between 1815 and 1850.[^69] New arrivals included refugees escaping the [Great Irish Famine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Irish_Famine "Great Irish Famine") as well as [Gaelic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic "Scottish Gaelic") -speaking Scots displaced by the [Highland Clearances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highland_Clearances "Highland Clearances").[^70] Infectious diseases killed between 25 and 33 percent of Europeans who immigrated to Canada before 1891.[^30]

The desire for [responsible government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Responsible_government "Responsible government") resulted in the abortive [Rebellions of 1837](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rebellions_of_1837 "Rebellions of 1837").[^71] The [Durham Report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durham_Report "Durham Report") subsequently recommended responsible government and the assimilation of French Canadians into English culture.[^23] The [Act of Union 1840](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Act_of_Union_1840 "Act of Union 1840") merged the Canadas into a united Province of Canada and responsible government was established for all provinces of British North America east of Lake Superior by 1855.[^72] The signing of the [Oregon Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oregon_Treaty "Oregon Treaty") by Britain and the United States in 1846 ended the [Oregon boundary dispute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oregon_boundary_dispute "Oregon boundary dispute"), extending the border westward along the [49th parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/49th_parallel_north "49th parallel north"). This paved the way for British colonies on [Vancouver Island (1849)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_of_Vancouver_Island "Colony of Vancouver Island") and in [British Columbia (1858)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_of_British_Columbia_\(1858%E2%80%931866\) "Colony of British Columbia (1858–1866)").[^73] The Anglo-Russian [Treaty of Saint Petersburg (1825)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Saint_Petersburg_\(1825\) "Treaty of Saint Petersburg (1825)") established the border along the Pacific coast, but, even after the US [Alaska Purchase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaska_Purchase "Alaska Purchase") of 1867, disputes continued about the exact demarcation of the Alaska–Yukon and Alaska–British Columbia border.[^74]

### Confederation and expansion

![Refer to caption](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Canada_provinces_evolution_2.gif/330px-Canada_provinces_evolution_2.gif)

Animated map showing the growth and change of Canada's provinces and territories since Confederation in 1867 75

Following three constitutional conferences, the *[British North America Act, 1867](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_Act,_1867 "Constitution Act, 1867")* officially proclaimed Canadian Confederation on July 1, 1867, initially with four provinces: [Ontario](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontario "Ontario"), Quebec, Nova Scotia, and New Brunswick.[^76] Canada assumed control of [Rupert's Land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rupert%27s_Land "Rupert's Land") and the [North-Western Territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North-Western_Territory "North-Western Territory") to form the [Northwest Territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northwest_Territories "Northwest Territories"), where the Métis' grievances ignited the [Red River Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_River_Rebellion "Red River Rebellion") and the creation of the province of [Manitoba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manitoba "Manitoba") in July 1870.[^77] British Columbia and Vancouver Island (which [had been united](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_of_British_Columbia_\(1866%E2%80%931871\) "Colony of British Columbia (1866–1871)") in 1866) joined the confederation in 1871 on the promise of a transcontinental railway extending to Victoria in the province within 10 years,[^78] while Prince Edward Island joined in 1873.[^79] In 1898, during the [Klondike Gold Rush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klondike_Gold_Rush "Klondike Gold Rush") in the Northwest Territories, Parliament created the Yukon Territory. [Alberta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alberta "Alberta") and [Saskatchewan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saskatchewan "Saskatchewan") became provinces in 1905.[^79] Between 1871 and 1896, almost one quarter of the Canadian population emigrated south to the US.[^80]

To settle [the West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Canada "Western Canada") and attract European immigrants, Canada sponsored the construction of three transcontinental railways, enacted the *[Dominion Lands Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominion_Lands_Act "Dominion Lands Act")* and founded the [North-West Mounted Police](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North-West_Mounted_Police "North-West Mounted Police") to maintain regional authority.[^81] This [period of westward expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-Confederation_Canada_\(1867%E2%80%931914\)#Expansion "Post-Confederation Canada (1867–1914)") and [nation building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Policy "National Policy") resulted in the displacement of many [Indigenous peoples of the Canadian Prairies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plains_Indians "Plains Indians") to " [Indian reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_reserve "Indian reserve") ",[^82] clearing the way for ethnic European [block settlements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Block_settlement "Block settlement").[^83] This caused the collapse of the [Plains Bison in western Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_bison_conservation_in_Canada#Plains_bison "History of bison conservation in Canada") and the introduction of European [cattle farms and wheat fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture_in_Canada "Agriculture in Canada") dominating the land.[^84] The Indigenous peoples saw widespread famine and disease due to the loss of the bison and their traditional hunting lands.[^85] The federal government did provide emergency relief, on condition of the Indigenous peoples moving to the reserves.[^86] During this time, Canada introduced the *[Indian Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Act "Indian Act")* extending its control over the First Nations to education, government and legal rights.[^87]

### Early 20th century

1918 [Canadian War bond posters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada_Savings_Bond "Canada Savings Bond") depicting three Frenchwomen pulling a [harrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_harrow "Drag harrow")

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Canada_WWI_l%27Emprunt_de_la_Victoire2.jpg/250px-Canada_WWI_l%27Emprunt_de_la_Victoire2.jpg)

Because Britain still maintained control of Canada's foreign affairs under the British North America Act, 1867, its declaration of war in 1914 automatically brought [Canada into the First World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_history_of_Canada_during_World_War_I "Military history of Canada during World War I").[^88] Volunteers sent to the [Western Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Front_\(World_War_I\) "Western Front (World War I)") later became part of the [Canadian Corps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Corps "Canadian Corps"), which played a substantial role in the [Battle of Vimy Ridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Vimy_Ridge "Battle of Vimy Ridge") and other major engagements of the war.[^89] The [Conscription Crisis of 1917](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conscription_Crisis_of_1917 "Conscription Crisis of 1917") erupted when the [Unionist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unionist_Party_\(Canada\) "Unionist Party (Canada)") Cabinet's proposal to augment the military's dwindling number of active members with [conscription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conscription "Conscription") was met with vehement objections from French-speaking Quebecers.[^90] In 1919, Canada joined the [League of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/League_of_Nations "League of Nations") independently of Britain.[^89] After the 1926 [King–Byng affair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King%E2%80%93Byng_affair "King–Byng affair") raised concerns over the governor general's use of [reserve powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reserve_power "Reserve power"), the [Balfour Declaration that year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balfour_Declaration_of_1926 "Balfour Declaration of 1926") put Canada on a legal path to full sovereignty equal to Britain's, and the *[Statute of Westminster, 1931](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster,_1931 "Statute of Westminster, 1931")*, which implemented the declaration, affirmed Canada's independence.[^91]

The [Great Depression in Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Depression_in_Canada "Great Depression in Canada") during the early 1930s saw an economic downturn, leading to hardship across the country.[^92] In response to the downturn, the [Co-operative Commonwealth Federation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-operative_Commonwealth_Federation "Co-operative Commonwealth Federation") (CCF) in Saskatchewan introduced many elements of a [welfare state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welfare_state "Welfare state") (as pioneered by [Tommy Douglas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tommy_Douglas "Tommy Douglas")) in the 1940s and 1950s.[^93] On the advice of Prime Minister [William Lyon Mackenzie King](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Lyon_Mackenzie_King "William Lyon Mackenzie King"), [war with Germany was declared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_war_by_Canada#Nazi_Germany "Declaration of war by Canada") effective September 10, 1939, by King [George VI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_VI "George VI"), seven days after the United Kingdom. The delay underscored Canada's independence.[^89]

The first Canadian Army units arrived in Britain in December 1939. In all, over a million Canadians served in the armed forces during the [Second World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_World_War "Second World War").[^94] Canadian troops played important roles in many key battles of the war, including the failed 1942 [Dieppe Raid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dieppe_Raid "Dieppe Raid"), the [Allied invasion of Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Italy "Allied invasion of Italy"), the [Normandy landings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normandy_landings "Normandy landings"), the [Battle of Normandy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Overlord "Operation Overlord"), and the [Battle of the Scheldt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Scheldt "Battle of the Scheldt") in 1944.[^89] Canada provided asylum for the [Dutch monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_monarchy "Dutch monarchy") while that country was [occupied](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reichskommissariat_Niederlande "Reichskommissariat Niederlande") and is credited by the Netherlands for major contributions to [its liberation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberation_Day_\(Netherlands\) "Liberation Day (Netherlands)") from [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany").[^95] Despite another [conscription crisis in Quebec in 1944](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conscription_Crisis_of_1944 "Conscription Crisis of 1944"), Canada finished the war with a large army and strong economy.[^96]

### Contemporary era

The financial crisis of the Great Depression led the [Dominion of Newfoundland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominion_of_Newfoundland "Dominion of Newfoundland") to relinquish responsible government in 1934 and become a [Crown colony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crown_colony "Crown colony") ruled by a British governor.[^97] After two [referendums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1948_Newfoundland_referendums "1948 Newfoundland referendums"), Newfoundlanders voted to join Canada in 1949 as a province.[^98]

Canada's post-war economic growth, combined with the policies of successive Liberal governments, led to the emergence of a new [Canadian identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_identity "Canadian identity"), marked by the adoption of the [maple leaf flag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_Canada "Flag of Canada") in 1965,[^99] the implementation of [official bilingualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Official_bilingualism "Official bilingualism") (English and French) in 1969,[^100] and the institution of [official multiculturalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiculturalism#Canada "Multiculturalism") in 1971.[^101] [Socially democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socially_democratic "Socially democratic") programs were also instituted, such as [Medicare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicare_\(Canada\) "Medicare (Canada)"), the [Canada Pension Plan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada_Pension_Plan "Canada Pension Plan"), and [Canada Student Loans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Student_financial_aid_in_Canada "Student financial aid in Canada"); though, provincial governments, particularly Quebec and Alberta, opposed many of these as incursions into their jurisdictions.[^102]

![refer to caption](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Canadian_Charter_of_Rights_and_Freedoms_%28English%29.jpg/250px-Canadian_Charter_of_Rights_and_Freedoms_%28English%29.jpg)

A copy of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms 103

Finally, another series of constitutional conferences resulted in the *Canada Act 1982*, the [patriation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriation "Patriation") of Canada's constitution from the United Kingdom, concurrent with the creation of the *[Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Charter_of_Rights_and_Freedoms "Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms")*.[^104] Canada had established complete sovereignty as an independent country under [its own monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_of_Canada "Monarchy of Canada").[^105] In 1999, [Nunavut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nunavut "Nunavut") became Canada's third territory after a series of negotiations with the federal government.<sup><a href="#cite_note-101">[100]</a></sup>

At the same time, Quebec underwent profound social and economic changes through the [Quiet Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quiet_Revolution "Quiet Revolution") of the 1960s, giving birth to a secular [nationalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quebec_nationalism "Quebec nationalism") movement.[^106] The radical [Front de libération du Québec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Front_de_lib%C3%A9ration_du_Qu%C3%A9bec "Front de libération du Québec") (FLQ) ignited the [October Crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/October_Crisis "October Crisis") with a series of bombings and kidnappings in 1970,[^107] and the [sovereigntist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quebec_sovereignty_movement "Quebec sovereignty movement") [Parti Québécois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parti_Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois "Parti Québécois") was elected in 1976, organizing an [unsuccessful referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1980_Quebec_referendum "1980 Quebec referendum") on sovereignty-association in 1980. Attempts to accommodate Quebec nationalism constitutionally through the [Meech Lake Accord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meech_Lake_Accord "Meech Lake Accord") failed in 1990.[^108] This led to the formation of the [Bloc Québécois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloc_Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois "Bloc Québécois") in Quebec and the invigoration of the [Reform Party of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reform_Party_of_Canada "Reform Party of Canada") in the West.[^109] A [second referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1995_Quebec_referendum "1995 Quebec referendum") followed in 1995, in which sovereignty was rejected by a slimmer margin of 50.6 to 49.4 percent.[^110] In 1997, the [Supreme Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada "Supreme Court of Canada") ruled [unilateral secession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reference_Re_Secession_of_Quebec "Reference Re Secession of Quebec") by a province would be unconstitutional, and the [Clarity Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clarity_Act "Clarity Act") was passed by Parliament, outlining the terms of a negotiated departure from Confederation.[^108]

In addition to the issues of Quebec sovereignty, a number of crises shook Canadian society in the late 1980s and early 1990s. These included the explosion of [Air India Flight 182](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_India_Flight_182 "Air India Flight 182") in 1985, the largest mass murder in Canadian history;[^111] the [École Polytechnique massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique_massacre "École Polytechnique massacre") in 1989, a [university shooting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/School_shooting "School shooting") targeting female students;[^112] and the [Oka Crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oka_Crisis "Oka Crisis") of 1990,[^113] the first of a number of violent confrontations between provincial governments and Indigenous groups.[^114] Canada joined the [Gulf War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_War "Gulf War") in 1990 and was active in [several peacekeeping missions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Canadian_peacekeeping_missions "List of Canadian peacekeeping missions") in the 1990s, including operations in the [Balkans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balkans "Balkans") during and after the [Yugoslav Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yugoslav_Wars "Yugoslav Wars"),[^115] and in [Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalia "Somalia"), resulting in an incident that has been described as " [the darkest era in the history of the Canadian military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalia_affair "Somalia affair") ".[^116] Canada sent [troops to Afghanistan in 2001](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada_in_the_War_in_Afghanistan "Canada in the War in Afghanistan"), resulting in the largest amount of [Canadian deaths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Forces_casualties_in_Afghanistan "Canadian Forces casualties in Afghanistan") for any single military mission since the [Korean War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada_in_the_Korean_War "Canada in the Korean War") in the early 1950s.[^117]

In 2011, Canadian forces participated in the [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") -led intervention into the [Libyan Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Libyan_Civil_War "First Libyan Civil War") [^118] and also became involved in battling the [Islamic State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_State "Islamic State") insurgency in Iraq in the mid-2010s.[^119] The [COVID-19 pandemic in Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_Canada "COVID-19 pandemic in Canada") began on January 27, 2020, causing widespread social and economic disruption.[^120] In 2021, [possible gravesites of Indigenous children](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Indian_residential_school_gravesites "Canadian Indian residential school gravesites") were found near former [Canadian residential schools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Indian_residential_school_system "Canadian Indian residential school system"), highlighting the [cultural genocide against Indigenous peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_genocide_of_Indigenous_peoples "Canadian genocide of Indigenous peoples").[^121] A [trade war involving the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_United_States_trade_war_with_Canada_and_Mexico "2025 United States trade war with Canada and Mexico") began on February 1, 2025, when U.S. president [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump") signed [orders imposing tariffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Trump_tariffs "Second Trump tariffs") on goods entering the United States, alongside rhetoric suggesting the [annexation of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Movements_for_the_annexation_of_Canada_to_the_United_States#Proposals_to_annex_Canada_by_Donald_Trump "Movements for the annexation of Canada to the United States").[^122]

## Geography

![refer to caption](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Canada_topo.jpg/330px-Canada_topo.jpg)

A topographic map of Canada, in polar projection (for 90° W), showing elevations shaded from green to brown (higher)

By total area (including its waters), Canada is the [second-largest country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_area "List of countries and dependencies by area").[^123] By land area alone, Canada ranks fourth, due to having the world's largest area of [fresh water lakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_lakes_of_Canada "List of lakes of Canada").[^124] Stretching from the Atlantic Ocean in the east, along the Arctic Ocean to the north, and to the Pacific Ocean in the west, the country encompasses 9,984,670 square kilometres (3,855,100 sq mi) of territory.[^125] Canada also has vast maritime terrain, with the world's longest coastline of 243,042 kilometres (151,019 mi) [^126] In addition to sharing [the world's largest land border with the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_border "Canada–United States border") —spanning 8,891 kilometres (5,525 mi) [^2] —Canada shares a short (1,280 metres (4,200 ft)) land border with [Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland "Greenland") (and hence the [Kingdom of Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Denmark "Kingdom of Denmark")) to the northeast, on [Hans Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Island "Hans Island"),[^127] and a [maritime boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maritime_boundary "Maritime boundary") with [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France") 's [overseas collectivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_collectivity "Overseas collectivity") of [Saint Pierre and Miquelon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Pierre_and_Miquelon "Saint Pierre and Miquelon") to the southeast.[^128] Canada is also home to the world's northernmost settlement, [Canadian Forces Station Alert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CFS_Alert "CFS Alert"), on the northern tip of [Ellesmere Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellesmere_Island "Ellesmere Island") —latitude 82.5°N—which lies 817 kilometres (508 mi) from the North Pole.[^129] In latitude, Canada's most northerly point of land is [Cape Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_Columbia "Cape Columbia") in Nunavut at 83°6′41″N, with its southern extreme at [Middle Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Island_\(Lake_Erie\) "Middle Island (Lake Erie)") in Lake Erie at 41°40′53″N. In longitude, Canada's land extends from [Cape Spear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_Spear "Cape Spear"), Newfoundland, at 52°37′W, to [Mount St. Elias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Saint_Elias "Mount Saint Elias"), Yukon Territory, at 141°W.[^130]

Canada can be divided into seven physiographic regions: the [Canadian Shield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Shield "Canadian Shield"), the [Interior Plains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_Plains "Interior Plains"), the [Great Lakes–St. Lawrence Lowlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Lakes%E2%80%93St._Lawrence_Lowlands "Great Lakes–St. Lawrence Lowlands"), the [Appalachian region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains "Appalachian Mountains"), the [Western Cordillera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Cordillera_\(Canada\) "Pacific Cordillera (Canada)"), [Hudson Bay Lowlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hudson_Bay_Lowlands "Hudson Bay Lowlands"), and the [Arctic Archipelago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_Archipelago "Arctic Archipelago").[^131] [Boreal forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiga "Taiga") prevail throughout the country, ice is prominent in [northern Arctic regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Canada "Northern Canada") and through the [Rocky Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocky_Mountains "Rocky Mountains"), and the relatively flat [Canadian Prairies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Prairies "Canadian Prairies") in the southwest facilitate productive agriculture.[^125] The Great Lakes feed the [St. Lawrence River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Lawrence_River "St. Lawrence River") (in the southeast) where the lowlands host much of Canada's economic output.[^125] Canada has over 2,000,000 lakes—563 of which are larger than 100 square kilometres (39 sq mi)—containing much of the world's [fresh water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fresh_water "Fresh water").[^132] There are also fresh-water glaciers in the [Canadian Rockies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Rockies "Canadian Rockies"), the [Coast Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coast_Mountains "Coast Mountains"), and the [Arctic Cordillera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_Cordillera "Arctic Cordillera").[^133] [Canada is geologically active](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology_of_Canada "Geology of Canada"), having [many earthquakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_earthquakes_in_Canada "List of earthquakes in Canada") and [potentially active volcanoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanism_of_Canada "Volcanism of Canada").[^134]

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Canada_K%C3%B6ppen.svg/330px-Canada_K%C3%B6ppen.svg.png)

Köppen climate classification types of Canada

Average winter and summer high [temperatures across Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature_in_Canada "Temperature in Canada") vary from region to region. Winters can be harsh in many parts of the country, particularly in the interior and Prairie provinces, which experience a [continental climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_climate "Continental climate"), where daily average temperatures are near −15 [°C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius "Celsius") (5 [°F](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahrenheit "Fahrenheit")), but can drop below −40 °C (−40 °F) with severe [wind chills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wind_chill "Wind chill").[^135] In non-coastal regions, snow can cover the ground for almost six months of the year, while in parts of the north snow can persist year-round. Coastal British Columbia has a temperate climate, with a mild and rainy winter. On the east and west coasts, average high temperatures are generally in the low 20s °C (70s °F), while between the coasts, the average summer high temperature ranges from 25 to 30 °C (77 to 86 °F), with temperatures in some interior locations occasionally exceeding 40 °C (104 °F).[^136]

Much of [Northern Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Canada "Northern Canada") is covered by ice and [permafrost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permafrost "Permafrost"). The future of the permafrost is uncertain because the Arctic has been warming at three times the global average as a result of [climate change in Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Canada "Climate change in Canada").[^137] Canada's annual average temperature over land has risen by 1.7 °C (3.1 °F), with changes ranging from 1.1 to 2.3 °C (2.0 to 4.1 °F) in various regions, since 1948.[^125] The rate of warming has been higher across the North and in the Prairies.[^138] In the southern regions of Canada, [air pollution from both Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_pollution_in_Canada "Air pollution in Canada") and the United States—caused by metal smelting, burning coal to power utilities, and vehicle emissions—has resulted in [acid rain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_rain "Acid rain"), which has severely impacted waterways, forest growth, and agricultural productivity.[^139] Canada is one of the largest [greenhouse gas emitters globally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_greenhouse_gas_emissions "List of countries by greenhouse gas emissions"),[^140] with emissions increased by 16.5 percent between 1990 and 2022.[^141]

### Biodiversity

![Map showing Canada divided into different ecozones](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Terrestrial_ecozones_and_ecoprovinces_of_Canada%2C_2017.gif/330px-Terrestrial_ecozones_and_ecoprovinces_of_Canada%2C_2017.gif)

Terrestrial ecozones and ecoprovinces of Canada. Ecozones are identified with a unique colour. Ecoprovinces are subdivisions of ecozones and are identified with a unique numeric code. 142

Canada is divided into [15 terrestrial and five marine ecozones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecozones_of_Canada "Ecozones of Canada").[^143] These ecozones encompass over 80,000 classified species of [Canadian wildlife](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildlife_of_Canada "Wildlife of Canada"), with an equal number yet to be formally recognized or discovered.[^144] Although Canada has a low percentage of [endemic species compared to other countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megadiverse_countries "Megadiverse countries"),[^145] due to human activities, [invasive species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildlife_of_Canada#Invasive_species "Wildlife of Canada"), and [environmental issues in the country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_issues_in_Canada "Environmental issues in Canada"), there are currently more than [800 species at risk of being lost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Wildlife_Species_at_Risk_\(Canada\) "List of Wildlife Species at Risk (Canada)").[^146] About 65 percent of Canada's resident species are considered "Secure".[^147] Over half of Canada's landscape is intact and relatively free of human development.[^148] The [boreal forest of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boreal_forest_of_Canada "Boreal forest of Canada") is considered to be the largest [intact forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intact_forest_landscape "Intact forest landscape") on Earth, with approximately 3,000,000 square kilometres (1,200,000 sq mi) undisturbed by roads, cities or industry.[^149] Since the end of the last [glacial period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glacial_period "Glacial period"), Canada has consisted of [eight distinct forest regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forests_of_Canada#Regions "Forests of Canada").[^150]

Approximately 12.1 percent of the nation's landmass and freshwater are [conservation areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Wildlife_Area "National Wildlife Area"), including 11.4 percent designated as [protected areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protected_areas_of_Canada "Protected areas of Canada").[^151] Approximately 13.8 percent of its territorial waters are conserved, including 8.9 percent designated as protected areas.[^151] Canada's first [National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Parks_of_Canada "National Parks of Canada"), [Banff National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banff_National_Park "Banff National Park") was established in 1885.[^152] Canada's oldest provincial park, [Algonquin Provincial Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algonquin_Provincial_Park "Algonquin Provincial Park") was established in 1893.[^153] Established in 2015, [Lake Superior National Marine Conservation Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Superior_National_Marine_Conservation_Area "Lake Superior National Marine Conservation Area") is the world's largest freshwater protected area.[^154] Canada's largest national wildlife region, established in 2018, is the [Scott Islands Marine National Wildlife Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_Islands_Marine_National_Wildlife_Area "Scott Islands Marine National Wildlife Area").[^155]

## Government and politics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Parliament_Hill_from_a_Hot_Air_Balloon%2C_Ottawa%2C_Ontario%2C_Canada%2C_Y2K_%287173715788%29.jpg/250px-Parliament_Hill_from_a_Hot_Air_Balloon%2C_Ottawa%2C_Ontario%2C_Canada%2C_Y2K_%287173715788%29.jpg)

Aerial view of Canadian Parliament Buildings and their surroundings

Canada is described as a " [full democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy_Index "Democracy Index") ",[^156] with a tradition of [liberalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberalism "Liberalism"),[^157] and an [egalitarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egalitarianism "Egalitarianism"),[^158] [moderate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_moderate "Political moderate") political ideology.[^159] Since the 1960s, an emphasis on [social justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_justice "Social justice") has been a distinguishing element of Canada's political culture.[^160] [Peace, order, and good government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peace,_order,_and_good_government "Peace, order, and good government"), alongside an [Implied Bill of Rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implied_Bill_of_Rights "Implied Bill of Rights"), are founding principles of [Canadian federalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_federalism "Canadian federalism").[^161]

At the federal level, Canada has been dominated by two relatively [centrist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrism "Centrism") parties practising "brokerage politics":[^3] the [centre-left](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre-left_politics "Centre-left politics") leaning [Liberal Party of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada "Liberal Party of Canada") [^164] and the [centre-right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre-right "Centre-right") leaning [Conservative Party of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_Canada "Conservative Party of Canada") (or [its predecessors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_Canada#Predecessors "Conservative Party of Canada")).[^165] The historically predominant Liberals position themselves at the centre of the political scale.[^165] Five parties had representatives elected to Parliament in the [2025 election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Canadian_federal_election "2025 Canadian federal election") —the Liberals, who formed a minority government that transitioned into a majority government in April 2026 owing to [defections from other parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crossing_the_floor "Crossing the floor"); the Conservatives, who became the [Official Opposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Official_Opposition_\(Canada\) "Official Opposition (Canada)"); the [Bloc Québécois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloc_Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois "Bloc Québécois"); the [New Democratic Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Democratic_Party "New Democratic Party") (occupying the [left](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-wing_politics "Left-wing politics") [^166]); and the [Green Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada "Green Party of Canada").[^167] [Far-right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far-right "Far-right") and [far-left](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far-left "Far-left") politics have never been a prominent force in Canadian society.[^168]

Canada has a [parliamentary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system") within the context of a [constitutional monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy") —the [monarchy of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_of_Canada "Monarchy of Canada") being the foundation of the executive, [legislative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislative "Legislative"), and [judicial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judicial "Judicial") branches.[^169] The [reigning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reign "Reign") monarch is also monarch of [14 other sovereign Commonwealth countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_realm "Commonwealth realm") [^170] and [Canada's 10 provinces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_in_the_Canadian_provinces "Monarchy in the Canadian provinces"). The monarch appoints a representative, the [governor general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada "Governor General of Canada"), on the [advice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advice_\(constitutional_law\) "Advice (constitutional law)") of the [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Canada "Prime Minister of Canada"), to carry out most of their ceremonial royal duties.[^171]
