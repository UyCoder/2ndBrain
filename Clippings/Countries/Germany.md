**Germany**,[^4] officially the **Federal Republic of Germany**,[^5] is a country in [Western](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Europe "Western Europe") and [Central Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Europe "Central Europe"). It lies between the [Baltic Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_Sea "Baltic Sea") and the [North Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Sea "North Sea") to the north with the [Alps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alps "Alps") to the south. Its sixteen [constituent states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_of_Germany "States of Germany") have [a total population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Germany "Demographics of Germany") of over 82 million, making it the most populous [member state of the European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union "Member state of the European Union") (EU). Germany borders [Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark "Denmark") to the north; [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland "Poland") and the [Czech Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_Republic "Czech Republic") to the east; [Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austria "Austria") and [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland") to the south; and [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France"), [Luxembourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luxembourg "Luxembourg"), [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium "Belgium"), and the [Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands") to the west. The [nation's capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_of_Germany "Capital of Germany") and [most populous city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Germany_by_population "List of cities in Germany by population") is [Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin "Berlin") and its main financial centre is [Frankfurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankfurt "Frankfurt"); the largest urban area is the [Ruhr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruhr "Ruhr").

Settlement in the territory of modern Germany began in the [Lower Paleolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_Paleolithic "Lower Paleolithic"), with various tribes inhabiting it from the [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") onward, chiefly the [Celts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celts "Celts"), and [Germanic tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanic_peoples "Germanic peoples") inhabiting the north. [Romans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") named the area [Germania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germania "Germania"). In 962, the [Kingdom of Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany "Kingdom of Germany") formed the bulk of the [Holy Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire "Holy Roman Empire"). During the 16th century, [northern German regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Germany "Northern Germany") became the centre of the [Protestant Reformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reformation "Reformation"). Following the [Napoleonic Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars "Napoleonic Wars") and [the dissolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire "Dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire") of the Holy Roman Empire in 1806, the [German Confederation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Confederation "German Confederation") was formed in 1815.

[Unification of Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unification_of_Germany "Unification of Germany") into the modern [nation-state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation_state "Nation state"), [led](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_German_Confederation_Treaty "North German Confederation Treaty") by [Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia "Kingdom of Prussia"), [established](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation_of_the_German_Empire "Proclamation of the German Empire") the [German Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Empire "German Empire") in 1871. After [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") and [a revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Revolution_of_1918%E2%80%931919 "German Revolution of 1918–1919"), the Empire was replaced by the [Weimar Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_Republic "Weimar Republic"). The [Nazi rise to power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Hitler%27s_rise_to_power "Adolf Hitler's rise to power") in 1933 led to the establishment of [a totalitarian dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany"), [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), and [the Holocaust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Holocaust "The Holocaust"). In 1949, [after the war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End_of_World_War_II_in_Europe "End of World War II in Europe") and [Allied occupation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allied-occupied_Germany "Allied-occupied Germany"), Germany was organised into [two separate polities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_German_relations "Inner German relations") with limited sovereignty: the Federal Republic of Germany (FRG), or [West Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Germany "West Germany"), and the German Democratic Republic (GDR), or [East Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Germany "East Germany"). The FRG was a founding member of the [European Economic Community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Economic_Community "European Economic Community"), while the GDR was a communist [Eastern Bloc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Bloc "Eastern Bloc") state and a founding member of the [Warsaw Pact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warsaw_Pact "Warsaw Pact"). After [the fall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peaceful_Revolution "Peaceful Revolution") of the [communist led-government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_Unity_Party_of_Germany "Socialist Unity Party of Germany") in East Germany, [German reunification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_reunification "German reunification") saw the [former East German states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_states_of_Germany "New states of Germany") join the FRG on [3 October 1990](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Unity_Day "German Unity Day").

Germany is a [developed country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country") with [a strong economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Germany "Economy of Germany"); it has the [largest economy in Europe by nominal GDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states_in_Europe_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of sovereign states in Europe by GDP (nominal)"). As a major force in several industrial, [scientific and technological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_and_technology_in_Germany "Science and technology in Germany") sectors, Germany is both the world's [third-largest exporter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_exports "List of countries by exports") and [third-largest importer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_imports "List of countries by imports"). Widely considered a [great power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_power "Great power"), Germany is part of [multiple international organisations and forums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_Germany#International_organizations "Foreign relations of Germany"). It has the [third-highest number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites_by_country "World Heritage Sites by country") of UNESCO [World Heritage Sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Site "World Heritage Site"): [55](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_Germany "List of World Heritage Sites in Germany"), of which 52 are cultural.

## Etymology

The [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language") word *Germany* derives from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") *Germania*, which came into use after [Julius Caesar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Caesar "Julius Caesar") adopted it for the peoples east of the [Rhine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhine "Rhine").[^18] The [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language") term *Deutschland*, originally *diutisciu land* ('the German lands'), is derived from *[deutsch](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/deutsch#German "wikt:deutsch")* ([cf.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cf. "Cf.") *[Dutch](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Dutch#English "wikt:Dutch")*), which descended from [Old High German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_High_German "Old High German") *diutisc* 'of the people' (from *diot* or *diota* 'people'), originally used to distinguish the [language of the common people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vernacular "Vernacular") from Latin and [its Romance descendants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romance_languages "Romance languages"). This in turn descends from [Proto-Germanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language "Proto-Germanic language") \* *[þiudiskaz](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/%C3%BEiudiskaz "wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/þiudiskaz")* 'of the people' (see also the Latinised form *[Theodiscus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodiscus "Theodiscus")*), derived from \* *[þeudō](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/%C3%BEeud%C5%8D "wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/þeudō")*, descended from [Proto-Indo-European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language "Proto-Indo-European language") \* *[tewtéh₂-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/tewt%C3%A9h%E2%82%82 "wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/tewtéh₂")* 'people', from which the word *[Teutons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teutons "Teutons")* also originates.[^19]

## History

### Prehistory

Ancient humans were present in Germany at least 600,000 years ago.[^20] The first non-modern human fossil (the [Neanderthal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neanderthal "Neanderthal")) was discovered in the [Neander Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neandertal_\(valley\) "Neandertal (valley)").[^21] Similarly dated evidence of modern humans has been found in the [Swabian Jura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caves_and_Ice_Age_Art_in_the_Swabian_Jura "Caves and Ice Age Art in the Swabian Jura"), including 42,000-year-old [flutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleolithic_flute "Paleolithic flute") which are the oldest musical instruments ever found,[^22] the 40,000-year-old [Lion Man](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lion-man "Lion-man"),[^23] and the 41,000-year-old [Venus of Hohle Fels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus_of_Hohle_Fels "Venus of Hohle Fels").[^24] [^25]

### Germanic tribes, Roman frontier and the Frankish Empire

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Trier_012.JPG/250px-Trier_012.JPG)

Basilica of Constantine in Trier ( Augusta Treverorum ), built in the 4th century

From southern [Scandinavia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scandinavia "Scandinavia") and [northern Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Germany "Northern Germany"), the [Germanic peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanic_peoples "Germanic peoples") expanded south, east, and west, coming into contact with the [Celtic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celts "Celts"), [Iranian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iranian_peoples "Iranian peoples"), [Baltic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balts "Balts"), and [Slavic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Slavs "Early Slavs") tribes.[^26] [^27] Southern Germany was inhabited by Celtic-speaking peoples, who belonged to the wider [La Tène culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture "La Tène culture"). They were later assimilated by the Germanic conquerors.[^28]

Under [Augustus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustus "Augustus"), the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") began to invade lands inhabited by the Germanic tribes, creating a short-lived Roman province of [Germania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germania_Antiqua "Germania Antiqua") between the Rhine and [Elbe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elbe "Elbe") rivers. In 9 AD, three [Roman legions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_legion "Roman legion") were defeated by [Arminius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arminius "Arminius") in the [Battle of the Teutoburg Forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Teutoburg_Forest "Battle of the Teutoburg Forest").[^29] [^30] The outcome of this battle dissuaded the Romans from their ambition of conquering [Germania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germania "Germania") and is thus considered one of the most important events in [European history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_history "European history").[^31] By 100 AD, when [Tacitus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tacitus "Tacitus") wrote *[Germania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germania_\(book\) "Germania (book)")*, Germanic tribes had settled along the Rhine and the Danube (the [Limes Germanicus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limes_Germanicus "Limes Germanicus")), occupying most of modern Germany.[^32] However, [Baden-Württemberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baden_W%C3%BCrttemberg "Baden Württemberg"), southern [Bavaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bavaria "Bavaria"), southern [Hesse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesse "Hesse") and the western [Rhineland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhineland "Rhineland") had [been incorporated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanic_Wars "Germanic Wars") into [Roman provinces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_province "Roman province").[^33] [^34] [^35]

Around 260, Germanic peoples broke into Roman-controlled lands.[^36] After the invasion of the [Huns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huns "Huns") in 375, and with the decline of Rome from 395, Germanic tribes moved farther southwest: the Franks established the [Frankish Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankish_Kingdom "Frankish Kingdom") and pushed east to subjugate [Saxony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxony "Duchy of Saxony") and [Bavaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_Bavaria "Duchy of Bavaria"). Areas of modern eastern Germany were inhabited by [Western Slavic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Slavs "West Slavs") tribes.[^33]

### East Francia and the Holy Roman Empire

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/East_Francia_843.svg/250px-East_Francia_843.svg.png)

East Francia in 843

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Lucas_Cranach_d.%C3%84._-_Martin_Luther%2C_1528_%28Veste_Coburg%29.jpg/250px-Lucas_Cranach_d.%C3%84._-_Martin_Luther%2C_1528_%28Veste_Coburg%29.jpg)

Martin Luther, born in Eisleben in 1483, challenged the indulgences of the Catholic Church, giving rise to the Reformation and Protestantism.

[Charlemagne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlemagne "Charlemagne") founded the [Carolingian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_Empire "Carolingian Empire") in 800; it was [divided in 843](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun "Treaty of Verdun").[^37] The eastern successor kingdom of [East Francia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Francia "East Francia") stretched from the Rhine in the west to the Elbe river in the east and from the North Sea to the Alps.[^37] Subsequently, the [Kingdom of Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany "Kingdom of Germany") and the Holy Roman Empire emerged from it. The [Ottonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottonian "Ottonian") rulers (919–1024) consolidated several major [duchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stem_duchy "Stem duchy").[^38] In 996, [Gregory V](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_V "Pope Gregory V") became the first German Pope, appointed by his cousin [Otto III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_III "Otto III"), whom he shortly after crowned Holy Roman Emperor. The Holy Roman Empire absorbed northern Italy and [Burgundy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burgundy_\(historical_region\) "Burgundy (historical region)") under the [Salian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salian "Salian") emperors (1024–1125), although the emperors lost power through the [Investiture Controversy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investiture_Controversy "Investiture Controversy").[^39]

Under the [Hohenstaufen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Hohenstaufen "House of Hohenstaufen") emperors (1138–1254), German princes encouraged German settlement to the south and east (*[Ostsiedlung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostsiedlung "Ostsiedlung")*).[^40] Members of the [Hanseatic League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanseatic_League "Hanseatic League"), mostly north German towns, prospered in the expansion of trade.[^41] The population declined starting with the [Great Famine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Famine_of_1315%E2%80%931317 "Great Famine of 1315–1317") in 1315, followed by the [Black Death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Death "Black Death") of 1348–1350.[^42] The [Golden Bull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_1356 "Golden Bull of 1356") issued in 1356 provided the constitutional structure of the Empire and codified the election of the emperor by seven [prince-electors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince-elector "Prince-elector").[^43]

[Johannes Gutenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg "Johannes Gutenberg") introduced moveable-type printing to Europe, laying the basis for the [democratisation of knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratization_of_knowledge "Democratization of knowledge").[^44] Following [Martin Luther](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Luther "Martin Luther") 's 1517 Protestant Reformation, bolstered by [his standardised Bible translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luther_Bible "Luther Bible"), led to the 1555 [Peace of Augsburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peace_of_Augsburg "Peace of Augsburg"), which recognised [Lutheranism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lutheranism "Lutheranism") under the principle of *[cuius regio, eius religio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuius_regio,_eius_religio "Cuius regio, eius religio")* (ruler's faith dictates subjects' faith).[^45] From the [Cologne War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cologne_War "Cologne War") through the [Thirty Years' Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War "Thirty Years' War") (1618–1648), religious conflict devastated German lands and significantly reduced the population.[^46] [^47]

The [Peace of Westphalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia "Peace of Westphalia") ended religious warfare among the [Imperial Estates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Estate "Imperial Estate").[^46] The legal system initiated by a series of [Imperial Reforms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Reform "Imperial Reform") (approximately 1495–1555) provided for considerable local autonomy and a stronger [Imperial Diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Diet_\(Holy_Roman_Empire\) "Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)").[^48] The [House of Habsburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Habsburg "House of Habsburg") held the imperial crown from 1438 until the death of [Charles VI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor "Charles VI, Holy Roman Emperor") in 1740. Following the [War of the Austrian Succession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Austrian_Succession "War of the Austrian Succession") and the [Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Aix-la-Chapelle_\(1748\) "Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle (1748)"), Charles VI's daughter [Maria Theresa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maria_Theresa "Maria Theresa") ruled as [empress consort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queen_consort "Queen consort") when her husband, [Francis I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor "Francis I, Holy Roman Emperor"), became emperor.[^49] [^50]

From 1740, [dualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_dualism "German dualism") between the Austrian [Habsburg monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habsburg_monarchy "Habsburg monarchy") and the [Kingdom of Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia "Kingdom of Prussia") dominated German history. In 1772, 1793, and 1795, Prussia and Austria, along with the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"), agreed to the [Partitions of Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland "Partitions of Poland").[^51] [^52] During the period of the [French Revolutionary Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars "French Revolutionary Wars"), the [Napoleonic era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_era "Napoleonic era") and the subsequent [final meeting of the Imperial Diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Final_Recess_of_the_Reichsdeputation "Final Recess of the Reichsdeputation"), most of the [Free Imperial Cities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_Imperial_Cities "Free Imperial Cities") were annexed by dynastic territories; the ecclesiastical territories were secularised and annexed. In 1806 the *Imperium* was dissolved; France, Russia, Prussia, and the Habsburgs (Austria) competed for hegemony in the German states during the [Napoleonic Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars "Napoleonic Wars").[^53]

### German Confederation and Empire

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/German_Confederation_%281815%29.svg/250px-German_Confederation_%281815%29.svg.png)

The German Confederation in 1815

Following the fall of [Napoleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon "Napoleon"), the [Congress of Vienna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna "Congress of Vienna") founded the German Confederation, a loose league of [39 sovereign states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_of_the_German_Confederation "States of the German Confederation"). The appointment of the [emperor of Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_of_Austria "Emperor of Austria") as the permanent president reflected the Congress' rejection of [Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussia "Prussia") 's rising influence. Disagreement within [restoration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concert_of_Europe "Concert of Europe") politics partly led to the rise of [liberal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberalism_in_Germany "Liberalism in Germany") movements, followed by new measures of repression by Austrian statesman [Klemens von Metternich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klemens_von_Metternich "Klemens von Metternich").[^54] [^55] The *[Zollverein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zollverein "Zollverein")*, a tariff union, furthered economic unity.[^56] In light of [revolutionary movements in Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848 "Revolutions of 1848"), intellectuals and commoners started the [revolutions of 1848 in the German states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_revolutions_of_1848%E2%80%931849 "German revolutions of 1848–1849"), raising the German question. King [Frederick William IV of Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_William_IV_of_Prussia "Frederick William IV of Prussia") was offered the title of emperor, but with a loss of power; he rejected the crown and the proposed constitution, a temporary setback for the movement.[^57]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Berlin_Nationaldenkmal_Kaiser_Wilhelm_mit_Schloss_1900.jpg/250px-Berlin_Nationaldenkmal_Kaiser_Wilhelm_mit_Schloss_1900.jpg)

Berlin Palace, the main residence of the Kings of Prussia and German Emperors

King [William I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_I,_German_Emperor "William I, German Emperor") appointed [Otto von Bismarck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck "Otto von Bismarck") as the [Minister President of Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_President_of_Prussia "Minister President of Prussia") in 1862. Bismarck successfully concluded the [war with Denmark in 1864](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Schleswig_War "Second Schleswig War"); the subsequent decisive Prussian victory in the [Austro-Prussian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War "Austro-Prussian War") of 1866 enabled him to create the [North German Confederation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_German_Confederation "North German Confederation") which excluded [Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austrian_Empire "Austrian Empire"). After the defeat of France in the [Franco-Prussian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War "Franco-Prussian War"), the German princes proclaimed the founding of the German Empire in 1871. Prussia was the dominant constituent state of the new empire; the King of Prussia ruled as its [Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Emperor "German Emperor") (*Kaiser*), and Berlin became its capital.[^58] [^59]

In the *[Gründerzeit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gr%C3%BCnderzeit "Gründerzeit")* period following the unification of Germany, Bismarck's foreign policy as [chancellor of Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany "Chancellor of Germany") secured Germany's position as a great nation by forging alliances and avoiding war.[^59] However, under [Wilhelm II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_II "Wilhelm II"), Germany took an [imperialistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Imperialism "New Imperialism") course, leading to friction with neighbouring countries.[^60] [A dual alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_Alliance_\(1879\) "Dual Alliance (1879)") was created with the [multinational realm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multinational_state "Multinational state") of [Austria-Hungary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austria-Hungary "Austria-Hungary"); the [Triple Alliance of 1882](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_Alliance_\(1882\) "Triple Alliance (1882)") included Italy. Britain, France and Russia also concluded alliances to protect against Habsburg interference with Russian interests in the Balkans or German interference against France.[^61] At the [Berlin Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Conference "Berlin Conference") in 1884, Germany claimed several [colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_former_German_colonies "List of former German colonies") including [German East Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_East_Africa "German East Africa"), [German South West Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_South_West_Africa "German South West Africa"), [Togoland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Togoland "Togoland"), and [Kamerun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kamerun "Kamerun").[^62] Germany later expanded its colonial empire to include holdings in the Pacific and [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China").[^63] The colonial government in South West Africa (present-day [Namibia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Namibia "Namibia")), from 1904 to 1908, carried out the [annihilation of the local Herero and Nama peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herero_and_Nama_genocide "Herero and Nama genocide") as punishment for an uprising;[^64] [^65] [^66] this was the 20th century's first [genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genocide "Genocide").[^65]

[The assassination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_of_Archduke_Franz_Ferdinand_of_Austria "Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria") of [Austria's crown prince](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archduke_Franz_Ferdinand_of_Austria "Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria") on 28 June 1914 provided the pretext for Austria-Hungary to attack Serbia and trigger [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"). After four years of warfare, in which approximately two million German soldiers were killed,[^67] [a general armistice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armistice_with_Germany "Armistice with Germany") ended the fighting. In the [German Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Revolution "German Revolution") (November 1918), Wilhelm II and the ruling princes [abdicated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdication "Abdication") their positions.

### Weimar Republic and Nazi Germany

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Hitler_portrait_crop.jpg/250px-Hitler_portrait_crop.jpg)

Adolf Hitler, dictatorial Führer of Nazi Germany from 1933 until his suicide in 1945

On 9 November 1918, [Philipp Scheidemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philipp_Scheidemann "Philipp Scheidemann"), a Social Democrat, [proclaimed the German Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation_of_the_republic_in_Germany "Proclamation of the republic in Germany"), marking Germany's transition to a [federal democratic state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_republic "Federal republic"). Germany's new leadership signed the [Treaty of Versailles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles "Treaty of Versailles") in 1919, accepting defeat by the [Allies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I "Allies of World War I"). Germans perceived the treaty as humiliating, which was seen by historians as influential in the rise of [Adolf Hitler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Hitler "Adolf Hitler").[^68] Germany lost around 13% of its European territory and ceded all of its colonial possessions.[^69]

On 11 August 1919, President [Friedrich Ebert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Ebert "Friedrich Ebert") signed the democratic [Weimar Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_Constitution "Weimar Constitution").[^70] [Communists briefly seized power in Bavaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bavarian_Soviet_Republic "Bavarian Soviet Republic") and a few larger cities, while conservative elements failed to overthrow the central government in the 1920 [Kapp Putsch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kapp_Putsch "Kapp Putsch"). The [occupation of the Ruhr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupation_of_the_Ruhr "Occupation of the Ruhr") by Belgian and French troops and a period of [hyperinflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperinflation_in_the_Weimar_Republic "Hyperinflation in the Weimar Republic") followed. A [plan to restructure Germany's war reparations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dawes_Plan "Dawes Plan") and the creation of [a new currency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Rentenmark "German Rentenmark") in 1924 helped stabilise the government and ushered in the [Golden Twenties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Twenties "Golden Twenties"), an era of artistic innovation and liberal cultural life.[^71] [^72] [^73]

In 1929, the [Great Depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Depression "Great Depression") hit Germany, and by 1932 the unemployment rate had risen to nearly 30%.[^74] The [Nazi Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Party "Nazi Party") led by Adolf Hitler became the largest party in the Reichstag after the [election of July 1932](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/July_1932_German_federal_election "July 1932 German federal election"), and [President Hindenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_Hindenburg "President Hindenburg") appointed Hitler chancellor on 30 January 1933.[^75] After the [Reichstag fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reichstag_fire "Reichstag fire"), [a decree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reichstag_Fire_Decree "Reichstag Fire Decree") abrogated basic [civil rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_rights "Civil rights"), and the first [Nazi concentration camp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camp "Nazi concentration camp") opened.[^76] [^77] On 23 March 1933, the [Enabling Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enabling_Act_of_1933 "Enabling Act of 1933") gave Hitler unrestricted legislative power, overriding the constitution,[^78] and marked the beginning of Nazi Germany. His government established a centralised [totalitarian state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarian_state "Totalitarian state"), [withdrew from the League of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1933_German_referendum "1933 German referendum"), and dramatically increased [Germany's rearmament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany%27s_rearmament "Germany's rearmament").[^79] A government-sponsored programme for economic renewal focused on public works, the most famous of which was the *[Autobahn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reichsautobahn "Reichsautobahn")*.[^80]

In 1935, the regime withdrew from the Treaty of Versailles and introduced the [Nuremberg Laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuremberg_Laws "Nuremberg Laws") which targeted [Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jews "Jews") and other [minorities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minority_group "Minority group").[^81] Germany also reacquired control of the [Saarland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territory_of_the_Saar_Basin "Territory of the Saar Basin") in 1935,[^82] [remilitarised the Rhineland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remilitarization_of_the_Rhineland "Remilitarization of the Rhineland") in 1936, [annexed Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anschluss "Anschluss") in 1938, [annexed the Sudetenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudetenland#Sudetenland_as_part_of_Nazi_Germany "Sudetenland") in 1938 with the [Munich Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munich_Agreement "Munich Agreement"), and, in violation of the agreement, [occupied Czechoslovakia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_occupation_of_Czechoslovakia "German occupation of Czechoslovakia") in March 1939.[^83] *[Kristallnacht](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kristallnacht "Kristallnacht")* (Night of Broken Glass) saw the burning of synagogues, the destruction of Jewish businesses, and mass arrests of Jewish people.[^84]

In August 1939, [Hitler's government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hitler%27s_government "Hitler's government") negotiated the [Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molotov%E2%80%93Ribbentrop_Pact "Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact") that divided Eastern Europe into German and [Soviet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") spheres of influence.[^85] On 1 September 1939, Germany [invaded Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasion_of_Poland "Invasion of Poland"), beginning [World War II in Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_theatre_of_World_War_II "European theatre of World War II");[^86] Britain and France declared war on Germany on 3 September.[^87] In spring 1940, Germany [conquered Denmark and Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Weser%C3%BCbung "Operation Weserübung"), [the Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_invasion_of_the_Netherlands "German invasion of the Netherlands"), [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_invasion_of_Belgium_\(1940\) "German invasion of Belgium (1940)"), [Luxembourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_invasion_of_Luxembourg "German invasion of Luxembourg"), and [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_France "Battle of France"). The British repelled German air attacks in the [Battle of Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Britain "Battle of Britain") in the same year. In 1941, German troops [invaded Yugoslavia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasion_of_Yugoslavia "Invasion of Yugoslavia"), [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Greece "Battle of Greece") and the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa "Operation Barbarossa"), and Germany declared war on the United States. By 1942, Germany and its allies controlled most of [continental Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German-occupied_Europe "German-occupied Europe") and [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa"), but following the Soviet victory at the [Battle of Stalingrad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Stalingrad "Battle of Stalingrad"), the Allied [reconquest of North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_African_Campaign "North African Campaign") and [invasion of Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Campaign_\(World_War_II\) "Italian Campaign (World War II)") in 1943, German forces suffered repeated military defeats. In 1944, the Soviets [pushed into Eastern Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Front_\(World_War_II\)#Summer_1944 "Eastern Front (World War II)"); the Western allies [landed in France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Overlord "Operation Overlord") and entered Germany despite a [final German counteroffensive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Bulge "Battle of the Bulge"). Following [Hitler's suicide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_of_Adolf_Hitler "Death of Adolf Hitler") during the [Battle of Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Berlin "Battle of Berlin"), [Germany signed the surrender document](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Instrument_of_Surrender "German Instrument of Surrender") on 8 May 1945, [ending World War II in Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End_of_World_War_II_in_Europe "End of World War II in Europe") [^86] [^88] and Nazi Germany. After the war, surviving Nazi officials were tried for [war crimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_crimes_of_the_Wehrmacht "War crimes of the Wehrmacht") at the [Nuremberg trials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuremberg_trials "Nuremberg trials").[^89] [^90]

Discrimination was institutionalised through legislation and perpetrated at an industrial scale with concentration and [death camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_camps "Death camps") across Europe. The [crimes against humanity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity "Crimes against humanity") culminated but were not limited to what later became known as [the Holocaust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Holocaust "The Holocaust"), the systematic murdering of around 6 million Jews. [Several other minority groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holocaust_victims "Holocaust victims") were targeted: at least 130,000 [Romani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porajmos "Porajmos"), 275,000 [disabled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aktion_T4 "Aktion T4"), thousands of [Jehovah's Witnesses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution_of_Jehovah%27s_Witnesses_in_Nazi_Germany "Persecution of Jehovah's Witnesses in Nazi Germany"), thousands of [homosexuals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution_of_homosexuals_in_Nazi_Germany "Persecution of homosexuals in Nazi Germany"), and hundreds of thousands of [political and religious opponents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nacht_und_Nebel "Nacht und Nebel").[^91] [Nazi policies in German-occupied countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalplan_Ost "Generalplan Ost") resulted in the deaths of an estimated 2.7 million Poles,[^92] 1.3 million Ukrainians, 1 million Belarusians and 3.5 million [Soviet prisoners of war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_mistreatment_of_Soviet_prisoners_of_war "German mistreatment of Soviet prisoners of war").[^93] [^90] [German military casualties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_casualties_in_World_War_II "German casualties in World War II") have been estimated at 5.3 million,[^94] and around 900,000 German civilians died.[^95]

During the later stages of and after World War II, around 12 million [ethnic Germans were expelled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_and_expulsion_of_Germans_\(1944%E2%80%931950\) "Flight and expulsion of Germans (1944–1950)") from across Eastern Europe, and Germany lost roughly [one-quarter of its pre-war territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany "Former eastern territories of Germany").[^96]

### East and West Germany

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/West_and_East_Germans_at_the_Brandenburg_Gate_in_1989.jpg/250px-West_and_East_Germans_at_the_Brandenburg_Gate_in_1989.jpg)

The fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 was one of the first developments in the end of the Cold War, leading ultimately to the dissolution of the Soviet Union.

After the surrender of Nazi Germany, the [Allies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II "Allies of World War II") *[de jure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_jure "De jure")* [abolished the German state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Declaration_\(1945\) "Berlin Declaration (1945)") and partitioned Berlin and Germany's remaining territory into [four occupation zones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allied-occupied_Germany "Allied-occupied Germany"). The western sectors, controlled by France, the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom"), and the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), were merged on 23 May 1949 to form the Federal Republic of Germany (FRG) ([German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language"): *Bundesrepublik Deutschland*; BRD); on 7 October 1949, the Soviet Zone became the [German Democratic Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Germany "East Germany") (GDR) (*Deutsche Demokratische Republik*; DDR). They were informally known as West Germany and East Germany.[^97] East Germany selected [East Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Berlin "East Berlin") as its capital, while West Germany chose [Bonn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonn "Bonn") as a provisional capital to emphasize its stance that the two-state solution was temporary.[^98]

West Germany was established as a federal parliamentary republic with a [social market economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_market_economy "Social market economy"). In 1948, West Germany became a major recipient of reconstruction aid under the American [Marshall Plan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Plan "Marshall Plan").[^99] [Konrad Adenauer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konrad_Adenauer "Konrad Adenauer") was elected the first [federal chancellor of Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Chancellor_of_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany "Federal Chancellor of the Federal Republic of Germany") in 1949. The country enjoyed prolonged economic growth (*[Wirtschaftswunder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wirtschaftswunder "Wirtschaftswunder")*) beginning in the early 1950s.[^100] West Germany joined [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") in 1955 and was a founding member of the [European Economic Community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Economic_Community "European Economic Community").[^101] On 1 January 1957, [the Saarland joined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1955_Saar_Statute_referendum "1955 Saar Statute referendum") West Germany.[^102]

East Germany remained under political and military control by the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") via occupation forces and became an [Eastern Bloc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Bloc "Eastern Bloc") state, joining the Soviet-led [Warsaw Pact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warsaw_Pact "Warsaw Pact") and [Comecon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comecon "Comecon"). Although East Germany claimed to be a democracy, political power was exercised solely by leading members (*[Politbüro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politburo "Politburo")*) of the [Socialist Unity Party of Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_Unity_Party_of_Germany "Socialist Unity Party of Germany") that was closely aligned with the [Communist Party of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union "Communist Party of the Soviet Union"). An extensive domestic intelligence programme, led by the intelligence agency [Stasi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stasi "Stasi") was launched, that utilized a vast network of informants to crush dissent.[^103] While [East German propaganda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_propaganda "Communist propaganda") was based on the benefits of the GDR's social programmes and the alleged threat of a West German invasion, many of its citizens looked to the West for freedom and prosperity.[^104] The [Berlin Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Wall "Berlin Wall"), built in 1961, prevented East German citizens from [escaping to West Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republikflucht "Republikflucht"), becoming a symbol of the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War").[^105]

Tensions between East and West Germany were reduced in the late 1960s by Chancellor [Willy Brandt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willy_Brandt "Willy Brandt") 's *[Ostpolitik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostpolitik "Ostpolitik")*.[^106] In 1989, Hungary dismantled the [Iron Curtain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Curtain "Iron Curtain") and [opened its Austrian border](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Removal_of_Hungary%27s_border_fence_with_Austria "Removal of Hungary's border fence with Austria"), triggering a mass emigration of East Germans to West Germany. This had devastating effects on the GDR, where [regular mass demonstrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monday_demonstrations_in_East_Germany "Monday demonstrations in East Germany") received increasing support. In an effort to help retain East Germany as a state, the East German authorities eased border restrictions, but this actually led to an acceleration of the *Wende* reform process, culminating in the *[Two Plus Four Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Final_Settlement_with_Respect_to_Germany "Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany")* under which Germany explicitly renounced claims to the former eastern territories and regained full sovereignty from the Allies. This permitted [German reunification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_reunification "German reunification") on 3 October 1990, with the accession of the [five re-established states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_states_of_Germany "New states of Germany") of the former GDR.[^107] The [fall of the Berlin Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_the_Berlin_Wall "Fall of the Berlin Wall") in 1989 became a symbol of the [fall of Communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_Communism "Fall of Communism"), the [dissolution of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union "Dissolution of the Soviet Union"), German reunification and *[Die Wende](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Die_Wende "Die Wende")* ("the turning point").[^108]

### Reunified Germany and the European Union

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Berlin_reichstag_west_panorama_2.jpg/250px-Berlin_reichstag_west_panorama_2.jpg)

The Berlin/Bonn Act made Berlin the capital of Germany again, with the Reichstag becoming the seat of the German parliament in 1999.

United Germany was considered the enlarged continuation of [West Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Germany "West Germany"), so it retained its memberships in international organisations.[^109] Based on the [Berlin/Bonn Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin/Bonn_Act "Berlin/Bonn Act") of 1994, [Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin "Berlin") again became the [capital of Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_of_Germany "Capital of Germany"), while [Bonn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonn "Bonn") obtained the unique status of a *Bundesstadt* (federal city), retaining some federal ministries.[^110] The relocation of the government was completed in 1999,[^111] and modernisation of the East German economy was scheduled to last until 2019.[^112]

Since reunification, Germany has taken a more active role in the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union"), signing the [Maastricht Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maastricht_Treaty "Maastricht Treaty") in 1992 and the [Lisbon Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisbon_Treaty "Lisbon Treaty") in 2007,[^113] and co-founding the [eurozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurozone "Eurozone").[^114] Germany sent a peacekeeping force to secure stability in the [Balkans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1999_NATO_bombing_in_Yugoslavia "1999 NATO bombing in Yugoslavia") and sent [German troops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bundeswehr "Bundeswehr") to [Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afghanistan "Afghanistan") as part of a NATO effort to provide [security in that country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_\(2001%E2%80%932021\) "War in Afghanistan (2001–2021)") after the ousting of the [Taliban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taliban "Taliban").[^115] [^116]

In the [2005 elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2005_German_federal_election "2005 German federal election"), [Angela Merkel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angela_Merkel "Angela Merkel") became the first female chancellor. In 2009, the German government approved a €50 billion stimulus plan.[^117] Among the major German political projects of the early 21st century are the advancement of [European integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_integration "European integration"), the [country's energy transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_transition_in_Germany "Energy transition in Germany") (*Energiewende*) for a [sustainable energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_energy "Sustainable energy") supply, the [debt brake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt_brake_\(Germany\) "Debt brake (Germany)") for balanced budgets, measures to increase the [fertility rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ageing_of_Europe#Germany "Ageing of Europe") ([pronatalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natalistic_politics "Natalistic politics")), and [high-tech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-tech "High-tech") strategies for the transition of the German economy, summarised as [Industry 4.0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industry_4.0 "Industry 4.0").[^118] During the [2015 European migrant crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015_European_migrant_crisis "2015 European migrant crisis"), the country took in over a million refugees and migrants.[^119]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Deutschland_topo.jpg/250px-Deutschland_topo.jpg)

A physical map of Germany

Germany is the [seventh-largest country in Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_European_countries_by_area "List of European countries by area").[^120] It borders [Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark "Denmark") to the north; [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland "Poland") and the [Czech Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_Republic "Czech Republic") to the east; [Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austria "Austria") and [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland") to the south; and [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France"), [Luxembourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luxembourg "Luxembourg"), [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium "Belgium"), and the [Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands") to the west. Germany is also bordered by the [North Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Sea "North Sea") and, at the north-northeast, the [Baltic Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_Sea "Baltic Sea"). German territory covers 357,022 km <sup>2</sup> (137,847 sq mi).[^121] Elevation ranges from the mountains of the Alps (with the highest point being the [Zugspitze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugspitze "Zugspitze") at 2,963 metres or 9,721 feet) in the south to the shores of the North Sea (*Nordsee*) in the northwest and the Baltic Sea (*Ostsee*) in the northeast. The forested uplands of central Germany and the lowlands of northern Germany (lowest point: in the municipality [Neuendorf-Sachsenbande](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuendorf-Sachsenbande "Neuendorf-Sachsenbande"), [Wilstermarsch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilstermarsch "Wilstermarsch"), at 3.54 metres or 11.6 feet below sea level [^122]) are traversed by such major rivers as the [Rhine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhine "Rhine"), [Danube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danube "Danube") and [Elbe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elbe "Elbe"). Significant natural resources include iron ore, coal, [potash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potash "Potash"), timber, [lignite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lignite "Lignite"), [uranium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranium "Uranium"), copper, natural gas, salt, and nickel.[^120]

### Climate

Most of Germany has a [temperate climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperate_climate "Temperate climate"), ranging from [oceanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceanic_climate "Oceanic climate") in the north and west to [continental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_climate "Continental climate") in the east and southeast. A small portion is [humid subtropical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humid_subtropical "Humid subtropical").[^123] Winters range from cold in the Southern Alps to cool and are generally overcast with limited precipitation, while summers can vary from hot and dry to cool and rainy. The northern regions have prevailing westerly winds that bring in moist air from the North Sea, moderating the temperature and increasing precipitation. Conversely, the southeast regions have more extreme temperatures.[^124] [Climate change in Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Germany "Climate change in Germany") is leading to long-term impacts on agriculture, more intense [heat waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_wave "Heat wave") and [cold waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_wave "Cold wave"), flash and [coastal flooding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastal_flooding "Coastal flooding"), and reduced water availability.[^125]

From February 2019–February 2020, average monthly temperatures in Germany ranged from a low of 3.3 °C (37.9 °F) in January 2020 to a high of 19.8 °C (67.6 °F) in June 2019.[^126] Average monthly precipitation ranged from 30 litres per square metre in February and April 2019 to 125 litres per square metre in February 2020.[^127] Average monthly hours of sunshine ranged from 45 in November 2019 to 300 in June 2019.[^128]

### Biodiversity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Herbst_in_den_Berchtesgadener_Alpen.jpg/250px-Herbst_in_den_Berchtesgadener_Alpen.jpg)

Berchtesgaden National Park in Bavaria

The territory of Germany can be divided into five terrestrial [ecoregions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecoregion "Ecoregion"): [Atlantic mixed forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_mixed_forests "Atlantic mixed forests"), [Baltic mixed forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_mixed_forests "Baltic mixed forests"), [Central European mixed forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_European_mixed_forests "Central European mixed forests"), [Western European broadleaf forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_European_broadleaf_forests "Western European broadleaf forests"), and [Alps conifer and mixed forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alps_conifer_and_mixed_forests "Alps conifer and mixed forests").[^129] As of 2016, 51% of Germany's land area is devoted to agriculture, while 30% is forested and 14% is covered by settlements or infrastructure.[^130]

Plants and animals include those generally common to Central Europe. According to the National Forest Inventory, [beeches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beeches "Beeches"), [oaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oak "Oak"), and other [deciduous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deciduous "Deciduous") trees constitute just over 40% of the forests; roughly 60% are [conifers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conifers "Conifers"), particularly [spruce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spruce "Spruce") and [pine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pine "Pine").[^131] There are many species of [ferns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fern "Fern"), [flowers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flowers "Flowers"), [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi "Fungi"), and [mosses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mosses "Mosses"). Wild animals include [roe deer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_European_red_deer "Central European red deer"), [wild boar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_European_boar "Central European boar"), [mouflon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouflon "Mouflon") (a subspecies of wild sheep), [fox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_fox "Red fox"), [badger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_badger "European badger"), [hare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_hare "European hare"), and small numbers of the [Eurasian beaver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_beaver "Eurasian beaver").[^132] The blue [cornflower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornflower "Cornflower") was once a German [national symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floral_emblem "Floral emblem").[^133]

The [16 national parks in Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_national_parks_of_Germany "List of national parks of Germany") include the [Jasmund National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jasmund_National_Park "Jasmund National Park"), the [Vorpommern Lagoon Area National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vorpommern_Lagoon_Area_National_Park "Vorpommern Lagoon Area National Park"), the [Müritz National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%BCritz_National_Park "Müritz National Park"), the [Wadden Sea National Parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wadden_Sea_National_Parks "Wadden Sea National Parks"), the [Harz National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harz_National_Park "Harz National Park"), the [Hainich National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hainich_National_Park "Hainich National Park"), the [Black Forest National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Forest_National_Park "Black Forest National Park"), the [Saxon Switzerland National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saxon_Switzerland_National_Park "Saxon Switzerland National Park"), the [Bavarian Forest National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bavarian_Forest_National_Park "Bavarian Forest National Park") and the [Berchtesgaden National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berchtesgaden_National_Park "Berchtesgaden National Park").[^134] In addition, there are [17 Biosphere Reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere_Reserves_in_Germany "Biosphere Reserves in Germany"),[^135] and [105 nature parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature_parks_\(Germany\) "Nature parks (Germany)").[^136] More than [400 zoos and animal parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_zoos_in_Germany "List of zoos in Germany") operate in Germany.[^137] The [Berlin Zoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Zoological_Garden "Berlin Zoological Garden"), which opened in 1844, is the oldest in Germany, and claims the most comprehensive collection of species in the world.[^138]
