**Kyrgyzstan**,[^1] officially the **Kyrgyz Republic**, formerly known as **Kirghizia**,[^2] [^26] is a [landlocked country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landlocked_country "Landlocked country") in the eastern regions of [Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia"), lying in the [Tian Shan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tian_Shan "Tian Shan") and [Pamir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pamir_Mountains "Pamir Mountains") mountain ranges. It is bordered by [Kazakhstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan "Kazakhstan") to the [north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan%E2%80%93Kyrgyzstan_border "Kazakhstan–Kyrgyzstan border"), [Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan "Uzbekistan") to the [west](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan%E2%80%93Uzbekistan_border "Kyrgyzstan–Uzbekistan border"), [Tajikistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tajikistan "Tajikistan") to the [south](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan%E2%80%93Tajikistan_border "Kyrgyzstan–Tajikistan border"), and [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") to the [east and southeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Kyrgyzstan_border "China–Kyrgyzstan border").[^27] [^28] [^29] Kyrgyzstan's capital and [largest city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Kyrgyzstan "List of cities in Kyrgyzstan") is [Bishkek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bishkek "Bishkek") which lies on the northern border with Kazakhstan. Ethnic [Kyrgyz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_people "Kyrgyz people") make up the majority of the country's over 7.4 million people, followed by significant minorities of [Uzbeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbeks "Uzbeks") and [Russians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians "Russians").[^30]

[Kyrgyzstan's history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Kyrgyzstan "History of Kyrgyzstan") spans a variety of cultures and empires. Although geographically isolated by its highly mountainous terrain, Kyrgyzstan has been at the crossroads of several civilizations as part of the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road") along with other commercial routes. Inhabited by a succession of tribes and clans, Kyrgyzstan has periodically fallen under larger domination, e.g., the [Turkic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_people "Turkic people") [nomads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomads "Nomads") who trace their ancestry to many Turkic states. It was first established as the [Yenisei Kyrgyz Khaganate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yenisei_Kyrgyz_Khaganate "Yenisei Kyrgyz Khaganate"). Later, in the 13th century, Kyrgyzstan was conquered by the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire") and under several [Mongol dynasties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Mongol_states "List of Mongol states"); it regained independence, but was later invaded by the [Dzungar Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dzungar_Khanate "Dzungar Khanate"). After the [Dzungar genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dzungar_genocide "Dzungar genocide") by the [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty"), Kyrgyz and Kipchaks were an integral part of the [Kokand Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kokand_Khanate "Kokand Khanate").

In 1876, Kyrgyzstan became part of the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"), and in 1936, the [Kirghiz Soviet Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kirghiz_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Kirghiz Soviet Socialist Republic") was formed to become a constituent republic of the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"). Following [Mikhail Gorbachev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev "Mikhail Gorbachev") 's democratic reforms in the USSR, in 1990 pro-independence candidate [Askar Akayev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Askar_Akayev "Askar Akayev") was elected president. On 31 August 1991, Kyrgyzstan declared independence from the [USSR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USSR "USSR") and a democratic government was established. Kyrgyzstan attained sovereignty as a nation state after the [breakup of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union "Dissolution of the Soviet Union") in 1991.

Tian Shan Mountains in East Kyrgyzstan

After independence, Kyrgyzstan was officially a [unitary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_state "Unitary state") [presidential republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_republic "Presidential republic"). Following the [Tulip Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulip_Revolution "Tulip Revolution") it became a [unitary parliamentary republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_parliamentary_republic "Unitary parliamentary republic"); however, it gradually developed an executive president and was governed as a [semi-presidential republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-presidential_republic "Semi-presidential republic") before reverting to a presidential system in 2021. Throughout its existence, the country has continued to endure ethnic conflicts,[^31] [^32] revolts,[^33] economic troubles,[^34] transitional governments [^35] and political conflict.[^36]

Kyrgyzstan is a member of the [Commonwealth of Independent States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States "Commonwealth of Independent States"), the [Eurasian Economic Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Economic_Union "Eurasian Economic Union"), the [Collective Security Treaty Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_Security_Treaty_Organization "Collective Security Treaty Organization"), the [Shanghai Cooperation Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Cooperation_Organisation "Shanghai Cooperation Organisation"), the [Organisation of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation"), the [Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_for_Security_and_Co-operation_in_Europe "Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe"), the [Organization of Turkic States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_of_Turkic_States "Organization of Turkic States"), the [Türksoy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C3%BCrksoy "Türksoy") community, and the United Nations. It is a [developing country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country") ranked 117th in the [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index"). Kyrgyzstan's [transition economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_economy "Transition economy") relies mainly on re-exporting Chinese goods and gold production. The country balances its international trade commitments as a World Trade Organization (WTO) member since 1998 with regional integration through the Eurasian Economic Union (EAEU), which it joined in 2015, and a delegation of parliamentarians sit at the [Parliamentary Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_Assembly_of_the_Council_of_Europe "Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe") of the [Council of Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Europe "Council of Europe").[^37]

## Etymology

There are many theories about the origin of the word *Kyrgyz*. One proposed etymology is that it means "forty tribes",[^38] which may refer to the forty clans of [Manas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epic_of_Manas "Epic of Manas"), a legendary hero who united forty regional clans. The [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_language "Persian language") *suffix [\-Stan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/-Stan "-Stan")* means "place of".[^39]

The 40-ray sun on the [flag of Kyrgyzstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_Kyrgyzstan "Flag of Kyrgyzstan") is a reference to those same forty tribes and the graphical element in the sun's center depicts the wooden crown, called tunduk, of a [yurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yurt "Yurt") —a portable dwelling traditionally used by nomads in the [steppes of Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Steppe "Eurasian Steppe").

The country's official name is *Kyrgyz Republic*, used in international arenas and foreign relations.[^40] [^41] In the English-speaking world, the spelling *Kyrgyzstan* is commonly used, while its former name *Kirghizia* [^3] is rarely used.[^42]

## History

### Early history

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Sary_-_Kamysh%2C_Kyrgyz_Republic_petroglyphs.jpg/250px-Sary_-_Kamysh%2C_Kyrgyz_Republic_petroglyphs.jpg)

Petroglyphs of local sheep, Sary Kamysh

The [Kyrgyz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_people "Kyrgyz people") state reached its greatest expansion after defeating the [Uyghur Khaganate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghur_Khaganate "Uyghur Khaganate") in 840 AD.[^43] From the tenth century, the Kyrgyz migrated as far as the Tian Shan range and maintained their dominance over this territory for about 200 years.

There is a storytelling tradition of the *[Epic of Manas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epic_of_Manas "Epic of Manas")*, which involves a warrior who unified all of the scattered tribes into a single nation in the 9th century. The trilogy, an element of the [UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage List](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO_Intangible_Cultural_Heritage_List "UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage List"), expresses the memory of the nomadic Kyrgyz people.[^44]

In the 12th century, the Kyrgyz dominion had shrunk to the [Altay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altay_Mountains "Altay Mountains") Range and [Sayan Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sayan_Mountains "Sayan Mountains") as a result of the [Mongol expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongols_before_Genghis_Khan "Mongols before Genghis Khan"). With the rise of the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire") in the thirteenth century, the Kyrgyz migrated south. The Kyrgyz peacefully became a part of the Mongol Empire in 1207.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Caravanserai_Tash_Rabat_on_Silk_Road_in_Kyrgyzstan.jpg/250px-Caravanserai_Tash_Rabat_on_Silk_Road_in_Kyrgyzstan.jpg)

Tash Rabat, a silk road caravansarai used during the Islamic Golden Age

[Issyk Kul Lake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Issyk_Kul_Lake "Issyk Kul Lake") was a stopover on the Silk Road, a land route for traders, merchants, and other travelers from the Far East to Europe. Kyrgyz tribes were overrun in the 17th century by the Mongols, in the mid-18th century by the [Manchu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchu_people "Manchu people") -led [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") of China, and in the early 19th century by the Uzbek [Khanate of Kokand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khanate_of_Kokand "Khanate of Kokand").[^45] In 1842, the Kyrgyz tribes broke away from Kokand and united into the [Kara-Kyrgyz Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kara-Kyrgyz_Khanate "Kara-Kyrgyz Khanate"), led by [Ormon Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ormon_Khan "Ormon Khan"). Following Ormon's death in 1854, the khanate disintegrated.[^46]

### Russian conquest

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Turgan_Berdike-%C3%BAlu.jpg/250px-Turgan_Berdike-%C3%BAlu.jpg)

Turgan Berdike-úlu, first Kyrgyz traveler to Europe - 1906

In the late nineteenth century, the eastern part of what is today Kyrgyzstan, mainly the [Issyk-Kul Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Issyk-Kul_Region "Issyk-Kul Region"), was ceded to the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") by [Qing China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") through the [Treaty of Tarbagatai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Tarbagatai "Treaty of Tarbagatai").[^47] The territory, then known in Russian as "Kirghizia", was formally incorporated into the Empire in 1876. The Russian takeover was met with numerous revolts, and many of the Kyrgyz opted to relocate to the [Pamir Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pamir_Mountains "Pamir Mountains") and Afghanistan.

In addition, the suppression of the [1916 rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asian_revolt_of_1916 "Central Asian revolt of 1916") against Russian rule in Central Asia caused many Kyrgyz later to migrate to China.[^48] Since many ethnic groups in the region were, and still are, split between neighboring states at a time when borders were more porous and less regulated, it was common to move back and forth over the mountains, depending on where life was perceived as better; this might mean better rains for pasture or better government during oppression.

### Soviet Kyrgyzstan

Soviet power was initially established in the region in 1919, and the Kara-Kyrgyz Autonomous Oblast was created within the [Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Soviet_Federative_Socialist_Republic "Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic") (SFSR). The phrase Kara-Kirghiz was used until the mid-1920s by the Russians to distinguish them from the [Kazakhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhs "Kazakhs"), who were also referred to as Kirghiz. On 5 December 1936, the [Kirghiz Soviet Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kirghiz_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Kirghiz Soviet Socialist Republic") was established as a constituent [Union Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republics_of_the_Soviet_Union "Republics of the Soviet Union") of the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union").[^4]

After the [Russian Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Civil_War "Russian Civil War"), the period of the [New Economic Policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Economic_Policy "New Economic Policy") (NEP), began, which lasted roughly to 1928.[^50] The [Bolsheviks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolsheviks "Bolsheviks") made an effort to establish a standardized tax system, with higher taxes for nomads to discourage the wandering livelihood and they divided the Central Asia region into five nation-states.[^50] [^51] [^52] Kyrgyzstan developed considerably in cultural, educational, and social life, literacy was greatly improved. Economic and social development also was notable.[^53] Under [Stalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Stalin "Joseph Stalin") a great focus was put on Kyrgyz [national identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korenizatsiya "Korenizatsiya"). The Soviet state was fighting tribalism: its social organization based on patrilineal kinship contradicted the concept of the modern nation state.[^51] [^53] In a region that did not previously know national institutions or consciousness, the process of nation-building was, from the indigenous perspective, a difficult and ambivalent one.[^52]

By the end of the 1920s, the Soviet Union developed a series of [five-year plans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five-year_plans_for_the_national_economy_of_the_Soviet_Union "Five-year plans for the national economy of the Soviet Union"), centered around [industrialization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrialization_in_the_Soviet_Union "Industrialization in the Soviet Union") and the [collectivization of agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collectivization_in_the_Soviet_Union "Collectivization in the Soviet Union"), including the creation of huge " [kolkhoz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kolkhoz "Kolkhoz") " [collective farming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_farming "Collective farming") systems, needed to feed the new workers in the industries.[^54] Because of the plan's reliance on rapidity, major economic and cultural changes had to occur, which led to conflicts. In Kyrgyzstan, Russian settlers acquired the best pasture land, creating much hardship for most of its original inhabitants, [Kazakh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhs "Kazakhs"), Kyrgyz and [Turkmen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkmens "Turkmens") nomads, who were also forced to settle down on soil that hadn't enough agricultural potential.[^52] [^55] The changes caused unrest, and between 1928 and 1932, nomads and peasants made it clear through methods like passive resistance that they did not agree with these policies, in the Kirgiziya area also guerrilla opposition occurred.[^50] [^52] [^55] The region suffered relatively more deaths from collectivization than any other.[^50]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Suusamyr_Valley_%283968060227%29.jpg/250px-Suusamyr_Valley_%283968060227%29.jpg)

Nomadic farming in the Suusamyr Valley, Kyrgyzstan

The early years of [glasnost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glasnost "Glasnost"), in the late 1980s, had little effect on the political climate in Kyrgyzstan.[^56] However, the Republic's press was permitted to adopt a more liberal stance and to establish a new publication, *Literaturny Kirghizstan*, by the Union of Writers. Unofficial political groups were forbidden, but several groups that emerged in 1989 to deal with the acute housing crisis were permitted to function.

According to the last Soviet census in 1989, ethnic Kyrgyz made up only 22% of the residents of the northern city of [Frunze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bishkek "Bishkek") (now Bishkek), while more than 60% were Russians, Ukrainians, and people from other [Slavic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_peoples "Slavic peoples") nations. Nearly 10% of the capital's population were Jewish (very unusual in the Soviet Union, the [Jewish Autonomous Oblast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish_Autonomous_Oblast "Jewish Autonomous Oblast") being an exception).

In June 1990, ethnic tensions between [Uzbeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbeks "Uzbeks") and Kyrgyz surfaced in the [Osh Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osh_Region "Osh Region") (southern Kyrgyzstan), where Uzbeks form a minority of the population.[^57] The tensions between Kyrgyzs and Uzbeks in Osis led to 186 deaths.[^58] Attempts to appropriate Uzbek collective farms for housing development triggered the [Osh Riots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osh_riots_\(1990\) "Osh riots (1990)"). A state of emergency and curfew were introduced [^59] and [Askar Akayev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Askar_Akayev "Askar Akayev"), the youngest of five sons born into a family of collective farm workers (in northern Kyrgyzstan), was elected president in October of that same year. By then, the [Kyrgyzstan Democratic Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Movement_of_Kyrgyzstan "Democratic Movement of Kyrgyzstan") (KDM) had developed into a significant political force with support in Parliament. On 15 December 1990, the Supreme Soviet voted to change the republic's name to the *Republic of Kyrgyzstan*. The following January, Akayev introduced new government structures and appointed a new cabinet composed mainly of younger, reform-oriented politicians. In February 1991, the name of the capital, Frunze, was changed back to its pre-revolutionary name of Bishkek.[^60]

Despite these political moves toward independence, economic realities seemed to work against secession from the Soviet Union. In a referendum on the preservation of the Soviet Union in March 1991, 88.7% of the voters approved the proposal to retain the Soviet Union as a "renewed federation".[^55] Nevertheless, secessionist forces pushed Kyrgyzstan's independence through in August of that same year.

On 19 August 1991, when the [State Emergency Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Committee_on_the_State_of_Emergency "State Committee on the State of Emergency") assumed power in Moscow, there was an attempt to depose Akayev in Kyrgyzstan. After the coup collapsed the following week, Akayev and Vice President [German Kuznetsov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Kuznetsov "German Kuznetsov") announced their resignations from the [Communist Party of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union "Communist Party of the Soviet Union") (CPSU), and the entire bureau and secretariat resigned. This was followed by the Supreme Soviet vote declaring independence from the Soviet Union on 31 August 1991 as the *Republic of Kyrgyzstan*.[^61]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Stamps_of_Kyrgyzstan%2C_2011-05.jpg/250px-Stamps_of_Kyrgyzstan%2C_2011-05.jpg)

Wild sheep, urial, on a Kyrgyzstan stamp

According to a 2013 [Gallup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallup_\(company\) "Gallup (company)") poll, 62% of Kyrgyz people say that the collapse of the Soviet Union harmed their country, while only 16% said that the collapse benefitted it.[^62]

### Independence

In October 1991, Akayev ran unopposed and was elected president of the new independent republic by direct ballot, receiving 95 percent of the votes cast. Together with representatives of seven other Republics that same month, he signed the [Treaty of the Economic Community](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Treaty_of_the_Economic_Community&action=edit&redlink=1 "Treaty of the Economic Community (page does not exist)"). The new leaders of three out of four Soviet Union's founding republics, Russia, Belarus and Ukraine, on 8 December 1991 signed the [Belavezha Accords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belavezha_Accords "Belavezha Accords"), denouncing the [Union Treaty of 1922](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Creation_of_the_USSR "Treaty on the Creation of the USSR"), declaring that the Union would cease to exist and proclaimed the [Commonwealth of Independent States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States "Commonwealth of Independent States") (CIS) in its place.[^63]

On 21 December 1991, Kyrgyzstan [^5] agreed with the other four Central Asian Republics, Tajikistan,[^6] [Turkmenistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkmenistan "Turkmenistan"),[^7] Uzbekistan,[^8] and Kazakhstan [^9] to the Alma-Ata Protocols, formally entering the Commonwealth with [Armenia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenia "Armenia"),[^10] [Azerbaijan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azerbaijan "Azerbaijan"),[^11] [Moldova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moldova "Moldova"),[^12] and [Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ukraine "Ukraine").[^13] Finally, Kyrgyzstan gained full independence on 25 December 1991. The following day, on 26 December 1991, the Soviet Union ceased to exist. In 1992, Kyrgyzstan joined the United Nations and the [Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_for_Security_and_Co-operation_in_Europe "Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe") (OSCE). On 5 May 1993, the official name changed from the *Republic of Kyrgyzstan* to the *Kyrgyz Republic* following the adoption of a new [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Kyrgyzstan "Constitution of Kyrgyzstan").[^73]

Kyrgyzstan celebrates its Independence Day annually on 31 August, the anniversary of its declaration of independence in 1991.[^74]

### 21st century

#### 2000–2009

In February and March 2005, parliamentary elections were held, which the opposition and international observers criticized for alleged irregularities. Widespread protests erupted, leading to the ousting of President Askar Akayev, who fled the country and resigned in April 2005. This series of events became known as the [Tulip Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulip_Revolution "Tulip Revolution"). Subsequently, [Kurmanbek Bakiyev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurmanbek_Bakiyev "Kurmanbek Bakiyev"), a former prime minister and opposition leader, assumed the presidency after winning the July 2005 presidential election with a significant majority.[^75] [^76] [^77]

After assuming office, President Bakiyev faced challenges, including political unrest and demands for constitutional reforms. In 2006 and 2007, large-scale protests occurred, with citizens calling for a reduction in presidential powers and increased governmental transparency. In response, Bakiyev implemented constitutional amendments and appointed opposition figures to key positions. Despite these measures, tensions persisted throughout his tenure.[^76]

Throughout the decade, Kyrgyzstan grappled with economic difficulties, including energy shortages and rising utility prices. In 2009, the government announced significant increases in electricity and heating tariffs, leading to public discontent. Additionally, the country faced challenges related to corruption and organized crime, which impacted its social and economic development.[^78] [^79]

In 2009, Bakiyev announced the eviction of the U.S. military from the [Manas Air Base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_Center_at_Manas "Transit Center at Manas"), a strategic transit center supporting operations in Afghanistan.[^80] The Manas Air Base, hosting approximately 1,000 U.S. military personnel since 2001, served as a crucial staging post for coalition forces in Afghanistan.[^81]

#### 2010–2019

In April 2010, widespread protests erupted against President Kurmanbek Bakiyev's administration, driven by public discontent over corruption and rising energy prices. These demonstrations [culminated in Bakiyev's ousting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Kyrgyz_Revolution "2010 Kyrgyz Revolution") and the establishment of a provisional government led by [Roza Otunbayeva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roza_Otunbayeva "Roza Otunbayeva"). Subsequently, in June 2010, violent ethnic clashes occurred in the southern cities of [Osh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osh "Osh") and [Jalal-Abad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jalal-Abad "Jalal-Abad") between Kyrgyz and Uzbek communities, resulting in over 400 deaths and displacing thousands.[^82] [^83]

Following these events, a referendum was held in June 2010 to adopt a new constitution aimed at reducing presidential powers and enhancing parliamentary authority. The referendum passed with approximately 90% approval and a 70% voter turnout, despite challenges posed by recent unrest. This constitution introduced a single six-year term for the president without the possibility of re-election and limited any single political party to 65 of the 120 parliamentary seats to prevent power concentration.[^82]

Under the new constitutional framework, Kyrgyzstan held its first parliamentary elections in October 2010, which were noted for their peaceful conduct and absence of major voting irregularities. In 2011, [Almazbek Atambayev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almazbek_Atambayev "Almazbek Atambayev") was elected president, serving until 2017. His tenure focused on stabilizing the country and implementing democratic reforms, including the introduction of biometric registration to enhance electoral transparency. In 2017, [Sooronbay Jeenbekov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sooronbay_Jeenbekov "Sooronbay Jeenbekov") succeeded Atambayev as president, continuing efforts to strengthen democratic institutions and address ongoing challenges such as corruption and economic development.[^61]

Significant reforms were undertaken during this period, including the abolition of military courts in December 2016 to streamline the judicial system and enhance civilian oversight. Additionally, the Ministry of Defense was restructured into the State Committee for Defense Affairs, with operational control of the armed forces transferred to the [General Staff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staff_\(military\) "Staff (military)") to improve military efficiency and accountability.

In 2016, Kyrgyzstan commemorated the centennial of the 1916 uprising against Tsarist Russia, known as [Urkun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asian_revolt_of_1916 "Central Asian revolt of 1916"), by unveiling a monument at the [Ata-Beyit memorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ata-Beyit "Ata-Beyit") complex.

#### Since 2020

In October 2020, [widespread protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Kyrgyz_Revolution "2020 Kyrgyz Revolution") erupted in response to disputed [parliamentary election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Kyrgyz_parliamentary_election "2020 Kyrgyz parliamentary election") results, leading to the annulment of the elections and the resignation of President [Sooronbay Jeenbekov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sooronbay_Jeenbekov "Sooronbay Jeenbekov"). Amid the unrest, [Sadyr Japarov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sadyr_Japarov "Sadyr Japarov"), a nationalist politician previously imprisoned, was released and subsequently appointed as interim president and prime minister. His rapid ascent to power was facilitated by parliamentary approval under contentious circumstances. In January 2021, Japarov was [elected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Kyrgyz_presidential_election "2021 Kyrgyz presidential election") president in a landslide victory.[^82] [^84] [^85] [^86]

Following his election, President Japarov initiated a series of constitutional reforms aimed at shifting the country's governance structure from a parliamentary to a presidential system. A [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Kyrgyz_government_system_referendum "2021 Kyrgyz government system referendum") held in January 2021 approved these changes, granting the president expanded powers, including the authority to appoint judges and diminishing the role of parliament. Critics labeled the new constitution the "Khanstitution", expressing concerns over the potential for authoritarian rule.[^84] [^82] [^85] [^87]

The period also witnessed increased pressure on independent media and civil society organizations. In 2024, the Kyrgyz government implemented measures to intimidate and silence journalists and critics. The [Supreme Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Kyrgyzstan "Supreme Court of Kyrgyzstan") ordered the closure of a leading investigative media outlet, sentencing two journalists to prison terms and placing two others on probation for their reporting. Additionally, a " [foreign representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_agent "Foreign agent") " law came into effect in April 2024, imposing strict government oversight on NGOs receiving foreign funding.[^88] [^89] [^85] [^90]

Kyrgyzstan's longstanding border disputes with neighboring Tajikistan escalated into violent clashes, notably around the town of [Batken](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batken "Batken") in 2021 and 2022, culminating in a six-day conflict in September 2022. In March 2025, Presidents Japarov and [Emomali Rahmon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emomali_Rahmon "Emomali Rahmon") of Tajikistan signed an agreement to demarcate their shared border, aiming to resolve the territorial conflicts and reopen transportation links that had been closed since the 2022 clashes.[^86]

In December 2024, President Japarov dismissed [Prime Minister Akylbek Japarov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akylbek_Japarov "Akylbek Japarov"), who had served since 2021, citing a transfer to another position. [First Deputy Prime Minister Adylbek Kasymaliyev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adylbek_Kasymaliev "Adylbek Kasymaliev") was appointed as acting prime minister.[^87]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Kyrgyzstan_Topography.png/250px-Kyrgyzstan_Topography.png)

Kyrgyzstan's topography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Bringing_the_sheep_home%2C_on_the_southern_shore_of_Issyk-Kul_%283968109583%29.jpg/250px-Bringing_the_sheep_home%2C_on_the_southern_shore_of_Issyk-Kul_%283968109583%29.jpg)

On the southern shore of Issyk Kul lake, Issyk Kul Region

Kyrgyzstan is a landlocked country in Central Asia, bordering Kazakhstan, China, Tajikistan and Uzbekistan. It lies between latitudes [39°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/39th_parallel_north "39th parallel north") and [44° N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/44th_parallel_north "44th parallel north"), and longitudes [69°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/69th_meridian_east "69th meridian east") and [81° E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/81st_meridian_east "81st meridian east"). It is farther from the sea than any other individual country, and is an [endorheic basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endorheic_basin "Endorheic basin") in which all its rivers flow into closed drainage systems which do not reach the sea. The mountainous region of the Tian Shan covers over 80% of the country (Kyrgyzstan is occasionally referred to as "the Switzerland of Central Asia", as a result),[^91] with the remainder made up of valleys and basins.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Kyrgyzstan.png/500px-Kyrgyzstan.png)

A map of Kyrgyzstan

[Issyk-Kul Lake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Issyk-Kul_Lake "Issyk-Kul Lake"), or Ysyk-Köl in [Kyrgyz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_Language "Kyrgyz Language"), in the north-eastern Tian Shan is the largest lake in Kyrgyzstan and the second largest mountain lake in the world after [Titicaca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titicaca "Titicaca"). The lowest point is in [Kara-Daryya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kara_Darya "Kara Darya") (Karadar'ya) at 132 meters and the highest peaks are in the Kakshaal-Too range, forming the Chinese border. Peak [Jengish Chokusu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jengish_Chokusu "Jengish Chokusu"), at 7,439 m (24,406 ft), is the highest point and is considered by geologists to be the northernmost peak over 7,000 m (22,966 ft) in the world. Heavy snowfall in winter leads to spring floods which often cause serious damage downstream. The runoff from the mountains is also used for [hydro-electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydro-electricity "Hydro-electricity").

Kyrgyzstan has significant deposits of metals including gold and [rare-earth metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rare-earth_metals "Rare-earth metals"). Due to the country's predominantly mountainous terrain, less than 8% of the land is cultivated, and this is concentrated in the northern lowlands and the fringes of the [Fergana Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fergana_Valley "Fergana Valley").

Bishkek in the north is the capital and largest city, with 1,321,900 inhabitants (as of 2025). The second city is the ancient town of Osh, located in the Fergana Valley near the border with Uzbekistan. The principal river is the Kara Darya, which flows west through the Fergana Valley into Uzbekistan. Across the border in Uzbekistan it meets another major Kyrgyz river, the [Naryn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naryn_River "Naryn River").

The confluence forms the [Syr Darya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syr_Darya "Syr Darya"), which originally flowed into the [Aral Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aral_Sea "Aral Sea"). As of 2010, it no longer reaches the sea, as its water is withdrawn upstream to irrigate cotton fields in Tajikistan, Uzbekistan, and southern Kazakhstan. The [Chu River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chu_River "Chu River") also briefly flows through Kyrgyzstan before entering Kazakhstan.

Kyrgyzstan contains seven terrestrial ecosystems: [Tian Shan montane conifer forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tian_Shan_montane_conifer_forests "Tian Shan montane conifer forests"), [Alai-Western Tian Shan steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alai-Western_Tian_Shan_steppe "Alai-Western Tian Shan steppe"), [Gissaro-Alai open woodlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gissaro-Alai_open_woodlands "Gissaro-Alai open woodlands"), [Tian Shan foothill arid steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tian_Shan_foothill_arid_steppe "Tian Shan foothill arid steppe"), [Pamir alpine desert and tundra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pamir_alpine_desert_and_tundra "Pamir alpine desert and tundra"), [Tian Shan montane steppe and meadows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tian_Shan_montane_steppe_and_meadows "Tian Shan montane steppe and meadows"), and [Central Asian northern desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asian_northern_desert "Central Asian northern desert").[^92] It had a 2019 [Forest Landscape Integrity Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_Landscape_Integrity_Index "Forest Landscape Integrity Index") mean score of 8.86/10, ranking it 13th globally out of 172 countries.[^93]

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Koppen-Geiger_Map_KGZ_present.svg/500px-Koppen-Geiger_Map_KGZ_present.svg.png)

Kyrgyzstan map of Köppen climate classification

The climate varies regionally. The low-lying [Fergana Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fergana_Valley "Fergana Valley") in the southwest is [subtropical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtropical_climate "Subtropical climate") and extremely hot in summer, with temperatures reaching 40 °C (104 °F). The northern [foothills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foothill "Foothill") are [temperate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperate_climate "Temperate climate") and the Tian Shan varies from dry [continental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_climate "Continental climate") to [polar climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_climate "Polar climate"), depending on elevation. In the coldest areas, winter temperatures drop below freezing for approximately 40 days, and even some [desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert "Desert") areas experience constant snowfall during this period. In the lowlands the temperature ranges from around −6 °C (21 °F) in January to 24 °C (75 °F) in July.

#### Glaciers and climate change

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Climate_change_Kyrgyzstan.pdf/page1-250px-Climate_change_Kyrgyzstan.pdf.jpg)

Reduction of glacier area in the Borkoldoy range (1965–2010)

[Climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Kyrgyzstan "Climate change in Kyrgyzstan") is evident in Kyrgyzstan. Among the countries in [Eastern Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Europe "Eastern Europe") and [Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia"), Kyrgyzstan is the third most vulnerable to the [effects of climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change "Effects of climate change"), such as changes in weather patterns that could lead to prolonged periods of precipitation and drought.[^94] Moreover, the frequent occurrence of extreme weather and climate events such as heatwaves, droughts, heavy rainfalls, and biotic/abiotic catastrophes during recent years are evidence of climate change.[^95] Kyrgyzstan is threatened with [glacier melting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850 "Retreat of glaciers since 1850") and a lack of freshwater balance, which are accelerated by global warming.[^96] The average temperature has increased from 4.8 °C to 6 °C so far within the last 20 years.[^97] In 2013 the World Bank estimated a likely increase of 2 °C in average mean temperature by 2060 and of 4–5 °C by 2100, noting that the country's glaciers were significantly reduced and projected to decline further.[^98] A significant warming trend in Kyrgyzstan with a projected increase of 6°C under the Shared Socioeconomic Pathway (SSP) scenario SSP5-8.5 from 2076 to 2096.[^99] However the very slight increase in temperature is expected to positively affect climate-sensitive sectors such as agriculture, energy, and forestry as more land is within the optimum temperature band.

### Enclaves and exclaves

There is one [exclave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enclave_and_exclave "Enclave and exclave"), the tiny village of [Barak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barak,_Kyrgyzstan "Barak, Kyrgyzstan") [^100] (population 627), in the Fergana Valley. The village is surrounded by Uzbek territory. It is located on the road from [Osh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osh "Osh") (Kyrgyzstan) to Khodjaabad (Uzbekistan) about 4 kilometres (2 miles) north-west from the [Kyrgyz–Uzbek border](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz%E2%80%93Uzbek_border "Kyrgyz–Uzbek border") in the direction of [Andijan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andijan "Andijan").[^101] Barak is administratively part of [Kara-Suu District](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kara-Suu_District "Kara-Suu District") in Kyrgyzstan's Osh Region.

There are four Uzbek enclaves within Kyrgyzstan. Two of them are the towns of [Sokh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sokh_District "Sokh District"), with an area of 325 km <sup>2</sup> (125 sq mi) and a population of 42,800 in 1993, although some estimates go as high as 70,000 (99% are [Tajiks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tajik_people "Tajik people"), the remainder Uzbeks); and [Shakhimardan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shohimardon "Shohimardon") (also known as Shahimardan, Shohimardon, or Shah-i-Mardan), with an area of 90 km <sup>2</sup> (35 sq mi) and a population of 5,100 in 1993; 91% are Uzbeks, and the remaining 9% are Kyrgyz; the other two are the tiny territories of Chong-Kara (roughly 3 km (2 mi) long by 1 km (0.6 mi) wide) and Jangy-ayyl (a dot of land barely 2–3 km (1–2 mi) across). Chong-Kara is on the [Sokh river](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sokh_river "Sokh river"), between the Uzbek border and the Sokh enclave. Jangy-ayyl is about 60 kilometres (37 mi) east of Batken, in a northward projection of the Kyrgyz-Uzbek border near [Khalmion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalmion "Khalmion").

There are also two enclaves belonging to Tajikistan on the [Kyrgyz-Tajik border](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz-Tajik_border "Kyrgyz-Tajik border"): [Vorukh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vorukh "Vorukh"), and [Lolazor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lolazor,_Jabbor_Rasulov_District "Lolazor, Jabbor Rasulov District"). Vorukh has an area between 95–130 km <sup>2</sup> (37–50 sq mi), and a population estimated between 23,000 and 29,000, 95% Tajiks and 5% Kyrgyz. It is distributed among 17 villages and is located around 45 kilometres (28 mi) south of [Isfara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isfara "Isfara") on the right bank of the river [Karavshin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karavshin "Karavshin"). Lolazor (Western Qalacha or Kayragach) is a small settlement near the Kyrgyz railway station of [Kairagach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kairagach "Kairagach").

## Politics

### Political system

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Official_Photo_of_the_President_of_the_Kyrgyz_Republic_H.E._Mr._Sadyr_Zhaparov.jpg/250px-Official_Photo_of_the_President_of_the_Kyrgyz_Republic_H.E._Mr._Sadyr_Zhaparov.jpg)

Sadyr Japarov, President

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/85/White_House_in_Bishkek.jpg/250px-White_House_in_Bishkek.jpg)

Supreme Council building in Bishkek, house of the unicameral parliament. View from the north-west side (facade from the south)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Kyrgyz_women_parliamentarians.jpg/250px-Kyrgyz_women_parliamentarians.jpg)

Kyrgyz women parliamentarians, 2016

The [1993 constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1993%E2%80%932010_Constitution_of_Kyrgyzstan "1993–2010 Constitution of Kyrgyzstan") defines the form of government as a democratic [unicameral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicameral "Unicameral") republic. The executive branch includes a president and prime minister. The parliament currently is unicameral. The judicial branch comprises a supreme court, local courts and a chief prosecutor.

In March 2002, in the southern district of [Aksy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aksy "Aksy"), five people protesting the [arbitrary arrest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arbitrary_arrest_and_detention "Arbitrary arrest and detention") of an opposition politician were shot dead by police, sparking nationwide [protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protest "Protest"). President Askar Akayev initiated a constitutional reform process which initially included the participation of a broad range of government, civil and social representatives in an open dialogue, leading to a February 2003 referendum marred by voting irregularities.

The amendments to the constitution approved by the referendum resulted in stronger control by the president and weakened the parliament and the Constitutional Court. Parliamentary elections for a new, 75-seat unicameral legislature were held on 27 February and 13 March 2005, but were widely viewed as corrupt. The subsequent protests led to a bloodless coup on 24 March 2005, after which Akayev fled the country with his family and was replaced by acting president Kurmanbek Bakiyev.

On 10 July 2005, acting president Bakiyev won the [presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_presidential_election,_2005 "Kyrgyz presidential election, 2005") in a landslide, with 88.9% of the vote, and was inaugurated on 14 August. However, initial public support for the new administration substantially declined in subsequent months as a result of its apparent inability to solve the corruption problems that had plagued the country since its independence from the Soviet Union, along with the murders of several members of parliament. Large-scale protests against president Bakiyev took place in Bishkek in April and November 2006, with opposition leaders accusing the president of failing to live up to his election promises to reform the country's constitution and transfer many of his presidential powers to parliament.[^102]

Kyrgyzstan is also a member of the [Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_for_Security_and_Cooperation_in_Europe "Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe") (OSCE), a league of 57 participating states committed to peace, transparency, and the protection of human rights in Eurasia. As an OSCE participating state, Kyrgyzstan's international commitments are subject to monitoring under the mandate of the [U.S. Helsinki Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Helsinki_Commission "U.S. Helsinki Commission").

In December 2008, the state-owned broadcast [KTRK](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_Television "Kyrgyz Television") announced that it would require prior submission of [Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_Free_Europe/Radio_Liberty "Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty") programmes, which KTRK are required to retransmit according to a 2005 agreement.[^103] KTRK had stopped retransmitting RFE/RL programming in October 2008, a week after it failed to broadcast an RFE/RL programme called *Inconvenient Questions* which covered the October elections, claiming to have lost the missing material. President Bakiyev had criticised this programme in September 2008, while KTRK told RFE/RL that its programming was too negative. [Reporters Without Borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reporters_Without_Borders "Reporters Without Borders"), which ranks Kyrgyzstan 111th out of 173 countries on its [Press Freedom Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Press_Freedom_Index "Press Freedom Index"), strongly criticised the decision.

On 3 February 2009, President Bakiyev announced the imminent closure of the [Manas Air Base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manas_Air_Base "Manas Air Base"), the only US military base remaining in Central Asia.[^104] The closure was approved by Parliament on 19 February 2009 by a vote of 78–1 for the government-backed bill.[^105] However, after much behind-the-scenes negotiation between Kyrgyz, Russian and American diplomats, the decision was reversed in June 2009. The Americans were allowed to remain under a new contract, whereby rent would increase from $17.4 million to $60 million annually.[^106] The US military fully withdrew from Manas Air Base in 2014.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/SCO_summit_%282018-06-10%29_7.jpg/250px-SCO_summit_%282018-06-10%29_7.jpg)

President Jeenbekov at the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation summit in China, June 2018

Kyrgyzstan is among the fifty countries in the world with the highest perceived level of corruption: the 2016 [Corruption Perception Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corruption_Perception_Index "Corruption Perception Index") for Kyrgyzstan is 28 on a scale of 0 (most corrupt) to 100 (least corrupt).[^107]

In 2010, another revolution erupted in the country (see: [April uprising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Kyrgyzstani_revolution "2010 Kyrgyzstani revolution")). President Bakiyev, together with his relatives, including his son [Maksim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxim_Bakiyev "Maxim Bakiyev") [^108] and brother [Janish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janish_Bakiyev "Janish Bakiyev") —were forced to flee to Kazakhstan and then sought asylum in Belarus. Roza Otunbayeva, who was appointed interim president, announced that she did not intend to run for the [Presidential elections in 2011](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_presidential_election,_2011 "Kyrgyz presidential election, 2011"). The election was held in November and won by Prime Minister Almazbek Atambayev, leader of the [Social Democratic Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Kyrgyzstan "Social Democratic Party of Kyrgyzstan"), and Atambayev was sworn in as president on 1 December 2011. [Omurbek Babanov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omurbek_Babanov "Omurbek Babanov") was appointed prime minister on the same day and was confirmed on 23 December 2011.[^109]

In 2015, Kyrgyzstan became a full-fledged member of the [Eurasian Economic Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Economic_Union "Eurasian Economic Union") (EES) after it formally abolished customs controls along its border with Kazakhstan, other members are the former Soviet republics Russia, Kazakhstan, Belarus, and Armenia.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/2023_Moscow_Victory_Day_Parade_01.jpg/250px-2023_Moscow_Victory_Day_Parade_01.jpg)

President Japarov (right) with Vladimir Putin and other post-Soviet leaders at the 2023 Moscow Victory Day Parade 110

In October 2017, Sooronbay Jeenbekov, a former prime minister backed by incumbent Almazbek Atambayev, was elected as the new President of Kyrgyzstan.[^111] In foreign policy he saw the [Kremlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kremlin "Kremlin") as the country's "main strategic partner" and China as an "important strategic and trade partner", but he intended to seek more collaborative bilateral ties with European partners.[^112] On 7 August 2019, the Special Forces of Kyrgyzstan launched an operation against the residence of former President Almazbek Atambayev, supposedly based on charges of corruption made against him.[^113] [^114] In a meeting of the [Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Security_Council_of_Kyrgyzstan "Security Council of Kyrgyzstan"), President Jeenbekov accused Atambayev of violating the constitution.[^115] In October 2020, President Sooronbay Jeenbekov resigned after protests caused by irregularities in parliamentary [elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Kyrgyz_parliamentary_election "2020 Kyrgyz parliamentary election") on 4 October 2020.[^116]

In January 2021, Sadyr Japarov was elected as the new president after winning the presidential [election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Kyrgyz_presidential_election "2021 Kyrgyz presidential election") by a landslide.[^117]

In April 2021, the majority of voters approved in the constitutional [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Kyrgyz_constitutional_referendum "2021 Kyrgyz constitutional referendum") a new constitution that will give new powers to the president, significantly strengthening the power of the presidency.[^118]

### Administrative divisions

Kyrgyzstan is divided into seven [regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regions_of_Kyrgyzstan "Regions of Kyrgyzstan") ([Kyrgyz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_language "Kyrgyz language"): облустар). The regions are subdivided into 44 [districts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Districts_of_Kyrgyzstan "Districts of Kyrgyzstan") ([Kyrgyz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_language "Kyrgyz language"): аймактар, *ajmaktar*;). The districts are further subdivided into rural districts at the lowest level of administration, which include all rural settlements (*aýyl ökmötü*) and villages without an associated municipal government.

The cities of Biškek and Oš have status "state importance" and do not belong to any region.

Each region is headed by an akim (regional governor) appointed by the president. District akims are appointed by regional akims.

### Military

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Multinational_forces_participate_in_Regional_Cooperation_12_Exercise_-06.JPG/250px-Multinational_forces_participate_in_Regional_Cooperation_12_Exercise_-06.JPG)

Kyrgyz soldiers conducting mine sweeping exercises

The armed forces of Kyrgyzstan were formed after the [collapse of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collapse_of_the_Soviet_Union "Collapse of the Soviet Union") and consist of the [Land Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_Army "Kyrgyz Army"), [Air Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_Air_Force "Kyrgyz Air Force"), [internal troops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_Troops_of_Kyrgyzstan "Internal Troops of Kyrgyzstan"), [National Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Guard_\(Kyrgyzstan\) "National Guard (Kyrgyzstan)"), and the [border guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Border_Guard_Service_of_Kyrgyzstan "State Border Guard Service of Kyrgyzstan"). The military works with the [US Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_Armed_Forces "US Armed Forces"), which leased a facility named the [Transit Center at Manas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_Center_at_Manas "Transit Center at Manas") at Manas International Airport near Bishkek until June 2014.[^119] In recent years, the armed forces have begun developing better relations with Russia including signing modernization deals worth $1.1bn and participating in more exercises with Russian troops.[^120] The Agency of National Security works with the military and serves similar purposes to its Soviet predecessor, the [KGB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KGB "KGB"). It oversees an elite counterterrorism special forces unit known as "Alfa", the same name used by other former Soviet countries, including Russia and Uzbekistan. The police are commanded by the Ministry of the Interior Affairs, along with the border guard.[^121]

### Human rights

Kyrgyzstan is classified as a " [hybrid regime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybrid_regime "Hybrid regime") " in the [Democracy Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy_Index "Democracy Index"), ranking 107th out of 167 for 2020.[^122] Kyrgyzstan was also ranked "not free" in the 2021 [Freedom in the World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_in_the_World "Freedom in the World") report with a score of 28/100. In 2020, it was ranked "partly free" with a score of 39/100.[^123] After the installation of a more democratic government, many human rights violations still take place. In a move that alarmed human-rights groups, dozens of prominent Uzbek religious and community leaders were arrested by security forces following the [2010 South Kyrgyzstan riots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_South_Kyrgyzstan_ethnic_clashes "2010 South Kyrgyzstan ethnic clashes"), including journalist and human-rights activist [Azimzhan Askarov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azimzhan_Askarov "Azimzhan Askarov").[^124] A law banning women under the age of 23 from traveling abroad without a parent or guardian, with the purpose of "increased morality and preservation of the gene pool" passed in the Kyrgyz parliament in June 2013.[^125] American diplomats expressed concern in October 2014 when Kyrgyzstan lawmakers passed a law that imposes jail terms on [gay-rights activists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_Kyrgyzstan "LGBT rights in Kyrgyzstan") and others, including journalists, who create "a positive attitude toward non-traditional sexual relations".[^126] Kyrgyzstani activist and journalist Azimzhan Askarov was sentenced to life in prison in 2010.[^127] On 24 January 2017, a Kyrgyz court has reinstated a sentence of life imprisonment for Askarov.[^128] In February 2024, the independent investigative media organization [Kloop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kloop "Kloop") was ordered shut down by the Kyrgyz courts. This move drew criticism within the country and abroad.[^129] [^130]

## Economy

Kyrgyzstan was among the less economically developed republics of the former Soviet Union and is currently the second-lowest in terms of income levels in Central Asia, after Tajikistan. 22.4% of the country's population lives below the poverty line.[^131]

Despite the backing of major Western lenders, including the [International Monetary Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund "International Monetary Fund") (IMF), the [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank") and the [Asian Development Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_Development_Bank "Asian Development Bank"), Kyrgyzstan has had economic difficulties following independence. Initially, these were a result of the breakup of the Soviet trade bloc and resulting loss of markets, which impeded the republic's transition to a demand economy. The government reduced public spending, eliminated most price subsidies, and implemented a value-added tax. These measures reflected its commitment to transitioning toward a market-based economy. Through economic stabilization and structural reforms, the government aimed to promote sustained long-term growth. Reforms led to Kyrgyzstan's accession to the [World Trade Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Trade_Organization "World Trade Organization") (WTO) on 20 December 1998.

The Kyrgyz economy was severely affected by the collapse of the Soviet Union and the resulting loss of its vast market. In 1990, some 98% of Kyrgyz exports went to other parts of the Soviet Union. Thus, the nation's economic performance in the early 1990s was worse than any other former Soviet republic except war-torn Armenia, Azerbaijan and Tajikistan, as factories and state farms collapsed with the disappearance of their traditional markets in the former Soviet Union. While economic performance has improved considerably in the last few years, and particularly since 1998, difficulties remain in securing adequate [fiscal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiscal_Policy "Fiscal Policy") revenues and providing an adequate [social safety net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_safety_net "Social safety net"). [Remittances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remittance "Remittance") of around 350,000 Kyrgyz migrants working in Russia contribute to the economy, however in recent years remittances have decreased.[^132] [^133] Reliance on remittances—currently about 15% of GDP—has declined from a previous high of 35%.

Agriculture is an important sector of the economy in Kyrgyzstan (see [agriculture in Kyrgyzstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture_in_Kyrgyzstan "Agriculture in Kyrgyzstan")). By the early 1990s, the private agricultural sector provided between one-third and one-half of some harvests. In 2002, agriculture accounted for 35.6% of GDP and about half of employment. Kyrgyzstan's terrain is mountainous, which accommodates livestock raising, the largest agricultural activity, so the resulting [wool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wool "Wool"), meat and [dairy products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dairy_product "Dairy product") are major commodities. Main crops include wheat, [sugar beets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar_beets "Sugar beets"), [potatoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potatoes "Potatoes"), cotton, tobacco, vegetables, and fruit. As the prices of imported [agrichemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agrichemicals "Agrichemicals") and petroleum are so high, much farming is being done by hand and by horse, as it was generations ago. Agricultural processing is a key component of the industrial economy as well as one of the most attractive sectors for foreign investment.

Kyrgyzstan is rich in mineral resources but has negligible petroleum and natural gas reserves; it imports petroleum and gas. Among its mineral reserves are substantial deposits of coal, gold, [uranium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranium "Uranium"), [antimony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimony "Antimony"), and other valuable metals. [Metallurgy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallurgy "Metallurgy") is an important industry, and the government hopes to attract foreign investment in this field. The government has actively encouraged foreign involvement in extracting and processing gold from the [Kumtor Gold Mine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kumtor_Gold_Mine "Kumtor Gold Mine") and other regions. The country's plentiful water resources and mountainous terrain enable it to produce and export large quantities of [hydroelectric energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroelectric_energy "Hydroelectric energy").

The principal exports are nonferrous metals and minerals, woollen goods and other agricultural products, electric energy and certain engineering goods. Imports include petroleum and natural gas, ferrous metals, chemicals, most machinery, wood and paper products, some foods and some construction materials. Its leading trade partners include Germany, Russia, China, Kazakhstan, and Uzbekistan. After Beijing launched the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) in 2013, China has expanded its economic presence and initiated a number of sizable infrastructure projects in Kyrgyzstan.[^134] After the 2022 [sanctions on Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanctions_involving_Russia "Sanctions involving Russia"), several regional supply routes were redirected through Kyrgyzstan, boosting the local economy.

The COVID-19 pandemic is expected to have a negative impact on the Kyrgyz economy that is reliant on services, remittances and natural resources. As a result, to mitigate the economic shock and preserve much of the development progress achieved in recent years the World Bank will provide support by financing several projects in the country.[^135]

Kyrgyzstan registered rapid economic growth over 2022–2024, averaging 9% annually in real terms. These changes highlight Kyrgyzstan's strategic importance in Central Asia and its emerging role in regional trade and infrastructure networks.

Kyrgyzstan demonstrated strong macroeconomic performance between 2022 and 2024. The country maintained a fiscal surplus for two consecutive years, in 2023 and 2024, reflecting prudent public financial management and stronger-than-expected revenue collection.

The government is embarking on several large infrastructure megaprojects, notably the [Kambarata-1 Hydropower Plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kambarata-1_Dam "Kambarata-1 Dam") and the [China-Kyrgyzstan–Uzbekistan railway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China-Kyrgyzstan-Uzbekistan_International_Highway "China-Kyrgyzstan-Uzbekistan International Highway"), which have the potential to significantly reshape the country's economic landscape.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Osh_Bazar.jpg/250px-Osh_Bazar.jpg)

Bazar in Osh

One of the most popular tourist destination points in Kyrgyzstan is the lake [Issyk-Kul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Issyk-Kul "Issyk-Kul"). Numerous hotels, resorts and boarding houses are located along its northern shore. The most popular beach zones are in the city of [Cholpon-Ata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cholpon-Ata "Cholpon-Ata") and the settlements nearby, such as Kara-Oi (Dolinka), Bosteri and Korumdy. The number of tourists visiting the lake was more than a million a year in 2006 and 2007. However, due to the economic and political instability in the region, the number has declined in recent years.[^136]

### Science and technology

The headquarters of the Kyrgyz Academy of Sciences is located in Bishkek, where several research institutes are located. Kyrgyz researchers are developing useful technologies based on natural products, such as heavy metal remediation for purifying waste water.[^137] Kyrgyzstan was ranked 96th in the [Global Innovation Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Innovation_Index "Global Innovation Index") in 2025.[^138] [^139]

### Ethnic groups

The nation's largest [ethnic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_group "Ethnic group") are the [Kyrgyz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_people "Kyrgyz people"), a [Turkic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_peoples "Turkic peoples") people, who comprise 77.8% of the population. Other ethnic groups include the [Russians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians "Russians") (3.7%) concentrated in the north and the Uzbeks (14.3%) living in the south. Small but noticeable minorities include the [Dungans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dungans "Dungans") (1.0%), [Tajiks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C4%81jik_people "Tājik people") (0.9%), [Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghur_people "Uyghur people") (0.5%), Kazakhs (0.4%), and other smaller ethnic minorities.[^15] The country has over 80 ethnic groups.[^143]

The Kyrgyz have historically been semi- [nomadic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomad "Nomad") herders, living in round tents called [yurts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yurt "Yurt") and tending sheep, horses and [yaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yak "Yak"). This nomadic tradition continues to function seasonally (see [transhumance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transhumance "Transhumance")) as herding families return to the high mountain pasture (or *jailoo*) in the summer. The sedentary Uzbeks and Tajiks traditionally have farmed lower-lying irrigated land in the [Fergana Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fergana_Valley "Fergana Valley").[^144]

Kyrgyzstan has undergone a pronounced change in its ethnic composition since independence.[^145] [^146] [^147] The percentage of ethnic Kyrgyz has increased from around 50% in 1979 to over 70% in 2013, while the percentage of ethnic groups, such as Russians, Ukrainians, Germans and Tatars dropped from 35% to about 7%.[^141] Since 1991, a large number of [Germans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germans_in_Kyrgyzstan "Germans in Kyrgyzstan"), who in 1989 numbered 101,000 persons, have emigrated to Germany.[^148]

### Languages

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Kyrgyz_traditionalscript_2.jpg/250px-Kyrgyz_traditionalscript_2.jpg)

The name of Kyrgyzstan rendered in the traditional script in use from the 13th century to 1920

[Kyrgyz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyz_language "Kyrgyz language") is the state language of Kyrgyzstan. [Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language "Russian language") is additionally an official language. Kyrgyzstan is one of five former Soviet republics to have Russian as a *[de jure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_jure "De jure")* [official language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Official_language "Official language"), along with Russia, Belarus, Kazakhstan, and Tajikistan.[^154] After the dissolution of the Soviet Union, Kyrgyz was adopted as the state language of Kyrgyzstan in 1991. Kyrgyzstan adopted Russian as an official language in 2000.[^155] The languages have different legal statuses.

Kyrgyz is a [Turkic language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_languages "Turkic languages") of the [Kipchak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kipchak_languages "Kipchak languages") branch, closely related to [Kazakh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_language "Kazakh language"), [Karakalpak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakalpak_language "Karakalpak language"), and [Nogay Tatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nogay_language "Nogay language"). It was written in the [Arabic alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_alphabet "Arabic alphabet") until the twentieth century. The [Latin script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_alphabet "Latin alphabet") was introduced and adopted on Stalin's orders in 1928, and was subsequently replaced by [Cyrillic script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrillic_script "Cyrillic script") in 1941.[^156] A reformed Perso-Arabic alphabet, created by the Kyrgyz intellectual and scientist [Kasym Tynystanov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kasym_Tynystanov "Kasym Tynystanov"), is the official script of the Kyrgyz language in the People's Republic of China.[^157] As a result of the pending language reform in neighboring Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan will be the only independent Turkic-speaking country in a few years that exclusively uses the Cyrillic alphabet.[^158] In April 2023, Russia suspended dairy exports to Kyrgyzstan after the chairman of Kyrgyzstan's National Commission for the State Language and Language Policies, Kanybek Osmonaliev, proposed to change the official script from Cyrillic to Latin to bring the country in line with other Turkic-speaking nations. Osmonaliev was reprimanded by President [Sadyr Japarov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sadyr_Japarov "Sadyr Japarov") who then clarified that Kyrgyzstan had no plans to replace the Cyrillic alphabet.[^159]

Russian television media enjoy enormous popularity in Kyrgyzstan, especially in the Russophone city of Bishkek and the Chüy Region, despite the percentage of Russians today being a fraction of that in 1989. According to [World Values Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Values_Survey "World Values Survey") in 2020, Russian was the language spoken at home for 55.6% of the population of Bishkek, and Kyrgyz was the second with 43.6%. However, the countrywide figure for Russian was only 16.3%, whereas Kyrgyz was the home language of 70.9%. The [Uzbek language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbek_language "Uzbek language") was the third most spoken home language with 10.7% according to the same survey.[^160] Russian media outlets have an enormous influence on public opinion in Kyrgyzstan, especially in areas such as human rights and international political developments.[^161]

Many business and political affairs are carried out in Russian. Until recently, Kyrgyz remained a language spoken at home and was rarely used during meetings or other events. However, most parliamentary meetings today are conducted in Kyrgyz, with simultaneous interpretation available for those not speaking Kyrgyz. According to an [RFE/RL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RFE/RL "RFE/RL") article from 2014, despite the attempts to raise the status of Kyrgyz, thousands of Kyrgyz are [russifying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russifying "Russifying") their names every year (around 40,000), mostly for career prospects, and to remove themselves from the Russian blacklists (people who are to be deported upon entrance) by registering different names. There are also many Russian-language medium schools that are supported from the Russian foundations via the embassy of Russia in Bishkek which are better funded than the Kyrgyz language medium schools. Due to this, many ethnic Kyrgyz go to Russian language medium schools. Many high school students change their surnames annually; for example, 800 such changes were recorded in high school students in the region of Naryn.[^162]

### Religion

<table><caption><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_in_Kyrgyzstan">Religion in Kyrgyzstan</a> (2017 estimate)</caption><tbody><tr><th colspan="2"></th><td></td><th colspan="2"></th></tr><tr><td colspan="2"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Kyrgyzstan">Islam</a></td><td></td><td colspan="2">90%</td></tr><tr><td colspan="2"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_in_Kyrgyzstan">Christianity</a></td><td></td><td colspan="2">7%</td></tr><tr><td colspan="2"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_in_Kyrgyzstan">other</a></td><td></td><td colspan="2">3%</td></tr></tbody></table>

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Karakol-Dungan-Mosque-Exterior-1.jpg/250px-Karakol-Dungan-Mosque-Exterior-1.jpg)

Karakol Dungan Mosque

[Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") is the dominant religion in Kyrgyzstan, and most of the Kyrgyz are Muslim. The CIA World Factbook estimates that as of 2017, 90% of the population is Muslim, with the majority being [Non-denominational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-denominational_Muslim "Non-denominational Muslim") and [Sunni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunni "Sunni"); 7% are Christian, including 3% [Russian Orthodoxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church "Russian Orthodox Church"), and the remainder are other religions.[^163] A 2009 [Pew Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pew_Research_Center "Pew Research Center") report indicated 86.3% of Kyrgyzstan's population adhering to Islam.[^164] The great majority of Muslims are Sunni, adhering to the [Hanafi school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanafi "Hanafi") of thought,[^165] although a 2012 Pew survey report showed that only 23% of respondents to a questionnaire chose to identify themselves as Sunni, with 64% volunteering that they were "just a Muslim".[^166] There are a few [Ahmadiyya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmadiyya "Ahmadiyya") Muslims, though unrecognized by the country.[^167]

During Soviet times, [state atheism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_atheism "State atheism") was encouraged. Today, however, Kyrgyzstan is a [secular state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secular_state "Secular state"), although Islam has exerted a growing influence in politics.[^168] For instance, there has been an attempt to arrange for officials to travel on *[hajj](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hajj "Hajj")* (the pilgrimage to [Mecca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mecca "Mecca")) under a tax-free arrangement.

While [Islam in Kyrgyzstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Kyrgyzstan "Islam in Kyrgyzstan") is more of a cultural background than a devout daily practice for many, public figures have expressed support for restoring religious values. For example, human rights [ombudsman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ombudsman "Ombudsman") Tursunbay Bakir-Ulu noted, "In this era of independence, it is not surprising that there has been a return to spiritual roots not only in Kyrgyzstan, but also in other post-communist republics. It would be immoral to develop a market-based society without an ethical dimension." [^168]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Bishkek_church_01.jpg/250px-Bishkek_church_01.jpg)

Bishkek Eastern Orthodox Church

Additionally, [Bermet Akayeva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermet_Akayeva "Bermet Akayeva"), the daughter of Askar Akayev, the former President of Kyrgyzstan, stated during a July 2007 interview that Islam is increasingly taking root across the nation.[^169] She emphasized that many mosques have recently been built and that the Kyrgyz are increasingly devoting themselves to Islam, which she noted was "not a bad thing in itself. It keeps our society more moral, cleaner." [^169] There is a contemporary [Sufi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sufi "Sufi") order present which adheres to a somewhat different form of Islam than orthodox Islam.[^170]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Mosque_under_construction_in_Kyrgyzstan.jpg/250px-Mosque_under_construction_in_Kyrgyzstan.jpg)

Mosque under construction in Kyrgyzstan

The other faiths practiced in Kyrgyzstan include Russian Orthodox and [Ukrainian Orthodox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthodox_Church_of_Ukraine "Orthodox Church of Ukraine") versions of Christianity, practiced primarily by Russians and Ukrainians respectively. A small minority of ethnic Germans are also Christian, mostly [Lutheran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lutheran_Church "Lutheran Church") and [Anabaptist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anabaptist "Anabaptist"), and there is a [Roman Catholic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Catholic "Roman Catholic") community of approximately 600.[^171] [^172]

As part of the historic [Kyrgyzstan German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan_Germans "Kyrgyzstan Germans") minority, there were around 200 [Mennonites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mennonites "Mennonites") in Kyrgyzstan in 2022.[^173] One Mennonite community continues in the settlement of [Rot-Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rot-Front "Rot-Front").[^174]

A community of 5,000 to 10,000 Jehovah's Witnesses gathers in both Kyrgyz and Russian-speaking congregations and some Chinese- and Turkish-speaking groups.[^175] [^176]

A few [Animistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animism "Animism") traditions survive, as do influences from [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") such as the tying of [prayer flags](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prayer_flag "Prayer flag") onto sacred trees, though some view this practice as rooted within Sufi Islam.[^177] There is also a small number of [Bukharian Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bukharian_Jews "Bukharian Jews") living in Kyrgyzstan, but during the collapse of the Soviet Union most fled to other countries, mainly the United States and Israel. In addition, there is a small community of [Ashkenazi Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews "Ashkenazi Jews"), who fled to the country from eastern Europe during the [Second World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_World_War "Second World War").[^178]

On 6 November 2008, the Kyrgyz parliament unanimously passed a law increasing the minimum number of adherents for recognizing a religion from 10 to 200. It also outlawed "aggressive action aimed at [proselytism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proselytism "Proselytism") ", and banned religious activity in schools and all activity by unregistered organizations. It was signed by President Kurmanbek Bakiyev on 12 January 2009.[^179]

There have been several reported police raids against peaceful minority religious meetings,[^180] as well as reports of officials planting false evidence,[^181] but also some court decisions in favour of religious minorities.[^182]

Some of the confusion may stem from the fact that [arranged marriages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arranged_marriage "Arranged marriage") were traditional, and one of the ways to escape an arranged marriage was to arrange a consensual "kidnapping".[^186]

### Flag

The 40-rayed yellow sun in the center of [the national flag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_Kyrgyzstan "Flag of Kyrgyzstan") represent the 40 tribes that once made up the entirety of Kyrgyz culture before the intervention of Russia during the rise of the Soviet Union. The lines inside the sun represent the crown or tündük (Kyrgyz түндүк) of a yurt, a symbol replicated in many facets of Kyrgyz architecture. The red portion of the flag represents peace and openness of Kyrgyzstan.

Under Soviet rule and before 1992, it had the [flag of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_the_Soviet_Union "Flag of the Soviet Union") with two large blue stripes and a thin white stripe in the middle.

### Public holidays

In addition to celebrating the New Year each 1 January, the Kyrgyz observe the traditional New Year festival [Nowruz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nowruz "Nowruz") on the vernal equinox. This spring holiday is celebrated with feasts and festivities such as the horse game [Ulak Tartish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buzkashi "Buzkashi").

This is the list of public holidays in Kyrgyzstan:

- 1 January – New Year's Day
- 7 January – Orthodox Christmas
- 23 February – Fatherland Defender's Day
- 8 March – Women's Day
- 21–23 March – Nooruz Mairamy, Persian New Year (spring festival)
- 7 April – Day of National Revolution
- 1 May – Labor Day
- 5 May – Constitution Day
- 8 May – Remembrance Day
- 9 May – [Victory Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victory_Day_\(9_May\) "Victory Day (9 May)")
- 31 August – Independence Day
- 7–8 November – Days of History and Commemoration of Ancestors

Two additional Muslim holidays *[Orozo Ayt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eid_al-Fitr "Eid al-Fitr")* and *[Qurman (or Qurban) Ayt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eid_al-Adha "Eid al-Adha")* are defined by the lunar calendar.

### Sports

In the [2000 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2000_Summer_Olympics "2000 Summer Olympics") in [Sydney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sydney "Sydney"), Kyrgyzstan received its first ever Olympic medal when [Aidyn Smagulov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aidyn_Smagulov "Aidyn Smagulov") won bronze in the men's 60 kg competition final in judo.[^187] [^188]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Bandy_2012._KYR_-_JPN.JPG/250px-Bandy_2012._KYR_-_JPN.JPG)

Bandy: Kyrgyzstan in red against Japan

[Football](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_football "Association football") is one of the most popular sports in Kyrgyzstan.[^189] [^190] The official governing body is the [Football Federation of Kyrgyz Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Football_Federation_of_Kyrgyz_Republic "Football Federation of Kyrgyz Republic"), which was founded in 1992, after the split of the Soviet Union. It administers the [Kyrgyzstan national football team](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan_national_football_team "Kyrgyzstan national football team").[^191]

[Wrestling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wrestling "Wrestling") is also very popular. Since the [2008 Summer Olympic Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympic_Games "2008 Summer Olympic Games"), Kyrgyzstani wrestlers have won six medals in [Greco-Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Roman_wrestling "Greco-Roman wrestling") and [freestyle wrestling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freestyle_wrestling "Freestyle wrestling"): three in [2008](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wrestling_at_the_2008_Summer_Olympics "Wrestling at the 2008 Summer Olympics") and three in [2020](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wrestling_at_the_2020_Summer_Olympics "Wrestling at the 2020 Summer Olympics").[^192]

[Ice hockey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_hockey "Ice hockey") was not as popular in Kyrgyzstan until the first [Ice Hockey Championship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan_Championship_\(ice_hockey\) "Kyrgyzstan Championship (ice hockey)") was organized in 2009. In 2011, the [Kyrgyzstan men's national ice hockey team](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan_men%27s_national_ice_hockey_team "Kyrgyzstan men's national ice hockey team") won [2011 Asian Winter Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_Asian_Winter_Games "2011 Asian Winter Games") Premier Division dominating in all six games with six wins. It was the first major international event that Kyrgyzstan's ice hockey team took part in.[^193] The Kyrgyzstan men's ice hockey team joined the [IIHF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IIHF "IIHF") in July 2011.

[Bandy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bandy "Bandy") is becoming increasingly popular in the country. The Kyrgyz national team took Kyrgyzstan's first medal at the [Asian Winter Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_Winter_Games "Asian Winter Games"), when they captured the bronze. They played in the [Bandy World Championship 2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bandy_World_Championship_2012 "Bandy World Championship 2012"), their first appearance in that tournament.[^194]

[Kyrgyzstan's national basketball team](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan%27s_national_basketball_team "Kyrgyzstan's national basketball team") had its best performance at the official [1995 Asian Basketball Championship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1995_Asian_Basketball_Championship "1995 Asian Basketball Championship") where the team finished ahead of favorites including Iran, Philippines and Jordan.

XXI International Issyk-Kul Sports Games (SCO + CIS) was held in 9–17 September 2022 in [Baktuu-Dolonotu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baktuu-Dolonotu "Baktuu-Dolonotu") village ([Issyk-Kul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Issyk-Kul "Issyk-Kul")).[^195] The first three World Nomad Games were held in Cholpon-Ata, Kyrgyzstan. The 6th International Sports Festival Pearl of Kyrgyzstan were held in Issyk-Kul region from 15 June to 3 July 2022.

### Horse riding

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Kok-boru%2C_Kyrgyzstan.jpg/250px-Kok-boru%2C_Kyrgyzstan.jpg)

Ulak Tartysh

The traditional national sports reflect the importance of [horse riding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horse_riding "Horse riding") in Kyrgyz culture.

Very popular, as in all of Central Asia, is *[Ulak Tartysh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buzkashi "Buzkashi")*, a [team game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Team_sports "Team sports") resembling a cross between [polo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polo "Polo") and [rugby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rugby_football "Rugby football") in which two teams of riders wrestle for possession of the headless carcass of a goat, which they attempt to deliver across the opposition's goal line, or into the opposition's goal: a big tub or a circle marked on the ground.

Other popular games on horseback include:

- *At Chabysh* – a long-distance horse race, sometimes over a distance of more than 50 km
- *Jumby Atmai* – a large bar of precious metal (the "jumby") is tied to a pole by a thread and contestants attempt to break the thread by shooting at it, while at a gallop
- *[Kyz Kuumai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyz_Kuumai "Kyz Kuumai")* – a man chases a girl to win a kiss from her, while she gallops away; if he is not successful she may in turn chase him and attempt to beat him with her "kamchi" (horsewhip)
- *Oodarysh* – two contestants wrestle on horseback, each attempting to be the first to throw the other from his horse
- *Tyin Emmei* – picking up a coin from the ground at full gallop

## Education

The school system in Kyrgyzstan also includes primary (grades 1 to 4, some schools have optional 0 grade), secondary (grades 5 to 9) and high (grades 10 to 11) divisions within one school.[^196] Children are usually accepted to primary schools at the age of 6 or 7. It is required that every child finishes 9 grades of school and receives a certificate of completion. Grades 10–11 are optional, but it is necessary to complete them to graduate and receive a state-accredited school diploma. To graduate, a student must complete the 11-year school course and pass 4 mandatory state exams in writing, maths, history, and a foreign language.

As of 2023, there were 4,989 primary and secondary schools in the country, including 445 in Bishkek;[^197] [^198] the large majority of these (4,537) were [public schools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_school "State school").[^199] The country also counted 58 higher educational institutions and universities, out of which 42 were public and 16 private.[^199]

In September 2016, the [University of Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Central_Asia "University of Central Asia") was launched in Naryn, Kyrgyzstan. There are also various Russian-language medium schools in Bishkek, Osh and other areas. Because of the better funding that they receive in comparation with Kyrgyz state schools, many Kyrgyz go there. In March 2021 Russia announced its plans to create approximately 30 new Russian-language schools in Kyrgyzstan. Teachers from Russia are also working here. However, the existence of these schools has been criticised, for reasons such as the fact that Russian language education has flaws compared to the Turkish and American schools in the country, but also because many ethnic Kyrgyz born after Kyrgyz independence in 1991 can't speak Kyrgyz, but only Russian, according to a Bishkek resident.[^200]

### Libraries

Kyrgyzstan is home to 1,066 libraries.[^201] The [National Library of the Kyrgyz Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Library_of_the_Kyrgyz_Republic "National Library of the Kyrgyz Republic") is the oldest library in the country, which was established in 1934. Kyrgyz Libraries are working towards expanding access to communities, evident in projects such as the signing of the [Marrakesh VIP Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marrakesh_VIP_Treaty "Marrakesh VIP Treaty") and the [Open access](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_access "Open access") Portal.[^202] [^203]

## Transport

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Bishkek_03-2016_img47_minibus_at_Chuy_Prospekt.jpg/250px-Bishkek_03-2016_img47_minibus_at_Chuy_Prospekt.jpg)

Marshrutka in Bishkek

Transport in Kyrgyzstan is severely constrained by the country's alpine topography. Roads have to snake up steep valleys, cross passes of 3,000 metres (9,800 ft) altitude and more, and are subject to frequent mudslides and snow avalanches. Winter travel is close to impossible in many of the more remote and high-altitude regions.

Additional problems come from the fact that many roads and railway lines built during the Soviet period are today intersected by international boundaries, requiring time-consuming border formalities to cross where they are not completely closed. Horses are still a much-used transport option, especially in more rural areas; Kyrgyzstan's road infrastructure is not extensive, so horses are able to reach locations that motor vehicles cannot, and they do not require expensive, imported [fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuel "Fuel").

### Airports

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Bishkek_03-2016_img51_Manas_Airport.jpg/250px-Bishkek_03-2016_img51_Manas_Airport.jpg)

Manas International Airport

At the end of the Soviet period there were about 50 airports and airstrips in Kyrgyzstan, many of them built primarily to serve military purposes in this border region so close to China. Only a few of them remain in service today. The [Kyrgyzstan Air Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan_Air_Company "Kyrgyzstan Air Company") provides air transport to China, Russia, and other local countries.

- Manas International Airport near Bishkek is the main international airport, with services to Moscow, [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent"), [Almaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almaty "Almaty"), [Ürümqi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%9Cr%C3%BCmqi "Ürümqi"), [Istanbul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Istanbul "Istanbul"), [Baku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baku "Baku"), and [Dubai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dubai "Dubai").
- [Osh Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osh_Airport "Osh Airport") is the main air terminal in the south of the country, with daily connections to Bishkek, and services to Moscow, Krasnoyarsk, Almaty and more international places.
- [Jalal-Abad Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jalal-Abad_Airport "Jalal-Abad Airport") is linked to Bishkek by daily flights. The national flag carrier, Kyrgyzstan, operates flights on [BAe-146](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BAe-146 "BAe-146") aircraft. During the summer months, a weekly flight links Jalal-Abad with the Issyk-Kul Region.
- Other facilities built during the Soviet era are either closed down, used only occasionally or restricted to military use (e.g., [Kant Air Base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kant_Air_Base "Kant Air Base") near Bishkek, which is used by the [Russian Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Air_Force "Russian Air Force")).

### Banned airline status

Kyrgyzstan appears on the European Union's [list of prohibited countries for the certification of airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_air_carriers_banned_in_the_European_Union "List of air carriers banned in the European Union"). This means that no airline that is registered in Kyrgyzstan may operate services of any kind within the European Union, due to safety standards that fail to meet European regulations.[^204]

### Railways

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/3%D0%A2%D0%AD10%D0%9C-1024%2C_%D0%9A%D1%8B%D1%80%D0%B3%D1%8B%D0%B7%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B0%D0%BD%2C_%D0%A7%D1%83%D0%B9%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B0%D1%8F_%D0%BE%D0%B1%D0%BB%D0%B0%D1%81%D1%82%D1%8C%2C_%D0%BF%D0%B5%D1%80%D0%B5%D0%B3%D0%BE%D0%BD_%D1%80%D0%B0%D0%B7%D1%8A%D0%B5%D0%B7%D0%B4_%E2%84%96_148_-_%D0%9A%D0%B0%D1%8F%D0%BC%D0%B0%D1%82-%D0%9A%D1%83%D1%80%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%BB_%28Trainpix_204613%29.jpg/250px-3%D0%A2%D0%AD10%D0%9C-1024%2C_%D0%9A%D1%8B%D1%80%D0%B3%D1%8B%D0%B7%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B0%D0%BD%2C_%D0%A7%D1%83%D0%B9%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B0%D1%8F_%D0%BE%D0%B1%D0%BB%D0%B0%D1%81%D1%82%D1%8C%2C_%D0%BF%D0%B5%D1%80%D0%B5%D0%B3%D0%BE%D0%BD_%D1%80%D0%B0%D0%B7%D1%8A%D0%B5%D0%B7%D0%B4_%E2%84%96_148_-_%D0%9A%D0%B0%D1%8F%D0%BC%D0%B0%D1%82-%D0%9A%D1%83%D1%80%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%BB_%28Trainpix_204613%29.jpg)

A train passing through the Chüy Valley

The [Chüy Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ch%C3%BCy_Valley "Chüy Valley") in the north and the Fergana valley in the south were endpoints of the Soviet Union's rail system in Central Asia. Following the emergence of independent post-Soviet states, the rail lines which were built without regard for administrative boundaries have been cut by borders, and traffic is therefore severely curtailed. The small bits of rail lines within Kyrgyzstan, about 370 km (230 mi) (1,520 mm (59.8 in) broad gauge) in total, have little economic value in the absence of the former bulk traffic over long distances to and from such centres as Tashkent, Almaty, and the cities of Russia.

In 2022, construction began on a new 186 km extension of the existing railway from [Balykchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balykchy "Balykchy") to [Karakeche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakeche "Karakeche"), primarily meant to carry coal from mines at Karakeche to Bishkek.[^205] In June 2023, a railway between Balykchy and Bishkek was officially opened.[^206] [^207]

The planned construction of a 523 km [China–Kyrgyzstan–Uzbekistan railway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Kyrgyzstan%E2%80%93Uzbekistan_railway "China–Kyrgyzstan–Uzbekistan railway") (CKU) was announced in 2022, comprising 213 km (132 mi) in China, 260 km (162 mi) in Kyrgyzstan and 50 km (31 mi) in Uzbekistan.[^208] The railway, conceived as part of China's [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative"), is planned to lead from [Kashgar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashgar "Kashgar") through the [Torugart Pass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torugart_Pass "Torugart Pass") to Jalal-Abad, and further on to the Uzbek city of Andijan.[^209] Construction is set to begin in July 2025.[^209]

### Highways

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Kyrgyzstan_%286052094329%29.jpg/250px-Kyrgyzstan_%286052094329%29.jpg)

Highway A363 Bishkek towards Balykchy, Lake Issyk-Kul and Chinese border ( Xinjiang )

With support from the Asian Development Bank, a major road linking the north and southwest of the country from the capital city of Bishkek to Osh has recently been completed. This considerably eases communication between the two major population centres of the country—the Chüy Valley in the north and the Fergana Valley in the South. An offshoot of this road branches off across a 3,500-meter [pass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain_pass "Mountain pass") into the [Talas Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talas_Valley "Talas Valley") in the northwest. Plans are now being formulated to build a major road from Osh into China.

- *total:* 34,000 km (21,127 mi) (including 140 km (87 mi) of expressways)
- *paved:* 22,600 km (14,043 mi) (includes some all-weather gravel-surfaced roads)
- *unpaved:* 7,700 km (4,785 mi) (these roads are made of unstabilized earth and are difficult to negotiate in wet weather) (1990)
