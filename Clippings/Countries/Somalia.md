**Somalia**,[^2] officially the **Federal Republic of Somalia**,[^3] is the easternmost country in continental [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa"). Stretching across the [Horn of Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horn_of_Africa "Horn of Africa"), it borders [Ethiopia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopia "Ethiopia") to the west, [Djibouti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djibouti "Djibouti") [^4] to the northwest, [Kenya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenya "Kenya") to the southwest, the [Gulf of Aden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden "Gulf of Aden") to the north, and the [Indian Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean") to the east. Somalia has the longest coastline on Africa's mainland. Somalia has an estimated population of more than 18 million, of which 2.7 million live in the capital and largest city, [Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mogadishu "Mogadishu"). As one of Africa's most ethnically homogenous countries, around 85% of its residents are ethnic [Somalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_people "Somali people"). The official and national language of the country is [Somali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_language "Somali language") while [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic") is recognised as a second language. The overwhelming majority of the population are [Sunni Muslims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunni_Islam "Sunni Islam").

In antiquity, Somalia was an important commercial centre.[^21] [^22] During the Middle Ages, several powerful Somali empires dominated the regional trade, including the [Ajuran Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajuran_Sultanate "Ajuran Sultanate"), [Adal Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adal_Sultanate "Adal Sultanate"), and the [Sultanate of the Geledi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultanate_of_the_Geledi "Sultanate of the Geledi"). In the late 19th century, the Somali [sultanates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan "Sultan") were colonised by the [Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Empire "Italian Empire") and [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") empires,[^23] [^24] who merged all of these tribal territories into two [colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonisation_of_Africa "Colonisation of Africa"): [Italian Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Somaliland "Italian Somaliland") and [British Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Somaliland "British Somaliland").[^25] [^26] In 1960, the two territories united to form the independent [Somali Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Republic "Somali Republic") under a civilian government. [Siad Barre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siad_Barre "Siad Barre") of the [Supreme Revolutionary Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Revolutionary_Council_\(Somalia\) "Supreme Revolutionary Council (Somalia)") (SRC) seized power in 1969 and established the [Somali Democratic Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Democratic_Republic "Somali Democratic Republic"), brutally attempting to quash the [Somaliland War of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somaliland_War_of_Independence "Somaliland War of Independence") in the north of the country.[^27] The SRC collapsed in 1991 with the onset of the [Somali Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Civil_War "Somali Civil War").[^28] The [Transitional National Government of Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitional_National_Government_of_Somalia "Transitional National Government of Somalia") (TNG) was established in 2000, followed by the formation of the [Transitional Federal Government of Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitional_Federal_Government_of_Somalia "Transitional Federal Government of Somalia") (TFG) in 2004, which reestablished the [Somali Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Armed_Forces "Somali Armed Forces").[^29]

At the end of 2006, a [US-backed Ethiopian invasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_in_Somalia_\(2006%E2%80%932009\) "War in Somalia (2006–2009)") overthrew the [Islamic Courts Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Courts_Union "Islamic Courts Union") (ICU), leading to the installation of the [TFG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitional_Federal_Government_of_Somalia "Transitional Federal Government of Somalia") in [Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mogadishu "Mogadishu") under an Ethiopian [military occupation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_occupation "Military occupation").[^30] The subsequent [insurgency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurgency "Insurgency") which emerged saw the ICU fragment into various rebel factions, including the militant group [al-Shabaab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Shabaab_\(militant_group\) "Al-Shabaab (militant group)"), which waged a protracted conflict against Ethiopian forces.[^31] Al-Shabaab soon began asserting territorial control for the first time,[^32] and by late 2008 the insurgency had driven the Ethiopian army out of much of Somalia.[^33] In 2009, a new TFG government was established.[^34] [^35] By mid-2012, al-Shabaab lost most of its territories during fighting against the TFG and [African Union troops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union_Mission_to_Somalia "African Union Mission to Somalia"). That same year, al-Shabaab pledged allegiance to [al-Qaeda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Qaeda "Al-Qaeda").[^36] The insurgents still control much of central and southern Somalia, and wield influence in government-controlled areas,[^37] with the city of [Jilib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jilib "Jilib") acting as the *de facto* capital for Al-Shabaab-controlled [Islamic Emirate of Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Emirate_of_Somalia "Islamic Emirate of Somalia").[^38] [^39] A new provisional [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Somalia "Constitution of Somalia") was passed in August 2012, reforming Somalia as a [federation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federation "Federation").[^40] The same month, the [Federal Government of Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Government_of_Somalia "Federal Government of Somalia") was formed and a period of reconstruction began in Mogadishu.[^41]

Somalia is among the [least developed countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_developed_countries "Least developed countries") in the world, as evidenced by its ranking in metrics such as [GDP per capita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\)_per_capita "List of countries by GDP (nominal) per capita") and its position near the bottom of the [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index"), above only [South Sudan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Sudan "South Sudan").[^42] [^14] It has maintained an [informal economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informal_economy "Informal economy") mainly based on livestock, remittances from [Somalis working abroad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_diaspora "Somali diaspora"), and telecommunications. It is a member of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League"), [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union"), [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement"), [East African Community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_African_Community "East African Community"), and the [Organisation of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation").

## Etymology

[Samaale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samaale "Samaale"), the oldest common ancestor of several [Somali clans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_clans "Somali clans"), is generally regarded as the source of the [ethnonym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnonym "Ethnonym") *Somali*. One other theory is that the name is held to be derived from the words *soo* and *maal*, which together mean "go" and "milk". This interpretation differs depending on region with northern Somalis imply it refers to go and milk in regards to the camel's milk, southern Somalis use the transliteration "sa'maal" which refers to cow's milk.[^43] This is a reference to the ubiquitous [pastoralism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pastoralism "Pastoralism") of the Somali people.[^44] Another plausible [etymology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Etymology "Etymology") proposes that the term *Somali* is derived from the [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language") word for "wealthy" (*zāwamāl*), again referring to Somali riches in livestock.[^45] [^46]

A [Tang Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty") document from the 9th century AD referred to the northern coast of Somalia — which was then part of a broader region in [Northeast Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northeast_Africa "Northeast Africa") known as [Barbaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbara_\(region\) "Barbara (region)"), in reference to the area's Barbars ([Cushitic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cushitic-speaking_peoples "Cushitic-speaking peoples")) inhabitants [^47] — as *Po-pa-li*.[^48] [^49]

The first clear written reference of the sobriquet *Somali* dates back to the early 15th century AD during the reign of Ethiopian Emperor [Yeshaq I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yeshaq_I "Yeshaq I") who had one of his court officials compose a [hymn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hymn "Hymn") celebrating a military victory over the [Sultanate of Ifat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultanate_of_Ifat "Sultanate of Ifat").[^50] *Simur* was also an ancient [Harari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harari_people "Harari people") alias for the Somali people.[^51]

Somalis overwhelmingly prefer the demonym *Somali* over the incorrect *Somalian* since the former is an endonym, while the latter is an exonym with double suffixes.[^52] [^53] The [hypernym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypernym "Hypernym") of the term *Somali* from a geopolitical sense is *[Horner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horner_\(demography\) "Horner (demography)")* and from an ethnic sense, it is *[Cushite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cushitic_peoples "Cushitic peoples")*.[^54]

## History

### Prehistory

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Laas_Geel_single_cow.jpg/250px-Laas_Geel_single_cow.jpg)

Neolithic rock art at the Laas Geel complex depicting a long-horned cow

Somalia was likely one of the first lands to be settled by early humans due to its location. [Hunter-gatherers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hunter-gatherer "Hunter-gatherer") who would later migrate out of Africa likely settled here before their migrations.[^55] During the Stone Age, the Doian and Hargeisan cultures flourished here.[^56] [^57] [^58] [^55] [^59] [^60] The oldest evidence of burial customs in the Horn of Africa comes from cemeteries in Somalia dating back to the 4th millennium BC.[^61] The stone implements from the Jalelo site in the north were also characterised in 1909 as important artefacts demonstrating the archaeological universality during the Paleolithic between the East and the West.[^62]

According to linguists, the first [Afroasiatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages "Afroasiatic languages") -speaking populations arrived in the region during the ensuing [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") period from the family's proposed [urheimat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afroasiatic_Urheimat "Afroasiatic Urheimat") ("original homeland") in the [Nile Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile_Valley "Nile Valley"),[^63] or the [Near East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_East "Near East").[^64]

The [Laas Geel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laas_Geel "Laas Geel") complex on the outskirts of [Hargeisa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hargeisa "Hargeisa") in northwestern Somalia dates back approximately 5,000 years, and has [rock art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_art "Rock art") depicting both wild animals and decorated cows.[^65] Other [cave paintings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cave_painting "Cave painting") are found in the northern [Dhambalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhambalin "Dhambalin") region, which feature one of the earliest known depictions of a hunter on horseback. The rock art is dated to 1,000 to 3,000 BC.[^66] [^67] Additionally, between the towns of [Las Khorey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Las_Khorey "Las Khorey") and [El Ayo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Ayo "El Ayo") in northern Somalia lies [Karinhegane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karinhegane "Karinhegane"), the site of numerous cave paintings, which collectively have been estimated to be around 2,500 years old.[^68] [^69]

The [camel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camel "Camel") is believed to have been domesticated in the Horn region sometime between the 2nd and 3rd millennium BC. From there, it spread to [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") and the [Maghreb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maghreb "Maghreb").[^70]

### Antiquity and classical era

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Men_from_Punt_Carrying_Gifts%2C_Tomb_of_Rekhmire_MET_30.4.152_EGDP013029.jpg/250px-Men_from_Punt_Carrying_Gifts%2C_Tomb_of_Rekhmire_MET_30.4.152_EGDP013029.jpg)

Men from Punt carrying Gifts, Tomb of Rekhmire.

Ancient [pyramidical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid "Pyramid") structures, [mausoleums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mausoleum "Mausoleum"), ruined cities and stone walls, such as the [Wargaade Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wargaade_Wall "Wargaade Wall"), are evidence of an old civilisation that once thrived in the Somali peninsula.[^71] [^72] This civilisation enjoyed a trading relationship with [ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") and [Mycenaean Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece "Mycenaean Greece") since the second millennium BC, supporting the hypothesis that Somalia or adjacent regions were the location of the ancient [Land of Punt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_of_Punt "Land of Punt").[^71] [^73] The Puntites native to the region traded [myrrh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myrrh "Myrrh"), spices, gold, ebony, short-horned cattle, ivory and [frankincense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankincense "Frankincense") with the Egyptians, Phoenicians, Babylonians, Indians, Chinese and Romans through their commercial ports. An Egyptian expedition sent to Punt by the [18th dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eighteenth_dynasty_of_Egypt "Eighteenth dynasty of Egypt") Queen [Hatshepsut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hatshepsut "Hatshepsut") is recorded on the temple reliefs at [Deir el-Bahari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deir_el-Bahari "Deir el-Bahari"), during the reign of the Puntite King Parahu and Queen Ati.[^71]

In the [classical era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_antiquity "Classical antiquity"), the [Macrobians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrobians "Macrobians"), who may have been ancestral to Somalis, established a powerful kingdom that ruled large parts of modern Somalia. They were reputed for their longevity and wealth, and were said to be the "tallest and handsomest of all men".[^74] The Macrobians were warrior herders and seafarers. According to Herodotus' account, the [Persian Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Emperor "Persian Emperor") [Cambyses II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambyses_II "Cambyses II"), upon his [conquest of Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Achaemenid_Egypt "History of Achaemenid Egypt") in 525 BC, sent ambassadors to Macrobia, bringing luxury gifts for the Macrobian king to entice his submission. The Macrobian ruler, who was elected based on his stature and beauty, replied instead with a challenge for his Persian counterpart in the form of an unstrung bow: if the Persians could manage to draw it, they would have the right to invade his country; but until then, they should thank the gods that the Macrobians never decided to invade their empire.[^74] [^75] The Macrobians were a regional power reputed for their advanced architecture and [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold "Gold") wealth, which was so plentiful that they shackled their prisoners in golden chains.[^75]

During the classical period, the [Barbara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbaria_\(region\) "Barbaria (region)") city-states of [Mosylon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mosylon "Mosylon"), [Opone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opone "Opone"), [Mundus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heis_\(town\) "Heis (town)"), [Isis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulhar "Bulhar"), [Malao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malao "Malao"), [Avalites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeila "Zeila"), [Essina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essina "Essina"), [Nikon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikon_\(Somalia\) "Nikon (Somalia)") and [Sarapion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarapion "Sarapion") developed a lucrative trade network, connecting with merchants from [Ptolemaic Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemaic_Kingdom "Ptolemaic Kingdom"), [Ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece"), [Phoenicia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia"), [Parthian Persia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parthian_Empire "Parthian Empire"), [Saba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabaeans "Sabaeans"), the [Nabataean Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nabataean_Kingdom "Nabataean Kingdom"), and the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire"). They used the ancient Somali maritime vessel known as the *[beden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beden "Beden")* to transport their cargo.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Beden.jpg/250px-Beden.jpg)

The Beden is a fast, ancient Somali single or double-masted maritime ship.

After the [Roman conquest of the Nabataean Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nabatea#Roman_annexation "Nabatea") and the Roman naval presence at [Aden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aden "Aden") to curb piracy, Arab and Somali merchants agreed with the Romans to bar Indian ships from trading in the free port cities of the [Arabian peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula") [^76] to protect the interests of Somali and Arab merchants in the lucrative commerce between the Red and Mediterranean Seas.[^77] However, Indian merchants continued to trade in the port cities of the Somali peninsula, which was free from Roman interference.[^78] For centuries, Indian merchants brought large quantities of cinnamon to Somalia and Arabia from [Ceylon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceylon "Ceylon") and the [Spice Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maluku_Islands "Maluku Islands"). The source of the cinnamon and other spices is said to have been the best-kept secret of Arab and Somali merchants in their trade with the Roman and Greek world; the Romans and Greeks believed the source to have been the Somali peninsula.[^79] The collusive agreement among Somali and Arab traders inflated the price of Indian and Chinese cinnamon in North Africa, the Near East, and Europe, and made the cinnamon trade a very profitable revenue generator, especially for the Somali merchants.[^77]

### Birth of Islam and the Middle Ages

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Silk_route.jpg/500px-Silk_route.jpg)

The Silk Road extending from China to southern Europe, Arabia, Somalia, Egypt, Persia, India, and Java

[Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") was introduced to the area early on by the first Muslims of Mecca fleeing prosecution during the first [Hejira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hegira "Hegira") with [Masjid al-Qiblatayn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masjid_al-Qiblatayn_\(Somalia\) "Masjid al-Qiblatayn (Somalia)") in [Zeila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeila "Zeila") being built before the [Qiblah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qibla "Qibla") towards [Mecca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mecca "Mecca"). It is one of the oldest [mosques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mosque "Mosque") in Africa.[^80] In the late 9th century, [Al-Yaqubi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Yaqubi "Al-Yaqubi") wrote that Muslims were living along the northern Somali seaboard.[^8] He also mentioned that the [Adal Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adal_Sultanate "Adal Sultanate") had its capital in the city.[^8] [^81] According to [Leo Africanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leo_Africanus "Leo Africanus"), the [Adal Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adal_Sultanate "Adal Sultanate") was governed by local [Somali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_people "Somali people") dynasties and its realm encompassed the geographical area between the Bab el Mandeb and Cape Guardafui. It was thus flanked to the south by the [Ajuran Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajuran_Empire "Ajuran Empire") and to the west by the [Abyssinian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abyssinian_Empire "Abyssinian Empire").[^82]

Throughout the Middle Ages, Arab immigrants arrived in Somaliland, a historical experience which would later lead to the legendary stories about Muslim [sheikhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheikh "Sheikh") such as [Daarood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdirahman_bin_Isma%27il_al-Jabarti "Abdirahman bin Isma'il al-Jabarti") and [Ishaaq bin Ahmed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ishaaq_bin_Ahmed "Ishaaq bin Ahmed") (the purported ancestors of the [Darod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darod "Darod") and [Isaaq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaaq "Isaaq") clans, respectively) travelling from [Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabia "Arabia") to Somalia and marrying into the local [Dir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dir_\(clan\) "Dir (clan)") clan.[^83] The oldest writing in Somalia dates to this time, with the funerary inscription of Ḥajj Shānid in the al-Jami mosque in [Barawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barawa "Barawa") dating to 1104 or 1105 CE (year 498 [AH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hijri_era "Hijri era")).[^84]<sup><span title="Page: 37">: 37</span> </sup> Although these medieval inscriptions are in Arabic, they were clearly written by people who were well-integrated into Somali culture and society. For example, an inscription from 1365 CE calls this year a Saturday year, using the traditional [Somali calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_calendar "Somali calendar") where years are named after the weekday on which the year begins.[^84]<sup><span title="Page: 6">: 6</span></sup>

In 1332, the Zeila-based King of Adal was slain in a military campaign aimed at halting Abyssinian emperor [Amda Seyon I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amda_Seyon_I "Amda Seyon I") 's march toward the city.[^85] When the last Sultan of Ifat, [Sa'ad ad-Din II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sa%27ad_ad-Din_II "Sa'ad ad-Din II"), was also killed by Emperor [Dawit I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dawit_I_of_Ethiopia "Dawit I of Ethiopia") in Zeila in 1410, his children escaped to Yemen, before returning in 1415.[^86] In the early 15th century, Adal's capital was moved further inland to the town of [Dakkar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dakkar "Dakkar"), where [Sabr ad-Din II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabr_ad-Din_II "Sabr ad-Din II"), the eldest son of Sa'ad ad-Din II, established a new base after his return from Yemen.[^87] [^88]

Muslim Chinese diplomat [Zheng He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zheng_He "Zheng He") visited Mogadishu in his voyages to the horn of Africa.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Imaam_Ahmed_Gurey.jpg/250px-Imaam_Ahmed_Gurey.jpg)

Statue of Ahmed ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi, Imam of the Adal Empire

Adal's headquarters were again relocated the following century, this time southward to [Harar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harar "Harar"). From this new capital, Adal organised an effective army led by Imam [Ahmad ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmad_ibn_Ibrahim_al-Ghazi "Ahmad ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi"), (Ahmad "Gurey" or "Gran"; both meaning "the left-handed") and his closest top general and brother in law [Garad Matan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matan_ibn_Uthman_Al_Somali "Matan ibn Uthman Al Somali"). Imam Ahmed clan is documented to be from the [Geri Koombe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geri_Koombe "Geri Koombe"), a sub clan of Darod.[^84] [^89] This 16th-century campaign is historically known as the [Conquest of Abyssinia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abyssinian%E2%80%93Adal_war "Abyssinian–Adal war") (*Futuh al-Habash*). During the war, Imam Ahmad pioneered the use of cannons supplied by the Ottoman Empire, which he imported through Zeila and deployed against Abyssinian forces and their Portuguese allies led by [Cristóvão da Gama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crist%C3%B3v%C3%A3o_da_Gama "Cristóvão da Gama").[^90]

During the [Ajuran Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajuran_Sultanate "Ajuran Sultanate") period, the city-states and republics of [Merca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merca "Merca"), [Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mogadishu "Mogadishu"), [Barawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barawa "Barawa"), [Hobyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hobyo "Hobyo") and their respective ports flourished and had a lucrative foreign commerce with ships sailing to and from Arabia, India, [Venetia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venetia_\(region\) "Venetia (region)"),[^91] Persia, Egypt, Portugal, and as far away as China. [Vasco da Gama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama "Vasco da Gama"), who passed by Mogadishu in the 15th century, noted that it was a large city with houses several storeys high and large palaces in its centre, in addition to many mosques with cylindrical minarets.[^92] The [Harla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harla_people "Harla people"), an early [Hamitic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamitic "Hamitic") group of tall stature who inhabited parts of Somalia, Tchertcher and other areas in the Horn, also erected various [tumuli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tumulus "Tumulus").[^93] These masons are believed to have been ancestral to ethnic Somalis.[^94]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/ShenDuGiraffePainting.jpg/250px-ShenDuGiraffePainting.jpg)

The Ajuran Sultanate maintained commercial ties with the Ming dynasty and other kingdoms.

In the 16th century, [Duarte Barbosa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duarte_Barbosa "Duarte Barbosa") noted that many ships from the [Kingdom of Cambaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khambhat "Khambhat") in modern-day India sailed to Mogadishu with cloth and spices, for which they in return received gold, wax and ivory. Barbosa also highlighted the abundance of meat, wheat, barley, horses, and fruit on the coastal markets, which generated enormous wealth for the merchants.[^95] Mogadishu, the centre of a thriving textile industry known as *toob benadir* (specialised for the markets in Egypt, among other places [^96]), together with Merca and Barawa, also served as a transit stop for [Swahili](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swahili_people "Swahili people") merchants from [Mombasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mombasa "Mombasa") and [Malindi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malindi "Malindi") and for the gold trade from [Kilwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilwa_Sultanate "Kilwa Sultanate").[^97] [Jewish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish "Jewish") merchants from the [Hormuz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Hormuz "Strait of Hormuz") brought their Indian textile and fruit to the Somali coast in exchange for [grain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grain "Grain") and wood.[^98]

Trading relations were established with [Malacca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malacca "Malacca") in the 15th century,[^99] with cloth, ambergris and porcelain being the main commodities of the trade.[^100] Giraffes, zebras and incense were exported to the [Ming Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_Empire "Ming Empire") of China, which established Somali merchants as leaders in the commerce between East Asia and the Horn.[^101] Hindu merchants from [Surat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surat "Surat") and Southeast African merchants from [Pate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pate_Island "Pate Island"), seeking to bypass both the [Portuguese India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_India "Portuguese India") blockade (and later the Omani interference), used the Somali ports of Merca and Barawa (which were out of the two powers' direct jurisdiction) to conduct their trade in safety and without interference.[^102]

### Early modern era and the scramble for Africa

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Mogadishu1936.jpg/250px-Mogadishu1936.jpg)

Mogadishu, capital of Italian Somaliland, with the Catholic Cathedral at the centre and the Arch monument in honour of King Umberto I of Italy

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Reception_of_Olal_Dinle_in_Italy.jpg/330px-Reception_of_Olal_Dinle_in_Italy.jpg)

Sultan Olol Dinle (third from right) and his delegation, in Benito Mussolini 's Italy, during the festivities of Adolf Hitler 's visit to Rome, 1938

In the [early modern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_modern "Early modern") period, successor states to the Adal Sultanate and [Ajuran Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajuran_Sultanate "Ajuran Sultanate") began to flourish in Somalia. These included the [Hiraab Imamate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiraab_Imamate "Hiraab Imamate"), the [Isaaq Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaaq_Sultanate "Isaaq Sultanate") led by the [Guled dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guled_Dynasty "Guled Dynasty"),[^103] [^104] the [Habr Yunis Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habr_Yunis_Sultanate "Habr Yunis Sultanate") led by the [Ainanshe dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rer_Ainanshe "Rer Ainanshe"),[^105] the [Sultanate of the Geledi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultanate_of_the_Geledi "Sultanate of the Geledi") (Gobroon dynasty), the [Majeerteen Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majeerteen_Sultanate "Majeerteen Sultanate") (Migiurtinia), and the [Sultanate of Hobyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultanate_of_Hobyo "Sultanate of Hobyo") (Obbia). They continued the tradition of castle-building and seaborne trade established by previous Somali empires.

Sultan [Yusuf Mahamud Ibrahim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yusuf_Mahamud_Ibrahim "Yusuf Mahamud Ibrahim"), the third Sultan of the House of Gobroon, started the golden age of the Gobroon Dynasty. His army came out victorious during the Bardheere Jihad, which restored stability in the region and revitalised the East African [ivory trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivory_trade "Ivory trade"). He also had cordial relations and received gifts from the rulers of neighbouring and distant kingdoms such as the Omani, [Witu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wituland "Wituland") and Yemeni Sultans.

Sultan Ibrahim's son [Ahmed Yusuf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmed_Yusuf_\(Gobroon\) "Ahmed Yusuf (Gobroon)") succeeded him as one of the most important figures in 19th-century East Africa, receiving tribute from Omani governors and creating alliances with important Muslim families on the East African coast.

In [Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somaliland "Somaliland"), the Isaaq Sultanate was established in 1750. The Isaaq Sultanate was a Somali kingdom that ruled parts of the [Horn of Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horn_of_Africa "Horn of Africa") during the 18th and 19th centuries.[^103] It spanned the territories of the [Isaaq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaaq "Isaaq") clan, descendants of the [Banu Hashim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banu_Hashim "Banu Hashim") clan,[^106] in modern-day Somaliland and Ethiopia. The sultanate was governed by the Rer Guled branch established by the first sultan, Sultan [Guled Abdi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guled_Abdi_\(Sultan\) "Guled Abdi (Sultan)"), of the [Eidagale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eidagale "Eidagale") clan.[^107] [^108] [^109] According to oral tradition, prior to the Guled dynasty the Isaaq clan-family were ruled by a dynasty of the Tolje'lo branch starting from, descendants of Ahmed nicknamed Tol Je'lo, the eldest son of [Sheikh Ishaaq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ishaaq_bin_Ahmed "Ishaaq bin Ahmed") 's [Harari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harari_people "Harari people") wife. There were eight Tolje'lo rulers in total, starting with Boqor Harun ([Somali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_language "Somali language"): *Boqor Haaruun*) who ruled the Isaaq Sultanate for centuries starting from the 13th century.[^110] [^111] The last Tolje'lo ruler [Garad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garad "Garad") Dhuh Barar ([Somali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_language "Somali language"): *Dhuux Baraar*) was overthrown by a coalition of Isaaq clans. The once strong Tolje'lo clan were scattered and took refuge amongst the [Habr Awal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habr_Awal "Habr Awal") with whom they still mostly live.[^112] [^113]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Horn_of_Africa_1865.jpg/250px-Horn_of_Africa_1865.jpg)

Somali territories depicted on map before 1884 Berlin Conference to divide Africa.

In the late 19th century, after the [Berlin Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Conference "Berlin Conference") of 1884, European powers began the [Scramble for Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa "Scramble for Africa"). In that year, a British protectorate was declared over part of Somalia, on the African coast opposite South Yemen.[^114] Initially, this region was under the control of the Indian Office, and so administered as part of the Indian Empire; in 1898 it was transferred to control by London.[^114] In 1889, the protectorate and later colony of [Italian Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Somalia "Italian Somalia") was officially established by [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy "Kingdom of Italy") through various treaties signed with a number of chiefs and sultans;[^115] Sultan [Yusuf Ali Kenadid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yusuf_Ali_Kenadid "Yusuf Ali Kenadid") first sent a request to Italy in late December 1888 to make his [Sultanate of Hobyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultanate_of_Hobyo "Sultanate of Hobyo") an Italian protectorate before later signing a treaty in 1889.[^116]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Hobyo_Sultanate_Cavalry_And_Fort.jpg/250px-Hobyo_Sultanate_Cavalry_And_Fort.jpg)

Hobyo Sultanate cavalry and fort

The Dervish movement successfully repulsed the British Empire four times and forced it to retreat to the coastal region.[^117] The [Darawiish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dervish_movement_\(Somali\) "Dervish movement (Somali)") defeated the Italian, British, Abyssinian colonial powers on numerous occasions, most notably, the 1903 victory at Cagaarweyne commanded by [Suleiman Aden Galaydh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suleiman_Aden_Galaydh "Suleiman Aden Galaydh"),[^118] forcing the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") to retreat to the coastal region in the early 1900s.[^119] The Dervishes were finally defeated in 1920 by British airpower.[^120]

The dawn of [fascism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fascism "Fascism") in the early 1920s heralded a change of strategy for Italy, as the north-eastern sultanates were soon to be forced within the boundaries of *La Grande Somalia* (" *[Greater Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Somalia "Greater Somalia")* ") according to the plan of Fascist Italy. With the arrival of Governor [Cesare Maria De Vecchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cesare_Maria_De_Vecchi "Cesare Maria De Vecchi") on 15 December 1923, things began to change for that part of Somaliland known as [Italian Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Somaliland "Italian Somaliland"). De Vecchi was responsible for an aggressive expansion campaign and large construction projects, including the [cathedral of Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mogadishu_Cathedral "Mogadishu Cathedral").[^121] [^122] The last piece of land acquired by Italy in Somalia was [Oltre Giuba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oltre_Giuba "Oltre Giuba"), present-day [Jubaland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jubaland "Jubaland") region, in 1925.[^116]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Sayyid_Mohammed_Abdullah_Hassan.jpeg/250px-Sayyid_Mohammed_Abdullah_Hassan.jpeg)

Statue of Muhammed Abdullah Hassan, Leader of the Dervishes

The Italians began local infrastructure projects, including the construction of hospitals, farms and schools.[^123] [Fascist Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy#Fascist_regime_\(1922%E2%80%931943\) "Kingdom of Italy"), under [Benito Mussolini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benito_Mussolini "Benito Mussolini"), attacked Abyssinia (Ethiopia) in 1935, with an aim to colonise it. The invasion was condemned by the [League of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/League_of_Nations "League of Nations"), but little was done to stop it or to liberate occupied Ethiopia. In 1936, Italian Somalia was integrated into [Italian East Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_East_Africa "Italian East Africa"), alongside Eritrea and Ethiopia, as the [Somalia Governorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalia_Governorate "Somalia Governorate"). On 3 August 1940, Italian troops, including Somali colonial units, crossed from Ethiopia to [invade British Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_conquest_of_British_Somaliland "Italian conquest of British Somaliland"), and by 14 August, succeeded in taking [Berbera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berbera "Berbera") from the British.

A British force, including troops from several African countries, launched the [campaign in January 1941](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_African_Campaign_\(World_War_II\) "East African Campaign (World War II)") from Kenya to liberate British Somaliland and Italian-occupied Ethiopia and conquer Italian Somaliland. By February most of Italian Somaliland was captured and, in March, British Somaliland was retaken from the sea. The forces of the British Empire operating in Somaliland comprised the three divisions of South African, West African, and East African troops. They were assisted by Somali forces led by Abdulahi Hassan with Somalis of the [Isaaq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaaq "Isaaq"), [Dhulbahante](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhulbahante "Dhulbahante"), and [Warsangali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warsangali "Warsangali") clans prominently participating. The number of [Italian Somalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Somalis "Italian Somalis") began to decline after World War II, with fewer than 10,000 remaining in 1960.[^124]

### Independence (1960–1969)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Somaliland_Flying_for_the_first_time_The_White_and_Blue_Somali_Flag_at_the_Independence_Celebrations_on_26_June_1960.jpg/250px-Somaliland_Flying_for_the_first_time_The_White_and_Blue_Somali_Flag_at_the_Independence_Celebrations_on_26_June_1960.jpg)

Somali flag flying at the independence celebrations on 26 June 1960

Following World War II, Britain retained control of both [British Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Somaliland "British Somaliland") and Italian Somaliland as protectorates. In 1945, during the [Potsdam Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potsdam_Conference "Potsdam Conference"), the United Nations granted Italy trusteeship of Italian Somaliland as the [Trust Territory of Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trust_Territory_of_Somaliland "Trust Territory of Somaliland"), on the condition first proposed by the Somali Youth League (SYL) and other nascent Somali political organisations, such as Hizbia Digil Mirifle Somali (HDMS) and the Somali National League (SNL)—that Somalia achieve independence within ten years.[^125] [^126] British Somaliland remained a protectorate of Britain until 1960.[^124]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Isaaq_Chiefs_Hargeisa.jpg/250px-Isaaq_Chiefs_Hargeisa.jpg)

Leaders of the Isaaq clan photographed in Hargeisa in 1958 during the Duke & Duchess of Gloucester's visit to Somaliland

To the extent that [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy") held the territory by UN mandate, the trusteeship provisions gave the Somalis the opportunity to gain experience in Western political education and self-government. These were advantages that British Somaliland, which was to be incorporated into the new Somali state, did not have. Although in the 1950s British colonial officials attempted, through various administrative development efforts, to make up for past neglect, the protectorate stagnated in political administrative development. The disparity between the two territories in economic development and political experience would later cause serious difficulties integrating the two parts.[^127]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Mogadishu_city_centre_-_1960s.jpg/250px-Mogadishu_city_centre_-_1960s.jpg)

An avenue in downtown Mogadishu in 1963

Meanwhile, in 1948, under pressure from their World War II allies and to the dismay of the Somalis,[^128] the British returned the [Haud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haud "Haud") (an important Somali grazing area that was presumably protected by British treaties with the Somalis in 1884 and 1886) and the [Somali Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Region "Somali Region") to Ethiopia, based on a treaty they signed in 1897 in which the British ceded Somali territory to the Ethiopian Emperor [Menelik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menelek_II_of_Ethiopia "Menelek II of Ethiopia") in exchange for his help against possible advances by the French.[^129]

Britain included the conditional provision that the Somali residents would retain their autonomy, but Ethiopia immediately claimed sovereignty over the area. This prompted an unsuccessful bid by Britain in 1956 to buy back the Somali lands it had turned over.[^125] Britain also granted administration of the almost exclusively Somali-inhabited [Northern Frontier District](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Frontier_District "Northern Frontier District") (NFD) to Kenyan nationalists.[^130] [^131] This was despite a [plebiscite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plebiscite "Plebiscite") in which, according to a British colonial commission, almost all of the territory's ethnic Somalis favoured joining the newly formed Somali Republic.[^132]

A [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Somaliland_overseas_territory_referendum,_1958 "French Somaliland overseas territory referendum, 1958") was held in neighbouring [Djibouti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djibouti "Djibouti") (then known as [French Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Somaliland "French Somaliland")) in 1958, on the eve of Somalia's independence in 1960, to decide whether or not to join the Somali Republic or to remain with France. The referendum turned out in favour of a continued association with France, largely due to a combined yes vote by the sizeable [Afar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afar_people "Afar people") ethnic group and resident Europeans.[^133] There was also widespread vote rigging, with the French expelling thousands of Somalis before the referendum reached the polls.[^134]

The majority of those who voted "no" were Somalis who were strongly in favour of joining a united Somalia, as had been proposed by [Mahmoud Harbi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahmoud_Harbi "Mahmoud Harbi"), Vice President of the Government Council. Harbi was killed in a plane crash two years later.[^133] Djibouti finally gained independence from [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France") in 1977, and [Hassan Gouled Aptidon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassan_Gouled_Aptidon "Hassan Gouled Aptidon"), a Somali who had campaigned for a "yes" vote in the referendum of 1976, eventually became Djibouti's first president (1977–1999).[^133]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/President_Aden_Adde_alongside_Prime_minister_Sharmarke.jpg/250px-President_Aden_Adde_alongside_Prime_minister_Sharmarke.jpg)

President Aden Adde alongside Prime Minister Abdirashiid Ali Sharmarke praying Eid in 1964 at Arba'a Rukun Mosque

The British Somaliland protectorate obtained independence as the [State of Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_of_Somaliland "State of Somaliland") on 26 June 1960. On 1 July 1960 the Trust Territory of Somaliland gained independence and the two united to form the [Somali Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Republic "Somali Republic"),[^135] albeit within boundaries drawn up by Italy and Britain.[^136] [^137] A government was formed by [Abdullahi Issa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdullahi_Issa "Abdullahi Issa") and [Muhammad Haji Ibrahim Egal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_Haji_Ibrahim_Egal "Muhammad Haji Ibrahim Egal") with other members of the trusteeship and protectorate governments, with [Abdulcadir Muhammed Aden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdulcadir_Muhammed_Aden "Abdulcadir Muhammed Aden") as President of the [Somali National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Somalia "Parliament of Somalia"), [Aden Abdullah Osman Daar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aden_Abdullah_Osman_Daar "Aden Abdullah Osman Daar") as [President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_Somalia "List of Presidents of Somalia") of the Somali Republic, and [Abdirashid Ali Shermarke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdirashid_Ali_Shermarke "Abdirashid Ali Shermarke") as [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Somalia "Prime Minister of Somalia") (later to become president from 1967 to 1969). On 20 July 1961 and through a [popular referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_constitutional_referendum,_1961 "Somali constitutional referendum, 1961"), was ratified popularly by the people of Somalia under Italian trusteeship. Most of the people from the former Somaliland Protectorate did not participate in the referendum, although only a small number of Somalilanders who participated the referendum voted against the [new constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Somalia "Constitution of Somalia"),[^138] which was first drafted in 1960.[^139] In 1967, Muhammad Haji Ibrahim Egal became Prime Minister, a position to which he was appointed by Shermarke. Egal would later become the President of the autonomous [Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somaliland "Somaliland") region in northwestern Somalia.

### Somali Democratic Republic (1969–1991)

#### Coup d'État

On 15 October, while President Abdirashid Ali Sharmarke was touring drought-stricken Las Anood, his personal bodyguard shot and killed him. In a memo to then-president [Richard Nixon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Nixon "Richard Nixon"), former US Secretary of State [Henry Kissinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Kissinger "Henry Kissinger") concluded that the bodyguard was acting of his own accord.[^140] Six days later, on 21 October, General [Siad Barre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siad_Barre "Siad Barre") led a military coup and successfully overthrew the parliamentary government. Modern political analysts assert that the coup was motivated by corruption in the parliamentary government.[^141] The bodyguard was tried, tortured and executed by the [Supreme Revolutionary Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Revolutionary_Council_\(Somalia\) "Supreme Revolutionary Council (Somalia)") (SRC). He came from the same clan background as the president whom he killed.[^142]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Mohamed_Ainanshe_and_Kim_Il_Sung_-1970.jpg/250px-Mohamed_Ainanshe_and_Kim_Il_Sung_-1970.jpg)

Mohamed Ainanshe Guled in North Korea meeting President Kim Il Sung 1970

Alongside Barre, the SRC that assumed power after President Sharmarke's assassination was led by Brigadier General [Mohamed Ainanshe Guled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohamed_Ainanshe_Guled "Mohamed Ainanshe Guled"), Lieutenant Colonel [Salaad Gabeyre Kediye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salaad_Gabeyre_Kediye "Salaad Gabeyre Kediye"), and Chief of Police [Jama Korshel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jama_Ali_Korshel "Jama Ali Korshel"). Kediye officially held the title "Father of the Revolution", and Barre shortly afterwards became the head of the SRC.[^143] The SRC subsequently renamed the country the Somali Democratic Republic,[^144] [^145] dissolved the parliament and the Supreme Court, and suspended the constitution.[^146]

The revolutionary government established large-scale public works programs and successfully implemented an urban and rural [literacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literacy "Literacy") campaign, which helped dramatically increase the literacy rate. The Somali Democratic Republic achieved a literacy rate of 70%, one of the highest in Africa at the time.[^147] [^148]

In addition to a nationalisation program of industry and land, the new regime's foreign policy placed an emphasis on Somalia's traditional and religious links with the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world"), eventually joining the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League") in February 1974.[^149] That same year, Barre also served as chairman of the [Organisation of African Unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_African_Unity "Organisation of African Unity") (OAU), the predecessor of the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union") (AU).[^150]

In July 1976, Barre's SRC disbanded itself and established in its place the [Somali Revolutionary Socialist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Revolutionary_Socialist_Party "Somali Revolutionary Socialist Party") (SRSP), a one-party government based on [scientific socialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_socialism "Scientific socialism") and Islamic tenets. The SRSP was an attempt to reconcile the official state ideology with the official state religion by [adapting Marxist precepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revisionism_\(Marxism\) "Revisionism (Marxism)") to local circumstances. Emphasis was placed on the Muslim principles of social progress, equality, and justice, which the government argued formed the core of scientific socialism and its own accent on self-sufficiency, public participation, and popular control, as well as direct ownership of the means of production. While the SRSP encouraged private investment on a limited scale, the administration's overall direction was essentially [communist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communism "Communism").[^146]

In July 1977, the [Ogaden War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ogaden_War "Ogaden War") broke out after Barre's government used a plea for national unity to justify an [aggressive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_aggression "War of aggression") incorporation of the predominantly Somali-inhabited [Ogaden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ogaden "Ogaden") region of Ethiopia into a Pan-Somali [Greater Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Somalia "Greater Somalia"), along with the rich agricultural lands of south-eastern Ethiopia, infrastructure, and strategically important areas as far north as Djibouti.[^151] In the first week of the conflict, Somali armed forces took southern and central Ogaden, and for most of the war, the Somali army scored continuous victories over the Ethiopian army and followed them as far as [Sidamo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidamo_Province "Sidamo Province"). By September 1977, Somalia controlled 90% of the Ogaden and captured strategic cities such as [Jijiga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jijiga "Jijiga") and put heavy pressure on [Dire Dawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dire_Dawa "Dire Dawa"), threatening the train route from the latter city to Djibouti. After the siege of Harar, a massive, unprecedented Soviet intervention consisting of 20,000 [Cuban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuba "Cuba") forces and several thousand Soviet experts came to the aid of Ethiopia's communist [Derg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derg "Derg") regime. By 1978, the Somali troops were ultimately pushed out of the Ogaden. This shift in support by the Soviet Union motivated the Barre government to seek allies elsewhere. It eventually settled on the Soviets' [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") arch-rival, the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), which had been courting the Somali government for some time. Somalia's initial friendship with the Soviet Union and later partnership with the United States enabled it to build the largest army in Africa.[^152]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Muhammad_Siad_Barre_-_40866X9X9.jpg/250px-Muhammad_Siad_Barre_-_40866X9X9.jpg)

Major General Mohamed Siad Barre, Chairman of the Supreme Revolutionary Council, meeting with President of Romania Nicolae Ceauşescu

A new constitution was promulgated in 1979 under which elections for a People's Assembly were held. However, Barre's Somali Revolutionary Socialist Party [politburo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politburo "Politburo") continued to rule.[^145] In October 1980, the SRSP was disbanded, and the Supreme Revolutionary Council was reestablished in its place.[^146] By that time, Barre's government had become increasingly unpopular, with many Somalis becoming disillusioned with life under a military dictatorship.

The regime was weakened further in the 1980s as the Cold War drew to a close and Somalia's strategic importance was diminished. The government became increasingly [authoritarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism"), and [resistance movements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resistance_movement "Resistance movement"), encouraged by Ethiopia, sprang up across the country, eventually leading to the [Somali Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Civil_War "Somali Civil War"). Among the militia groups were the [Somali Salvation Democratic Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Salvation_Democratic_Front "Somali Salvation Democratic Front") (SSDF), [United Somali Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Somali_Congress "United Somali Congress") (USC), [Somali National Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_National_Movement "Somali National Movement") (SNM) and the [Somali Patriotic Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Patriotic_Movement "Somali Patriotic Movement") (SPM), together with the non-violent political oppositions of the [Somali Democratic Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Democratic_Movement "Somali Democratic Movement") (SDM), the [Somali Democratic Alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factions_in_the_Somali_Civil_War#Somali_Democratic_Alliance_\(SDA\) "Factions in the Somali Civil War") (SDA) and the Somali Manifesto Group (SMG).

#### Somali Civil War

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Map_of_the_Somali_Civil_War.svg/250px-Map_of_the_Somali_Civil_War.svg.png)

As the [moral authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moral_authority "Moral authority") of Barre's government was gradually eroded, many Somalis became disillusioned with life under military rule. By the mid-1980s, resistance movements supported by Ethiopia's communist [Derg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derg "Derg") administration had sprung up across the country. Barre responded by ordering punitive measures against those he perceived as locally supporting the guerrillas, especially in the northern regions. The clampdown included bombing of cities, with the northwestern administrative centre of [Hargeisa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hargeisa "Hargeisa"), a [Somali National Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_National_Movement "Somali National Movement") (SNM) stronghold, among the targeted areas in 1988.[^153] [^154]

The clampdown initiated by [Barre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siad_Barre "Siad Barre") 's government extended its reach beyond the initial bombings in the north to encompass various regions across the country. This reproduction of aggressive strategies aimed at stifling dissent and retaining authority over the populace was a hallmark of the government's repressive actions in the South. One of the most notable instances occurred in 1991, when Barre's regime initiated a ruthless aerial assault that led to the deaths of numerous individuals in the town of [Beledwene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beledweyne "Beledweyne"), situated in southern Somalia.[^155]

Another notable instance of [Barre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siad_Barre "Siad Barre") 's repressive policies occurred in the city of [Baidoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baidoa "Baidoa"), which earned the nickname 'the city of death' due to the events that unfolded there during the famine and [civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Civil_War "Somali Civil War").[^156] Hundreds of thousands of individuals lost their lives as a consequence of governmental strategies specifically aimed at the [Rahanweyn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rahanweyn "Rahanweyn") community residing in these areas.[^157]

During 1990, in the capital city of Mogadishu, the residents were prohibited from gathering publicly in groups greater than three or four. Fuel shortages, inflation, and currency devaluation impacted the economy. A thriving black market existed in the centre of the city as banks experienced shortages of local currency for exchange. Harsh [exchange control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exchange_control "Exchange control") regulations were introduced to prevent export of foreign currency. Although no travel restrictions were placed on foreigners, photographing many locations was banned. During daytime in Mogadishu, the appearance of any government military force was extremely rare. Alleged late-night operations by government authorities, however, included "disappearances" of individuals from their homes.[^158]

In 1991, the Barre administration was ousted by a coalition of clan-based opposition groups, backed by Ethiopia's then-ruling [Derg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derg "Derg") regime and [Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Socialist_People%27s_Libyan_Arab_Jamahiriya "Great Socialist People's Libyan Arab Jamahiriya").[^159] Following a meeting of the [Somali National Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_National_Movement "Somali National Movement") and northern clans' elders, the northern former British portion of the country [declared its independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somaliland_Declaration_of_Independence "Somaliland Declaration of Independence") as the [Republic of Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somaliland "Somaliland") in May 1991. Although *de facto* independent and relatively stable compared to the tumultuous south, it was not recognised by any foreign government,[^160] [^161] until [Israel recognised it](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel%E2%80%93Somaliland_relations "Israel–Somaliland relations") in 2025.[^162]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Mogadishu.jpg/250px-Mogadishu.jpg)

Prior to the civil war, Mogadishu was known as the "White pearl of the Indian Ocean". 163

Many of the opposition groups subsequently began competing for influence in the power vacuum that followed the ouster of Barre's regime. In the south, armed factions led by USC commanders General [Mohamed Farah Aidid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohamed_Farah_Aidid "Mohamed Farah Aidid") and [Ali Mahdi Mohamed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ali_Mahdi_Mohamed "Ali Mahdi Mohamed"), in particular, clashed as each sought to exert authority over the capital.[^164] In 1991, a multi-phased international conference on Somalia was held in neighbouring Djibouti.[^165] Owing to the legitimacy bestowed on Muhammad by the Djibouti conference, he was subsequently recognised by the international community as the new President of Somalia.[^165] He was not able to exert his authority beyond parts of the capital. Power was instead vied with other faction leaders in the southern half of Somalia and with autonomous sub-national entities in the north.[^166] The Djibouti conference was followed by two abortive agreements for national reconciliation and disarmament, which were signed by 15 political stakeholders: an agreement to hold an Informal Preparatory Meeting on National Reconciliation, and the 1993 Addis Ababa Agreement made at the Conference on National Reconciliation.

In the early 1990s, due to the protracted lack of a permanent central authority, Somalia began to be characterised as a " [failed state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Failed_state "Failed state") ".[^167] [^168] [^169]

### Transitional institutions

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Abdullahi_Yusuf_Ahmed_%2828-03-2006%29.jpg/250px-Abdullahi_Yusuf_Ahmed_%2828-03-2006%29.jpg)

Abdullahi Yusuf Ahmed, president of the transitional government of Somalia

The Transitional National Government (TNG) was established in April–May 2000 at the Somalia National Peace Conference (SNPC) held in Arta, Djibouti. [Abdiqasim Salad Hassan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdiqasim_Salad_Hassan "Abdiqasim Salad Hassan") was selected as the President of the nation's new Transitional National Government (TNG), an interim administration formed to guide Somalia to its third permanent republican government.[^170] The TNG's internal problems led to the replacement of the Prime Minister four times in three years, and the administrative body's reported bankruptcy in December 2003. Its mandate ended at the same time.[^171]

On 10 October 2004, legislators elected [Abdullahi Yusuf Ahmed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdullahi_Yusuf_Ahmed "Abdullahi Yusuf Ahmed") as the first President of the [Transitional Federal Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitional_Federal_Government "Transitional Federal Government") (TFG), the Transitional National Government's successor.[^172] The TFG was the second interim administration aiming to restore national institutions to Somalia after the 1991 collapse of the Siad Barre regime and the ensuing civil war.[^173]

The Transitional Federal Government (TFG) was the internationally recognised government of Somalia until 20 August 2012, when its tenure officially ended.[^174] It was established as one of the [Transitional Federal Institutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitional_Federal_Institutions "Transitional Federal Institutions") (TFIs) of government as defined in the [Transitional Federal Charter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitional_Federal_Charter "Transitional Federal Charter") (TFC) adopted in November 2004 by the [Transitional Federal Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitional_Federal_Parliament "Transitional Federal Parliament") (TFP). The Transitional Federal Government officially comprised the [executive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_\(government\) "Executive (government)") branch of government, with the TFP serving as the [legislative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature "Legislature") branch. The government was headed by the [President of Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Somalia "President of Somalia"), to whom the [cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_\(government\) "Cabinet (government)") reported through the [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Somalia "Prime Minister of Somalia"). However, it was also used as a general term to refer to all three branches collectively.

### Islamic Courts Union

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Icu_somalia_map.png/500px-Icu_somalia_map.png)

Map showing the ICU at the peak of its influence

In 2006, the [Islamic Courts Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Courts_Union "Islamic Courts Union") (ICU) assumed control of much of the southern part of the country for 6 months and imposed [Shari'a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shari%27a "Shari'a") law. Top UN officials have referred to this brief period as a 'Golden era' in the history of Somali politics.[^175] [^176]

### Transitional Federal Government

The Transitional Federal Government sought to reestablish its authority, and, with the assistance of [Ethiopian troops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopian_National_Defense_Force "Ethiopian National Defense Force"), [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union") peacekeepers and air support by the United States, drove out the ICU and solidified its rule.[^177] On 8 January 2007, TFG President [Abdullahi Yusuf Ahmed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdullahi_Yusuf_Ahmed "Abdullahi Yusuf Ahmed") entered Mogadishu with the Ethiopian military support for the first time since being elected to office. The government then relocated to [Villa Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Villa_Somalia "Villa Somalia") in the capital from its interim location in [Baidoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baidoa "Baidoa"). This marked the first time since the fall of the Siad Barre regime in 1991 that the federal government controlled most of the country.[^178]

### Al-Shabaab insurgency

[Al-Shabaab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Shabaab_\(Somalia\) "Al-Shabaab (Somalia)") opposed the Ethiopian military's presence in Somalia and continued an insurgency against the TFG. Throughout 2007 and 2008, Al-Shabaab scored military victories, seizing control of key towns and ports in both central and southern Somalia. By January 2009, Al-Shabaab and other militias had forced the Ethiopian troops to retreat, leaving behind an under-equipped African Union peacekeeping force to assist the Transitional Federal Government's troops.[^179]

Owing to a lack of funding and human resources, an [arms embargo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arms_embargo "Arms embargo") that made it difficult to reestablish a national security force, and general indifference on the part of the international community, Yusuf found himself obliged to deploy thousands of troops from Puntland to Mogadishu to sustain the battle against insurgent elements in the southern part of the country. Financial support for this effort was provided by the autonomous region's government. This left little revenue for Puntland's own security forces and civil service employees, leaving the territory vulnerable to piracy and terrorist attacks.[^180] [^181]

On 29 December 2008, Yusuf announced before a united parliament in Baidoa his resignation as President of Somalia. In his speech, which was broadcast on national radio, Yusuf expressed regret at failing to end the country's seventeen-year conflict as his government had been mandated to do.[^182] He also blamed the international community for their failure to support the government, and said that the speaker of parliament would succeed him in office per the [Charter of the Transitional Federal Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitional_Federal_Charter_of_the_Somali_Republic "Transitional Federal Charter of the Somali Republic").[^183]

### End of transitional period

Between 31 May and 9 June 2008, representatives of Somalia's federal government and the Alliance for the Re-liberation of Somalia (ARS) participated in peace talks in Djibouti brokered by the former United Nations Special Envoy to Somalia, [Ahmedou Ould-Abdallah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmedou_Ould-Abdallah "Ahmedou Ould-Abdallah"). The conference ended with a signed agreement calling for the withdrawal of Ethiopian troops in exchange for the cessation of armed confrontation. Parliament was subsequently expanded to 550 seats to accommodate ARS members, which then elected [Sheikh Sharif Sheikh Ahmed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheikh_Sharif_Sheikh_Ahmed "Sheikh Sharif Sheikh Ahmed"), as president.[^5] With the help of a small team of African Union troops, the TFG began a [counteroffensive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Civil_War_\(2009%E2%80%93present\) "Somali Civil War (2009–present)") in February 2009 to assume full control of the southern half of the country. To solidify its rule, the TFG formed an alliance with the Islamic Courts Union, other members of the Alliance for the Re-liberation of Somalia, and [Ahlu Sunna Waljama'a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahlu_Sunna_Waljama%27a "Ahlu Sunna Waljama'a"), a moderate [Sufi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sufism "Sufism") militia.[^184] Furthermore, Al-Shabaab and Hizbul Islam, the two main Islamist groups in opposition, began to fight amongst themselves in mid-2009.[^185] As a truce, in March 2009, the TFG announced that it would reimplement Shari'a as the nation's official judicial system.[^186] However, conflict continued in the southern and central parts of the country. Within months, the TFG had gone from holding about 70% of south-central Somalia's conflict zones, to losing control of over 80% of the disputed territory to the Islamist insurgents.[^178]

In October 2011, a coordinated operation, [Operation Linda Nchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Linda_Nchi "Operation Linda Nchi") between the Somali and Kenyan militaries and multinational forces began against the Al-Shabaab in southern Somalia.[^187] [^188] By September 2012, Somali, Kenyan, and [Raskamboni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raskamboni_movement "Raskamboni movement") forces had managed to capture Al-Shabaab's last major stronghold, the southern port of Kismayo.[^189] In July 2012, three European Union operations were launched to engage with Somalia: [EUTM Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union_Training_Mission_Somalia "European Union Training Mission Somalia"), EU Naval Force Somalia [Operation Atalanta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Atalanta "Operation Atalanta") off the Horn of Africa, and EUCAP Nestor.[^190]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Parlament_of_Somalia_2012-2016.svg/250px-Parlament_of_Somalia_2012-2016.svg.png)

Structure of the Federal Parliament of Somalia

As part of the official "Roadmap for the End of Transition", a political process that provided clear benchmarks leading toward the formation of permanent democratic institutions in Somalia, the Transitional Federal Government's interim mandate ended on 20 August 2012.[^191] The [Federal Parliament of Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Parliament_of_Somalia "Federal Parliament of Somalia") was concurrently inaugurated.[^174]

### Federal government

The [Federal Government of Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Government_of_Somalia "Federal Government of Somalia"), the first permanent central government in the country since the start of the civil war, was established in August 2012 with [Hassan Sheikh Mohamud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassan_Sheikh_Mohamud "Hassan Sheikh Mohamud") elected as president. In August 2014, the Somali government-led [Operation Indian Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Indian_Ocean "Operation Indian Ocean") was launched against insurgent-held pockets in the countryside.[^192] The next election took place in 2017 with [Mohamed Abdullahi Farmajo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohamed_Abdullahi_Farmajo "Mohamed Abdullahi Farmajo") succeeding as president.[^193] Near the end of his presidency, disagreements over the upcoming election prompted Farmajo to sign a controversial 2 year extension that was eventually overturned.[^194] In 2022, Hassan Sheikh Mohamud was re-elected for a second term.[^195] The beginning of his term coincided with the [2022 Central Somalia offensive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Central_Somalia_offensive "2022 Central Somalia offensive") that recaptured many settlements from Al-Shabaab.[^196] Some of these gains would eventually be reversed by the [2025 Shabelle offensive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Southern_Somalia_offensive "2025 Southern Somalia offensive").[^197] On 15 May 2026, Mohamud's presidential term officially ended with no election agreement, resulting in a political dispute between state and opposition figures.[^198]

### 2021–2023 drought

The [2021–2023 Somali drought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021%E2%80%932023_Somali_drought "2021–2023 Somali drought") was Somalia's most severe drought in 40 years, and affected 7.8 million people.[^199]

### 2024 Constitutional Crisis

On 30 March 2024, a series of constitutional amendments approved by the [Federal Government of Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Government_of_Somalia "Federal Government of Somalia") led to disagreements with some federal member states, triggering a [political crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_crisis_in_Somalia "Constitutional crisis in Somalia"). Three out of six member states withdrew their recognition of the federal government's authority and local [clashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jubaland_crisis "Jubaland crisis") erupted in [Jubaland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jubaland "Jubaland") and [South West State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_West_State_of_Somalia "South West State of Somalia").[^200] [^201]

## Geography

Somalia borders [Djibouti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djibouti "Djibouti") to the northwest, [Ethiopia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopia "Ethiopia") to the west, the [Gulf of Aden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden "Gulf of Aden") to the north, the Somali Sea and [Guardafui Channel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guardafui_Channel "Guardafui Channel") to the east, and [Kenya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenya "Kenya") to the southwest. With a land area of 637,657 square kilometres, Somalia's terrain consists mainly of [plateaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plateau "Plateau"), [plains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plain "Plain") and [highlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highland "Highland").[^202] [Its coastline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastline_of_Somalia "Coastline of Somalia") is more than 3,333 kilometres in length, the longest of mainland Africa.[^203] It has been described as being roughly shaped "like a tilted number seven".[^204]

In the far north, the rugged east–west ranges of the [Ogo Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ogo_Mountains "Ogo Mountains") lie at varying distances from the Gulf of Aden coast. Hot conditions prevail year-round, along with periodic [monsoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsoon "Monsoon") winds and irregular rainfall.[^205] Geology suggests the presence of valuable mineral deposits. Somalia is separated from [Seychelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seychelles "Seychelles") by the Somali Sea and is separated from [Socotra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socotra "Socotra") by the [Guardafui Channel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guardafui_Channel "Guardafui Channel").

### Location

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Somalia_svg_map.svg/250px-Somalia_svg_map.svg.png)

Orthographic projection of Somalia

Somalia is bordered by Kenya to the southwest, the [Gulf of Aden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden "Gulf of Aden") to the north, the [Guardafui Channel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guardafui_Channel "Guardafui Channel") and Indian Ocean to the east, and Ethiopia to the west. The country borders [Djibouti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djibouti "Djibouti"). It lies between latitudes [2°S](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2nd_parallel_south "2nd parallel south") and [12°N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/12th_parallel_north "12th parallel north"), and longitudes [41°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/41st_meridian_east "41st meridian east") and [52°E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/52nd_meridian_east "52nd meridian east"). Strategically located at the mouth of the [Bab el Mandeb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bab_el_Mandeb "Bab el Mandeb") gateway to the [Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea") and the [Suez Canal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Canal "Suez Canal"), the country occupies the tip of a region that, due to its resemblance on the map to a [rhinoceros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhinoceros "Rhinoceros") ' horn, is commonly referred to as the [Horn of Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horn_of_Africa "Horn of Africa").[^5] [^206]

### Waters

Somalia has the longest coastline on the mainland of Africa,[^207] with a seaboard that stretches 3,333 km (2,071 mi). Its terrain consists mainly of [plateaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plateau "Plateau"), [plains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plain "Plain") and [highlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highland "Highland"). The nation has a total area of 637,657 km <sup>2</sup> (246,201 sq mi) of which constitutes land, with 10,320 km <sup>2</sup> (3,980 sq mi) of water. Somalia's land boundaries extend to about 2,340 km (1,450 mi); 58 km (36 mi) of that is shared with Djibouti, 682 km (424 mi) with Kenya, and 1,626 km (1,010 mi) with Ethiopia. Its maritime claims include [territorial waters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_waters "Territorial waters") of 200 nautical miles (370 km; 230 mi).[^5]

Somalia has several islands and archipelagos on its coast, including the [Bajuni Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bajuni_Islands "Bajuni Islands") and the [Saad ad-Din Archipelago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saad_ad-Din_Islands "Saad ad-Din Islands"): see [islands of Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islands_of_Somalia "Islands of Somalia").

### Habitat

Somalia contains seven terrestrial ecoregions: [Ethiopian montane forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopian_montane_forests "Ethiopian montane forests"), [Northern Zanzibar–Inhambane coastal forest mosaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Zanzibar%E2%80%93Inhambane_coastal_forest_mosaic "Northern Zanzibar–Inhambane coastal forest mosaic"), [Somali Acacia–Commiphora bushlands and thickets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Acacia%E2%80%93Commiphora_bushlands_and_thickets "Somali Acacia–Commiphora bushlands and thickets"), [Ethiopian xeric grasslands and shrublands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopian_xeric_grasslands_and_shrublands "Ethiopian xeric grasslands and shrublands"), [Hobyo grasslands and shrublands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hobyo_grasslands_and_shrublands "Hobyo grasslands and shrublands"), [Somali montane xeric woodlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_montane_xeric_woodlands "Somali montane xeric woodlands"), and [East African mangroves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_African_mangroves "East African mangroves").[^208]

In the north, a scrub-covered, semi-desert plain referred as the *[Guban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guban "Guban")* lies parallel to the Gulf of Aden [littoral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Littoral_zone "Littoral zone"). With a width of twelve kilometres in the west to as little as two kilometres in the east, the plain is bisected by watercourses that are essentially beds of dry sand except during the rainy seasons. When the rains arrive, the Guban's low bushes and grass clumps transform into lush vegetation.[^206] This coastal strip is part of the [Ethiopian xeric grasslands and shrublands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopian_xeric_grasslands_and_shrublands "Ethiopian xeric grasslands and shrublands") [ecoregion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecoregion "Ecoregion").

[Cal Madow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cal_Madow "Cal Madow") is a [mountain range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain_range "Mountain range") in the northeastern part of the country. Extending from several kilometres west of the city of [Bosaso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosaso "Bosaso") to the northwest of [Erigavo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erigavo "Erigavo"), it features Somalia's highest [peak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summit_\(topography\) "Summit (topography)"), [Shimbiris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shimbiris "Shimbiris"), which sits at an elevation of about 2,416 metres (7,927 ft).[^5] The rugged east–west ranges of the Karkaar Mountains also lie to the interior of the Gulf of Aden littoral.[^206] In the central regions, the country's northern mountain ranges give way to shallow plateaus and typically dry watercourses that are referred to locally as the *Ogo*. The Ogo's western plateau, in turn, gradually merges into the [Haud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haud "Haud"), an important grazing area for livestock.[^206]

Somalia has only two permanent rivers, the [Jubba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jubba_River "Jubba River") and [Shabele](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shebelle_River "Shebelle River"), both of which begin in the [Ethiopian Highlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopian_Highlands "Ethiopian Highlands"). These rivers mainly flow southwards, with the Jubba River entering the Indian Ocean at [Kismayo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kismayo "Kismayo"). The Shabele River at one time apparently used to enter the sea near [Merca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merca "Merca"), but now reaches a point just southwest of Mogadishu. After that, it consists of swamps and dry reaches before finally disappearing in the desert terrain east of [Jilib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jilib "Jilib"), near the Jubba River.[^206]

### Environment

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Somcoralreef.jpg/500px-Somcoralreef.jpg)

Somalia's coral reefs, ecological parks and protected areas

Somalia is a [semi-arid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-arid_climate "Semi-arid climate") country with about 1.6% [arable land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arable_land "Arable land").[^5] The first local environmental organisations were Ecoterra Somalia and the Somali Ecological Society, both of which helped promote awareness about ecological concerns and mobilised environmental programs in all governmental sectors as well as in civil society. From 1971 onward, a massive tree-planting campaign on a nationwide scale was introduced by the Siad Barre government to halt the advance of thousands of acres of wind-driven [sand dunes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand_dune "Sand dune") that threatened to engulf towns, roads and farm land.[^209] By 1988, 265 hectares of a projected 336 hectares had been treated, with 39 range reserve sites and 36 forestry plantation sites established.[^206] In 1986, the Wildlife Rescue, Research and Monitoring Centre was established by Ecoterra International, with the goal of sensitising the public to ecological issues. This educational effort led in 1989 to the so-called "Somalia proposal" and a decision by the Somali government to adhere to the [Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CITES "CITES") (CITES), which established for the first time a worldwide ban on the trade of elephant [ivory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivory "Ivory").

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Aerial_views_of_Kismayo_06_%288071381265%29.jpg/250px-Aerial_views_of_Kismayo_06_%288071381265%29.jpg)

The coast south of Mogadishu

Later, [Fatima Jibrell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatima_Jibrell "Fatima Jibrell"), a prominent Somali environmental activist, mounted a successful campaign to conserve old-growth forests of [acacia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acacia "Acacia") trees in the northeastern part of Somalia.[^210] These trees, which can live for 500 years, were being cut down to make charcoal which was highly in demand in the Arabian Peninsula, where the region's [Bedouin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bedouin "Bedouin") tribes believe the acacia to be sacred.[^210] [^211] [^212] Being a relatively inexpensive fuel, the production of charcoal often leads to [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation "Deforestation") and [desertification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desertification "Desertification").[^212] As a way of addressing this problem, Jibrell and the Horn of Africa Relief and Development Organisation (Horn Relief; now [Adeso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adeso "Adeso")), an organisation of which she was the founder and executive director, trained a group of teens to educate the public on the permanent damage that producing charcoal can create. In 1999, Horn Relief coordinated a peace march in the northeastern Puntland region of Somalia to put an end to the so-called "charcoal wars". As a result of Jibrell's lobbying and education efforts, the Puntland government in 2000 prohibited the exportation of charcoal. The government has also since enforced the ban, which has reportedly led to an 80% drop in exports of the product.[^213] Jibrell was awarded the [Goldman Environmental Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldman_Environmental_Prize "Goldman Environmental Prize") in 2002 for her efforts against environmental degradation and desertification.[^213] In 2008, she also won the [National Geographic Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Geographic_Society "National Geographic Society") / [Buffett Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffett_Foundation "Buffett Foundation") Award for Leadership in Conservation.[^214]

Following the massive [tsunami of December 2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake "2004 Indian Ocean earthquake"), there have also emerged allegations that after the outbreak of the [Somali Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Civil_War "Somali Civil War") in the late 1980s, Somalia's long, remote shoreline was used as a dump site for the disposal of toxic waste. The huge waves that battered northern Somalia after the tsunami are believed to have stirred up tons of nuclear and toxic waste that might have been dumped illegally in the country by foreign firms.[^215]

The [European Green Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Green_Party "European Green Party") followed up these revelations by presenting before the press and the [European Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Parliament "European Parliament") in [Strasbourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strasbourg "Strasbourg") copies of contracts signed by two European companies — the Italian Swiss firm, Achair Partners, and an Italian [waste broker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waste_broker "Waste broker"), Progresso — and representatives of the then President of Somalia, the faction leader Ali Mahdi Mohamed, to accept 10 million tonnes of toxic waste in exchange for $80 million (then about £60 million).[^215]

According to reports by the [United Nations Environment Programme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme "United Nations Environment Programme") (UNEP), the waste has resulted in far higher than normal cases of respiratory infections, mouth ulcers and bleeding, abdominal haemorrhages and unusual skin infections among many inhabitants of the areas around the northeastern towns of [Hobyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hobyo "Hobyo") and [Benadir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benadir "Benadir") on the Indian Ocean coast — diseases consistent with radiation sickness. UNEP adds that the situation along the Somali coastline poses a very serious environmental hazard not only in Somalia, but also in the eastern Africa sub-region.[^215]

This dumping of toxic waste was one factor in the emergence of [piracy off the coast of Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piracy_off_the_coast_of_Somalia "Piracy off the coast of Somalia").

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Koppen-Geiger_Map_v2_SOM_1991%E2%80%932020.svg/330px-Koppen-Geiger_Map_v2_SOM_1991%E2%80%932020.svg.png)

Köppen–Geiger climate classification map at 1-km resolution for Somalia 1991–2020

Owing to Somalia's proximity to the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator"), there is not much seasonal variation in its climate. Hot conditions prevail year-round along with periodic [monsoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsoon "Monsoon") winds and irregular rainfall. Mean daily maximum temperatures range from 30–40 °C (86–104 °F), except at higher elevations along the eastern seaboard, where the effects of a cold offshore current can be felt. In Mogadishu, for instance, average afternoon highs range from 28–32 °C (82–90 °F) in April. Some of the highest mean annual temperatures in the world have been recorded in the country; [Berbera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berbera "Berbera") on the northwestern coast has an afternoon high that averages more than 38 °C (100 °F) from June through September. Nationally, mean daily minimums usually vary from about 15–30 °C (59–86 °F).[^206] The greatest range in climate occurs in northern Somalia, where temperatures sometimes surpass 45 °C (113 °F) in July on the littoral plains and drop below the freezing point during December in the highlands.[^205] [^206] In this region, relative humidity ranges from about 40% in the mid-afternoon to 85% at night, changing somewhat according to the season.[^206] Unlike the climates of most other countries at this latitude, conditions in Somalia range from arid in the northeastern and central regions to [semiarid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-arid_climate "Semi-arid climate") in the northwest and south. In the northeast, annual rainfall is less than 100 mm (4 in); in the central plateaus, it is about 200 to 300 mm (8 to 12 in). The northwestern and southwestern parts of the nation, however, receive considerably more rain, with an average of 510 to 610 mm (20 to 24 in) falling per year. Although the coastal regions are hot and humid throughout the year, the hinterland is typically dry and hot.[^206]

There are four main seasons around which pastoral and agricultural life revolve, and these are dictated by shifts in the wind patterns. From December to March is the *Jilal*, the harshest dry season of the year. The main rainy season, referred to as the *Gu*, lasts from April to June. This period is characterised by the southwest monsoons, which rejuvenate the pasture land, especially the central plateau, and briefly transform the desert into lush vegetation. From July to September is the second dry season, the *Xagaa* (pronounced "Hagaa"). The *Dayr*, which is the shortest rainy season, lasts from October to December.[^206] The *tangambili* periods that intervene between the two monsoons (October–November and March–May) are hot and humid.[^206]

### Wildlife

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Sambalaxx.jpg/250px-Sambalaxx.jpg)

A camel in the northern mountains

Somalia contains a variety of mammals due to its geographical and climatic diversity. Wildlife still occurring includes [cheetah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheetah "Cheetah"), [lion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lion "Lion"), [reticulated giraffe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reticulated_giraffe "Reticulated giraffe"), [baboon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baboon "Baboon"), [serval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serval "Serval"), [elephant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_bush_elephant "African bush elephant"), [bushpig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bushpig "Bushpig"), [gazelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gazelle "Gazelle"), [ibex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibex "Ibex"), [kudu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kudu "Kudu"), [dik-dik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dik-dik "Dik-dik"), [oribi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oribi "Oribi"), [Somali wild ass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_wild_ass "Somali wild ass"), [reedbuck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reedbuck "Reedbuck") and [Grévy's zebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gr%C3%A9vy%27s_zebra "Grévy's zebra"), [elephant shrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elephant_shrew "Elephant shrew"), [rock hyrax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_hyrax "Rock hyrax"), [golden mole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_mole "Golden mole") and [antelope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antelope "Antelope"). It also has a large population of the [dromedary camel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dromedary_camel "Dromedary camel").[^216]

Somalia is home to around 727 species of birds. Of these, eight are endemic, one has been introduced by humans, and one is rare or accidental. Fourteen species are globally threatened. Birds species found exclusively in the country include the [Somali pigeon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_pigeon "Somali pigeon"); *[Alaemon hamertoni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaemon_hamertoni "Alaemon hamertoni")* (Alaudidae), Lesser Hoopoe-Lark; *[Heteromirafra archeri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heteromirafra_archeri "Heteromirafra archeri")* (Alaudidae), Archer's Lark; *[Mirafra ashi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirafra_ashi "Mirafra ashi")*, Ash's Bushlark; *[Mirafra somalica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirafra_somalica "Mirafra somalica")* (Alaudidae), Somali Bushlark; *[Spizocorys obbiensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spizocorys_obbiensis "Spizocorys obbiensis")* (Alaudidae), Obbia Lark; *[Carduelis johannis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carduelis_johannis "Carduelis johannis")* (Fringillidae); and Warsangli Linnet.[^217]

Somalia's territorial waters are prime fishing grounds for highly migratory marine species, such as tuna. A narrow but productive continental shelf contains several [demersal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demersal "Demersal") fish and [crustacean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crustacean "Crustacean") species.[^218] Fish species found exclusively in the nation include *Cirrhitichthys randalli* ([Cirrhitidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cirrhitidae "Cirrhitidae")), *Symphurus fuscus* ([Cynoglossidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynoglossidae "Cynoglossidae")), *Parapercis simulata* OC ([Pinguipedidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinguipedidae "Pinguipedidae")), *Cociella somaliensis* OC ([Platycephalidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platycephalidae "Platycephalidae")), and *Pseudochromis melanotus* ([Pseudochromidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudochromidae "Pseudochromidae")).

There are roughly 235 species of reptiles. Of these, almost half live in the northern areas. Reptiles endemic to Somalia include the Hughes' [saw-scaled viper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saw-scaled_viper "Saw-scaled viper"), the Southern Somali garter snake, a racer (*Platyceps messanai*), a diadem snake (*Spalerosophis josephscorteccii*), the Somali [sand boa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand_boa "Sand boa"), the [angled worm lizard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angled_worm_lizard "Angled worm lizard"), a spiny-tailed lizard (*Uromastyx macfadyeni*), Lanza's agama, a gecko (*Hemidactylus granchii*), the Somali semaphore [gecko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gecko "Gecko"), and a sand lizard ([Mesalina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesalina "Mesalina") or [Eremias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eremias "Eremias")). A colubrid snake (*Aprosdoketophis andreonei*) and Haacke-Greer's skink (*Haackgreerius miopus*) are endemic species.[^219]
