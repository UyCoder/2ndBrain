**Palestine**,[^1] [^11] officially the **State of Palestine**,[^2] is a country in the [Southern Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Levant "Southern Levant") region of [West Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Asia "West Asia"). It encompasses the [West Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Bank "West Bank"), including [East Jerusalem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Jerusalem "East Jerusalem"), and the [Gaza Strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_Strip "Gaza Strip"), both of which are [occupied by Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli-occupied_territories "Israeli-occupied territories"). These territories are collectively known as the **Palestinian territories**, or **occupied Palestinian territory**. They share the vast majority of their borders with [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel"), with the West Bank bordering [Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan "Jordan") to the east and the Gaza Strip bordering [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") to the southwest. It has a total land area of 6,020 square kilometers (2,320 sq mi) while [its population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Palestine "Demographics of Palestine") exceeds five million. Its [proclaimed capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Status_of_Jerusalem "Status of Jerusalem") is [Jerusalem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerusalem "Jerusalem"), while [Ramallah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramallah "Ramallah") serves as its *[de facto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_facto "De facto")* administrative center. [Gaza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_City "Gaza City") was its largest city prior to [Israel's forced evacuations in 2023](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_Strip_evacuations "Gaza Strip evacuations").[^15] [^16]

Situated at [a continental crossroad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levantine_corridor "Levantine corridor"), the [Palestine region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestine_\(region\) "Palestine (region)") was ruled by various empires and experienced [various demographic changes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographic_history_of_Palestine_\(region\) "Demographic history of Palestine (region)") from antiquity to the modern era. It was treading ground for the [Nile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile "Nile") and [Mesopotamian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia") armies and merchants from North Africa, China and India. The region [has religious significance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Land "Holy Land"). The ongoing [Israeli–Palestinian conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli%E2%80%93Palestinian_conflict "Israeli–Palestinian conflict") dates back to the rise of the [Zionist movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zionism "Zionism"), supported by the United Kingdom during [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"). The war saw Britain occupying Palestine from the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"), where it set up [Mandatory Palestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine "Mandatory Palestine") under the auspices of the [League of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/League_of_Nations "League of Nations"). Increased [Jewish immigration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish_immigration_to_Palestine "Jewish immigration to Palestine") led to [intercommunal conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercommunal_conflict_in_Mandatory_Palestine "Intercommunal conflict in Mandatory Palestine") between Jews and [Palestinian Arabs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_Arab "Palestinian Arab"), which escalated into [a civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1947%E2%80%931948_civil_war_in_Mandatory_Palestine "1947–1948 civil war in Mandatory Palestine") in 1947 after [a proposed partitioning by the United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Partition_Plan_for_Palestine "United Nations Partition Plan for Palestine") was rejected by the Palestinians and other [Arab nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_nations "Arab nations").

The [1948 Palestine war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1948_Palestine_war "1948 Palestine war") saw the [forcible displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1948_Palestinian_expulsion_and_flight "1948 Palestinian expulsion and flight") of a majority of the Arab population, and consequently the establishment of Israel; these events are referred to by Palestinians as the [Nakba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nakba "Nakba") ('catastrophe'). In the [Six-Day War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six-Day_War "Six-Day War") in 1967, Israel [occupied the West Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_occupation_of_the_West_Bank "Israeli occupation of the West Bank") and the [Gaza Strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_occupation_of_the_Gaza_Strip "Israeli occupation of the Gaza Strip"), which had been held by [Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Transjordan "Emirate of Transjordan") and [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Egypt_\(1953%E2%80%931958\) "Republic of Egypt (1953–1958)") respectively. The [Palestine Liberation Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestine_Liberation_Organization "Palestine Liberation Organization") (PLO) [declared independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_Declaration_of_Independence "Palestinian Declaration of Independence") in 1988. In 1993, the PLO signed the [Oslo Accords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oslo_Accords "Oslo Accords") with Israel, creating limited PLO governance in the West Bank and Gaza Strip through the [Palestinian Authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_Authority "Palestinian Authority") (PA). Israel withdrew from Gaza in [its unilateral disengagement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_disengagement_from_Gaza "Israeli disengagement from Gaza") in 2005. In 2007, [internal divisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatah%E2%80%93Hamas_conflict "Fatah–Hamas conflict") between political factions led to a [takeover of Gaza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Gaza_\(2007\) "Battle of Gaza (2007)") by [Hamas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamas "Hamas"). Since then, the West Bank has been governed in part by the [Fatah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatah "Fatah") -led PA, while the Gaza Strip has remained under the [control of Hamas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_Strip_under_Hamas "Gaza Strip under Hamas").

The Gaza Strip has long been subject to Israeli military occupation and an illegal [blockade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blockade_of_the_Gaza_Strip "Blockade of the Gaza Strip"). Israel has also constructed [large settlements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_settlement "Israeli settlement") in the [occupied West Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_occupation_of_the_West_Bank "Israeli occupation of the West Bank") and East Jerusalem since 1967, which currently house more than 737,000 Israeli settlers, which are [illegal under international law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legality_of_Israeli_settlements "Legality of Israeli settlements").[^38] Following the [October 7 attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/October_7_attacks "October 7 attacks") by [Hamas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamas "Hamas") in Israel, Israel launched [a large-scale military campaign in Gaza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_war "Gaza war") that has killed over 72,000 [Palestinians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinians "Palestinians"), caused [mass population displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_Strip_evacuations "Gaza Strip evacuations"), [a humanitarian crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_humanitarian_crisis_\(2023%E2%80%93present\) "Gaza humanitarian crisis (2023–present)"), and [a famine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_Strip_famine "Gaza Strip famine") in the Gaza Strip;[^39] [^40] [^41] these acts have been recognised as constituting [genocide against the Palestinian people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_genocide "Gaza genocide") by a growing scholarly consensus.[^42] [^43] [^44]

Palestine is a permanent [non-member observer state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_observers "United Nations General Assembly observers") at the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") (UN) and is recognized as a sovereign state by [157 of the UN's 193 member states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_recognition_of_Palestine "International recognition of Palestine").[^45] [^46] The questions of [Palestine's borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borders_of_Israel "Borders of Israel"), legal and diplomatic status of Jerusalem, and the [right of return](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_right_of_return "Palestinian right of return") of [Palestinian refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_refugees "Palestinian refugees") remain unsolved. Some of the other challenges to Palestine include [restrictions on movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_freedom_of_movement "Palestinian freedom of movement"), ineffective government and [Israeli settlements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_settlement "Israeli settlement") and [settler violence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_settler_violence "Israeli settler violence"), as well as an overall poor security situation. Despite these challenges, the country maintains [an emerging economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Palestine "Economy of Palestine") and sees frequent [tourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourism_in_Palestine "Tourism in Palestine"). [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic") is the official language of the country. While the majority of Palestinians [practice Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Palestine "Islam in Palestine"), [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity") also has [a presence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_Christians "Palestinian Christians"). Palestine is also a member of several [international organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_organizations "International organizations"), including the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League") and the [Organization of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organization of Islamic Cooperation"), [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO"),[^47] and a delegation of parliamentarians sit at the [Parliamentary Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_Assembly_of_the_Council_of_Europe "Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe") of the [Council of Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Europe "Council of Europe").[^48]

## Etymology

The term "Palestine" (in Latin, *Palæstina*) comes via ancient Greek from a Semitic [toponym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toponym "Toponym") for the general area dating back to the late second millennium BCE, a reflex of which is also to be found in the Biblical [ethnonym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnonym "Ethnonym") [Philistines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philistines "Philistines"). The term "Palestine" has been used to refer to the area at the southeast corner of the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") beside [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria"). In the 5th century BCE, [Herodotus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus"), in his work *[The Histories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histories_\(Herodotus\) "Histories (Herodotus)")*, used the term to describe a "district of Syria, called *Palaistínē* " ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Συρίη ἡ Παλαιστίνη καλεομένη),[^49] in which [Phoenicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia") interacted with other maritime peoples.[^50]

Currently, the terms "Palestine", "State of Palestine", and "occupied Palestinian territory (oPt or OPT)" are interchangeable depending on context. Specifically, the term "occupied Palestinian territory" refers as a whole to the geographical area of the Palestinian territory occupied by Israel since 1967. Palestine can, depending on contexts, be referred to as a country or a state, and its authorities can generally be identified as the [Government of Palestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Palestine "Government of Palestine").[^51] [^52]

## History

### From prehistory to the Ottoman era

The region of [Palestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestine_\(region\) "Palestine (region)") is part of the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant"), a [land bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_bridge "Land bridge") between [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa") and [Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia") that has traditionally served as the "crossroads of [Western Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Asia "Western Asia"), the [Eastern Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean "Eastern Mediterranean"), and [Northeast Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northeast_Africa "Northeast Africa") ".[^53] [^54] Lying to the west of the [Jordan Rift Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_Rift_Valley "Jordan Rift Valley"), Palestine is, in [tectonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tectonics "Tectonics") terms, located in the "northwest of the [Arabian Plate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Plate "Arabian Plate") ".[^55]

A crossroads for religion, culture, commerce, and politics, Palestine was among the earliest regions to see human habitation, agricultural communities and [civilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civilization "Civilization"). In the [Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age"), the [Canaanites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canaanites "Canaanites") established [city-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City-states "City-states") influenced by surrounding civilizations, among them Egypt, which ruled the area in the Late Bronze Age. During the [Iron Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age"), the [kingdoms of Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Israel_\(Samaria\) "Kingdom of Israel (Samaria)") and [Judah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Judah "Kingdom of Judah"), emerged in the interior, while kingdoms belonging to [Philistia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philistia "Philistia") and [Phoenicia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia") ruled the Palestinian coast. The [Assyrians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire "Neo-Assyrian Empire") [conquered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sennacherib%27s_campaign_in_the_Levant "Sennacherib's campaign in the Levant") the region in the 8th century BCE, then the [Babylonians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-Babylonian_Empire "Neo-Babylonian Empire") c. 601 BCE, followed by the Persian [Achaemenid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") that conquered the Babylonian Empire in 539 BCE. [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") conquered the Persian Empire in the late 330s BCE, intensifying [Hellenizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenization "Hellenization") influences.

In the late 2nd-century BCE [Maccabean Revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maccabean_Revolt "Maccabean Revolt"), the Jewish [Hasmonean Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasmonean_Judea "Hasmonean Judea") conquered most of Palestine; the kingdom subsequently became a vassal of [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome"), which annexed it in 63 BCE. [Roman Judea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Judea "Roman Judea") was troubled by [Jewish revolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish%E2%80%93Roman_wars "Jewish–Roman wars") in 66 CE, so Rome [destroyed Jerusalem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Destruction_of_Jerusalem "Destruction of Jerusalem") and the [Second Jewish Temple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Jewish_Temple "Second Jewish Temple") in 70 CE. In the 4th century, as the [Roman Empire adopted Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantine_the_Great_and_Christianity "Constantine the Great and Christianity"), Palestine became a center for the religion, attracting pilgrims, monks and scholars. Following [Muslim conquest of the Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_the_Levant "Muslim conquest of the Levant") in 636–641, ruling dynasties succeeded each other: the [Rashiduns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashidun "Rashidun"); [Umayyads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_dynasty "Umayyad dynasty"); [Abbasids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate"); the semi-independent [Tulunids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulunids "Tulunids") and [Ikhshidids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ikhshidids "Ikhshidids"); [Fatimids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimids "Fatimids"); and the [Seljuks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seljuks "Seljuks"). In 1099, the [First Crusade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Crusade "First Crusade") resulted in [Crusaders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusaders "Crusaders") establishing of the [Kingdom of Jerusalem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Jerusalem "Kingdom of Jerusalem"), which was [reconquered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_\(1187\) "Siege of Jerusalem (1187)") by the [Ayyubid Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayyubid_Sultanate "Ayyubid Sultanate") in 1187. Following the [invasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_raids_into_Palestine "Mongol raids into Palestine") of the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire") in the late 1250s, the Egyptian [Mamluks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mamluk_Sultanate_\(Cairo\) "Mamluk Sultanate (Cairo)") reunified Palestine under its control, before the region was [conquered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman%E2%80%93Mamluk_War_\(1516%E2%80%931517\) "Ottoman–Mamluk War (1516–1517)") by the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") in 1516, being ruled as [Ottoman Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Syria "Ottoman Syria") until the 20th century largely without dispute.

### Rise of Palestinian nationalism

Though Palestinian elites, in particular urban notable families who worked within the Ottoman bureaucracy, generally retained their loyalty to the Ottomans, they also played a significant role proportionately in the rise of [Arab nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_nationalism "Arab nationalism"), and the [Pan-Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan-Arabism "Pan-Arabism") movements that arose in response to both the emergence of the [Young Turks movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young_Turks "Young Turks") and the subsequent weakening of Ottoman power in World War I.[^56] The onset of the [Zionist movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zionist_movement "Zionist movement"), which sought to establish a [Jewish homeland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish_homeland "Jewish homeland") in Palestine, also exercised a strong influence on Palestinian national consciousness.[^57]

[Abdul Hamid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdul_Hamid_II "Abdul Hamid II"), the last sultan of the Ottoman Empire to exert effective control over a fracturing state, opposed the Zionist movement's efforts in Palestine. The [end of the Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Ottoman_Empire "Dissolution of the Ottoman Empire") 's rule in Palestine coincided with the [conclusion of World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End_of_World_War_I "End of World War I"). The failure of [Emir Faisal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faisal_I_of_Iraq "Faisal I of Iraq") to establish [a Greater Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria_\(region\) "Syria (region)") in the face of French and British colonial claims to the area, also shaped Palestinian elites' efforts to secure local autonomy.[^57] In the aftermath of the war, Palestine came under [British control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British-controlled_Palestine "British-controlled Palestine") with the implementation of the [British Mandate for Palestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandate_for_Palestine "Mandate for Palestine") in 1920.[^58] [^59]

### British Mandate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Dayr_Yassin_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Dayr_Yassin_%28cropped%29.jpg)

Deir Yassin massacre of Palestinian villagers was carried out by Zionist militias, the Irgun and Lehi during the 1948 Palestine war.

The defeat of the Ottoman Empire in World War I resulted in the dismantling of their rule. In 1920, the [League of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/League_of_Nations "League of Nations") granted [Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Northern_Ireland "United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland") the mandate to govern Palestine, leading to the subsequent period of British administration. In 1917, [Jerusalem was captured](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Jerusalem "Battle of Jerusalem") by [British forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_forces "British forces") led by [General Allenby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Allenby "General Allenby"), marking the end of Ottoman rule in the city.[^60] By 1920, tensions escalated between Jewish and Arab communities, resulting in violent clashes and riots across Palestine.[^60]

The League of Nations approved the British Mandate for Palestine in 1922, entrusting Britain with the administration of the region. Throughout the 1920s, Palestine experienced growing resistance from both Jewish and Arab nationalist movements, which manifested in sporadic violence and protests against British policies. In 1929, [violent riots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1929_Palestine_riots "1929 Palestine riots") erupted in Palestine due to disputes over [Jewish immigration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aliyah "Aliyah") and access to the Western Wall in Jerusalem.[^60]

The 1930s witnessed the outbreak of the [Arab Revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1936%E2%80%931939_Arab_revolt_in_Palestine "1936–1939 Arab revolt in Palestine"), as Arab nationalists demanded an end to Jewish immigration and the establishment of an independent Arab state. In response to the Arab Revolt, the British deployed military forces and implemented stringent security measures in an effort to quell the uprising.[^60]

Arab nationalist groups, led by the [Arab Higher Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Higher_Committee "Arab Higher Committee"), called for an end to Jewish immigration and land sales to Jews. The issuance of the [1939 White Paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Paper_of_1939 "White Paper of 1939") by the British government aimed to address escalating tensions between Arabs and Jews in Palestine. This policy document imposed restrictions on Jewish immigration and land purchases, with the intention to limit the establishment of a Jewish state. Met with strong opposition from the Zionist movement, the White Paper was perceived as a betrayal of the Balfour Declaration and Zionist aspirations for a Jewish homeland.[^61]

In response to the White Paper, the Zionist community in Palestine organized a strike in 1939, rallying against the restrictions on Jewish immigration and land acquisition. This anti-White Paper strike involved demonstrations, civil disobedience, and a shutdown of businesses. Supported by various Zionist organizations, including the Jewish Agency and the Histadrut (General Federation of Jewish Labor), the anti-White Paper strike aimed to protest and challenge the limitations imposed by the British government.[^61]

In the late 1930s and 1940s, several Zionist militant groups, including the [Irgun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irgun "Irgun"), [Hagana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hagana "Hagana"), and [Lehi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lehi_\(militant_group\) "Lehi (militant group)"), carried out [acts of violence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish_insurgency_in_Mandatory_Palestine "Jewish insurgency in Mandatory Palestine") against British military and civilian targets in their pursuit of an independent Jewish state.[^61] [Menachem Begin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menachem_Begin "Menachem Begin") and [Yitzhak Shamir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yitzhak_Shamir "Yitzhak Shamir"), who later became Prime Ministers of Israel, were behind these terrorist attacks.[^62] In 1946, a [bombing orchestrated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_David_Hotel_bombing "King David Hotel bombing") by the Irgun at the King David Hotel in Jerusalem resulted in the deaths of 91 people, including British officials, civilians, and hotel staff.[^61]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Map_of_Mandatory_Palestine_in_1946_with_major_cities_%28in_English%29.svg/250px-Map_of_Mandatory_Palestine_in_1946_with_major_cities_%28in_English%29.svg.png)

Mandatory Palestine in 1946

The [Exodus 1947](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exodus_1947 "Exodus 1947") incident unfolded when a ship carrying Jewish Holocaust survivors, who sought refuge in Palestine, was intercepted by the British navy, leading to clashes and the eventual deportation of the refugees back to Europe. During [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), Palestine served as a strategically significant location for British military operations against Axis forces in [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa").[^61] The Grand Mufti of Jerusalem, [Haj Amin al-Husseini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amin_al-Husseini "Amin al-Husseini"), collaborated with [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") while in exile during World War II.[^63]

### Arab–Israeli wars

In 1947, [the United Nations proposed a partition plan for Palestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UN_Partition_Plan_for_Palestine "UN Partition Plan for Palestine"), suggesting separate Jewish and Arab states, but it was rejected by the Palestinians and by neighboring Arab nations, leading to the outbreak of a [civil war in Palestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1947-1948_civil_war_in_Mandatory_Palestine "1947-1948 civil war in Mandatory Palestine"), the first phase of the broader [1948 Palestine war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1948_Palestine_war "1948 Palestine war").[^61]

During the war, Israel gained additional territories that were designated to be part of the Arab state under the UN plan. On May 14, 1948, on the eve of final British withdrawal, the [Jewish Agency for Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish_Agency_for_Israel "Jewish Agency for Israel"), headed by [David Ben-Gurion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Ben-Gurion "David Ben-Gurion"), [declared the establishment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Establishment_of_the_State_of_Israel "Declaration of the Establishment of the State of Israel") of the [State of Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_of_Israel "State of Israel"). The neighboring Arab states of [Transjordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Transjordan "Emirate of Transjordan"), Egypt, and the other members of the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League") of the time entered the war in Palestine beginning the [1948 Arab–Israeli war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1948_Arab%E2%80%93Israeli_war "1948 Arab–Israeli war").

By the end of the war, [Egypt occupied the Gaza Strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupation_of_the_Gaza_Strip_by_the_United_Arab_Republic "Occupation of the Gaza Strip by the United Arab Republic"), and [Transjordan occupied and then annexed the West Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordanian_annexation_of_the_West_Bank "Jordanian annexation of the West Bank"). Egypt initially supported the creation of an [All-Palestine Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All-Palestine_Government "All-Palestine Government") but disbanded it in 1959. Transjordan never recognized it and instead decided to incorporate the West Bank with its own territory to form [Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan "Jordan"). The annexation was ratified in 1950 but was rejected by the international community.

In 1964, when the West Bank was controlled by Jordan, the [Palestine Liberation Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestine_Liberation_Organization "Palestine Liberation Organization") was established there with the goal to confront Israel. The [Palestinian National Charter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_National_Charter "Palestinian National Charter") of the PLO defines the boundaries of Palestine as the whole remaining territory of the mandate, including Israel.

The [Six-Day War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six-Day_War "Six-Day War") in 1967, when Israel fought against Egypt, Jordan, and Syria, ended with [Israel occupying the West Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_occupation_of_the_West_Bank "Israeli occupation of the West Bank") and the Gaza Strip, besides other territories.[^64] Following the Six-Day War, the PLO moved to Jordan, but relocated to [Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanon "Lebanon") in 1971.[^65]

The October [1974 Arab League summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1974_Arab_League_summit "1974 Arab League summit") designated the PLO as the "sole legitimate representative of the Palestinian people" and reaffirmed "their right to establish an independent state of urgency".[^66] In November 1974, the PLO was recognized as competent on all matters concerning the question of Palestine by the [UN General Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly "United Nations General Assembly") granting them [observer status](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_states_of_the_United_Nations#Observers_and_non-members "Member states of the United Nations") as a "non-state entity" at the UN.[^67] [^68] Through the [Camp David Accords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camp_David_Accords "Camp David Accords") of 1979, Egypt signaled an end to any claim of its own over the Gaza Strip. In July 1988, Jordan [ceded its claims to the West Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan%27s_disengagement_from_the_West_Bank "Jordan's disengagement from the West Bank") —with the exception of guardianship over Haram al-Sharif—to the PLO.

After Israel captured and occupied the West Bank from Jordan and Gaza Strip from Egypt, it began to establish [Israeli settlements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_settlement "Israeli settlement") there. Administration of the Arab population of these territories was performed by the [Israeli Civil Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_Civil_Administration "Israeli Civil Administration") of the [Coordinator of Government Activities in the Territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinator_of_Government_Activities_in_the_Territories "Coordinator of Government Activities in the Territories") and by local [municipal councils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Municipality_\(Palestinian_Authority\) "Municipality (Palestinian Authority)") present since before the Israeli takeover. In 1980, Israel decided to freeze elections for these councils and to establish instead [Village Leagues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_Village_Leagues "Palestinian Village Leagues"), whose officials were under Israeli influence. Later this model became ineffective for both Israel and the Palestinians, and the Village Leagues began to break up, with the last being the Hebron League, dissolved in February 1988.[^69]

### Uprising, declaration and peace treaty

The [First Intifada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Intifada "First Intifada") broke out in 1987, characterized by widespread protests, strikes, and acts of civil disobedience by Palestinians in the Gaza Strip and the West Bank against Israeli occupation. In November 1988, the PLO legislature, while in exile, declared the [establishment of the "State of Palestine"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_declaration_of_independence "Palestinian declaration of independence"). In the month following, it was quickly recognized by many states, including Egypt and Jordan. In the Palestinian Declaration of Independence, the State of Palestine is described as being established on the "Palestinian territory", without explicitly specifying further.[^37] [^70]

After the 1988 Declaration of Independence, the UN General Assembly officially acknowledged the proclamation and decided to use the designation "Palestine" instead of "Palestine Liberation Organization" in the UN.[^37] [^70] In spite of this decision, the PLO did not participate at the UN in its capacity of the State of Palestine's government.[^71] Violent clashes between Palestinian protesters and Israeli forces intensified throughout 1989, resulting in a significant loss of life and escalating tensions in the occupied territories. 1990 witnessed the imposition of strict measures by the Israeli government, including curfews and closures, in an attempt to suppress the Intifada and maintain control over the occupied territories.[^70]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Intifada_in_Gaza_Strip_%28FL45956496%29.jpg/250px-Intifada_in_Gaza_Strip_%28FL45956496%29.jpg)

The First Intifada in Gaza, 1987

The [1990–1991 Gulf War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_War "Gulf War") brought increased attention to the conflict, leading to heightened diplomatic efforts to find a peaceful resolution.[^72] [^73] [Saddam Hussein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saddam_Hussein "Saddam Hussein") was a supporter of Palestinian cause and won support from Arafat during the war. Following the [invasion of Kuwait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasion_of_Kuwait "Invasion of Kuwait"), Saddam surprised the international community by presenting a peace offer to Israel and withdrawing Iraqi forces from Kuwait, in exchange of withdrawal from the West Bank, the Gaza Strip, East Jerusalem and Golan Heights.[^72] [^73] The peace offer was rejected, and Saddam then ordered firing of scud missiles into Israeli territory. This movement was supported by Palestinians. The war also led to the [expulsion of Palestinians from Kuwait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_exodus_from_Kuwait_\(1990%E2%80%9391\) "Palestinian exodus from Kuwait (1990–91)") and [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia"), as their government supported [Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq "Iraq").[^72] [^73]

In 1993, the Oslo Accords were signed between Israel and the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO), leading to the establishment of the Palestinian Authority (PA) and a potential path to peace.[^74] Yasser Arafat was elected as president of the newly formed Palestinian Authority in 1994, marking a significant step towards self-governance.[^10]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Leader_of_the_PLO%2C_Yasser_Arafat%2C_1996_Dan_Hadani_Archive.jpg/250px-Leader_of_the_PLO%2C_Yasser_Arafat%2C_1996_Dan_Hadani_Archive.jpg)

Yasser Arafat was the first president of Palestine, serving from 1989 to 2004.

Israel acknowledged the PLO negotiating team as "representing the Palestinian people", in return for the PLO recognizing Israel's right to exist in peace, acceptance of [UN Security Council resolutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UN_Security_Council_resolution "UN Security Council resolution") [242](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_242 "United Nations Security Council Resolution 242") and [338](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_338 "United Nations Security Council Resolution 338"), and its rejection of "violence and terrorism".[^74] As a result, in 1994 the PLO established the [Palestinian National Authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_National_Authority "Palestinian National Authority") (PNA or PA) territorial administration, that exercises some governmental functions [^10] in [parts of](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Bank_Areas_in_the_Oslo_II_Accord "West Bank Areas in the Oslo II Accord") the [West Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Bank "West Bank") and the Gaza Strip. As envisioned in the Oslo Accords, Israel allowed the PLO to establish interim administrative institutions in the Palestinian territories, which came in the form of the PNA.[^83] [^84] It was given civilian control in [Area B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_enclaves "Palestinian enclaves") and civilian and security control in Area A, and remained without involvement in [Area C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_C_\(West_Bank\) "Area C (West Bank)").[^84]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Clashes_following_the_Cave_of_the_Patriarchs_massacre%2C_1994_Dan_Hadani_Archive_V.jpg/250px-Clashes_following_the_Cave_of_the_Patriarchs_massacre%2C_1994_Dan_Hadani_Archive_V.jpg)

Friday prayer in Jerusalem after the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre

The peace process gained opposition from both Palestinians and Israelis. Islamist militant organizations such as Hamas and Islamic Jihad opposed the attack and responded by conducting attacks on civilians across Israel. In 1994, [Baruch Goldstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baruch_Goldstein "Baruch Goldstein"), an Israeli extremist shot 29 people to death in Hebron, known as the [Cave of the Patriarchs massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cave_of_the_Patriarchs_massacre "Cave of the Patriarchs massacre"). These events led an increase in Palestinian opposition to the peace process. In 1995, Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin was assassinated by [Yigal Amir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yigal_Amir "Yigal Amir"), causing political instability in the region.

The first-ever [Palestinian general elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1996_Palestinian_general_election "1996 Palestinian general election") took place in 1996, resulting in Arafat's re-election as president and the formation of a [Palestinian Legislative Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_Legislative_Council "Palestinian Legislative Council"). Initiating the implementation of the Oslo Accords, Israel began redeploying its forces from select Palestinian cities in the West Bank in 1997. Negotiations between Israel and the Palestinian Authority continued, albeit with slow progress and contentious debates on Jerusalem, settlements, and refugees in 1998.[^85]

In 1997, Israeli government led by [Benjamin Netanyahu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Netanyahu "Benjamin Netanyahu") and the Palestinian government signed the [Hebron Protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebron_Protocol "Hebron Protocol"), which outlined the redeployment of Israeli forces from parts of Hebron in the West Bank, granting the government greater control over the city. Israel and the Palestinian government signed the [Wye River Memorandum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wye_River_Memorandum "Wye River Memorandum") in 1998, aiming to advance the implementation of the Oslo Accords. The agreement included provisions for Israeli withdrawals and security cooperation.[^85] [^86]

The period of the Oslo Years brought a great prosperity to the government-controlled areas, despite some economic issues. The Palestinian Authority built the country's second airport in Gaza, after the [Jerusalem International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerusalem_International_Airport "Jerusalem International Airport"). Inaugural ceremony of the airport was attended by [Bill Clinton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Clinton "Bill Clinton") and [Nelson Mandela](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nelson_Mandela "Nelson Mandela"). In 1999, Ehud Barak assumed the position of Israeli Prime Minister, renewing efforts to reach a final status agreement with the Palestinians. The Camp David Summit in 2000 aimed to resolve the remaining issues but concluded without a comprehensive agreement, serving as a milestone in the peace process.

### Second intifada and civil war

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/%D7%90%D7%95%D7%A7%D7%98%D7%95%D7%91%D7%A8_2000_1.jpg/250px-%D7%90%D7%95%D7%A7%D7%98%D7%95%D7%91%D7%A8_2000_1.jpg)

Civil unrest in Hebron, 2000

A peace summit between Yasser Arafat and [Ehud Barak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehud_Barak "Ehud Barak") was mediated by [Bill Clinton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Clinton "Bill Clinton") in 2000. It was intended to be the final agreement, permanently ending conflict; however, the agreement failed to address the Palestinian refugee issues, the status of Jerusalem and Israeli security concerns. Both sides blamed each other for the summit failures. This became one of the main triggers for the resulting uprising.[^87] [^88]

In September 2000, then opposition leader from the [Likud Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Likud "Likud"), [Ariel Sharon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ariel_Sharon "Ariel Sharon"), made a proactive visit to the Temple Mount and delivered a controversial speech, which angered [Palestinian Jerusalemites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerusalemite "Jerusalemite"). The tensions escalated into riots. Bloody clashes took place around Jerusalem. Escalating violence resulted in the closure of [Jerusalem Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerusalem_International_Airport "Jerusalem International Airport"), which has not operated to date. More and more riots between Jews and Arabs took place in [October 2000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/October_2000_protests_in_Israel "October 2000 protests in Israel") in Israel.[^87] [^88]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/%D7%97%D7%95%D7%9E%D7%AA_%D7%9E%D7%92%D7%9F_21.jpg/250px-%D7%97%D7%95%D7%9E%D7%AA_%D7%9E%D7%92%D7%9F_21.jpg)

The Church of the Nativity under siege in 2002

In the same month, two Israeli soldiers were [lynched and killed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2000_Ramallah_lynching "2000 Ramallah lynching") in [Ramallah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramallah "Ramallah"). Between November and December clashes between Palestinians and Israelis increased further. In 2001 the [Taba summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taba_summit "Taba summit") was held between Israel and Palestine, but the summit failed and Ariel Sharon became prime minister in the 2001 elections. By 2001, attacks by Palestinian militant groups on Israel increased. [Gaza Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_Airport "Gaza Airport") was destroyed in an airstrike by the Israeli army in 2001, with Israel claiming this was retaliation for previous attacks by Hamas.[^87] [^88]

In January 2002, the [IDF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel_Defense_Forces "Israel Defense Forces") [Shayetet 13](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shayetet_13 "Shayetet 13") naval commandos captured the *[Karine A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karine_A "Karine A")*, a freighter carrying weapons from [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran "Iran") towards Israel. [UNSC Resolution 1397](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_1397 "United Nations Security Council Resolution 1397") was passed, which reaffirmed a [two-state solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-state_solution "Two-state solution") and laid the groundwork for a [road map for peace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Road_map_for_peace "Road map for peace"). [Another attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passover_massacre "Passover massacre") by Hamas left 30 people killed in [Netanya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netanya "Netanya").[^87] During a [peace summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2002_Arab_League_Peace_Initiative "2002 Arab League Peace Initiative") in [Beirut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beirut "Beirut"), a [proposal for an end to the Arab–Israeli conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Peace_Initiative "Arab Peace Initiative") was unanimously endorsed by all [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League") members,[^89] but rejected by Israel.[^90]

In 2002, Israel launched [Operation Defensive Shield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Defensive_Shield "Operation Defensive Shield") after the [Passover massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passover_massacre "Passover massacre"). Heavy [fighting between IDF and Palestinian fighters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Jenin_\(2002\) "Battle of Jenin (2002)") took place in [Jenin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jenin "Jenin").[^88] [^86] [^91] The [Church of the Nativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_of_the_Nativity "Church of the Nativity") was [besieged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_the_Church_of_the_Nativity "Siege of the Church of the Nativity") by the [IDF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IDF "IDF") for one week until successful negotiations took place, which resulted in withdrawal of the Israeli troops from the church. Between 2003 and 2004, people from [Qawasameh tribe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qawasameh_tribe "Qawasameh tribe") in Hebron were either killed or blew themselves up in suicide bombing.[^88] [^85] Ariel Sharon ordered [construction of barriers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_West_Bank_barrier "Israeli West Bank barrier") across Palestinian-controlled areas and Israeli settlements in the West Bank to prevent future attacks.[^88]

Saddam Hussein provided financial support to Palestinian militants from Iraq during the intifada period, from 2000 until his overthrow in 2003. A peace proposal was made in 2003, which was supported by Arafat and rejected by Sharon. In 2004 Hamas's leader and co-founder, [Ahmed Yassin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmed_Yassin "Ahmed Yassin"), was assassinated by the Israeli army in Gaza.[^88] [^85] Yasser Arafat was confined to his headquarters in Ramallah. On 11 November, Yasser Arafat died in Paris.[^88]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Demonstration_against_road_block%2C_Kafr_Qaddum%2C_March_2012_3.JPG/250px-Demonstration_against_road_block%2C_Kafr_Qaddum%2C_March_2012_3.JPG)

A demonstration against a road block, Kafr Qaddum, March 2012

In the first week of 2005, Mahmoud Abbas was elected as the president of the State of Palestine. In 2005, Israel completely withdrew from the Gaza Strip by destroying its settlements there. By 2005, the situation began de-escalating.[^88] In 2006, Hamas won in Palestinian legislative elections. This led to a political standoff with Fatah. Armed clashes took place across both the West Bank and the Gaza Strip. The clashes turned into a civil war, which ended in bloody clashes on the Gaza Strip. As a result, Hamas gained control over all the territory of Gaza.[^92]

Hundreds of people were killed in the civil war, including militants and civilians. Since then Hamas has gained more independence in its military practices. Since 2007, Israel has been leading a partial blockade on Gaza. Another peace summit was organized by the Arab League in 2007, with the same offer which was presented at the 2002 summit. However, the peace process could not progress.[^92] [^93] [^94] The PNA gained full control of the Gaza Strip with the exception of its borders, airspace, and [territorial waters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_waters "Territorial waters").[^10]

### Continued conflict

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Westbank_Control_%26_Access_Restrictions_Dec_2012.png/250px-Westbank_Control_%26_Access_Restrictions_Dec_2012.png)

Israeli settlements in the West Bank, 2012

The division between the West Bank and Gaza complicated efforts to achieve [Palestinian unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_unity_government_of_2014 "Palestinian unity government of 2014") and negotiate a comprehensive [peace agreement with Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli%E2%80%93Palestinian_peace_process "Israeli–Palestinian peace process"). Multiple rounds of reconciliation talks were held, but no lasting agreement was reached. The division also hindered the establishment of a unified Palestinian state and led to different governance structures and policies in the two territories.[^95]

Following the intra-Palestinian conflict in 2006, Hamas took over control of the Gaza Strip (it already had majority in the PLC), and Fatah took control of the West Bank. From 2007, the Gaza Strip was governed by Hamas, and the West Bank by the Fatah-party–led Palestinian Authority.[^96]

International efforts to revive the peace process continued. The United States, under the leadership of different administrations, made various attempts to broker negotiations between Israel and the Palestinians.[^97]

Significant obstacles, such as settlement expansion, the status of Jerusalem, [borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borders_of_Israel "Borders of Israel"), and the [right of return for Palestinian refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_right_of_return "Palestinian right of return"), remained unresolved.[^98] [^99] [^100] [^101] In recent years, diplomatic initiatives have emerged, including the [normalization agreements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab%E2%80%93Israeli_normalization "Arab–Israeli normalization") between Israel and several [Arab states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_states "Arab states"), known as the [Abraham Accords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Accords "Abraham Accords").[^102] These agreements, while not directly addressing the Israeli–Palestinian conflict, have reshaped regional dynamics and raised questions about the future of Palestinian aspirations for statehood.[^103] [^104] The status quo remains challenging for Palestinians, with ongoing issues of occupation, settlement expansion, restricted movement, and economic hardships.[^105]

The Palestinian led [October 7 attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/October_7_attacks "October 7 attacks") on Israel in 2023 were followed by the [Gaza war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_war "Gaza war"). The war has caused widespread destruction, a [humanitarian crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_humanitarian_crisis_\(2023%E2%80%93present\) "Gaza humanitarian crisis (2023–present)"), and an [famine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_Strip_famine "Gaza Strip famine") in the Gaza Strip.[^39] [^40] [^41] Most of the Palestinian population in Gaza was [forcibly displaced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_Strip_evacuations "Gaza Strip evacuations").[^39] Since the start of the war, over 70,000 Palestinians in Gaza [have been killed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casualties_of_the_Gaza_war "Casualties of the Gaza war"), almost half of them [women](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_the_Gaza_war#Gaza_Strip "Women in the Gaza war") and [children](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effect_of_the_Gaza_war_on_children_in_the_Gaza_Strip "Effect of the Gaza war on children in the Gaza Strip"), and more than 148,000 injured.[^106] [^107] [^108] Israel [has been accused of genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_genocide "Gaza genocide") against the [Palestinian people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_people "Palestinian people") during [its ongoing invasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_invasion_of_the_Gaza_Strip_\(2023%E2%80%93present\) "Israeli invasion of the Gaza Strip (2023–present)") and [bombing of the Gaza Strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bombing_of_the_Gaza_Strip "Bombing of the Gaza Strip").[^42] [^43] [^44] A study in *[The Lancet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Lancet "The Lancet")* estimated 64,260 deaths in Gaza from [traumatic injuries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traumatic_injuries "Traumatic injuries") by June 2024, while noting a potentially larger death toll when "indirect" deaths are included.[^109] [^110] [^111] [^112] As of May 2025, a comparable figure for traumatic-injury deaths would be 93,000.[^113] There was also a [spillover of the war occurring in the West Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_incursions_in_the_West_Bank_during_the_Gaza_war "Israeli incursions in the West Bank during the Gaza war").

In an attempt to keep alive the possibility of peace and a two-state solution following the Gaza war, the UK, Australia, Canada and France have recognized a Palestinian state in late 2025.[^114]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Mt-Grizim-national-park-7920.jpg/250px-Mt-Grizim-national-park-7920.jpg)

Located around Nablus, Mount Gerizim is the holiest site for Samaritans.

Areas claimed by the country, known as the Palestinian territories, lie in the [Southern Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Levant "Southern Levant") of the [Middle East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_East "Middle East") region. Palestine is part of the [Fertile Crescent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertile_Crescent "Fertile Crescent"), along with Israel, Jordan, Lebanon, Iraq and Syria. The [Gaza Strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_Strip "Gaza Strip") borders the Mediterranean Sea to the west, Egypt to the south, and Israel to the north and east. The West Bank is bordered by [Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan "Jordan") to the east, and Israel to the north, south, and west. Palestine shares its maritime borders with [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel"), [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") and [Cyprus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyprus "Cyprus"). Thus, the two enclaves constituting the area claimed by the State of Palestine have no geographical border with one another, being separated by Israel.[^115] These areas would constitute the world's [163rd largest country by land area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_area "List of countries and dependencies by area").[^26] [^115] [^116]

The West Bank is a mountainous region. It is divided in three regions, namely the [Mount Nablus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jibal_Nablus "Jibal Nablus") (*Jabal Nablus*), the [Hebron Hills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebron_Hills "Hebron Hills") and Jerusalem Mountains (*Jibal al–Quds*).[^117] The [Samarian Hills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarian_Hills "Samarian Hills") and [Judean Hills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judean_Hills "Judean Hills") are mountain ranges in the West Bank, with [Mount Nabi Yunis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Nabi_Yunis,_Palestine "Mount Nabi Yunis, Palestine") at a height of 1,030 meters (3,380 ft) in [Hebron Governorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebron_Governorate "Hebron Governorate") as their highest peak.[^118] [^119] Until 19th century, [Hebron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebron "Hebron") was highest city in the Middle East.[^119]

Jerusalem is located on a plateau in the central highlands and is surrounded by valleys.[^119] The territory consists of fertile valleys, such as the [Jezreel Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jezreel_Valley "Jezreel Valley") and the [Jordan River Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_River_Valley "Jordan River Valley"). Palestine is home to world's largest olive tree, located in Jerusalem.[^120] [^119] Around 45% of Palestine's land is dedicated to growing olive trees.[^121]

Palestine features significant lakes and rivers that play a vital role in its geography and ecosystems.[^122] The [Jordan River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_River "Jordan River") flows southward, forming part of Palestine's eastern border and passing through the [Sea of Galilee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_of_Galilee "Sea of Galilee") before reaching the Dead Sea. According to Christian traditions, it is site of the [baptism of Jesus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baptism_of_Jesus "Baptism of Jesus").[^123] The [Dead Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_Sea "Dead Sea"), bordering the country's east is the lowest point on the earth.[^124] [Jericho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jericho "Jericho"), located nearby, is the lowest city in the world.[^125] Villages and suburban areas around Jerusalem are home to ancient water bodies.[^126] There are several river valleys (*[wadi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wadi "Wadi"))* across the country. These waterways provide essential resources for agriculture and recreation while supporting various ecosystems.[^122]

Three terrestrial ecoregions are found in the area: [Eastern Mediterranean conifer–sclerophyllous–broadleaf forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean_conifer%E2%80%93sclerophyllous%E2%80%93broadleaf_forests "Eastern Mediterranean conifer–sclerophyllous–broadleaf forests"), [Arabian Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Desert "Arabian Desert"), and [Mesopotamian shrub desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamian_shrub_desert "Mesopotamian shrub desert").[^127] Palestine has a number of environmental issues; issues facing the Gaza Strip include [desertification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desertification "Desertification"); [salination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosalinity "Biosalinity") of fresh water; [sewage treatment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sewage_treatment "Sewage treatment"); [water-borne diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water-borne_disease "Water-borne disease"); [soil degradation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_degradation "Soil degradation"); and depletion and contamination of underground water resources. In the West Bank, many of the same issues apply; although fresh water is much more plentiful, access is restricted by the ongoing [dispute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_status_of_Palestine "Legal status of Palestine").[^128]

### Climate

Temperatures in Palestine vary widely. The climate in the West Bank is mostly [Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_climate "Mediterranean climate"), slightly cooler at elevated areas compared with the shoreline, west to the area. In the east, the West Bank includes much of the Judean Desert including the western shoreline of the Dead Sea, characterized by dry and hot climate. Gaza has a [hot semi-arid climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_semi-arid_climate "Hot semi-arid climate") ([Köppen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification "Köppen climate classification"): BSh) with mild winters and dry hot summers.[^129] Spring arrives around March–April. The hottest months are July and August, with the average high being 33 °C (91 °F). The coldest month is January, with temperatures usually at 7 °C (45 °F). Rain is scarce and generally falls between November and March. Annual precipitation is approximately 4.57 inches (116 mm).[^130]

### Biodiversity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Camel_-_panoramio.jpg/250px-Camel_-_panoramio.jpg)

Camels in the Judaean Desert

Palestine does not have officially recognized national parks or protected areas. However, there are areas within the West Bank that are considered to have ecological and cultural significance and are being managed with conservation efforts. These areas are often referred to as nature reserves or protected zones.

Located near Jericho in the West Bank, [Wadi Qelt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wadi_Qelt "Wadi Qelt") is a desert valley with unique flora and fauna. The reserve is known for its rugged landscapes, natural springs, and historical sites such as the [St. George Monastery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monastery_of_Saint_George_of_Choziba "Monastery of Saint George of Choziba").[^131] Efforts have been made to protect the biodiversity and natural beauty of the area.[^132] The Judaean Desert is known for its Judaean Camels. [Qalqilya Zoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qalqilya_Zoo "Qalqilya Zoo") in [Qalqilya Governorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qalqilya_Governorate "Qalqilya Governorate"), is the only zoo currently active in the country. Gaza Zoo was closed due to poor conditions. The Israeli government has established various national parks in [Area C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_C "Area C"), which is considered [illegal under international law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_occupation_of_the_West_Bank "Israeli occupation of the West Bank").
