**Kazakhstan**,[^5] officially the **Republic of Kazakhstan**,[^6] is a [landlocked country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landlocked_country "Landlocked country") situated primarily in [Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia"), with [a portion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Kazakhstan "European Kazakhstan") of its territory extending into [Eastern Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Europe "Eastern Europe").[^7] It borders Russia to the [north and west](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan%E2%80%93Russia_border "Kazakhstan–Russia border"), China to the [east](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Kazakhstan_border "China–Kazakhstan border"), [Kyrgyzstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan "Kyrgyzstan") to the [southeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan%E2%80%93Kyrgyzstan_border "Kazakhstan–Kyrgyzstan border"), [Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan "Uzbekistan") to the [south](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan%E2%80%93Uzbekistan_border "Kazakhstan–Uzbekistan border"), and [Turkmenistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkmenistan "Turkmenistan") to the [southwest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan%E2%80%93Turkmenistan_border "Kazakhstan–Turkmenistan border"), and it has a coastline along the [Caspian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caspian_Sea "Caspian Sea"). The capital is [Astana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astana "Astana") and the country's [largest city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_most_populous_cities_in_Kazakhstan "List of most populous cities in Kazakhstan") and principal cultural and economic center is [Almaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almaty "Almaty"), which served as the capital until 1997.

Kazakhstan is the world's [ninth-largest country by land area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_area "List of countries and dependencies by area") and the largest landlocked country. [Hilly plateaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steppe "Steppe") and plains account for nearly half its vast territory, with [lowlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upland_and_lowland "Upland and lowland") composing another third; its southern and eastern frontiers are composed of mountainous regions. Kazakhstan has a population of 20.5 million and [one of the lowest population densities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_population_density "List of countries and dependencies by population density") in the world, with fewer than 8 people per square kilometre (21 people/mi <sup>2</sup>).[^25] Ethnic [Kazakhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhs "Kazakhs") constitute a majority, while [ethnic Russians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians_in_Kazakhstan "Russians in Kazakhstan") form a significant minority. Officially secular, Kazakhstan is a [Muslim-majority country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_world "Muslim world") with a sizeable [Christian community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy_in_Kazakhstan "Eastern Orthodoxy in Kazakhstan").

Kazakhstan has been inhabited since the [Paleolithic era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleolithic "Paleolithic"). In antiquity, various nomadic [Iranian peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iranian_peoples "Iranian peoples") such as the [Saka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saka "Saka"), [Massagetae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massagetae "Massagetae"), and [Scythians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scythians "Scythians") dominated the territory, with the [Achaemenid Persian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") expanding towards the south. [Turkic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_peoples "Turkic peoples") nomads entered the region from the sixth century. In the 13th century, the area was subjugated by the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire") under [Genghis Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genghis_Khan "Genghis Khan"). Following the disintegration of the [Golden Horde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Horde "Golden Horde") in the 15th century, the [Kazakh Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Khanate "Kazakh Khanate") was established over an area roughly corresponding with modern Kazakhstan. By the 18th century, the Kazakh Khanate had fragmented into three *[jüz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%BCz "Jüz")* (tribal divisions), which were gradually absorbed and conquered by the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"); by the mid-19th century, all of Kazakhstan was nominally under Russian rule.[^26] Following the 1917 [Russian Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Revolution "Russian Revolution") and subsequent [Russian Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Civil_War "Russian Civil War"), it became [an autonomous republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Autonomous_Socialist_Soviet_Republic "Kazakh Autonomous Socialist Soviet Republic") of the [Russian SFSR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_SFSR "Russian SFSR") within the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"). Its status was elevated to that of [a union republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Kazakh Soviet Socialist Republic") in 1936. The Soviet government settled Russians and other ethnicities in the republic, which resulted in ethnic Kazakhs being a minority during the Soviet era. Kazakhstan was the last [constituent republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republics_of_the_Soviet_Union "Republics of the Soviet Union") of the Soviet Union to [declare independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_the_State_Independence_of_the_Republic_of_Kazakhstan "On the State Independence of the Republic of Kazakhstan") in 1991 during [its dissolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union "Dissolution of the Soviet Union").

Kazakhstan dominates Central Asia both [economically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Kazakhstan "Economy of Kazakhstan") and [politically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Kazakhstan "Politics of Kazakhstan"), accounting for 60% of the region's GDP, primarily through its [oil and gas industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_and_gas_industry_in_Kazakhstan "Oil and gas industry in Kazakhstan"); it also has vast mineral resources,[^27] ranking among the highest producers of iron and silver in the world.[^28] Kazakhstan also has the highest [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_Human_Development_Index "List of countries by Human Development Index") ranking in the region. It is a [unitary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_state "Unitary state") constitutional republic;[^29] however, its government is authoritarian.[^30] [^31] Nevertheless, there have been incremental efforts at democratization and political reform since the resignation of [Nursultan Nazarbayev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursultan_Nazarbayev "Nursultan Nazarbayev") in 2019, who had led the country since independence. Kazakhstan is a member state of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") (UN), [World Trade Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Trade_Organization "World Trade Organization") (WHO), [Commonwealth of Independent States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States "Commonwealth of Independent States") (CIS), [Shanghai Cooperation Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Cooperation_Organization "Shanghai Cooperation Organization") (SCO), [Eurasian Economic Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Economic_Union "Eurasian Economic Union") (EAEU), [Collective Security Treaty Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_Security_Treaty_Organization "Collective Security Treaty Organization") (CSTO), [Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_for_Security_and_Co-operation_in_Europe "Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe") (OSCE), [Organization of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation") (OIC), [Organization of Turkic States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_of_Turkic_States "Organization of Turkic States") (OTS), [International Organization of Turkic Culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_of_Turkic_Culture "International Organization of Turkic Culture") and Special Guest status with the [Council of Europe Parliamentary Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Europe "Council of Europe").

## Etymology

### Turkic

According to the most widely accepted theory, the word *qazaq* (Kazakh) derives from a Turkic root meaning "free", "independent", or "wanderer" [^32] and *[stan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/-stan "-stan")* meaning settlement.

The Turkic word *qazaq* (قازاق) was reliably recorded in 13th–14th century dictionaries, including the Codex Cumanicus [^33] and a Mamluk-Kipchak Arabic dictionary published by Martin Houtsma.[^34] In these sources, the word meant "unattached", "homeless", "loner", or "exile", and later acquired the meaning "free man".[^35]

In [Turko-Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turko-Persian "Turko-Persian") sources, the term *Özbek-Qazaq* first appeared during the mid-16th century, in the *Tarikh-i-Rashidi* by [Mirza Muhammad Haidar Dughlat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirza_Muhammad_Haidar_Dughlat "Mirza Muhammad Haidar Dughlat"), a [Chagatayid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chagatai_Khan "Chagatai Khan") prince of [Kashmir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashmir "Kashmir"), which locates Kazakh in the eastern part of *[Desht-i Qipchaq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desht-i_Qipchaq "Desht-i Qipchaq")*.[^36] According to [Vasily Bartold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasily_Bartold "Vasily Bartold"), the Kazakhs likely began using that name during the 15th century.[^37]

### Non-Turkic

The English word *Kazakh*, meaning a member of the Kazakh people, derives from [Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language "Russian language"): казах.[^38] The native name is *қазақ, *qazaq**. It might originate from the Turkic word verb *qaz-*, 'to wander', reflecting the Kazakhs' [nomadic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_nomads "Eurasian nomads") culture.[^39] The term *[Cossack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cossacks "Cossacks")* is of the same origin.[^39]

Though *Kazakh* traditionally referred only to [ethnic Kazakhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhs "Kazakhs"), including those living in China, Russia, Turkey, Uzbekistan, and other neighbouring countries, the term is increasingly being used to refer to any inhabitant of Kazakhstan, including residents of other ethnicities.[^40] In the Kazakh language, the country is called *Qazaqstan* in the [Latin script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_script "Latin script").[^41]

## History

### Antiquity

Kazakhstan has been inhabited since the [Paleolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleolithic "Paleolithic") era.[^42] The [Botai culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Botai_culture "Botai culture") (3700–3100 BC) is credited with the first domestication of horses. The Botai population derived most of their ancestry from a deeply European-related population known as [Ancient North Eurasians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_North_Eurasian "Ancient North Eurasian"), while also displaying some [Ancient East Asian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_history_of_East_Asians "Genetic history of East Asians") admixture.[^43] [Pastoralism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pastoralism "Pastoralism") developed during the [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic"). The population was [Caucasoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caucasian_race "Caucasian race") during the [Bronze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") and [Iron Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age") period.[^44] [^45]

The Kazakh territory was a key constituent of the Eurasian trading [Steppe Route](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steppe_Route "Steppe Route"), the ancestor of the terrestrial [Silk Roads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road_UNESCO_World_Heritage_Sites "Silk Road UNESCO World Heritage Sites"). Archaeologists believe that humans first [domesticated the horse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domestication_of_the_horse "Domestication of the horse") in the region's vast steppes. During recent prehistoric times, Central Asia was inhabited by groups such as the possibly Indo-European [Afanasievo culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afanasievo_culture "Afanasievo culture"),[^46] later early [Indo-Iranian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Iranians "Indo-Iranians") cultures such as [Andronovo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andronovo_culture "Andronovo culture"),[^47] and later Indo-Iranians such as the [Saka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saka "Saka") and [Massagetae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massagetae "Massagetae").[^48] [^49] Other groups included the nomadic [Scythians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scythians "Scythians") and the Persian [Achaemenid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") in the southern territory of the modern country. The Andronovo and [Srubnaya cultures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Srubnaya_culture "Srubnaya culture"), precursors to the peoples of the [Scythian cultures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scythian_cultures "Scythian cultures"), were found to harbour mixed ancestry from the [Yamnaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yamnaya_culture "Yamnaya culture") [Steppe herders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Steppe_Herders "Western Steppe Herders") and peoples of the Central European Middle Neolithic.[^50]

### Turkic Khaganate and Cuman–Kipchak period

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Turanid_looking_Western_Gokturk%E2%80%93Ak-Hun_Turkic_men%2C_Miho_Museum.jpg/250px-Turanid_looking_Western_Gokturk%E2%80%93Ak-Hun_Turkic_men%2C_Miho_Museum.jpg)

Funerary depiction of long-haired Göktürks in the Kazakh Steppe. Miho funerary couch, c. 570 CE. 51

The main [migrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_migration "Turkic migration") of the [Turkic peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_peoples "Turkic peoples") took place between the 5th and 11th centuries, during which Turkic-speaking groups spread across much of Central Asia. Over time, they gradually assimilated or replaced many of the earlier [Iranian-speaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iranian_languages "Iranian languages") populations, transforming Central Asia from a predominantly [Iranian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Iranians "Indo-Iranians") region into one largely Turkic-speaking.[^52]

Medieval statehood on the territory of present-day Kazakhstan began to emerge with the establishment of the [Turkic Khaganate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Turkic_Khaganate "First Turkic Khaganate") in the mid-6th century. In 630, the Eastern Turkic Khaganate was defeated by the Chinese and incorporated into the [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty"). In 658, the Western Turkic Khaganate suffered a similar fate after its defeat by Tang forces. Turkic political power was later restored when [Kutlug Ilterish Qaghan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ilterish_Qaghan "Ilterish Qaghan") revived the Eastern Turkic Khaganate in 682.[^53]

On the territory of the former Western Turkic Khaganate, the [Turgesh Khaganate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turgesh_Khaganate "Turgesh Khaganate") emerged in 699 under the leadership of Üch-elik. The Turgesh actively resisted the Arab expansion into Central Asia during the 720s and 730s, but were ultimately defeated in the mid-8th century. During the same period, the Eastern Turkic Khaganate fragmented as a result of internal conflicts and political disunity.[^53]

From the 8th to the early 13th centuries, the territory of Kazakhstan was successively dominated by the [Karluks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karluks "Karluks"), [Oghuz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oghuz_Turks "Oghuz Turks"), [Kimeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kimek_Khanate "Kimek Khanate"), and [Kipchaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumania "Cumania").[^53]

In southern Kazakhstan, the Karluks established a new khaganate in 840, although their rulers retained the title of *yabghu*. They later came under pressure from the [Samanid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samanid_Empire "Samanid Empire") and eventually became associated with the rise of the [Karakhanid state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kara-Khanid_Khanate "Kara-Khanid Khanate").[^54]

The Oghuz tribes established themselves in the [Aral Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aral_Sea "Aral Sea") region and made [Yangikent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yangikent "Yangikent") (Dzhankent) their capital. Despite attempts at political consolidation, internal conflicts weakened the Oghuz state.[^54]

Between the 9th and 11th centuries, much of western and central Kazakhstan came under the control of the [Kimek Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kimek_Khanate "Kimek Khanate"), whose rulers adopted the title of khagan. The Kimek state was eventually conquered by the [Kipchaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kipchaks "Kipchaks").[^54]

The Kipchaks, known in East Slavic chronicles as the Polovtsians, became one of the dominant powers of the Eurasian steppe. Their ruling elites played an influential role in the politics of [Khwarazm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khwarazm "Khwarazm"), and by the 12th century Kipchak cavalry had become a major military force within the [Khwarazmian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khwarazmian_Empire "Khwarazmian Empire"). They contributed significantly to the empire’s expansion, including the conquest of the Karakhanid state and neighbouring Central Asian territories. In 1218, however, Khwarazm came into conflict with [Genghis Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genghis_Khan "Genghis Khan"), leading to the Mongol conquest of the region and its incorporation into the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire").[^54]

### Mongol Empire and Golden Horde

The [Cumans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuman_people "Cuman people") entered the steppes of modern-day Kazakhstan around the early 11th century, where they later joined with the [Kipchak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kipchak_people "Kipchak people") and established the vast Cuman-Kipchak confederation. While ancient cities [Taraz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taraz "Taraz") (Aulie-Ata) and [Hazrat-e Turkestan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hazrat-e_Turkestan "Hazrat-e Turkestan") had long served as important way-stations along the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road") connecting Asia and Europe, true political consolidation began only with the Mongol rule of the early 13th century. Under the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire"), the first strictly structured administrative districts (Ulus) were established. After the [division of the Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_of_the_Mongol_Empire "Division of the Mongol Empire") in 1259, the land that would become modern-day Kazakhstan was ruled by the [Golden Horde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Horde "Golden Horde"), also known as the Ulus of Jochi. During the Golden Horde period, a [Turco-Mongol tradition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turco-Mongol_tradition "Turco-Mongol tradition") emerged among the ruling elite wherein [Turkicized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkification "Turkification") descendants of [Genghis Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genghis_Khan "Genghis Khan") followed [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") and continued to reign over the lands.

### Kazakh Khanate

Due to the collapse of the [Golden Horde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Horde "Golden Horde") by the mid-15th century, a number of independent khanates emerged, one of which was the [Kazakh Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Khanate "Kazakh Khanate"). Its founders were [Kerei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerei "Kerei") and [Janibek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janibek_Khan "Janibek Khan"). The Khanate reached its peak under the leadership of [Kasym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kasym_Khan "Kasym Khan"), [Haqnazar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haqnazar_Khan "Haqnazar Khan"), Tawakul, and Esim, during which the Kazakh khans, through wars with the [Uzbek khanates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbek_khanates "Uzbek khanates") and the [Nogai Horde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nogai_Horde "Nogai Horde"), gained control over [Turkestan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkestan "Turkestan"), [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent"), and the Nogai lands up to the [Ural River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ural_\(river\) "Ural (river)"). The creation of the legal codes "The Bright Path of Khan Kasym" and "The Ancient Road of Khan Yesim," which are associated with Kasym and Yesim, are surrounded by legends, though they have not survived to the present day. According to Russian sources, by the 17th century, the Kazakh Khanate was divided into three Zhuzes (Hordes) — Senior (Great), Middle (Central), and Junior each with its own nomadic territories.[^55] [^56]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Mausoleum_Turkestan_2021.jpg/250px-Mausoleum_Turkestan_2021.jpg)

Mausoleums of Rabiga Sultān Begim and Khoja Ahmad Yasawi in the city of Turkestan

In the early 16th century Kazakhs transformed [the Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Khanate "Kazakh Khanate") into a [nomadic empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomadic_empire "Nomadic empire") stretching across the steppes east of the Caspian Sea and north of the [Aral Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aral_Sea "Aral Sea") as far as the upper [Irtysh River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irtysh "Irtysh") and the western approaches to the [Altai Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altai_Mountains "Altai Mountains"). During the reigns of [Burunduk Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burunduk_Khan "Burunduk Khan") (ruled 1488–1509) and [Kasym Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kasym_Khan "Kasym Khan") (1509–18), the Kazakhs were the masters of virtually the entire steppe region, reportedly able to bring 200,000 cavalry into the field and feared by all their neighbours. Many historians consider Kasym Khan's leadership the starting point of a distinct and sovereign Kazakh state. His influence extended Kazakh authority from the southeastern regions of modern Kazakhstan to the [Ural Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ural_Mountains "Ural Mountains").[^57] [^56]

However, during the successive rule (1518–1538) of the three sons of Kasym Khan, the power of the Khanate weakened somewhat, accompanied by a trend that later intensified: the disintegration of the Khanate into three separate "hordes." The youngest son of Kasym Khan, who ruled the Kazakh steppes, Haq-Nazar Khan, managed to overcome these difficulties. By restoring the unity of the three Hordes, he began systematic campaigns into [Transoxiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transoxiana "Transoxiana"). This policy continued with his immediate successors until the reign of Tawakul Khan, who, for a brief time, [seized Samarkand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_invasion_of_Northern_Bukhara "Kazakh invasion of Northern Bukhara").[^57] Esim Khan ascended the throne after the death of his brother Tawakul. He fought against the Bukharan khans, Baki-Muhammad and Imamkuli, for control over the [Syr Darya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syr_Darya "Syr Darya") and [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent"), seeking to restore the unity of the Kazakh state while combating the separatism of the sultans. After the usurpation of power by Tursun Muhammad Khan in the 1610s, Yesim retreated to the [Yarkent Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yarkent_Khanate "Yarkent Khanate"), where he united part of the Kyrgyz and formed an alliance with Khan Abd ar-Rahim. In the 1620s, he defeated Tursun, regained the khanate title, destroyed the [Katagan clan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katagans "Katagans") who had supported Tursun Khan, and achieved victories over the [Oirats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oirats "Oirats"), temporarily bringing them under his rule while forcing Imamkuli to renounce his claims on the cities of the Syr Darya region.[^58]

After Tauke's death in 1715/1718 the Kazakh Khanate lost its unity, and the three hordes effectively became separate khanates.[^55]

During the 17th century the Kazakhs fought the [Oirats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oirats "Oirats"), [including the Dzungars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh%E2%80%93Dzungar_Wars "Kazakh–Dzungar Wars").[^60] The beginning of the 18th century marked the zenith of the Kazakh Khanate. During this period the Little Horde participated in the 1723–1730 [war against the Dzungar Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh-Dzungar_Wars "Kazakh-Dzungar Wars"), following their "Great Disaster" invasion of Kazakh territory. Under the leadership of [Abul Khair Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abul_Khair_Khan "Abul Khair Khan"), the Kazakhs won major victories over the Dzungar at the Bulanty River in 1726 and at the [Battle of Añyraqai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_A%C3%B1yraqai "Battle of Añyraqai") in 1729.[^61]

The collapse of the [Dzungar Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dzungar_Khanate "Dzungar Khanate") shifted the balance of power in [Inner Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_Asia "Inner Asia"). For a time, the Kazakh khanates became the dominant political and military force in the region. At the same time, the positions of [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") and [Qing China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_Empire "Qing Empire") strengthened, and they began to define the system of international relations in the northwestern part of Central Asia.[^62] [Ablai Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ablai_Khan "Ablai Khan") pursued a policy of balancing between Russia and China, which allowed the [Kazakh Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Khanate "Kazakh Khanate") to maintain relative independence for a time. After his death, his son, Vali Khan, abandoned this independent policy and recognized the supreme authority of the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire").[^55]

In 1822 the Khanate institution among the Kazakh [Hordes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhuz "Zhuz") was abolished, sparking the [uprising of Sultan Qasym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenesary%27s_Rebellion "Kenesary's Rebellion") in 1824–1827. The final attempt to restore the Khanate was led by Sultan [Kenesary Qasymov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenesary_Qasymuly "Kenesary Qasymuly"), who proclaimed himself khan in 1837 and conducted an armed struggle against Russian authorities until his death in 1847.[^55] The assessment of Kenesary's actions has been influenced by various political circumstances up to the present day. Between 1917 and 1953, this assessment changed several times in Soviet, Russian, and Kazakh historiography: from "hero" of [national liberation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_liberation "National liberation") and [anti-colonial struggle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-colonial "Anti-colonial") to " [reactionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactionary "Reactionary")." Kenesary Qasymuly's activities were multifaceted, contradictory, and ambiguous. The dramatic events in the [Kazakh Steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Steppe "Kazakh Steppe") associated with Kenesary's figure resonated in [Western Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Europe "Western Europe") in [Jules Verne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jules_Verne "Jules Verne") 's novel *[Michael Strogoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Strogoff "Michael Strogoff")* (1876).[^63]

### Russian Kazakhstan

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Battle_Cossacks_with_Kyrgyz_1826.JPG/250px-Battle_Cossacks_with_Kyrgyz_1826.JPG)

Ural Cossacks skirmish with Kazakhs

In the first half of the 18th century the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") constructed the [Irtysh line](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Irtysh_line&action=edit&redlink=1 "Irtysh line (page does not exist)"), a series of forty-six forts and ninety-six redoubts, including [Omsk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omsk "Omsk") (1716), [Semipalatinsk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semey "Semey") (1718), [Pavlodar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pavlodar "Pavlodar") (1720), [Orenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orenburg "Orenburg") (1743) and [Petropavlovsk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petropavl "Petropavl") (1752),[^64] to prevent Kazakh and Oirat raids into Russian territory.[^65] In the late 18th century the Kazakhs took advantage of [Pugachev's Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pugachev%27s_Rebellion "Pugachev's Rebellion"), which was centred on the Volga area, to raid Russian and [Volga German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volga_Germans "Volga Germans") settlements.[^66] In the 19th century, the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") began to expand its influence into Central Asia. The " [Great Game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Game "Great Game") " period is generally regarded as running from approximately 1813 to the [Anglo-Russian Convention of 1907](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Russian_Convention_of_1907 "Anglo-Russian Convention of 1907"). The [tsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsar "Tsar") effectively ruled over most of the territory belonging to what is now the Republic of Kazakhstan.

The Russian Empire introduced an administrative system and built military garrisons and barracks in its effort to establish a presence in Central Asia in the so-called "Great Game" for dominance in the area against the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire"), Russia, which was expanding its influence southward into India and Southeast Asia, established its first outpost, Orsk, in 1735. The Russian language was introduced in schools and government institutions throughout the region.

Russian attempts to impose their political and cultural system provoked resentment among the Kazakhs. By the 1860s, resistance to Russian rule had intensified. The traditional nomadic way of life and the livestock-based economy were severely disrupted, leading to famine and the decline of some Kazakh tribes.

Beginning in the late nineteenth century, the Kazakh national movement sought to preserve the native language and cultural identity in opposition to the Russian Empire's assimilation policies. At the same time, Russian-style education introduced modern ideas to the steppe, and figures like [Shoqan Walikhanov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoqan_Walikhanov "Shoqan Walikhanov") and [Abay Kunanbayev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abai_Qunanbaiuly "Abai Qunanbaiuly") adapted these ideas to the specific needs of Kazakh society, creating a secular culture unparalleled in other parts of Asian Russia.[^57]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Valikhanov.jpg/250px-Valikhanov.jpg)

Shoqan Walikhanov and Fyodor Dostoyevsky

From the 1890s onward, ever-larger numbers of settlers from the Russian Empire began [colonizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonization "Colonization") the territory of present-day Kazakhstan, in particular, the province of [Semirechye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jetisu "Jetisu"). The number of settlers rose still further once the [Trans-Aral Railway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Aral_Railway "Trans-Aral Railway") from [Orenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orenburg "Orenburg") to [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent") was completed in 1906. A specially created Migration Department (Переселенческое Управление) in [St. Petersburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Petersburg "St. Petersburg") oversaw and encouraged the migration to expand Russian influence in the area. During the 19th century, about 400,000 Russians immigrated to Kazakhstan, and about one million Slavs, Germans, Jews, and others immigrated to the region during the first third of the 20th century.[^67] [Vasile Balabanov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasile_Balabanov "Vasile Balabanov") was the administrator responsible for the resettlement during much of this time.

The competition for land and water that ensued between the Kazakhs and the newcomers caused great resentment against colonial rule during the final years of the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"). The most serious uprising, the [Central Asian revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asian_revolt_of_1916 "Central Asian revolt of 1916"), occurred in 1916. The Kazakhs attacked Russian and [Cossack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cossacks "Cossacks") settlers and military garrisons. The revolt resulted in a series of clashes and in brutal massacres committed by both sides.[^68] Both sides resisted the communist government until late 1919.

In the wake of the [Russian Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Revolution "Russian Revolution"), the [Alash Orda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alash_Autonomy "Alash Autonomy") government was formed in 1917 as an attempt to secure Kazakh autonomy. Although it existed only in name, Alash Orda represented the Kazakh push for self-rule. The Bolshevik Red Army eventually defeated White Russian forces in the region by 1920, and Kazakhstan was incorporated into the Soviet Union.[^57]

### Kazakh SSR

Following the [collapse of central government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/October_Revolution "October Revolution") in [Petrograd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Petersburg "Saint Petersburg") in November 1917, the Kazakhs (then in Russia officially referred to as "Kirghiz") experienced a brief period of autonomy (the [Alash Autonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alash_Autonomy "Alash Autonomy")) before eventually succumbing to the [Bolsheviks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolsheviks "Bolsheviks") ' rule. On 26 August 1920 the [Kirghiz Autonomous Socialist Soviet Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kirghiz_Autonomous_Socialist_Soviet_Republic_\(1920%E2%80%9325\) "Kirghiz Autonomous Socialist Soviet Republic (1920–25)") within the [Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Soviet_Federative_Socialist_Republic "Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic") (RSFSR) was established. The Kirghiz ASSR included the territory of present-day Kazakhstan, but its administrative centre was the mainly Russian-populated town of [Orenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orenburg "Orenburg"). In June 1925, the Kirghiz ASSR was renamed the [Kazak ASSR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Autonomous_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Kazakh Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic") and its administrative centre was transferred to the town of [Kyzylorda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyzylorda "Kyzylorda"), and in April 1927 to [Alma-Ata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almaty "Almaty").

Despite their traditionally nomadic lifestyle, the Kazakhs were among the most literate groups in Central Asia, which set them apart in the region. However, Soviet policies, including forced [collectivization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collectivization "Collectivization") and repression of the traditional elite in the late 1920s and 1930s, led to devastating consequences for the Kazakh population. These policies caused widespread famine and high fatalities, with an estimated 1.5 million deaths between 1926 and 1939, mostly due to starvation and related diseases, as well as violence (see also: [Famine in Kazakhstan of 1932–33](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Famine_in_Kazakhstan_of_1932%E2%80%9333 "Famine in Kazakhstan of 1932–33")). Thousands of Kazakhs fled to China, but many did not survive the journey.[^57] [^69] [^70] During the 1930s, some members of the Kazakh intelligentsia were executed – as part of the [policies of political reprisals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_repression_in_the_Soviet_Union "Political repression in the Soviet Union") pursued by the Soviet government in Moscow.

On 5 December 1936 the [Kazakh Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Autonomous_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Kazakh Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic") (whose territory by then corresponded to that of modern Kazakhstan) was detached from the [Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Soviet_Federative_Socialist_Republic "Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic") (RSFSR) and made the [Kazakh Soviet Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Kazakh Soviet Socialist Republic"), a full [union republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republics_of_the_Soviet_Union "Republics of the Soviet Union") of the USSR, one of eleven such republics at the time, along with the [Kirghiz Soviet Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kirghiz_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Kirghiz Soviet Socialist Republic").

The republic was one of the destinations for exiled and convicted persons, as well as for mass resettlements, or deportations affected by the central USSR authorities during the 1930s and 1940s, such as approximately 400,000 [Volga Germans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volga_Germans "Volga Germans") deported from the [Volga German Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volga_German_Autonomous_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Volga German Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic") in September–October 1941, and then later the [Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greeks_in_Kazakhstan "Greeks in Kazakhstan") and [Crimean Tatars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deportation_of_the_Crimean_Tatars "Deportation of the Crimean Tatars"). Deportees and prisoners were interned in some of the biggest [Soviet labour camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulag "Gulag") (the Gulag), including [ALZhIR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akmol "Akmol") camp outside Astana, which was reserved for the wives of men considered "enemies of the people".[^71] Many moved due to the policy of [population transfer in the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_transfer_in_the_Soviet_Union "Population transfer in the Soviet Union") and others were forced into [involuntary settlements in the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Involuntary_settlements_in_the_Soviet_Union "Involuntary settlements in the Soviet Union").

The [Soviet-German War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Front_\(World_War_II\) "Eastern Front (World War II)") (1941–1945) led to an increase in industrialization and [mineral extraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineral_extraction "Mineral extraction") in support of the war effort. At the time of [Joseph Stalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Stalin "Joseph Stalin") 's death in 1953, however, Kazakhstan still had an overwhelmingly agricultural economy. In 1953, Soviet leader [Nikita Khrushchev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev "Nikita Khrushchev") initiated the [Virgin Lands Campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgin_Lands_Campaign "Virgin Lands Campaign") designed to turn the traditional pasturelands of Kazakhstan into a major grain-producing region for the Soviet Union. The Virgin Lands policy brought mixed results. However, along with later modernizations under Soviet leader [Leonid Brezhnev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev "Leonid Brezhnev") (in power 1964–1982), it accelerated the development of the agricultural sector, which remains the source of livelihood for a large percentage of Kazakhstan's population. Because of the decades of privation, war and resettlement, by 1959 the [Kazakhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhs "Kazakhs") had become a minority, making up 30% of the population. Ethnic [Russians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians "Russians") accounted for 43%.[^72]

In 1947, the USSR, as part of its [atomic bomb project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_atomic_bomb_project "Soviet atomic bomb project"), founded an [atomic bomb test site](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semipalatinsk_Test_Site "Semipalatinsk Test Site") near the north-eastern town of [Semipalatinsk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semey "Semey"), where the [first Soviet nuclear bomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RDS-1 "RDS-1") test was conducted in 1949. Hundreds of nuclear tests were conducted until 1989 with adverse consequences for the nation's environment and population.[^73] The [Anti-nuclear movement in Kazakhstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement_in_Kazakhstan "Anti-nuclear movement in Kazakhstan") became a major political force in the late 1980s.

In April 1961 [Baikonur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baikonur "Baikonur") became the springboard of [Vostok 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vostok_1 "Vostok 1"), a spacecraft with Soviet cosmonaut [Yuri Gagarin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin "Yuri Gagarin") being the first human to enter space.

In December 1986 mass demonstrations by young ethnic Kazakhs, later called the [Jeltoqsan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeltoqsan "Jeltoqsan") riot, took place in Almaty to protest the replacement of the [First Secretary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary "General Secretary") of the [Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Kazakhstan "Communist Party of Kazakhstan") of the Kazakh SSR [Dinmukhamed Konayev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinmukhamed_Konayev "Dinmukhamed Konayev") with [Gennady Kolbin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gennady_Kolbin "Gennady Kolbin") from the [Russian SFSR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_SFSR "Russian SFSR"). Governmental troops suppressed the unrest, several people were killed, and many demonstrators were jailed.[^74] In the waning days of Soviet rule, discontent continued to grow and found expression under Soviet leader [Mikhail Gorbachev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev "Mikhail Gorbachev") 's policy of *[glasnost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glasnost "Glasnost")* ("openness").

### Independence

Kazakhstan declared its sovereignty within the Soviet Union on 25 October 1990. Following the failed August 1991 [coup attempt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1991_Soviet_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_attempt "1991 Soviet coup d'état attempt") in Moscow, the country proclaimed full independence on 16 December 1991, becoming the last Soviet republic to do so. Ten days after Kazakhstan's declaration, the Soviet Union itself dissolved. This period marked a significant turning point in Kazakhstan's history, as it embarked on a new political and economic path separate from Moscow's control.

[Nursultan Nazarbayev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursultan_Nazarbayev "Nursultan Nazarbayev"), the communist-era leader of Kazakhstan, became the country's first president. Under his leadership, Kazakhstan transitioned from a Soviet-era planned economy to a market economy, focusing on privatization and foreign investments. The emphasis on economic reform, particularly in the oil sector, helped Kazakhstan become one of Central Asia's most economically powerful nations. By 2006, Kazakhstan contributed around 60% of the region's GDP, primarily through its oil exports. However, political reforms lagged behind these economic strides, and Nazarbayev maintained an authoritarian rule throughout his presidency.[^27]

In 1997, Nazarbayev [moved the capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_relocation_in_Kazakhstan "Capital relocation in Kazakhstan") from Almaty, the country's largest city, to [Astana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astana "Astana") (later renamed Nur-Sultan in 2019),[^75] a decision that symbolized the government's desire to modernize and assert control over the country's vast territories. The capital city change was part of broader efforts to establish a new national identity and shift the political center from former Soviet-era heartlands.[^76]

Kazakhstan's political landscape during Nazarbayev's rule was characterized by limited political pluralism. In the 2004 parliamentary elections, the pro-government Otan Party, led by Nazarbayev, dominated the Majilis (the lower house of parliament). Other parties sympathetic to the president, such as the agrarian-industrial bloc AIST and the Asar party (founded by Nazarbayev's daughter), secured most of the remaining seats, while opposition parties struggled to gain representation. International observers, including the Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe, criticized the elections for not meeting democratic standards.[^77]

Despite claims of progress toward democracy, Kazakhstan's political system remained authoritarian well into the 21st century.[^78] In 2010, the country was still ranked as an [authoritarian regime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") on *[The Economist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economist "The Economist") '* s [Democracy Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economist_Democracy_Index "The Economist Democracy Index").[^30] [^31] Nazarbayev, who had ruled since independence, announced his resignation on 19 March 2019, after nearly three decades in power.[^79] His successor, [Kassym-Jomart Tokayev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kassym-Jomart_Tokayev "Kassym-Jomart Tokayev"), won the 2019 presidential election and took office on 12 June 2019.[^80] [^81] Tokayev's first official act was to rename the capital city to Nur-Sultan, in honour of Nazarbayev's legacy.[^82]

But Tokayev's presidency faced significant challenges. In January 2022, Kazakhstan was gripped by [large-scale protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Kazakh_unrest "2022 Kazakh unrest") following a sharp rise in fuel prices.[^83] The unrest quickly escalated, and Tokayev responded decisively by assuming control of the country's Security Council, removing Nazarbayev from the post and consolidating his own power. This marked a dramatic shift in Kazakhstan's political dynamics, with Tokayev signalling a departure from the old Nazarbayev-era system.[^84] In September 2022, the capital's name was reverted to Astana, a move seen as part of the broader efforts to distance the country from the former president's influence.[^85]

## Geography

Since it extends across both sides of the [Ural River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ural_River "Ural River"), considered the dividing line separating Europe and Asia, Kazakhstan is one of only two [landlocked countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landlocked_country "Landlocked country") in the world that [has territory in two continents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcontinental_countries "Transcontinental countries") (the other is [Azerbaijan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azerbaijan "Azerbaijan")).

With an area of 2,700,000 square kilometres (1,000,000 sq mi)—equivalent in size to Western Europe—Kazakhstan is the ninth-largest country and largest landlocked country in the world. While it was part of the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"), Kazakhstan lost some of its territory to China's [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang") province,[^86] and some to Uzbekistan's [Karakalpakstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakalpakstan "Karakalpakstan") autonomous republic during Soviet years.

It shares borders of 6,846 kilometres (4,254 mi) with Russia, 2,203 kilometres (1,369 mi) with [Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan "Uzbekistan"), 1,533 kilometres (953 mi) with China, 1,051 kilometres (653 mi) with [Kyrgyzstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan "Kyrgyzstan"), and 379 kilometres (235 mi) with [Turkmenistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkmenistan "Turkmenistan"). Major cities include [Almaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almaty "Almaty"), [Astana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astana "Astana"), [Shymkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shymkent "Shymkent"), [Aktöbe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aktobe "Aktobe") and [Karagandy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karaganda "Karaganda"). It lies between latitudes [40°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/40th_parallel_north "40th parallel north") and [56° N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/56th_parallel_north "56th parallel north"), and longitudes [46°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/46th_meridian_east "46th meridian east") and [88° E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/88th_meridian_east "88th meridian east"). While located primarily in Asia, a [small portion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Kazakhstan "European Kazakhstan") of Kazakhstan is also located west of the [Urals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urals "Urals") in Eastern Europe.[^87]

Kazakhstan's terrain extends west to east from the [Caspian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caspian_Sea "Caspian Sea") to the [Altai Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altai_Mountains "Altai Mountains") and the [Tian Shan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tian_Shan "Tian Shan"), and north to south from the plains of [Western Siberia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberia "Siberia") to the oases and deserts of Central Asia. The [Kazakh Steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Steppe "Kazakh Steppe") (plain), with an area of around 804,500 square kilometres (310,600 sq mi), occupies one-third of the country and is the world's largest dry [steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steppe "Steppe") region. The steppe is characterized by large areas of [grasslands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grassland "Grassland") and sandy regions. Major seas, lakes and rivers include [Lake Balkhash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Balkhash "Lake Balkhash"), [Lake Zaysan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Zaysan "Lake Zaysan"), the [Charyn River and gorge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charyn_Canyon "Charyn Canyon"), the [Ili](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ili_River "Ili River"), [Irtysh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irtysh_River "Irtysh River"), [Ishim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ishim_River "Ishim River"), [Ural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ural_River "Ural River") and [Syr Darya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syr_Darya "Syr Darya") rivers, and the [Aral Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aral_Sea "Aral Sea") until it largely dried up in one of the world's worst environmental disasters.[^88]

### Natural resources

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Reserve_Karkaraly.jpg/250px-Reserve_Karkaraly.jpg)

Qarağandy Region

Kazakhstan has an abundant supply of accessible mineral and fossil fuel resources. Development of petroleum, natural gas, and mineral extractions has attracted most of the over $40 billion in foreign investment in Kazakhstan since 1993 and accounts for some 57% of the nation's industrial output (or approximately 13% of gross domestic product). According to some estimates,[^90] Kazakhstan has the second largest [uranium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranium "Uranium"), [chromium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromium "Chromium"), lead, and [zinc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc "Zinc") reserves; the third largest [manganese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manganese "Manganese") reserves; the fifth largest copper reserves; and ranks in the top ten for coal, iron, and gold. In 2015, Kazakhstan's gold production is 64 metric tonnes.[^91] It is also an exporter of diamonds. Perhaps most significant for economic development, Kazakhstan also has the 11th largest proven reserves of both petroleum and natural gas.[^92] One such location is the [Tokarevskoye gas condensate field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tokarevskoye_gas_condensate_field "Tokarevskoye gas condensate field").

In total, there are 160 deposits with over 2.7 billion tonnes (2.7 billion long tons) of petroleum. Oil explorations have shown that the deposits on the [Caspian shore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caspian_Sea "Caspian Sea") are only a small part of a much larger deposit. It is said that 3.5 billion tonnes (3.4 billion long tons) of oil and 2.5 billion cubic metres (88 billion cubic feet) of gas could be found in that area. Overall the estimate of Kazakhstan's oil deposits is 6.1 billion tonnes (6.0 billion long tons). However, there are only three [refineries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_refinery "Oil refinery") within the country, situated in [Atyrau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atirau "Atirau"),[^93] [Pavlodar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pavlodar "Pavlodar"), and [Şymkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C5%9Eymkent "Şymkent"). These are not capable of processing the total crude output, so much of it is exported to Russia. According to the US [Energy Information Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_Information_Administration "Energy Information Administration"), Kazakhstan was producing approximately 1,540,000 barrels (245,000 m <sup>3</sup>) of oil per day in 2009.[^94]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Kaindy_lake_south-east_Kazakhstan.jpg/250px-Kaindy_lake_south-east_Kazakhstan.jpg)

Lake Kaindy in south-eastern Kazakhstan

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Aral_Sea_1989-2008.jpg/250px-Aral_Sea_1989-2008.jpg)

Aral Sea in 1989 and 2008

There are several large navigable rivers in Kazakhstan. All of them flow along the edges of the country, mainly in the north, while the southern and central parts of the river are mostly steppe, often drying up completely in the height of summer. Kazakhstan also possesses large deposits of [phosphorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorite "Phosphorite"). Two of the largest deposits include the Karatau basin with 650 million tonnes of P <sub>2</sub> O <sub>5</sub> and the Chilisai deposit of the [Aqtobe phosphorite basin](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%90%D0%BA%D1%82%D0%BE%D0%B1%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D1%84%D0%BE%D1%81%D1%84%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B8%D1%82%D0%BE%D0%BD%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B9_%D0%B1%D0%B0%D1%81%D1%81%D0%B5%D0%B9%D0%BD "ru:Актобинский фосфоритоносный бассейн") located in northwestern Kazakhstan, with resources of 500–800 million tonnes of 9% ore.[^95] [^96]

On 17 October 2013 the [Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extractive_Industries_Transparency_Initiative "Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative") (EITI) accepted Kazakhstan as "EITI Compliant", meaning that the country has a basic and functional process to ensure the regular disclosure of natural resource revenues.[^97]

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Koppen-Geiger_Map_v2_KAZ_1991%E2%80%932020.svg/330px-Koppen-Geiger_Map_v2_KAZ_1991%E2%80%932020.svg.png)

Köppen–Geiger climate classification map at 1-km resolution for Kazakhstan 1991–2020

Kazakhstan has an "extreme" [continental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_climate "Continental climate") and [cold steppe climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_steppe "Cold steppe"), and sits solidly inside the [Eurasian Steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Steppe "Eurasian Steppe"), featuring the [Kazakh Steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Steppe "Kazakh Steppe"), with hot summers and cold winters. Indeed, Astana is the second coldest capital city in the world, after [Ulaanbaatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulaanbaatar "Ulaanbaatar"). [Precipitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation_\(meteorology\) "Precipitation (meteorology)") varies between arid and semi-arid conditions, the winter being particularly dry.[^98]

### Wildlife

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Vulpes_corsac.jpg/250px-Vulpes_corsac.jpg)

Corsac fox

There are ten [nature reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_protected_areas_of_Kazakhstan "List of protected areas of Kazakhstan") and ten [national parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_national_parks_of_Kazakhstan "List of national parks of Kazakhstan") in Kazakhstan that provide safe haven for many rare and endangered plants and animals. In total there are twenty five [areas of conservancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservancy_areas_of_Kazakhstan "Conservancy areas of Kazakhstan"). Common plants are *[Astragalus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astragalus_\(plant\) "Astragalus (plant)")*, *[Gagea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gagea "Gagea")*, *[Allium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allium "Allium")*, *[Carex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carex "Carex")* and *[Oxytropis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxytropis "Oxytropis")*; endangered plant species include native wild apple (*[Malus sieversii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malus_sieversii "Malus sieversii")*), wild grape (*[Vitis vinifera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitis_vinifera "Vitis vinifera")*) and several wild [tulip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulip "Tulip") species (e.g., *[Tulipa greigii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulipa_greigii "Tulipa greigii")*) and rare onion species *[Allium karataviense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allium_karataviense "Allium karataviense")*, also *[Iris willmottiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iris_willmottiana "Iris willmottiana")* and *[Tulipa kaufmanniana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulipa_kaufmanniana "Tulipa kaufmanniana")*.[^100] [^101] Kazakhstan had a 2019 [Forest Landscape Integrity Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_Landscape_Integrity_Index "Forest Landscape Integrity Index") mean score of 8.23/10, ranking it 26th globally out of 172 countries.[^102]

Common mammals include the [wolf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf "Wolf"), [red fox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_fox "Red fox"), [corsac fox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corsac_fox "Corsac fox"), [moose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moose "Moose"), [argali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argali "Argali") (the largest species of sheep), [Eurasian lynx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_lynx "Eurasian lynx"), [Pallas's cat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pallas%27s_cat "Pallas's cat"), and [snow leopards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snow_leopards "Snow leopards"), several of which are protected. Kazakhstan's Red Book of Protected Species lists 125 vertebrates including many birds and mammals, and 404 plants including fungi, algae and lichens.[^103]

[Przewalski's horse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Przewalski%27s_horse "Przewalski's horse") has been reintroduced to the steppes after nearly 200 years.[^104]
