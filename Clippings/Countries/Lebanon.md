**Lebanon**,[^3] officially the **Lebanese Republic**,[^4] is a country in the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant") region of [West Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Asia "West Asia"). Situated at the crossroads of the [Mediterranean Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin "Mediterranean Basin") and the [Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula"),[^19] it is bordered by [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria") to the north and east, [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel") to the south, and the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") to the west; [Cyprus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyprus "Cyprus") lies a short distance from the coastline. Lebanon has a population of more than five million and an area of 10,452 square kilometres (4,036 sq mi). [Beirut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beirut "Beirut") is the country's capital and largest city.

Human habitation in Lebanon dates to 5000 BC.[^18] From 3200 to 539 BC, it was part of [Phoenicia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia"), a maritime civilization that spanned the [Mediterranean Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin "Mediterranean Basin").[^21] In 64 BC, the region became part of the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") and the subsequent [Byzantine Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire"). After the seventh century, it [came under the rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_the_Levant "Muslim conquest of the Levant") of different Arab Islamic [caliphates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caliphates "Caliphates"), including the [Rashidun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashidun_Caliphate "Rashidun Caliphate"), [Umayyad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") and [Abbasid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate") caliphates. The 11th century saw the establishment of Christian [Crusader states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusader_states "Crusader states"), which fell to the [Ayyubids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayyubid_dynasty "Ayyubid dynasty") and the [Mamluks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mamluk_Sultanate "Mamluk Sultanate"). Lebanon came under [Ottoman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") rule in the early 16th century. Under Ottoman sultan [Abdulmejid I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdulmejid_I "Abdulmejid I"), the first Lebanese [proto-state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-state "Quasi-state"), the [Mount Lebanon Mutasarrifate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Lebanon_Mutasarrifate "Mount Lebanon Mutasarrifate"), was established as a home for [Maronite Christians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Maronite_Christians "Lebanese Maronite Christians"), as part of the [Tanzimat reforms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanzimat#Reforms "Tanzimat").

After the [dissolution of the Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Ottoman_Empire "Dissolution of the Ottoman Empire") following [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), Lebanon came under the [Mandate for Syria and the Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandate_for_Syria_and_the_Lebanon "Mandate for Syria and the Lebanon"), administered by [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Third_Republic "French Third Republic"), which established [Greater Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Lebanon "Greater Lebanon"). By 1943, Lebanon had gained independence from [Free France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_France "Free France") and established a distinct form of [confessional government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confessionalism_\(politics\) "Confessionalism (politics)"), with the state's major religious groups being [apportioned specific political powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consociationalism "Consociationalism"). The new Lebanese state was relatively stable,[^22] but this was ultimately shattered by the outbreak of the [Lebanese Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Civil_War "Lebanese Civil War") (1975–1990). Lebanon was also subjugated by two military occupations: [Syria from 1976 to 2005](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syrian_occupation_of_Lebanon "Syrian occupation of Lebanon") and [Israel from 1985 to 2000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_occupation_of_Southern_Lebanon_\(1982%E2%80%932000\) "Israeli occupation of Southern Lebanon (1982–2000)"). It has been the scene of several [conflicts with Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli%E2%80%93Lebanese_conflict "Israeli–Lebanese conflict"), of which the [ongoing war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel%E2%80%93Hezbollah_conflict_\(2023%E2%80%93present\) "Israel–Hezbollah conflict (2023–present)") marks the fourth Israeli invasion since 1978.

Lebanon is a [developing country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country"), ranked 102nd on the [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index").[^23] It has been classified as a [lower-middle-income country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GNI_\(nominal\)_per_capita "List of countries by GNI (nominal) per capita").[^24] The [Lebanese liquidity crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_liquidity_crisis "Lebanese liquidity crisis"), coupled with [nationwide corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corruption_in_Lebanon "Corruption in Lebanon") and disasters such as the [2020 Beirut explosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Beirut_explosion "2020 Beirut explosion"), precipitated the collapse of [Lebanon's currency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_pound "Lebanese pound") and fomented political instability, widespread resource shortages, and [high unemployment and poverty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poverty_in_Lebanon "Poverty in Lebanon"). The [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank") has defined Lebanon's economic crisis as one of the world's worst since the 19th century.[^25] [^26] Despite the country's small size,[^27] [Lebanese culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Lebanon "Culture of Lebanon") is renowned both in the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world") and globally, powered primarily by the large and influential [Lebanese diaspora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_diaspora "Lebanese diaspora").[^28] Lebanon is a founding member of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") and the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League"),[^29] and a member of the [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement"), the [Organization of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation"), the *[Organisation internationale de la Francophonie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_internationale_de_la_Francophonie "Organisation internationale de la Francophonie")*, and the [Group of 77](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_of_77 "Group of 77").

## Etymology

The name of [Mount Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Lebanon "Mount Lebanon") originates from the [Phoenician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenician_language "Phoenician language") [root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semitic_root "Semitic root") **lbn** ([𐤋𐤁𐤍](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%F0%90%A4%8B%F0%90%A4%81%F0%90%A4%8D "wikt:𐤋𐤁𐤍")) meaning "white", apparently from its snow-capped peaks.[^30] [^31] Occurrences of the name have been found in different [Middle Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Bronze_Age "Middle Bronze Age") texts from the library of [Ebla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebla "Ebla"),[^32] and three of the twelve tablets of the [Epic of Gilgamesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh "Epic of Gilgamesh"). The name is recorded in Egypt as *[rmnn](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rmnn "wikt:rmnn")* ([Ancient Egyptian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_language "Ancient Egyptian language"): 𓂋𓏠𓈖𓈖𓈉; it had no letter corresponding to *l*).[^33] The name occurs nearly 70 times in the [Hebrew Bible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_Bible "Hebrew Bible") as לְבָנוֹן *Ləḇānon*.[^34]

*Lebanon* as the name of an administrative unit (as opposed to the mountain range) that was introduced with the [Ottoman reforms of 1861](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanzimat "Tanzimat") as the *[Mount Lebanon Mutasarrifate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Lebanon_Mutasarrifate "Mount Lebanon Mutasarrifate")* ([Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"): متصرفية جبل لبنان, [romanized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic "Romanization of Arabic"): *Mutaṣarrifīyat Jabal Lubnān*; [Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_language "Turkish language"): *Cebel-i Lübnan Mutasarrıflığı*), continued in the name of [Greater Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Lebanon "Greater Lebanon") ([Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"): دولة لبنان الكبير **Dawlat Lubnān al-Kabīr**; [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language"): *État du Grand Liban*) in 1920, and eventually in the name of the sovereign *Republic of Lebanon* ([Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"): الجمهورية اللبنانية **al-Jumhūriyyah al-Lubnāniyyah**) upon its independence in 1943.

## History

### Antiquity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Byblos_Libanon_2003.JPG/250px-Byblos_Libanon_2003.JPG)

Byblos is believed to have been first occupied between 8800 and 7000 BC 35 and continuously inhabited since 5000 BC, 36 making it among the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world. 37 38 It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site 39

The [Natufian culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natufian_culture "Natufian culture") was the first to become sedentary at around 12000 BC.[^40]

Evidence of early settlement in Lebanon was found in [Byblos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byblos "Byblos"), considered among the [oldest continuously inhabited cities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_oldest_continuously_inhabited_cities "List of oldest continuously inhabited cities") in the world.[^20] The evidence dates back to earlier than 5000 BC. Archaeologists discovered remnants of prehistoric huts with crushed limestone floors, primitive weapons, and [burial jars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jar_burial "Jar burial") left by the [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") and [Chalcolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalcolithic "Chalcolithic") fishing communities who lived on the shore of the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") over 7,000 years ago.[^41]

Lebanon was part of northern [Canaan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canaan "Canaan"), and consequently became the homeland of Canaanite descendants, the [Phoenicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia"), a seafaring people based in the coastal strip of the northern [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant") who spread across the Mediterranean in the first millennium BC.[^42] The most prominent Phoenician cities were [Byblos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byblos "Byblos"), [Sidon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidon "Sidon") and [Tyre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyre,_Lebanon "Tyre, Lebanon"). According to the [Bible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bible "Bible"), King [Hiram of Tyre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiram_I "Hiram I") collaborated closely with [Solomon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon "Solomon"), supplying cedar logs for [Solomon's Temple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon%27s_Temple "Solomon's Temple") and sending skilled workers.[^43] The [Phoenicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canaanite_languages "Canaanite languages") are credited with the invention of the [oldest verified alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet "Phoenician alphabet"), which subsequently inspired the [Greek alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_alphabet "Greek alphabet") and the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_alphabet "Latin alphabet") one thereafter.[^44]

In the 9th century BC, [Phoenician colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenician_colonies "Phoenician colonies"), including [Carthage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carthage "Carthage") in present-day [Tunisia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunisia "Tunisia") and [Cádiz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%C3%A1diz "Cádiz") in present-day [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain"), flourished throughout the Mediterranean. Subsequently, foreign powers, starting with the [Neo-Assyrian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire "Neo-Assyrian Empire"), imposed tribute and attacked non-compliant cities. The [Neo-Babylonian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-Babylonian_Empire "Neo-Babylonian Empire") took control in the 6th century BC.[^43] In 539 BC, the cities of Phoenicia were then incorporated into the [Achaemenid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") by [Cyrus the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great "Cyrus the Great").[^45] The Phoenician city-states were later incorporated into the empire of [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") following the [siege of Tyre in 332 BCE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Tyre_\(332_BC\) "Siege of Tyre (332 BC)").[^45]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/PhoenicianTrade_EN.svg/250px-PhoenicianTrade_EN.svg.png)

A map of Phoenicia, trade routes and the Phoenician colony of Carthage

In 64 BC, the Roman general [Pompey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pompey "Pompey") had the region of Syria annexed into the [Roman Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic "Roman Republic"). The area was then split into two [Imperial Provinces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_province "Imperial province") under the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire"), [Coele-Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coele-Syria "Coele-Syria") and [Phoenice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenice_\(Roman_province\) "Phoenice (Roman province)"), the latter which the land of present-day Lebanon was a part of.

The region that is now Lebanon, as with the rest of Syria and much of [Anatolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolia "Anatolia"), became a major center of Christianity in the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") during the early spread of the faith. During the late 4th and early 5th century, a hermit named [Maron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maron "Maron") established a monastic tradition focused on the importance of [monotheism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotheism "Monotheism") and [asceticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asceticism "Asceticism") near the Mediterranean mountain range known as [Mount Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Lebanon "Mount Lebanon"). The monks who followed Maron spread his teachings among Lebanese in the region. These Christians became known as [Maronites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maronites "Maronites") and moved into the mountains to avoid religious persecution by Roman authorities.[^46] During the frequent [Roman–Persian Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman%E2%80%93Persian_Wars "Roman–Persian Wars") that lasted for many centuries, the [Sasanian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sasanian_Empire "Sasanian Empire") occupied what is now Lebanon from 619 till 629.[^47]

### Middle Ages

During the 7th century, [Muslims conquered Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_the_Levant "Muslim conquest of the Levant") from the Byzantines, incorporating the region, including modern-day Lebanon, under the [Islamic Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashidun_Caliphate "Rashidun Caliphate").[^48] In the era of [Uthman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uthman "Uthman") 's caliphate (644–656), Islam gained significant influence in Damascus, led by [Mu'awiya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu%27awiya_I "Mu'awiya I"), a relative of Uthman, serving as the governor. Mu'awiya sent forces to the coastal region of Lebanon, prompting conversions to Islam among the coastal population. The mountainous areas retained their Christian or other cultural practices.[^43] Despite Islam and Arabic becoming officially dominant, the population's conversion from Christianity and [Syriac language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syriac_language "Syriac language") was gradual. The Maronite community, in particular, managed to maintain a large degree of autonomy despite the succession of rulers over Lebanon and Syria. The relative isolation of the Lebanese mountains meant the mountains served as a refuge in the times of religious and political crises in the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant"). As such, the mountains displayed religious diversity and the existence of several well-established sects and religions, notably, [Maronites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maronites "Maronites"), [Druze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Druze "Druze"), [Shiite Muslims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shia_Islam "Shia Islam"), [Ismailis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isma%27ilism "Isma'ilism"), [Alawites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alawites "Alawites") and [Jacobites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Church "Syriac Orthodox Church").[^49]

After the Islamic conquest, Mediterranean trade declined for three centuries due to conflicts with the Byzantines. The ports of Tyre, Sidon, Beirut, and Tripoli struggled to recover, sustaining small populations under [Umayyad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") and [Abbasid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate") rule. Christians and Jews were often obligated to pay the *[jizya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jizya "Jizya")*, or poll tax levied on non-Muslims.[^48] During the 980s, the [Fatimid Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimid_Caliphate "Fatimid Caliphate") took control of the Levant, including Mount Lebanon, resulting in the rejuvenation of Mediterranean trade along the Lebanese coast through renewed connections with Byzantium and Italy. This resurgence saw Tripoli and Tyre flourishing well into the 11th century, focusing on exports such as textiles, sugar, and glassware.[^48]

During the 11th century, the [Druze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Druze_in_Lebanon "Druze in Lebanon") religion emerged from a branch of [Shia Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shia_Islam "Shia Islam"). The new religion gained followers in the southern portion of Mount Lebanon. The southern portion of Mount Lebanon was ruled by Druze feudal families till the early 14th century. The Maronite population increased gradually in Northern Mount Lebanon and the Druze have remained in Southern Mount Lebanon until the modern era. [Keserwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keserwan_District "Keserwan District"), [Jabal Amel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jabal_Amel "Jabal Amel") and the [Beqaa Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beqaa_Valley "Beqaa Valley") was ruled by Shia feudal families under the Mamluks and the Ottoman Empire. Major cities on the coast, [Sidon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidon "Sidon"), [Tyre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyre,_Lebanon "Tyre, Lebanon"), [Acre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acre,_Israel "Acre, Israel"), [Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripoli,_Lebanon "Tripoli, Lebanon"), [Beirut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beirut "Beirut"), and others, were directly administered by the Muslim Caliphs and the people became more fully absorbed by the Arab culture.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Siege_of_Tripoli_Painting_%281289%29.jpg/250px-Siege_of_Tripoli_Painting_%281289%29.jpg)

The Fall of Tripoli to the Egyptian Mamluks and destruction of the Crusader state, the County of Tripoli, 1289

Following the fall of Roman [Anatolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolia "Anatolia") to the Muslim Turks, the Byzantines put out a call to the Pope in Rome for assistance in the 11th century. The result was a series of wars known as the [Crusades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusades "Crusades") launched by the [Franks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankish_Empire "Frankish Empire") from Western Europe to reclaim the former Byzantine Christian territories in the Eastern Mediterranean, especially [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria") and [Palestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestine_\(region\) "Palestine (region)") (the *[Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant")*). The [First Crusade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Crusade "First Crusade") succeeded in temporarily establishing the [Kingdom of Jerusalem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Jerusalem "Kingdom of Jerusalem") and the [County of Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/County_of_Tripoli "County of Tripoli") as Roman Catholic Christian states along the coast.[^50] These crusader states made a lasting impact on the region, though their control was limited, and the region returned to full Muslim control after two centuries following the conquest by the Mamluks.

Among the most lasting effects of the Crusades in this region was the contact between the Franks (i.e., the French) and the Maronites. Unlike most other Christian communities in the [Eastern Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean "Eastern Mediterranean"), who swore allegiance to [Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantinople "Constantinople") or other local patriarchs, the Maronites proclaimed allegiance to the Pope in Rome. As such the Franks saw them as Roman Catholic brethren. These initial contacts led to centuries of support for the Maronites from France and Italy, even after the fall of the Crusader states in the region.

### Ottoman rule

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/DeirAlQamar-FakhredinePalace.jpg/250px-DeirAlQamar-FakhredinePalace.jpg)

Fakhreddine II Palace, 17th century

In 1516, Lebanon became part of the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"), with governance administered indirectly through local [emirs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emir "Emir").[^51] Lebanon's area was organized into provinces: Northern and Southern Mount Lebanon, Tripoli, Baalbek and Beqaa Valley, and [Jabal Amil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jabal_Amil "Jabal Amil").

In 1590, Druze tribal leader [Fakhr al-Din II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fakhr_al-Din_II "Fakhr al-Din II") succeeded [Korkmaz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Lebanon_Emirate#Qurqumaz_\(1544%E2%80%931585\) "Mount Lebanon Emirate") in southern Mount Lebanon and quickly asserted his authority as the paramount emir of the Druze in the Shouf region. Eventually, he was appointed [Sanjak-bey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanjak-bey "Sanjak-bey"), overseeing various Ottoman sub-provinces and tax collection. Expanding his influence extensively, he even constructed a fort in [Palmyra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palmyra_Castle "Palmyra Castle").[^52]

This expansion raised concerns for Ottoman Sultan Murad IV, leading to a punitive expedition in 1633. Fakhr al-Din II was captured, imprisoned for two years, and subsequently executed in April 1635, along with one of his sons.[^53] Surviving members of his family continued to govern a reduced area under closer Ottoman supervision until the late 17th century. On the death of the [last Maan emir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmad_Ma%27n "Ahmad Ma'n"), various members of the Shihab clan ruled Mount Lebanon until 1830.

While the history of Druze-Christian relations in Lebanon has generally been marked by harmony and peaceful coexistence,[^54] [^55] [^56] [^57] there were occasional periods of tension, notably during the [1860 Mount Lebanon civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1860_civil_conflict_in_Mount_Lebanon_and_Damascus "1860 civil conflict in Mount Lebanon and Damascus"), during which around 10,000 Christians were killed by the Druze.[^58] Shortly afterwards, the [Emirate of Mount Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Mount_Lebanon "Emirate of Mount Lebanon"), which lasted about 400 years, was replaced by the [Mount Lebanon Mutasarrifate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Lebanon_Mutasarrifate "Mount Lebanon Mutasarrifate"), as a result of a European-Ottoman treaty called the [Règlement Organique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%C3%A8glement_Organique_\(Mount_Lebanon\) "Règlement Organique (Mount Lebanon)"). The *Mount Lebanon Mutasarrifate* [^59] [^60] [^61] (1861–1918, [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"): متصرفية جبل لبنان; [Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_language "Turkish language"): *Cebel-i Lübnan Mutasarrıflığı*) was one of the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") 's subdivisions following the [Tanzimat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanzimat "Tanzimat") reform. After 1861 there existed an autonomous Mount Lebanon with a Christian [mutasarrıf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutasarr%C4%B1f "Mutasarrıf"), which had been created as a homeland for the [Maronites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Maronite_Christians "Lebanese Maronite Christians") under European diplomatic pressure following the 1860 massacres. The [Maronite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maronite "Maronite") [Catholics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic "Catholic") and the [Druze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Druze "Druze") founded modern Lebanon in the early eighteenth century, through the ruling and social system known as the " [Maronite-Druze dualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_and_Druze "Christianity and Druze") " in Mount Lebanon Mutasarrifate.[^62]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Carte_du_Liban_d%27apres_les_reconnaissances_de_la_Brigade_Topographique_du_Corps_Expeditionnaire_de_Syrie_en_1860-1861.jpg/250px-Carte_du_Liban_d%27apres_les_reconnaissances_de_la_Brigade_Topographique_du_Corps_Expeditionnaire_de_Syrie_en_1860-1861.jpg)

An 1862 map drawn by the French expedition of Beaufort d'Hautpoul, 63 later used as a template for the 1920 borders of Greater Lebanon. 64 65

The Baalbek and Beqaa Valley and Jabal Amel was ruled intermittently by various Shia feudal families, especially the [Al Ali Alsagheer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Assaad_Family "El Assaad Family") in Jabal Amel that remained in power until 1865 when Ottomans took direct ruling of the region. [Youssef Bey Karam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Youssef_Bey_Karam "Youssef Bey Karam"),[^66] a Lebanese nationalist played an influential role in Lebanon's independence during this era.

Lebanon experienced profound devastation in the [First World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") when the [Ottoman army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Army_\(1861%E2%80%931922\) "Ottoman Army (1861–1922)") assumed direct control, disrupting supplies and confiscating animals, ultimately leading to a severe famine.[^51] During the war, approximately 100,000 people in Beirut and Mount Lebanon died due to starvation.[^67]

### French Mandate

Amidst the height of the [First World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), the [Sykes–Picot Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sykes%E2%80%93Picot_Agreement "Sykes–Picot Agreement") of 1916, a secret pact between Britain and France, delineated Lebanon and its surrounding areas as regions open to potential French influence or control.[^51] After the [Allies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I "Allies of World War I") emerged victorious in the war, the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") ultimately collapsed, losing control over the area. Soon after the war, Patriarch [Elias Peter Hoayek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elias_Peter_Hoayek "Elias Peter Hoayek"), representing the Maronite Christians, successfully campaigned for an expanded territory at the 1919 [Paris Peace Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Peace_Conference_\(1919%E2%80%931920\) "Paris Peace Conference (1919–1920)"), also including areas with significant Muslim and Druze populations in addition to the Christian-dominated Mount Lebanon.[^51]

In 1920, King [Faisal I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faisal_I_of_Iraq "Faisal I of Iraq") proclaimed the [Arab Kingdom of Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Kingdom_of_Syria "Arab Kingdom of Syria") 's independence and asserted control over Lebanon. However, following a defeat to the French at the [Battle of Maysalun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Maysalun "Battle of Maysalun"), the kingdom was dissolved.[^51] Around the same time, at the [San Remo Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Remo_conference "San Remo conference"), tasked with deciding the fate of former Ottoman territories, it was determined that Syria and Lebanon would fall under French rule. Shortly afterward, the formal division of territories took place in the [Treaty of Sèvres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_S%C3%A8vres "Treaty of Sèvres"), signed a few months later.[^51]

On 1 September 1920, [Greater Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Lebanon "Greater Lebanon"), or *Grand Liban*, was established under French control as a [League of Nations Mandate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/League_of_Nations_mandate "League of Nations mandate"), following the terms outlined in the proposed [Mandate for Syria and the Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandate_for_Syria_and_the_Lebanon "Mandate for Syria and the Lebanon"). Greater Lebanon united the regions of Mount Lebanon, North Lebanon, South Lebanon, and the Bekaa, with Beirut as its designated capital.[^68] [^51] These specified boundaries later evolved into the present-day configuration of Lebanon. This arrangement was ratified in July 1922.[^51] The Lebanese Republic was officially proclaimed on 1 September 1926, with the [adoption of a constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Lebanon "Constitution of Lebanon") inspired by the French constitution on 23 May 1926. While a Lebanese government was established, the country continued to be under French control.[^51]

#### Pressure on German-occupied France

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/French_Mandate_for_Syria_and_the_Lebanon_map_en.svg/250px-French_Mandate_for_Syria_and_the_Lebanon_map_en.svg.png)

A map of the French Mandate and the states created in 1920

Lebanon gained a measure of independence while France was occupied by Germany.[^69] General [Henri Dentz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Dentz "Henri Dentz"), the [Vichy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vichy_France "Vichy France") [High commissioner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_commissioner "High commissioner") for Syria and Lebanon, played a major role in the independence of the nation. The Vichy authorities in 1941 allowed Germany to move aircraft and supplies through [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria") to [Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq "Iraq") where they were used against British forces. The United Kingdom, fearing that [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") would gain full control of Lebanon and [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria") by pressure on the weak Vichy government, [sent its army into Syria and Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria%E2%80%93Lebanon_campaign "Syria–Lebanon campaign").[^70]

After the fighting ended in Lebanon, General [Charles de Gaulle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle "Charles de Gaulle") visited the area. Under political pressure from both inside and outside Lebanon, de Gaulle recognized the independence of Lebanon. On 26 November 1941, General [Georges Catroux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Catroux "Georges Catroux") announced that Lebanon would become independent under the authority of the [Free French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_French "Free French") government. Elections were held in 1943 and on 8 November 1943 the new Lebanese government unilaterally abolished the mandate. The French reacted by imprisoning the new government. Lebanese nationalists declared a provisional government, and the British diplomatically intervened on their behalf. In the face of intense British pressure and protests by Lebanese nationalists, the French reluctantly released the government officials on 22 November 1943, and accepted the independence of Lebanon.[^71]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Beirut%27s_Martyrs%27_Square_during_celebrations_marking_the_release_by_the_French_of_Lebanon%27s_government_from_Rashayya_prison_on_November_22%2C_1943%2C_the_day_of_Lebanon%27s_independence._Adib_Ibrahim.jpg/250px-thumbnail.jpg)

Martyrs' Square in Beirut during celebrations marking the release by the French of Lebanon's government from Rashayya prison on 22 November 1943

### Independence from Free France

Following the end of World War II in Europe the French mandate may be said to have been terminated without any formal action on the part of the [League of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/League_of_Nations "League of Nations") or its successor the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"). The mandate was ended by the declaration of the mandatory power, and of the new states themselves, of their independence, followed by a process of piecemeal unconditional recognition by other powers, culminating in formal admission to the United Nations. Article 78 of the UN Charter ended the status of tutelage for any member state: "The trusteeship system shall not apply to territories which have become Members of the United Nations, relationship among which shall be based on respect for the principle of sovereign equality." [^72] So when the UN officially came into existence on 24 October 1945, after ratification of the [United Nations Charter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Charter "United Nations Charter") by the [five permanent members](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permanent_members_of_the_United_Nations_Security_Council "Permanent members of the United Nations Security Council"), as both Syria and Lebanon were founding member states, the French mandate for both was legally terminated on that date and full independence attained.[^73] The last French troops withdrew in December 1946.

Lebanon's unwritten [National Pact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Pact "National Pact") of 1943 required that its president be Maronite Christian, its [speaker of the parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speaker_\(politics\) "Speaker (politics)") to be a [Shia Muslim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Shia_Muslims "Lebanese Shia Muslims"), its prime minister be [Sunni Muslim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Sunni_Muslims "Lebanese Sunni Muslims"), and the Deputy Speaker of Parliament and the Deputy Prime Minister be [Greek Orthodox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Greek_Orthodox_Christians "Lebanese Greek Orthodox Christians").[^74]

Lebanon's history since independence has been marked by alternating periods of political stability and turmoil interspersed with prosperity built on [Beirut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beirut "Beirut") 's position as a regional center for finance and trade.[^75]

In May 1948, Lebanon supported neighboring Arab countries in a [war against Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1948_Arab%E2%80%93Israeli_War "1948 Arab–Israeli War"). While some irregular forces crossed the border and carried out minor skirmishes against Israel, it was without the support of the Lebanese government, and Lebanese troops did not officially invade.[^76] Lebanon agreed to support the forces with covering artillery fire, armored cars, volunteers and logistical support.[^77] On 5–6 June 1948, the Lebanese army – led by the then [Minister of National Defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_National_Defense_\(Lebanon\) "Ministry of National Defense (Lebanon)"), [Emir Majid Arslan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majid_Arslan "Majid Arslan") – captured [Al-Malkiyya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Malkiyya "Al-Malkiyya"). This was Lebanon's only success in the war.[^78]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/General_Fouad_Chehab.png/250px-General_Fouad_Chehab.png)

Under Fouad Chehab, Lebanon experienced economic growth and sectarian harmony

100,000 [Palestinians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinians_in_Lebanon "Palestinians in Lebanon") fled to Lebanon because of the war. Israel [did not permit their return](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_right_of_return "Palestinian right of return") after the cease-fire.[^79] As of 2017, between 174,000 and 450,000 Palestinian refugees live in Lebanon with about half in refugee camps (although these are often decades old and resemble neighborhoods).[^80] Often Palestinians are legally barred from owning property or performing certain occupations.[^81] According to [Human Rights Watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch "Human Rights Watch"), Palestinian refugees in Lebanon live in "appalling social and economic conditions."

In 1958, during the last months of President [Camille Chamoun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camille_Chamoun "Camille Chamoun") 's term, [an insurrection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1958_Lebanon_crisis "1958 Lebanon crisis") broke out, instigated by Lebanese Muslims who wanted to make Lebanon a member of the [United Arab Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Republic "United Arab Republic"). Chamoun requested assistance, and 5,000 [United States Marines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Marines "United States Marines") were briefly dispatched to Beirut on 15 July. After the crisis, a new government was formed, led by the popular former general [Fouad Chehab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fouad_Chehab "Fouad Chehab").

Until the early 1970s, Lebanon was dubbed "the Switzerland of the Middle East" for its unique status as both a snow-capped holiday destination and secure banking hub for [Gulf Arabs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Arab "Gulf Arab").[^82] Beirut was also nicknamed "the Paris of the Middle East." [^83]

### Civil War and occupation

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ac/Civil_war_Lebanon_map_1983a.gif)

Map showing power balance in Lebanon, 1983: controlled by Syria controlled by Christian groups controlled by Israel controlled by the UN

With the [defeat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_September_in_Jordan "Black September in Jordan") of the [Palestine Liberation Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PLO "PLO") in [Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan "Jordan"), many Palestinian militants relocated to Lebanon in 1970 and increased their [armed campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_insurgency_in_South_Lebanon "Palestinian insurgency in South Lebanon") against Israel. The relocation also led to sectarian tensions between Palestinians versus the Maronites and other Lebanese factions. In 1975, following increasing tensions, largely boosted by [Palestinian militant presence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatahland "Fatahland") in the south, a full-scale civil war broke out in Lebanon. The war pitted a coalition of Christian groups against the joint forces of the [PLO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_Liberation_Organization "Palestinian Liberation Organization"), left-wing Druze and Muslim militias. In October 1976 the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League") agreed to establish a predominantly Syrian [Arab Deterrent Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Deterrent_Force "Arab Deterrent Force"), which was charged with restoring calm.[^84]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Green_Line%2C_Beirut_1982.jpg/250px-Green_Line%2C_Beirut_1982.jpg)

The Green Line that separated west and east Beirut, 1982

PLO attacks from Lebanon into Israel in 1977 and 1978 escalated tensions between two countries. On 11 March 1978, Fatah militants in northern Israel, hijacked two passenger buses, that killed 37 Israelis before being killed in a firefight with Israeli forces.[^85] Israel [invaded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Litani "Operation Litani") Lebanon four days later and occupied most of the area south of the [Litani River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litani_River "Litani River"). The [UNSC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UN_Security_Council "UN Security Council") passed the [Resolution 425](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_425 "United Nations Security Council Resolution 425") calling for immediate Israeli withdrawal and creating the [U.N Interim Force in Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Interim_Force_in_Lebanon "United Nations Interim Force in Lebanon") (UNIFIL), charged with attempting to establish peace. Israeli forces withdrew later in 1978, but retained control of the southern region by managing a 19-kilometre-wide (12 mi) security zone along the border. These positions were held by the [South Lebanon Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Lebanon_Army "South Lebanon Army") (SLA), a Christian militia under the leadership of Major [Saad Haddad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saad_Haddad "Saad Haddad") backed by Israel. The Israeli Prime Minister [Menachem Begin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menachem_Begin "Menachem Begin"), compared the plight of the Christian minority in southern Lebanon (then about 5% of the population in SLA territory) to that of European Jews during World War II.[^86]

The PLO routinely [attacked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_insurgency_in_South_Lebanon "Palestinian insurgency in South Lebanon") Israel during the period of the cease-fire, with over 270 documented attacks.[^87] People in Galilee regularly had to leave their homes during these shellings. Documents captured in PLO headquarters after the invasion showed they had come from Lebanon.[^88] PLO leader [Yasser Arafat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yasser_Arafat "Yasser Arafat") refused to condemn these attacks on the grounds that the cease-fire was only relevant to Lebanon.[^89] In April 1980 the killing of two UNIFIL soldiers and the injuring of a third by the South Lebanon Army, near [At Tiri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/At_Tiri "At Tiri"), in the buffer zone led to a [military conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/At_Tiri_incident "At Tiri incident"). On 17 July 1981, Israeli aircraft bombed multi-story apartment buildings in Beirut that contained offices of PLO associated groups. The Lebanese delegate to the United Nations Security Council claimed that 300 civilians had been killed and 800 wounded. The bombing led to worldwide condemnation, and a temporary embargo on the export of U.S. aircraft to Israel.[^90]

In August 1981, defense minister [Ariel Sharon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ariel_Sharon "Ariel Sharon") began to draw up plans to attack PLO military infrastructure in West Beirut, where PLO headquarters and command bunkers were located.[^91] In 1982, [PLO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PLO_in_Lebanon "PLO in Lebanon") attacks from Lebanon on Israel led to [an Israeli invasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1982_Lebanon_War "1982 Lebanon War"), aiming to support Lebanese forces in driving out the PLO. A [multinational force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multinational_Force_in_Lebanon "Multinational Force in Lebanon") of American, French and Italian contingents (joined in 1983 by a British contingent) were deployed in Beirut after [the Israeli siege of the city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Beirut "Siege of Beirut"), to supervise the evacuation of the PLO. The civil war re-emerged in September 1982 after the assassination of President [Bachir Gemayel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bachir_Gemayel "Bachir Gemayel"), an Israeli ally, and subsequent fighting. During this time a number of sectarian massacres occurred, such as in [Sabra and Shatila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabra_and_Shatila_massacre "Sabra and Shatila massacre"), and in [several refugee camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Camps "War of the Camps").[^92] The multinational force was withdrawn in the spring of 1984, following [a devastating bombing attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1983_Beirut_barracks_bombings "1983 Beirut barracks bombings") during the previous year.

During the early 1980s, [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah "Hezbollah"), a Shiite Islamist militant group and political party, came into existence through the efforts of Shiite clerics who were [financially supported and trained by Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah%E2%80%93Iran_relations "Hezbollah–Iran relations"). Arising in the aftermath of the 1982 war and drawing inspiration from the [Islamic Revolution in Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iranian_Revolution "Iranian Revolution"), Hezbollah actively engaged in combat against Israel as well as [suicide attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suicide_attack "Suicide attack"), [car bombings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Car_bomb "Car bomb") and assassinations. Their objectives encompassed eliminating Israel, fighting for the Shia cause in the civil war, ending Western presence in Lebanon, and establishing a Shiite [Khomeinist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khomeinism "Khomeinism") [Islamic state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_state "Islamic state").[^93] [^48] [^94]

In the late 1980s, as [Amine Gemayel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine_Gemayel "Amine Gemayel") 's second term as president drew to an end, the Lebanese [pound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_pound "Lebanese pound") collapsed. At the end of 1987 US$1 was worth £L500. This meant the legal minimum wage was worth just $17 a month. Most goods in shops were priced in dollars. A [Save the Children](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Save_the_Children "Save the Children") director estimated that 200,000–300,000 children were in need of assistance and were living almost entirely on bread, which was subsidized by the government. Those who could relied on foreign assistance. Hezbollah was receiving about $3–5 million a month from [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran "Iran").[^95] In September 1988, the Parliament failed to elect a successor to President Gemayel as a result of differences between the Christians, Muslims, and Syrians. The Arab League Summit of May 1989 led to the formation of a Saudi–Moroccan–Algerian committee to solve the crisis.

On 16 September 1989 the committee issued a peace plan which was accepted by all. A ceasefire was established, the ports and airports were re-opened and refugees began to return.[^84] In the same month, the parliament agreed to the [Taif Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taif_Agreement "Taif Agreement"), which included an outline timetable for Syrian withdrawal from Lebanon and a formula for the de-confessionalization of the Lebanese political system.[^84] The civil war ended at the end of 1990 after 16 years; it had caused massive loss of human life and property and devastated the country's economy. It is estimated that 150,000 people were killed and another 200,000 wounded.[^96] Nearly a million civilians were displaced by the war, and some never returned.[^97] Parts of Lebanon were left in ruins.[^98] The agreement has still not been implemented in full and Lebanon's political system continues to be divided along sectarian lines.

Conflict between Israel and Lebanese militants continued, leading to a series of violent events and clashes including the [Qana massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qana_massacre "Qana massacre").[^99] [^100] [^101] [^102] In May 2000, Israeli forces fully withdrew from Lebanon.[^103] [^100] [^104] Since then, 25 May is regarded by the Lebanese as the [Liberation Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resistance_and_Liberation_Day_\(Lebanon\) "Resistance and Liberation Day (Lebanon)").[^105] [^106] [^100] The internal political situation in Lebanon significantly changed in the early 2000s. After the Israeli withdrawal from southern Lebanon and the death of former Syrian president [Hafez al-Assad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hafez_al-Assad "Hafez al-Assad") in 2000, the Syrian military presence faced criticism and resistance from the Lebanese population.[^107]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Cedar_Revolution_Demonstrators.jpg/250px-Cedar_Revolution_Demonstrators.jpg)

Demonstrations in Lebanon triggered by the assassination of Rafik Hariri on 14 February 2005

On 14 February 2005, former Prime Minister [Rafic Hariri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rafic_Hariri "Rafic Hariri") was assassinated in a [car bomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Car_bomb "Car bomb") explosion.[^108] Leaders of the [March 14 Alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_14_Alliance "March 14 Alliance") accused [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria") of the attack, while Syria and the [March 8 Alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_8_Alliance "March 8 Alliance") claimed that Israel was behind the assassination.[^109] It marked the beginning of a series of assassinations that resulted in the death of many prominent Lebanese figures and triggered the [demonstrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cedar_Revolution "Cedar Revolution") which demanded the withdrawal of the Syrian troops from Lebanon and the establishment of an international commission to investigate the assassination.[^6] Under the pressure from the West, Syria began withdrew from Lebanon by 26 April.[^110] [^111] [UNSC Resolution 1595](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_1595 "United Nations Security Council Resolution 1595") called for an investigation into the assassination.[^112] The [UN International Independent Investigation Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_International_Independent_Investigation_Commission "United Nations International Independent Investigation Commission") published preliminary findings on 20 October 2005 in the [Mehlis report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mehlis_report "Mehlis report"), which cited indications that the assassination was organized by Syrian and Lebanese intelligence services.[^113] [^114] [^115] [^116]

### Post-war revolution and spillover of the Syrian conflict

On 12 July 2006, [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah "Hezbollah") launched a series of rocket attacks and [raids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_Hezbollah_cross-border_raid "2006 Hezbollah cross-border raid") into Israeli territory, where they killed three Israeli soldiers and captured two others.[^117] Israel responded with [airstrikes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airstrike "Airstrike") and [artillery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artillery "Artillery") fire on targets in Lebanon, and a ground invasion of [southern Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Lebanon "Southern Lebanon"), resulting in the [2006 Lebanon War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_Lebanon_War "2006 Lebanon War"). The conflict was officially ended by the [UNSC Resolution 1701](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_1701 "United Nations Security Council Resolution 1701") on 14 August 2006, which ordered a ceasefire, the withdrawal of Israeli forces from Lebanon, and the disarmament of Hezbollah.[^118] [^119] Some 1,191 Lebanese [^120] and 160 Israelis [^121] were [killed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casualties_of_the_2006_Lebanon_War "Casualties of the 2006 Lebanon War") in the conflict. Beirut's southern suburb was heavily damaged by Israeli airstrikes.[^122]

In 2007, the [Nahr al-Bared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nahr_al-Bared_refugee_camp "Nahr al-Bared refugee camp") refugee camp became the center of the [2007 Lebanon conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2007_Lebanon_conflict "2007 Lebanon conflict") between the Lebanese Army and [Fatah al-Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatah_al-Islam "Fatah al-Islam"). At least 169 soldiers, 287 insurgents and 47 civilians were killed in the battle. Funds for the reconstruction of the area have been slow to materialize.[^123] Between 2006 and 2008, [a series of protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006%E2%80%932008_Lebanese_political_protests "2006–2008 Lebanese political protests") led by groups opposed to the pro-Western Prime Minister [Fouad Siniora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fouad_Siniora "Fouad Siniora") demanded the creation of a national unity government, over which the mostly Shia opposition groups would have veto power. When [Émile Lahoud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89mile_Lahoud "Émile Lahoud") 's presidential term ended in October 2007, the opposition refused to vote for a successor unless a power-sharing deal was reached, leaving Lebanon without a president.

On 7 May 2008, [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah "Hezbollah") and [Amal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amal_Movement "Amal Movement") forces, sparked by a government declaration that Hezbollah's communications network was illegal, seized western [Beirut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beirut "Beirut"),[^124] [^125] the most important Sunni center in Lebanon, leading to an [intrastate military conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Lebanon_conflict "2008 Lebanon conflict").[^126] [^127] The Lebanese government denounced the violence as a coup attempt.[^128] At least 62 people died in the resulting clashes between pro-government and opposition militias.[^129] On 21 May 2008, the signing of the [Doha Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doha_Agreement_\(2008\) "Doha Agreement (2008)") ended the fighting.[^124] [^129] As part of the accord, which ended 18 months of political paralysis,[^130] [Michel Suleiman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Suleiman "Michel Suleiman") became president and a national unity government was established, granting a veto to the opposition.[^124] The agreement was a victory for opposition forces, as the government caved in to all their main demands.[^129]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Shatila_-_street_view_%283%29.jpg/250px-Shatila_-_street_view_%283%29.jpg)

Over 20,000 Syrian and Palestinian refugees live in the Shatila refugee camp on the outskirts of Beirut.

In early January 2011, the [national unity government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_government_of_November_2009 "Lebanese government of November 2009") collapsed due to growing tensions stemming from the [Special Tribunal for Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_Tribunal_for_Lebanon "Special Tribunal for Lebanon"), which was expected to indict Hezbollah members for the Hariri assassination.[^131] The parliament elected [Najib Mikati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Najib_Mikati "Najib Mikati"), the candidate for the Hezbollah-led [March 8 Alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_8_Alliance "March 8 Alliance"), Prime Minister of Lebanon, making him responsible for forming a new government.[^132] Hezbollah leader [Hassan Nasrallah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassan_Nasrallah "Hassan Nasrallah") later accused Israel of assassinating Hariri.[^133] A report leaked by the [Al-Akhbar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Akhbar_\(Lebanon\) "Al Akhbar (Lebanon)") newspaper in November 2010 stated that Hezbollah had drafted plans for a violent takeover of the country in case the Special Tribunal for Lebanon issued an indictment against its members.[^134]

In 2012, the [Syrian civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syrian_civil_war "Syrian civil war") threatened to spill over in Lebanon, causing [incidents of sectarian violence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syrian_civil_war_spillover_in_Lebanon "Syrian civil war spillover in Lebanon") and armed clashes between [Sunnis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Sunni_Muslims "Lebanese Sunni Muslims") and [Alawites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alawites_in_Lebanon "Alawites in Lebanon") in Tripoli.[^135] According to [UNHCR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNHCR "UNHCR"), the number of [Syrian refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refugees_of_the_Syrian_civil_war "Refugees of the Syrian civil war") in Lebanon increased from around 250,000 in early 2013 to 1,000,000 in late 2014.[^136] In 2013, The [Lebanese Forces Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Forces_Party "Lebanese Forces Party"), the [Kataeb Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kataeb_Party "Kataeb Party") and the [Free Patriotic Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_Patriotic_Movement "Free Patriotic Movement") voiced concerns that the country's sectarian based political system is being undermined by the influx of Syrian refugees.[^137] On 6 May 2015, [UNHCR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNHCR "UNHCR") suspended registration of Syrian refugees at the request of the Lebanese government.[^138] In February 2016, the Lebanese government signed the Lebanon Compact, granting a minimum of €400 million of support for refugees and vulnerable Lebanese citizens.[^139] As of October 2016, the government estimated that the country hosts 1.5 million Syrians.[^140]

### National crisis (2019–present)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/-i---i-_%2849140926886%29.jpg/250px--i---i-_%2849140926886%29.jpg)

The 2019 protests were non-sectarian, crossing the Sunni–Shia Muslim / Christian sociological and religious divide.

On 17 October 2019, the first of a series of mass civil demonstrations erupted;[^141] [^142] [^143] they were initially triggered by planned taxes on gasoline, tobacco and online phone calls such as through [WhatsApp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WhatsApp "WhatsApp"),[^144] [^145] [^146] but quickly expanded into a country-wide condemnation of [sectarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sectarian "Sectarian") rule,[^147] a [stagnant economy and liquidity crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_liquidity_crisis "Lebanese liquidity crisis"), unemployment, endemic corruption in the public sector,[^147] legislation (such as banking secrecy) that is perceived to shield the ruling class from accountability [^148] [^149] and failures from the government to provide basic services such as electricity, water and sanitation.[^150]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/WomenLine_RiadElSolh_19Nov2019.jpg/250px-WomenLine_RiadElSolh_19Nov2019.jpg)

Women protesters forming a line between riot police and protesters in Riad el Solh, Beirut; 19 November 2019

As a result of the protests, Lebanon entered a political crisis, with Prime Minister [Saad Hariri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saad_Hariri "Saad Hariri") tendering his resignation and echoing protestors' demands for a government of independent specialists.[^151] Other politicians targeted by the protests have remained in power. On 19 December 2019, former Minister of Education [Hassan Diab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassan_Diab "Hassan Diab") was designated the next prime minister and tasked with forming a new cabinet.[^152] Protests and acts of [civil disobedience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_disobedience "Civil disobedience") continued, with protesters denouncing and condemning the designation of Diab as prime minister.[^153] [^154] [^155] By July 2020, Lebanon was suffering the worst [economic crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_liquidity_crisis "Lebanese liquidity crisis") in decades.[^156] [^157] Lebanon was the first country in the Middle East and North Africa to see its inflation rate exceed 50% for 30 consecutive days, according to Steve H. Hanke, professor of applied economics at the Johns Hopkins University.[^158]

On 4 August 2020, [an explosion at the port of Beirut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Beirut_explosion "2020 Beirut explosion"), Lebanon's main port, destroyed the surrounding areas, killing over 200 people, and injuring thousands more. The cause of the explosion was later determined to be 2,750 tonnes of ammonium nitrate that had been unsafely stored, and accidentally set on fire that Tuesday afternoon.[^159] Protests resumed within days following the explosion, which resulted in the resignation of Prime Minister Hassan Diab and his [cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Hassan_Diab "Cabinet of Hassan Diab") on 10 August 2020, nonetheless continuing to stay in office in a [caretaker capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caretaker_government "Caretaker government").[^160] Demonstrations continued into 2021 with Lebanese blocking the roads with burned tires protesting against the poverty and the economic crisis.

On 11 March 2021 the [caretaker minister of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Energy_and_Water_\(Lebanon\) "Ministry of Energy and Water (Lebanon)") [Raymond Ghajar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Ghajar "Raymond Ghajar") warned that Lebanon was threatened with "total darkness" at the end of March if no money was secured to buy fuel for power stations.[^161] In August 2021, a [large fuel explosion in northern Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Akkar_explosion "2021 Akkar explosion") killed 28 people.[^162] September saw the formation of a [new cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_Cabinet_of_Najib_Mikati "Third Cabinet of Najib Mikati") led by former prime minister [Najib Mikati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Najib_Mikati "Najib Mikati").[^163] On 9 October 2021, the entire nation lost power for 24 hours after its two main power stations ran out of power due to the currency and fuel shortage.[^164] Days later, [sectarian violence in Beirut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Beirut_clashes "2021 Beirut clashes") killed a number of people in the deadliest clashes in the country since 2008.[^165] By January 2022, [BBC News](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC_News "BBC News") reported that the crisis in Lebanon had deepened further, with the value of the [Lebanese pound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_pound "Lebanese pound") plummeting and a [scheduled general election expected to be delayed indefinitely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Lebanese_general_election "2022 Lebanese general election").[^166] The postponement of parliamentary elections was said to prolong the political deadlock in the country. The [European Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Parliament "European Parliament") called Lebanon's present situation a 'man-made disaster caused by a handful of men across the political class'.[^167]

In May 2022, Lebanon held its first [election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Lebanese_general_election "2022 Lebanese general election") since a painful economic crisis dragged it to the brink of becoming a [failed state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Failed_state "Failed state"). Lebanon's crisis has been so severe that more than 80 percent of the population is now considered poor by the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations").[^168] In the election the Shia Muslim [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah "Hezbollah") movement (and its allies) lost their parliamentary majority. Hezbollah did not lose any of its seats, but its allies lost seats. Hezbollah's ally, President [Michel Aoun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Aoun "Michel Aoun") 's [Free Patriotic Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_Patriotic_Movement "Free Patriotic Movement"), was no longer the biggest Christian party after the election. A rival Christian party, the [Lebanese Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Forces "Lebanese Forces") led by [Samir Geagea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samir_Geagea "Samir Geagea"), became the largest Christian-based party in parliament. The Sunni [Future Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Future_Movement "Future Movement"), led by former prime minister [Saad Hariri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saad_Hariri "Saad Hariri"), did not participate in the election, leaving a political vacuum for other Sunni politicians to fill.[^169] [^170] [^171] The Lebanese crisis became so severe that multiple boats left the coast holding migrants in a desperate run from the country. Many proved unsuccessful and fatal. In April 2022, 6 people died and around 50 people were rescued after an overloaded boat sunk in [Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripoli,_Lebanon "Tripoli, Lebanon").[^172] On 22 September, at least 94 people were killed when a boat carrying migrants from Lebanon capsized off Syria's coast. 9 people survived. Many were declared missing and some were found either dead or injured. Dead bodies were sent to nearby hospitals. 40 people remained missing as of 24 September.[^173]

On 1 February 2023, the [central bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banque_du_Liban "Banque du Liban") of Lebanon devalued the [Lebanese pound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_pound "Lebanese pound") by 90% amid the ongoing [financial crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_crisis "Financial crisis").[^174] This was the first time Lebanon had devalued its official exchange rate in 25 years.[^175] As of 2023, Lebanon is considered to have become a [failed state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Failed_state "Failed state"), suffering from chronic poverty, economic mismanagement and a banking collapse.[^176]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/2024_Bachoura_strike.jpg/250px-2024_Bachoura_strike.jpg)

Aftermath of the October 2024 Bachoura airstrike in central Beirut

The [Gaza war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_war "Gaza war") sparked a [renewed Israel–Hezbollah conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel%E2%80%93Hezbollah_conflict_\(2023%E2%80%93present\) "Israel–Hezbollah conflict (2023–present)").[^177] In 2024, Hezbollah said it would not stop attacking Israel until Israel ceases its attacks in [Gaza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_Strip "Gaza Strip").[^178] Starting with the Israeli [explosion of Lebanese pagers and walkie talkies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Lebanon_pager_explosions "2024 Lebanon pager explosions") in September 2024,[^179] the conflict escalated severely,[^180] with the [23 September 2024 Israeli airstrikes on Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/September_2024_Lebanon_strikes "September 2024 Lebanon strikes") killing at least 558 people,[^181] and sparking a mass exodus from southern Lebanon.[^182] On 27 September 2024, Hezbollah leader Hassan Nasrallah was [killed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Hezbollah_headquarters_strike "2024 Hezbollah headquarters strike") in an Israeli airstrike.[^183] On 1 October 2024, [Lebanon was invaded by Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Lebanon_war "2024 Lebanon war") with the objective of destroying infrastructure belonging to Hezbollah in the south of the country.[^184] In November 2024, a [ceasefire deal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Israel%E2%80%93Lebanon_ceasefire_agreement "2024 Israel–Lebanon ceasefire agreement") was signed between Israel and the Lebanese armed group Hezbollah to end 13 months of conflict. According to the agreement, Hezbollah was given 60 days to end its armed presence in southern Lebanon and Israeli forces were obliged to withdraw from the area over the same period.[^185]

[The fall of Assad's Baathist regime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_the_Assad_regime "Fall of the Assad regime") in Syria was another blow to its Lebanese ally, Hezbollah, which was already weakened because of Israeli military actions.[^186] The Syrian regime change in December 2024 was said to start a new chapter in Lebanese politics.[^187]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/8_April_2026_Isreali_attacks_on_Beirut_%282%29.jpg/250px-8_April_2026_Isreali_attacks_on_Beirut_%282%29.jpg)

Aftermath of the 8 April 2026 Israeli airstrikes on Lebanon, which killed 357 people.

In January 2025, [Joseph Aoun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Aoun "Joseph Aoun"), the Lebanese army commander, was elected Lebanese 14th [president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Lebanon "President of Lebanon") after a two-year vacancy.[^188] In February 2025, Prime Minister [Nawaf Salam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nawaf_Salam "Nawaf Salam"), former president of the [International Court of Justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice "International Court of Justice") (ICJ), formed a new government of 24 ministers after two-year caretaker cabinet.[^189] On 26 February 2025, Lebanon's government of Nawaf Salam won a confidence vote in parliament.[^190]

Since 2 March 2026, intensified [Israeli attacks across Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Lebanon_war "2026 Lebanon war") have killed over 1,000 people and displaced nearly 1 million,[^191] representing approximately 20% of the country's population.[^192] On 24 March, Israeli Defence Minister [Israel Katz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel_Katz "Israel Katz") announced plans to establish a "defensive buffer zone" by occupying southern Lebanon up to the [Litani River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litani_River "Litani River").[^193] This plan was expanded on 31 March, when Katz stated that residential structures in border villages would be demolished to prevent the return of approximately 600,000 displaced residents.[^194]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Qadisha_Valley%2C_Aerial_View_From_Qannoubine_Monastery.jpg/250px-Qadisha_Valley%2C_Aerial_View_From_Qannoubine_Monastery.jpg)

Kadisha Valley, a view from Qannoubine Monastery

Lebanon is located in West Asia between latitudes [33°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/33rd_parallel_north "33rd parallel north") and [35° N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/35th_parallel_north "35th parallel north") and longitudes [35°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/35th_meridian_east "35th meridian east") and [37° E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/37th_meridian_east "37th meridian east"). Its land straddles the "northwest of the [Arabian Plate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Plate "Arabian Plate") ".[^195] The country's surface area is 10,452 square kilometres (4,036 sq mi) of which 10,230 square kilometres (3,950 sq mi) is [land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_area "Land area"). Lebanon has a coastline and border of 225 kilometres (140 mi) on the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") to the west, a 375 kilometres (233 mi) border shared with [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria") to the north and east and a 79 kilometres (49 mi) long border with Israel to the south.[^196] The [border](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_Line_\(Lebanon\) "Blue Line (Lebanon)") with the Israeli-occupied [Golan Heights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golan_Heights "Golan Heights") is disputed by Lebanon in a small area called [Shebaa Farms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shebaa_Farms "Shebaa Farms").[^197]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Satellite_image_of_Lebanon_in_March_2002.jpg/250px-Satellite_image_of_Lebanon_in_March_2002.jpg)

Lebanon from space. Snow cover can be seen on the western Mount Lebanon and eastern Anti-Lebanon mountain ranges.

Lebanon is divided into four distinct [physiographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_geography "Physical geography") regions: the coastal plain, the [Lebanon mountain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanon_Mountains "Lebanon Mountains") range, the [Beqaa Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beqaa_Valley "Beqaa Valley") and the [Anti-Lebanon Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-Lebanon_Mountains "Anti-Lebanon Mountains"). The narrow and discontinuous [coastal plain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastal_plain "Coastal plain") stretches from the Syrian border in the north where it widens to form the [Akkar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akkar "Akkar") plain to [Ras al-Naqoura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naqoura "Naqoura") at the border with Israel in the south. The fertile coastal plain is formed of marine sediments and river deposited [alluvium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alluvium "Alluvium") alternating with sandy bays and rocky beaches. Lebanon's mountains rise steeply parallel to the Mediterranean coast and form a ridge of [limestone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limestone "Limestone") and [sandstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandstone "Sandstone") that runs for most of the country's length.

The mountain range varies in width between 10 km (6 mi) and 56 km (35 mi); it is carved by narrow and deep gorges. The Lebanon mountains peak at 3,088 metres (10,131 ft) [above sea level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level "Above mean sea level") in [Qurnat as Sawda'](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qurnat_as_Sawda%27 "Qurnat as Sawda'") in [North Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Governorate "North Governorate") and gradually slope to the south before rising again to a height of 2,695 metres (8,842 ft) in [Mount Sannine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Sannine "Mount Sannine"). The Beqaa valley sits between the Lebanon mountains in the west and the Anti-Lebanon range in the east; it is a part of the [Great Rift Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Rift_Valley "Great Rift Valley") system. The valley is 180 km (112 mi) long and 10 to 26 km (6 to 16 mi) wide, its fertile soil is formed by alluvial deposits. The Anti-Lebanon range runs parallel to the Lebanon mountains, its highest peak is in [Mount Hermon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Hermon "Mount Hermon") at 2,814 metres (9,232 ft).[^196]

The mountains of Lebanon are drained by [seasonal torrents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intermittent_stream "Intermittent stream") and [rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perennial_stream "Perennial stream") foremost of which is the 145 kilometres (90 mi) long [Leontes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litani_river "Litani river") that rises in the Beqaa Valley to the west of [Baalbek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baalbek "Baalbek") and empties into the Mediterranean Sea north of Tyre.[^196] Lebanon has [16 rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_rivers_of_Lebanon "List of rivers of Lebanon") all of which are [non-navigable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navigability "Navigability"); 13 rivers originate from [Mount Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Lebanon "Mount Lebanon") and run through the steep gorges and into the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea"), the other three arise in the [Beqaa Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beqaa_Valley "Beqaa Valley").[^198]

### Climate

Lebanon has a moderate [Mediterranean climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_climate "Mediterranean climate"). In coastal areas, winters are generally cool and rainy whilst summers are hot and humid. In more elevated areas, temperatures usually drop below freezing during the winter with heavy snow cover that remains until early summer on the higher mountaintops.[^196] [^199] Although most of Lebanon receives a relatively large amount of rainfall, when measured annually in comparison to its arid surroundings, certain areas in north-eastern Lebanon receives only little because of the [rain shadow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain_shadow "Rain shadow") created by the high peaks of the western mountain range.[^200] Storm Adam 2025 saw severe polar weather system affecting Lebanon with low temperatures and snow expected to fall at elevations as low as 300 meters above sea level.[^201]

### Environment

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Cedar_of_Lebanon_%28Cedar_of_God%29%2C_Lebanon.jpg/250px-Cedar_of_Lebanon_%28Cedar_of_God%29%2C_Lebanon.jpg)

The Lebanon cedar is the national emblem of Lebanon.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Pilgrimage_to_the_Cedars_of_Libanon.jpg/250px-Pilgrimage_to_the_Cedars_of_Libanon.jpg)

'Pilgrimage to the Cedars of Libanon' – painting by a Hungarian painter, Csontváry Kosztka Tivadar

In ancient times, Lebanon was covered by large forests of [cedar trees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cedrus_libani "Cedrus libani"), the national emblem of the country.[^202] Millennia of deforestation have altered the hydrology in Mount Lebanon and changed the regional climate adversely.[^203] As of 2012, forests covered 13.4% of the Lebanese land area;[^204] they are under constant threat from [wildfires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildfire "Wildfire") caused by the long dry summer season.[^205]

As a result of longstanding exploitation, few old cedar trees remain in pockets of forests in Lebanon, but there is an active program to conserve and regenerate the forests. The Lebanese approach has emphasized natural regeneration over planting by creating the right conditions for [germination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germination "Germination") and growth. The Lebanese state has created several nature reserves that contain cedars, including the [Shouf Biosphere Reserve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Shouf_Cedar_Nature_Reserve "Al Shouf Cedar Nature Reserve"), the Jaj Cedar Reserve, the [Tannourine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tannourine "Tannourine") Reserve, the Ammouaa and Karm Shbat Reserves in the Akkar district, and the Forest of the [Cedars of God](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cedars_of_God "Cedars of God") near [Bsharri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bsharri "Bsharri").[^206] [^207] [^208] Lebanon had a 2019 [Forest Landscape Integrity Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_Landscape_Integrity_Index "Forest Landscape Integrity Index") mean score of 3.76/10, ranking it 141st globally out of 172 countries.[^209]

In 2010, the Environment Ministry set a 10-year plan to increase the national forest coverage by 20%, which is equivalent to the planting of two million new trees each year.[^210] The plan, which was funded by the United States Agency for International Development ([USAID](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Agency_for_International_Development "United States Agency for International Development")), and implemented by the [U.S. Forest Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Forest_Service "United States Forest Service") (USFS), through the Lebanon Reforestation Initiative (LRI), was inaugurated in 2011 by planting cedar, pine, wild almond, juniper, fir, oak and other seedlings, in ten regions around Lebanon.[^210] As of 2016, forests covered 13.6% of Lebanon, and other wooded lands represented a further 11%.[^211] Since 2011, over 600,000 trees, including cedars and other native species, have been planted throughout the country as part of the Lebanon Reforestation Initiative (LRI).[^212]

Lebanon contains two terrestrial ecoregions: [Eastern Mediterranean conifer–sclerophyllous–broadleaf forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean_conifer%E2%80%93sclerophyllous%E2%80%93broadleaf_forests "Eastern Mediterranean conifer–sclerophyllous–broadleaf forests") and [Southern Anatolian montane conifer and deciduous forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Anatolian_montane_conifer_and_deciduous_forests "Southern Anatolian montane conifer and deciduous forests").[^213]

Beirut and Mount Lebanon have been facing a severe garbage crisis. After the closure of the Bourj Hammoud dump in 1997, the al-Naameh dumpsite was opened by the government in 1998. The al-Naameh dumpsite was planned to contain 2 million tons of waste for a limited period of six years at the most. It was designed to be a temporary solution, while the government would have devised a long-term plan. Sixteen years later al-Naameh was still open and exceeded its capacity by 13 million tons. In July 2015 the residents of the area, already protesting in the recent years, forced the closure of the dumpsite. The inefficiency of the government, as well as the corruption inside of the waste management company Sukleen in charge of managing the garbage in Lebanon, have resulted in piles of garbage blocking streets in Mount Lebanon and Beirut.[^214]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Bsharridistrictmountains.jpg/250px-Bsharridistrictmountains.jpg)

Mount Lebanon is a mountain range in Lebanon. It averages above 2,500 m (8,200 ft) in elevation.

In December 2015, the Lebanese government signed an agreement with Chinook Industrial Mining, part owned by [Chinook Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinook_Sciences "Chinook Sciences"), to export over 100,000 tons of untreated waste from [Beirut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beirut "Beirut") and the surrounding area. The waste had accumulated in temporary locations following the government closure of the county's largest land fill site five months earlier. The contract was jointly signed with Howa International which has offices in the Netherlands and Germany. The contract is reported to cost $212 per ton. The waste, which is compacted and infectious, would have to be sorted and was estimated to be enough to fill 2,000 containers.[^215] Initial reports that the waste was to be exported to [Sierra Leone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierra_Leone "Sierra Leone") have been denied by diplomats.[^216]

In February 2016, the government withdrew from negotiations after it was revealed that documents relating to the export of the trash to Russia were forgeries.[^217] On 19 March 2016, the Cabinet reopened the Naameh landfill for 60 days in line with a plan it passed a few days earlier to end the trash crisis. The plan also stipulates the establishment of landfills in [Bourj Hammoud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bourj_Hammoud "Bourj Hammoud") and Costa Brava, east and south of Beirut respectively. Sukleen trucks began removing piled garbage from Karantina and heading to Naameh. Environment Minister Mohammad Machnouk announced during a chat with activists that over 8,000 tons of garbage had been collected up to that point in only 24 hours as part of the government's trash plan. The plan's execution was ongoing at last report.[^218] In 2017, Human Rights Watch found that Lebanon's garbage crisis, and [open burning of waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_burning_of_waste "Open burning of waste") in particular, was posing a health risk to residents and violating the state's obligations under international law.[^219]

In September 2018, Lebanon's parliament passed a law that banned open dumping and burning of waste. Despite penalties set in case of violations, Lebanese municipalities have been openly burning the waste, putting the lives of people in danger. In October 2018, [Human Rights Watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch "Human Rights Watch") researchers witnessed the open burning of dumps in al-Qantara and [Qabrikha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qabrikha "Qabrikha").[^220] On Sunday 13 October 2019 at night, a series of about 100 [forest fires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanon_forest_fire_2019 "Lebanon forest fire 2019") according to [Lebanese Civil Defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_Defense "Civil Defense"), broke out and spread over large areas of Lebanon's forests. Lebanese Prime Minister [Saad Al-Hariri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saad_Hariri "Saad Hariri") confirmed his contact with a number of countries to send assistance via helicopters and firefighting planes,[^221] Cyprus, Jordan, Turkey and Greece participated in firefighting. According to press reports on Tuesday (15 October), fire has decreased in different places due to the rains.[^222] Lebanon's ongoing economic crisis has precipitated electricity shortages, prompting an increased reliance on diesel generators and subsequently contributing to environmental deterioration and health hazards. The scarcity of power has led to a heightened contamination of water sources. The compromised infrastructure, marked by sewage infiltrating drinking water, has given rise to significant health concerns, including an increase in cases of [Hepatitis A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hepatitis_A "Hepatitis A"). The health service, grappling with workforce shortages due to emigration, struggles amid a growing public health crisis.[^223]
