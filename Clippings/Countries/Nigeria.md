**Nigeria**,[^2] officially the **Federal Republic of Nigeria**, is a country in [West Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Africa "West Africa") between the [Sahel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahel "Sahel") to the north and the [Gulf of Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea "Gulf of Guinea") in the [Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") to the south. It covers an area of 923,769 square kilometres (356,669 mi <sup>2</sup>). With [a population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Nigeria "Demographics of Nigeria") of more than 242 million, it is the [most populous country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_African_countries_by_population "List of African countries by population") in [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa"), and the [world's sixth-most populous country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_population "List of countries and dependencies by population"). Nigeria borders [Niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger "Niger") in [the north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger%E2%80%93Nigeria_border "Niger–Nigeria border"), [Chad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chad "Chad") in [the northeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chad%E2%80%93Nigeria_border "Chad–Nigeria border"), [Cameroon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameroon "Cameroon") in [the east](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameroon%E2%80%93Nigeria_border "Cameroon–Nigeria border"), and [Benin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benin "Benin") in [the west](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benin%E2%80%93Nigeria_border "Benin–Nigeria border"). Nigeria is a [federal republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federation "Federation") comprising 36 [states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_of_Nigeria "States of Nigeria") and the [Federal Capital Territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Capital_Territory,_Nigeria "Federal Capital Territory, Nigeria"), where its capital, [Abuja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuja "Abuja"), is located. The [largest city in Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Nigerian_cities_by_population "List of Nigerian cities by population") by population is [Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos "Lagos"), one of the largest [metropolitan areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_largest_cities "List of largest cities") in the world and the [largest in Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Africa_by_population "List of cities in Africa by population").

Nigeria has been home to several indigenous material cultures, [pre-colonial states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_traditional_rulers "Nigerian traditional rulers") and [kingdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahelian_kingdoms "Sahelian kingdoms") since the second [millennium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millennium "Millennium") BC. The [Nok culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nok_culture "Nok culture"), c. 1500 BC, marks one of the earliest known civilisations in the region. The [Hausa Kingdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausa_Kingdoms "Hausa Kingdoms") inhabited the north, with the [Edo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edo_people "Edo people") [Kingdom of Benin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Benin "Kingdom of Benin") in the south, [Igbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igbo_people "Igbo people") [Kingdom of Nri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Nri "Kingdom of Nri") in the southeast and in the southwest, the [Yoruba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoruba_people "Yoruba people") [Empire of Oyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oyo_Empire "Oyo Empire"). The present day territory of Nigeria was home to a vast array of [city-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City-state "City-state"). In the early 19th century the migrating Fulani Jihadis culminated in the [Sokoto Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sokoto_Caliphate "Sokoto Caliphate"). The modern state originated with [British colonialization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_Nigeria "Colonial Nigeria") in the 19th century, taking its present territorial shape with the merging of the [Southern Nigeria Protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Nigeria_Protectorate "Southern Nigeria Protectorate") and the [Northern Nigeria Protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Nigeria_Protectorate "Northern Nigeria Protectorate") in 1914. The [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") set up administrative and legal structures and [incorporated traditional monarchs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Chieftaincy "Nigerian Chieftaincy") as a form of [indirect rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indirect_rule "Indirect rule").[^13] Nigeria became a formally [independent federation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federation_of_Nigeria "Federation of Nigeria") on 1 October 1960. It experienced a [civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Civil_War "Nigerian Civil War") from 1967 to 1970, followed by a succession of [military dictatorships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_military_juntas_of_1966%E2%80%9379_and_1983%E2%80%9399 "Nigerian military juntas of 1966–79 and 1983–99") and democratically elected civilian governments until achieving a stable government in the [1999 Nigerian presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1999_Nigerian_presidential_election "1999 Nigerian presidential election").

Nigeria is a [multinational state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multinational_state "Multinational state") inhabited by more than [250 ethnic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Nigeria "Demographics of Nigeria") speaking [500 distinct languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Languages_of_Nigeria "Languages of Nigeria"), all identifying with a wide variety of cultures.[^14] [^15] [^16] The three largest ethnic groups are the [Hausa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausa_people "Hausa people"), [Yoruba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoruba_people "Yoruba people"), and [Igbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igbo_people "Igbo people") together constituting over 60% of the total population.[^17] The [official language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Official_language "Official language") is [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_English "Nigerian English"), chosen to facilitate linguistic unity at the national level.[^18] [Nigeria's constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Nigeria "Constitution of Nigeria") ensures *[de jure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_jure "De jure")* [freedom of religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_religion "Freedom of religion"),[^19] and it is home to some of the world's largest Muslim and Christian populations.[^20] Nigeria is divided roughly in half between [Muslims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslims "Muslims"), who live mostly in the north part of the country, and [Christians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christians "Christians"), who live mostly in the south; [indigenous religions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_African_religions "Traditional African religions"), such as those native to the [Igbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odinani "Odinani") and [Yoruba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoruba_religion "Yoruba religion") ethnicities, are in the minority.[^21]

Nigeria is a [regional power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power") in Africa and a [middle power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_power "Middle power") in international affairs. [Nigeria's economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nigeria "Economy of Nigeria") is the [fourth-largest in Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_African_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of African countries by GDP (nominal)"), the [52nd-largest in the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of countries by GDP (nominal)") by nominal [GDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_domestic_product "Gross domestic product"), and [19th-largest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(PPP\) "List of countries by GDP (PPP)") by [PPP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity "Purchasing power parity"). Nigeria is often referred to as the Giant of Africa by its citizens due to its large population and [economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy "Economy"),[^22] and is considered to be an [emerging market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emerging_market "Emerging market") by the [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank"). Nigeria is a founding member of the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union") and a member of many international organisations, including the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), the [Commonwealth of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations"), [NAM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement"),[^23] the [Economic Community of West African States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_Community_of_West_African_States "Economic Community of West African States"), [Organisation of Islamic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation "Organisation of Islamic Cooperation") and [OPEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OPEC "OPEC"). It is one of the [Next Eleven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Next_Eleven "Next Eleven") economies, and also a member, along with [Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexico "Mexico"), [Indonesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesia "Indonesia"), and [Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey"), of the MINT group.

## Etymology

The name *[Nigeria](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Nigeria "wikt:Nigeria")* derives from the [Niger River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger_River "Niger River") running through the country. This name was coined on 8 January 1897, by the British journalist [Flora Shaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flora_Shaw,_Lady_Lugard "Flora Shaw, Lady Lugard"). The neighbouring [Republic of Niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Niger "Republic of Niger") takes its name from the same river. The origin of the name , which originally applied to only the middle reaches of the Niger River, is uncertain. The word is likely an alteration of the [Tuareg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuareg_people "Tuareg people") name *egerew n-iger ewen* ("river of rivers") used by inhabitants along the middle reaches of the river around [Timbuktu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timbuktu "Timbuktu") before 19th-century [European colonialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa "Scramble for Africa").[^24] [^25] Before Flora Shaw suggested the name *Nigeria*, other proposed names included *Royal Niger Company Territories*, *Central Sudan*, *Niger Empire*, *Niger Sudan*, and *Hausa Territories*.[^26]

## History

### Prehistory

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Nok_sculpture_Louvre_70-1998-11-1.jpg/250px-Nok_sculpture_Louvre_70-1998-11-1.jpg)

Nok sculpture, terracotta

[Kainji Dam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kainji_Dam "Kainji Dam") [excavations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeological_excavation "Archaeological excavation") showed ironworking by the 2nd century BC. The transition from [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") times to the [Iron Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age") was accomplished without intermediate [bronze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") production. Some have suggested the technology moved west from the [Nile Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile_Valley "Nile Valley"). But the [Iron Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_metallurgy_in_Africa "Iron metallurgy in Africa") in the [Niger River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger_River "Niger River") valley and the forest region appears to predate the introduction of metallurgy in the upper savanna by more than 800 years, as well as predating it in the Nile Valley. More recent research suggests that iron metallurgy was developed independently in [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa").[^27] [^28] [^29] [^30]

The [Nok civilisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nok_culture "Nok culture") thrived between 1500 BC and 200 AD.[^31] It produced life-sized [terracotta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terracotta "Terracotta") figures that are some of the earliest known sculptures in Africa [^32] [^33] [^34] [^35] [^36] and independently invented iron smelting by about 550 BC and possibly a few centuries earlier.[^27] [^28] [^29] Evidence of iron smelting has also been excavated at sites in the [Nsukka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nsukka "Nsukka") region of southeast Nigeria: dating to 2000 BC at the site of Lejja [^37] and to 750 BC at the site of [Opi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opi_\(archaeological_site\) "Opi (archaeological site)").

### Early history

The *[Kano Chronicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kano_Chronicle "Kano Chronicle")* highlights an ancient history dating to around 999 AD of the [Hausa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausa_Kingdoms "Hausa Kingdoms") city state of [Kano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kano "Kingdom of Kano"), with other major Hausa cities (or [Hausa Bakwai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausa_Bakwai "Hausa Bakwai")) of [Daura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daura "Daura"), [Biram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadejia "Hadejia"), [Katsina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Katsina "Kingdom of Katsina"), [Kingdom of Zazzau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Zazzau "Kingdom of Zazzau"), [Rano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rano "Rano"), and [Gobir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gobir "Gobir") all having recorded histories dating back to the 10th century.

The [Kingdom of Nri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Nri "Kingdom of Nri") of the Igbo people consolidated in the 10th century and continued until it lost its sovereignty to the British in 1911.[^38] [^39] Nri was ruled by the [Eze Nri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_rulers_of_Nri "List of rulers of Nri"), and the city of Nri is considered to be the foundation of [Igbo culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igbo_culture "Igbo culture"). Nri and [Aguleri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aguleri "Aguleri"), where the Igbo creation myth originates, are in the territory of the Umeuri clan. Members of the clan trace their lineages back to the patriarchal king-figure [Eri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eri_\(king\) "Eri (king)").[^40] In West Africa, the oldest bronzes made using the [lost wax process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lost_wax_process "Lost wax process") were from [Igbo-Ukwu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igbo-Ukwu "Igbo-Ukwu"), a city under Nri influence.[^38]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Queen_Mother_Pendant_Mask-_Iyoba_MET_DP231460.jpg/250px-Queen_Mother_Pendant_Mask-_Iyoba_MET_DP231460.jpg)

Royal Benin ivory mask, one of Nigeria's most recognised artifacts. Benin Empire, 16th century.

The [Yoruba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoruba_people "Yoruba people") had developed one of the earliest [city-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City-state "City-state") in Africa by the 8th century known as [Ile Ife](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If%E1%BA%B9 "Ifẹ"), which would become the heart of the later [Ife Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ife_Empire "Ife Empire") while [Oyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oyo_Empire "Oyo Empire") and other Yoruba states became more prominent in the 12th [^41] [^42] and 14th [^43] centuries, respectively. The oldest signs of [human](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human "Human") settlement at Ife's current site date back to the 9th century,[^41] and its material culture includes [Terracotta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terracotta "Terracotta") and Bronze figures.

### Pre-colonial era

In the 16th century, [Portuguese explorers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_discoveries "Portuguese discoveries") were the first [Europeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Europe "Ethnic groups in Europe") to begin important, direct trade with the peoples of southern Nigeria, at the port they named [Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos "Lagos") (formerly Eko) in [Yoruba land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yorubaland "Yorubaland") and in [Calabar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabar "Calabar") along the region [Slave Coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slave_Coast_of_West_Africa "Slave Coast of West Africa"). Europeans traded goods with peoples at the coast; coastal trade with Europeans also marked the beginnings of the [Atlantic slave trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade "Atlantic slave trade").[^44] The port of Calabar on the historical [Bight of Biafra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bight_of_Biafra "Bight of Biafra") (now commonly referred to as the Bight of Bonny) became one of the largest slave-trading posts in West Africa in this era. Other major slaving ports were located in [Badagry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Badagry "Badagry"), Lagos on the [Bight of Benin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bight_of_Benin "Bight of Benin"), and [Bonny Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonny_Island "Bonny Island") on the Bight of Biafra.[^44] [^45] The majority of those taken to these ports were captured in raids and wars.[^46] Usually, the captives were taken back to the conquerors' territory as [forced labour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forced_labour "Forced labour"); they were sometimes gradually acculturated and absorbed into the conquerors' society. Slave routes were established throughout Nigeria linking the hinterland areas with the major coastal ports. Several kingdoms that directly or indirectly participated in the Atlantic slave trade were associated with the [Benin Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Benin "Kingdom of Benin") in the south, [Oyo Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oyo_Empire "Oyo Empire") in the southwest, and the [Aro Confederacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aro_Confederacy "Aro Confederacy") in the southeast. In contrast, the kingdoms associated with the [Trans-Saharan slave trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Saharan_slave_trade "Trans-Saharan slave trade") were linked to the [Hausa states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausa_Kingdoms "Hausa Kingdoms"), as well as the [Kanuri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanuri_people "Kanuri people") -led [Kanem-Bornu Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanem-Bornu_Empire "Kanem-Bornu Empire").[^47] [^48] [^49] [^44] [^45] Benin's power lasted between the 15th and 19th centuries.[^50] Oyo, at its territorial zenith in the late 17th to early 18th centuries, extended its influence from western Nigeria to modern-day [Togo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Togo "Togo").

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Ancient_Benin_city.JPG/250px-Ancient_Benin_city.JPG)

Depiction of Benin City by a Dutch illustrator in 1668. The wall-like structure in the centre probably represents the walls of Benin, housing the Benin bronze decorated historic Benin City Palace.

In the north, the incessant fighting amongst the Hausa city-states and the decline of the [Bornu Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanem%E2%80%93Bornu_Empire "Kanem–Bornu Empire") allowed the Fulani people to gain headway into the region. Until this point, the Fulani, a [nomadic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomad "Nomad") foreign ethnic group, primarily traversed the semi-desert region with cattle. They largely avoided engaging in [trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade "Trade"), [agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture"), [state-building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State-building "State-building"), and other activities common among other West African peoples. At the beginning of the 19th century, [Usman dan Fodio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usman_dan_Fodio "Usman dan Fodio") led [a successful jihad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fulani_War "Fulani War") against the [Hausa Kingdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausa_Kingdoms "Hausa Kingdoms"), He accused the Hausa of not being true [Muslims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslims "Muslims") and subsequently established the [Sokoto Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sokoto_Caliphate "Sokoto Caliphate") [^51] [^52]. The Sokoto Caliphate relied heavily on [slavery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery "Slavery"), with large-scale plantation slavery, concubinage, and domestic servitude. While the primary source of new slaves were the newly conquered Hausa peoples, raids on non-Muslim communities and other ethnic groups accused of not being true Muslims persisted.[^52] [^53] The caliphate as a whole became one of the largest slave societies in 19th-century Africa. This empire, grew rapidly under his rule and that of [his descendants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Sultans_of_Sokoto "List of Sultans of Sokoto"), who sent out [invading armies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fula_jihads "Fula jihads") in every direction. The vast landlocked Sokoto Caliphate sought to link the eastern and western Sudan regions while attempting to push aggressively southward into the [Yoruba country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yorubaland "Yorubaland"). It conquered parts of the old [Oyo Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oyo_Empire "Oyo Empire") (modern-day [Kwara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kwara_State "Kwara State")), and attempted to penetrate towards deeper into the heart of Yoruba land. However, the Yoruba forces of [Ibadan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibadan "Ibadan") delivered a decisive defeat to the invaders at the [Battle of Osogbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_%C3%92sogbo "Battle of Òsogbo"), halting their jihad and successfully protecting the sovereign integrity of Yoruba territory. The sultan sent out emirs to establish [suzerainty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suzerainty "Suzerainty") over the previous conquered territories and promote Islamic civilisation; the emirs in turn became increasingly rich and powerful through trade and slavery. By the 1890s, the largest [slave population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery_in_Africa "Slavery in Africa"), about two million, was concentrated in the territories of the Sokoto [Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caliphate "Caliphate"). The use of slave labour was extensive, especially in agriculture.[^54] By the time of its break-up in 1903 into various European colonies, the Sokoto Caliphate was one of the largest pre-colonial African states.[^55] [^56]<sup><span title="Page: 136">: 136</span></sup>

A changing legal imperative (the outlawing of the [Atlantic slave trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade "Atlantic slave trade") in 1807) and economic imperative (a desire for political and social stability) led most European powers to support the widespread cultivation of agricultural products, such as the palm, for use in European industry. The slave trade continued after the ban, as illegal [smugglers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smugglers "Smugglers") purchased slaves along the coast from native slavers. Britain's [West Africa Squadron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Africa_Squadron "West Africa Squadron") sought to intercept the smugglers at sea. The [rescued slaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saro_people "Saro people") were taken to [Freetown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freetown "Freetown"), a colony in West Africa originally established for the resettlement of slaves freed by Britain.[^57]

### British colonisation and independence

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Flag_of_Lagos_Colony_%281888%E2%80%931906%29.svg/250px-Flag_of_Lagos_Colony_%281888%E2%80%931906%29.svg.png)

Flag of the Lagos Colony

Britain intervened in the Lagos kingship power struggle by bombarding Lagos in 1851, deposing the slave-trade-friendly [Oba Kosoko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kosoko "Kosoko"), helping to install the amenable Oba [Akitoye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akitoye "Akitoye") and signing the [Treaty between Great Britain and Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_Between_Great_Britain_and_Lagos,_1_January_1852 "Treaty Between Great Britain and Lagos, 1 January 1852") on 1 January 1852. [Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") annexed Lagos as a [crown colony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crown_colony "Crown colony") in August 1861 with the [Lagos Treaty of Cession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos_Treaty_of_Cession "Lagos Treaty of Cession"). British missionaries expanded their operations and travelled further inland. In 1864, [Samuel Ajayi Crowther](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Ajayi_Crowther "Samuel Ajayi Crowther") became the first African [bishop of the Anglican Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_of_Nigeria "Church of Nigeria").[^58] In 1885, British claims to a West African [sphere of influence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence "Sphere of influence") received recognition from other European nations at the [Berlin Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Conference "Berlin Conference"). The following year, it chartered the [Royal Niger Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Niger_Company "Royal Niger Company") under the leadership of Sir [George Taubman Goldie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Taubman_Goldie "George Taubman Goldie"). By the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the company had vastly succeeded in subjugating the independent southern kingdoms along the [Niger River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger_River "Niger River"), the British conquered [Benin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benin_Empire "Benin Empire") in 1897, and, in the [Anglo-Aro War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Aro_War "Anglo-Aro War") (1901–1902), defeated other opponents. The defeat of these states opened up the Niger area to British rule. In 1900, the company's territory came under the direct control of the British government and established the [Southern Nigeria Protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Nigeria_Protectorate "Southern Nigeria Protectorate") as a British [protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protectorate "Protectorate") and part of the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire").

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Tcitp_d012_frederick_john_dealtry_lugard_and_wife.jpg/250px-Tcitp_d012_frederick_john_dealtry_lugard_and_wife.jpg)

The Lord and Lady Lugard, 1908

By 1902, [the British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_British "The British") had begun plans to move north into the Sokoto Caliphate. British General Lord [Frederick Lugard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_Lugard,_1st_Baron_Lugard "Frederick Lugard, 1st Baron Lugard") was tasked by the [Colonial Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_Office "Colonial Office") to implement the agenda. Lugard used rivalries between many of the emirs in the southern reach of the caliphate and the central Sokoto administration to prevent any defence as he worked towards the capital. As the British approached the city of [Sokoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sokoto_\(city\) "Sokoto (city)"), Sultan [Muhammadu Attahiru I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammadu_Attahiru_I "Muhammadu Attahiru I") organised a quick defence of the city and fought the advancing British-led forces. The British force quickly won, sending Attahiru I and thousands of followers on a [Mahdist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahdi "Mahdi") *[hijra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hegira "Hegira")*. In the northeast, the decline of the [Bornu Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanem%E2%80%93Bornu_Empire "Kanem–Bornu Empire") gave rise to the British-controlled [Borno Emirate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borno_Emirate "Borno Emirate"), which established [Abubakar Garbai of Borno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abubakar_Garbai_of_Borno "Abubakar Garbai of Borno") as ruler.[^59]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Emir_of_Kano-1911.jpg/250px-Emir_of_Kano-1911.jpg)

Emir of Kano with cavalry, 1911

In 1903, the British victory in the [Battle of Kano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Kano_\(1903\) "Battle of Kano (1903)") gave them a logistical edge in [pacifying the heartland of the Sokoto Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Kwatarkwashi "Battle of Kwatarkwashi") and parts of the former Bornu Empire. On 13 March 1903, at the grand market square of Sokoto, the last [vizier of the caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sokoto_Grand_Vizier "Sokoto Grand Vizier") officially conceded to British rule. The British appointed [Muhammadu Attahiru II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammadu_Attahiru_II "Muhammadu Attahiru II") as the new caliph. Lugard abolished the caliphate but retained the title *sultan* as a symbolic position in the newly organised [Northern Nigeria Protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Nigeria_Protectorate "Northern Nigeria Protectorate"). This remnant became known as " [Sokoto Sultanate Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sokoto_Sultanate_Council "Sokoto Sultanate Council") ". In June 1903, the British defeated the remaining northern forces of Attahiru. The caliphate forces in the south continued to resist the British and Germans in the [Adamawa Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamawa_Wars "Adamawa Wars"). By 1907, resistance to European rule in the area had been quelled.[^60]

On 1 January 1914, the British formally united the [Southern Nigeria Protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Nigeria_Protectorate "Southern Nigeria Protectorate") and the [Northern Nigeria Protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Nigeria_Protectorate "Northern Nigeria Protectorate") into the [Colony and Protectorate of Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_Nigeria "Colonial Nigeria"). Administratively, Nigeria remained divided into the Northern and Southern Protectorates and [Lagos Colony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos_Colony "Lagos Colony"). Inhabitants of the southern region sustained more interaction, economic and cultural, with the British and other Europeans owing to the coastal economy.[^61] Christian missions established Western educational institutions in the protectorates. Under Britain's policy of indirect rule and validation of Islamic [legitimist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legitimists "Legitimists") tradition, the Crown did not encourage the operation of Christian missions in the northern, Islamic part of the country.[^62]

By the mid-20th century following [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), a [wave for independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decolonisation_of_Africa "Decolonisation of Africa") was sweeping across Africa, in response to the growth of [Nigerian nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_nationalism "Nigerian nationalism") and demands for independence, successive constitutions legislated by the British government moved Nigeria toward self-government on a representative and increasingly federal basis. By the eve of independence in 1960, regional differences in modern educational access were marked. The legacy, though less pronounced, continues to the present day. The balance between north and south was also expressed in Nigeria's political life. For instance, northern Nigeria did not outlaw slavery until 1936 whilst in other parts of Nigeria, slavery was abolished soon after colonialism.[^63] [^45]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Stamp_Cameroons_2d-600px.jpg/250px-Stamp_Cameroons_2d-600px.jpg)

1953 postage stamp with portrait of Queen Elizabeth II

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bb/Nnamdi_Azikiwe_PC_%28cropped%29.jpg)

Nnamdi Azikiwe, first President of Nigeria (1963–1966)

Nigeria gained a degree of self-rule in 1954, and full independence from the United Kingdom on [1 October 1960](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_Day_\(Nigeria\) "Independence Day (Nigeria)"), as the [Federation of Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federation_of_Nigeria "Federation of Nigeria") with [Abubakar Tafawa Balewa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abubakar_Tafawa_Balewa "Abubakar Tafawa Balewa") as its [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Nigeria "Prime Minister of Nigeria"), while retaining the [British monarch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom "Monarchy of the United Kingdom"), [Elizabeth II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elizabeth_II "Elizabeth II"), as nominal head of state and [Queen of Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queen_of_Nigeria "Queen of Nigeria"). [Nnamdi Azikiwe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nnamdi_Azikiwe "Nnamdi Azikiwe") replaced the colonial [governor-general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governor-general "Governor-general") in November 1960. At independence, the cultural and political differences were sharp among Nigeria's dominant ethnic groups: the Hausa in the north, Igbo in the east, and Yoruba in the west.[^64] The [Westminster system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westminster_system "Westminster system") of government was retained, and thus the [President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Nigeria "President of Nigeria") 's powers were generally ceremonial.[^65] The parliamentary system of government had Abubakar Tafawa Balewa as Prime Minister and Nnamdi Azikiwe as the ceremonial president. The founding government was a coalition of conservative parties: the [Northern People's Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_People%27s_Congress "Northern People's Congress") led by Sir [Ahmadu Bello](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmadu_Bello "Ahmadu Bello"), a party dominated by Muslim northerners, and the Igbo and Christian-dominated [National Council of Nigeria and the Cameroons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_of_Nigeria_and_the_Cameroons "National Council of Nigeria and the Cameroons") led by Nnamdi Azikiwe. The opposition consisted of the comparatively liberal [Action Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_Group_\(Nigeria\) "Action Group (Nigeria)"), which was largely dominated by the [Yoruba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoruba_people "Yoruba people") and led by [Obafemi Awolowo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obafemi_Awolowo "Obafemi Awolowo"). An imbalance was created in the polity as a result of the [1961 plebiscite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1961_British_Cameroons_referendum "1961 British Cameroons referendum"). [Southern Cameroons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Cameroons "Southern Cameroons") opted to join the [Republic of Cameroon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameroon "Cameroon") while [Northern Cameroons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Cameroon "British Cameroon") chose to join Nigeria. The northern part of the country became larger than the southern part.

![Abubakar Tafawa Balewa](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Abubakar_Tafawa_Balewa_%281962%29.jpg/250px-Abubakar_Tafawa_Balewa_%281962%29.jpg)

Abubakar Tafawa Balewa, Nigeria's first Prime Minister during the 1st Republic

### Early republic and civil war

At Nigeria's independence in 1960, the [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") colonial authorities, distrustful of the [Yoruba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoruba_people "Yoruba people") and [Igbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igbo_people "Igbo people") peoples, handed disproportionate power to the Northern elites [^66] [^67] [^68] [^69] [^70]. In large this was due to the fact that the British had enjoyed far greater cooperation through indirect rule with the Hausa-Fulani aristocracy, who had been far more submissive and offered significantly less resistance than the leaders of the Yoruba and Igbo peoples collectively [^66] [^67] [^68] [^69] [^70]. The [Northern Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Region,_Nigeria "Northern Region, Nigeria") gained more seats in parliament than the southerner [Eastern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Region,_Nigeria "Eastern Region, Nigeria") and [Western](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Region_of_Nigeria "Western Region of Nigeria") regions combined—this would cement Northern dominance in Nigerian politics for years to come. Resentment among southern politicians precipitated into political chaos in the country. [Obafemi Awolowo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obafemi_Awolowo "Obafemi Awolowo"), Premier of Western Region, was accused of attempting to overthrow the government. This followed a period of conflict between the AG regional government and the central government. In spite of the flimsiness of the evidence presented by the government's prosecutors, he was convicted. With incarceration of Awolowo, [Samuel Akintola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Akintola "Samuel Akintola") took over as the premier of Western Region. Because Akintola was an ally of Ahmadu Bello, the undisputed strong man of Nigeria, Akintola was criticised as being a tool of the North.[^71] As premier of the West, Akintola presided over the most chaotic era in Western Region—one which earned it the nickname " [the Wild-Wild West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wild_West "Wild West") ". However, as late as Thursday, 13 January 1966, Balewa had announced that the federal government was not going to intervene in the West.[^71] However, the very next day, Akintola, premier of the West met with his ally [Ahmadu Bello](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmadu_Bello "Ahmadu Bello"), the [Sardauna of Sokoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sardauna_of_Sokoto "Sardauna of Sokoto"), premier of the North and party boss of NPC party to which Balewa belonged.[^71] At the same time a top-level security conference in Lagos was taking place which was attended by most of the country's senior army officiers. All of this activity created rumours that the Balewa government would be forced to crack down on lawlessness in the West using military might.[^71]

The disequilibrium and perceived corruption of the electoral and political process led to two [military coups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "Coup d'état") in 1966. The [first coup was in January 1966](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1966_Nigerian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1966 Nigerian coup d'état") and was led mostly by soldiers under Majors [Emmanuel Ifeajuna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Ifeajuna "Emmanuel Ifeajuna") (of the [Igbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igbo_people "Igbo people") tribe), [Chukwuma Kaduna Nzeogwu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chukwuma_Kaduna_Nzeogwu "Chukwuma Kaduna Nzeogwu") ([Northerner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Region,_Nigeria "Northern Region, Nigeria") of [Eastern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Region,_Nigeria "Eastern Region, Nigeria") extraction) and [Adewale Ademoyega](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adewale_Ademoyega "Adewale Ademoyega") (a Yoruba from the West). The coup plotters succeeded in assassinating Sir [Ahmadu Bello](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmadu_Bello "Ahmadu Bello") and Sir [Abubakar Tafawa Balewa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abubakar_Tafawa_Balewa "Abubakar Tafawa Balewa") alongside prominent leaders of the Northern Region and Premier [Samuel Akintola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ladoke_Akintola "Ladoke Akintola") of the [Western Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_State_\(Nigeria\) "Western State (Nigeria)"), but the plotters struggled to form a central government. Senate President [Nwafor Orizu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nwafor_Orizu "Nwafor Orizu") handed over government control to the [Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Army "Nigerian Army"), under the command of another Igbo officer, Major General [^72] [Johnson Aguiyi-Ironsi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson_Aguiyi-Ironsi "Johnson Aguiyi-Ironsi"). Later, the [counter-coup of 1966](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1966_Nigerian_counter-coup "1966 Nigerian counter-coup"), supported primarily by Northern military officers, facilitated the rise of [Yakubu Gowon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yakubu_Gowon "Yakubu Gowon") as military head of state. Tension rose between north and south; [Igbos in northern cities suffered persecution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1966_anti-Igbo_pogrom "1966 anti-Igbo pogrom") and many fled to the Eastern Region.[^73]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Biafra_independent_state_map-en.svg/250px-Biafra_independent_state_map-en.svg.png)

The Republic of Biafra in June 1967, when it declared its independence from the rest of Nigeria

In May 1967, Governor of the [Eastern Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Region,_Nigeria "Eastern Region, Nigeria") Lt. Colonel [Emeka Ojukwu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C._Odumegwu_Ojukwu "C. Odumegwu Ojukwu") declared the region independent from the federation as a state called the [Republic of Biafra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biafra "Biafra") as a result of the continuous and systematically planned attacks against Igbos and those of Eastern extraction, popularly known as the [1966 pogroms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1966_anti-Igbo_pogrom "1966 anti-Igbo pogrom").[^74] [^75] This declaration precipitated the [Nigerian Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Civil_War "Nigerian Civil War") which began as the official Nigerian government side attacked Biafra on 6 July 1967, at Garkem. The 30-month war, with a long [blockade of Biafra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blockade_of_Biafra "Blockade of Biafra") and its isolation from trade and international relief, ended in January 1970.[^76] Estimates of the number of dead in the former Eastern Region during the 30-month civil war range from one to three million.[^77] The UK and [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") were the main military backers of the Nigerian government, with Nigeria utilising air support from Egyptian pilots provided by [Gamal Abdel Nasser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamal_Abdel_Nasser "Gamal Abdel Nasser"),[^78] [^79] while [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France") and [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel") aided the Biafrans. The [Congolese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Democratic Republic of the Congo") government under President [Joseph-Désiré Mobutu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobutu_Sese_Seko "Mobutu Sese Seko") took an early stand on the Biafran secession, voicing strong support for the Nigerian federal government [^80] and deploying thousands of troops to fight against the [secessionists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separatist_movements_of_Nigeria "Separatist movements of Nigeria").[^81] [^82]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Ondervoede_kinderen%2C_Bestanddeelnr_921-5788_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Ondervoede_kinderen%2C_Bestanddeelnr_921-5788_%28cropped%29.jpg)

The blockade of Biafra during the Nigerian Civil War (1967–1970) resulted in a famine that ultimately cost at least a million lives.

Following the war, Nigeria enjoyed an [oil boom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_boom "Oil boom") in the 1970s, during which the country joined [OPEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OPEC "OPEC") and received huge oil revenues. Despite these revenues, the military government did little to improve the [standard of living](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_of_living "Standard of living"), help small and medium businesses, or invest in infrastructure. As oil revenues fuelled the rise of federal subsidies to states, the federal government became the centre of political struggle and the threshold of power in the country. As oil production and revenue rose, the Nigerian government became increasingly dependent on oil revenues and international commodity markets for budgetary and economic concerns.[^83] The [coup in July 1975](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1975_Nigerian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1975 Nigerian coup d'état"), led by Generals [Shehu Musa Yar'Adua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shehu_Musa_Yar%27Adua "Shehu Musa Yar'Adua") and [Joseph Garba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Nanven_Garba "Joseph Nanven Garba"), ousted Gowon,[^84] who fled to Britain.[^85] The coup plotters wanted to replace Gowon's autocratic rule with a triumvirate of three brigadier generals whose decisions could be vetoed by a [Supreme Military Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Military_Council_of_Nigeria_\(1966%E2%80%931979\) "Supreme Military Council of Nigeria (1966–1979)"). For this triumvirate, they convinced General [Murtala Muhammed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murtala_Muhammed "Murtala Muhammed") to become military head of state, with General [Olusegun Obasanjo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olusegun_Obasanjo "Olusegun Obasanjo") as his second-in-command, and General [Theophilus Danjuma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theophilus_Danjuma "Theophilus Danjuma") as the third.[^86] Together, the triumvirate introduced austerity measures to stem inflation, established a Corrupt Practices Investigation Bureau, replaced all military governors with new officers, and launched "Operation Deadwood" through which they fired 11,000 officials from the civil service.[^87]

Colonel [Buka Suka Dimka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buka_Suka_Dimka "Buka Suka Dimka") launched a [February 1976 coup attempt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1976_Nigerian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_attempt "1976 Nigerian coup d'état attempt"),[^88] during which General Murtala Muhammed was assassinated. Dimka lacked widespread support among the military, and his coup failed, forcing him to flee.[^89] After the coup attempt, General Olusegun Obasanjo was appointed military head of state.[^90] Obasanjo vowed to continue Murtala's policies.[^91] Aware of the danger of alienating northern Nigerians, Obasanjo brought General Shehu Yar'Adua as his replacement and second-in-command as [Chief of Staff, Supreme Headquarters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vice_President_of_Nigeria "Vice President of Nigeria") completing the military triumvirate, with Obasanjo as head of state and General Theophilus Danjuma as [Chief of Army Staff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chief_of_Army_Staff_\(Nigeria\) "Chief of Army Staff (Nigeria)"), the three went on to re-establish control over the [military regime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_military_juntas_of_1966%E2%80%9379_and_1983%E2%80%9399 "Nigerian military juntas of 1966–79 and 1983–99") and organised the military's transfer of power programme: [states creation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_of_Nigeria "States of Nigeria") and [national delimitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_boundary_delimitation "Electoral boundary delimitation"), [local government reforms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_government_areas_of_Nigeria "Local government areas of Nigeria") and the [constitutional drafting committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Nigeria#1979_constitution_\(Second_Republic\) "Constitution of Nigeria") for a new republic.[^92]

### Military dictatorship and coups

The military carefully planned the return to civilian rule putting in place measures to ensure that political parties had broader support than witnessed during the first republic. In 1979, five political parties competed in a series of elections in which Alhaji [Shehu Shagari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shehu_Shagari "Shehu Shagari") of the [National Party of Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Party_of_Nigeria "National Party of Nigeria") (NPN) was elected president. All five parties won representation in the National Assembly. On 1 October 1979, Shehu Shagari was sworn in as the first [President and Commander-in-Chief](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Nigeria "President of Nigeria") of the Federal Republic of Nigeria. Obasanjo peacefully transferred power to Shagari, becoming the first head of state in Nigerian history to willingly step down.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Shehu_Shagari_1980-10-07.jpg/250px-Shehu_Shagari_1980-10-07.jpg)

Shehu Shagari was the first elected President of Nigeria from 1979 to 1983.

In [August 1983](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1983_Nigerian_presidential_election "1983 Nigerian presidential election"), Shagari and the NPN were returned to power in a landslide victory, with a [majority of seats in the National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1983_Nigerian_parliamentary_election "1983 Nigerian parliamentary election") and control of 12 state governments. But the elections were marred by violence, and allegations of widespread vote-rigging and electoral malfeasance led to legal battles over the results. There were also uncertainties, such as in the first republic, that political leaders may be unable to govern properly. The [1983 military coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1983_Nigerian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1983 Nigerian coup d'état") was coordinated by key officers of the Nigerian military and led to the overthrow of the government and the installation of Major General [Muhammadu Buhari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammadu_Buhari "Muhammadu Buhari") as head of state. The military coup of Muhammadu Buhari shortly after the regime's re-election in 1984 was generally viewed as a positive development.[^93] [Ibrahim Babangida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibrahim_Babangida "Ibrahim Babangida") overthrew Buhari in the [coup d'état of 1985](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1985_Nigerian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1985 Nigerian coup d'état"). Babangida established the [Nigerian Political Bureau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Political_Bureau_of_1986 "Nigerian Political Bureau of 1986") in 1986 which made recommendations for the transition to the Third Nigerian Republic. In 1989, Babangida started making plans for the transition to the Third Nigerian Republic. Babangida survived the [1990 Nigerian coup d'état attempt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1990_Nigerian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_attempt "1990 Nigerian coup d'état attempt"), then postponed a promised return to democracy to 1992.[^94]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Abdulsalami_Abubakar_detail_DF-SC-02-04323.jpg/250px-Abdulsalami_Abubakar_detail_DF-SC-02-04323.jpg)

Abdulsalami Abubakar, military ruler in 1998, saw the return to democracy in 1999.

Babangida legalised the formation of political parties and formed the two-party system with the [Social Democratic Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_\(Nigeria\) "Social Democratic Party (Nigeria)") and [National Republican Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Republican_Convention "National Republican Convention") ahead of the [1992 general elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1992_Nigerian_parliamentary_election "1992 Nigerian parliamentary election"). He urged all Nigerians to join either of the parties, which Chief [Bola Ige](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bola_Ige "Bola Ige") referred to as "two leper hands". The [1993 presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1993_Nigerian_presidential_election "1993 Nigerian presidential election") held on 12 June was the first since the military coup of 1983. The results, though not officially declared by the National Electoral Commission, showed the duo of [Moshood Abiola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moshood_Abiola "Moshood Abiola") and [Baba Gana Kingibe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baba_Gana_Kingibe "Baba Gana Kingibe") of the Social Democratic Party defeated [Bashir Tofa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bashir_Tofa "Bashir Tofa") and [Sylvester Ugoh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvester_Ugoh "Sylvester Ugoh") of the National Republican Convention by over 2.3 million votes. However, Babangida annulled the elections, leading to massive civilian protests that effectively shut down the country for weeks. In August 1993, Babangida finally kept his promise to relinquish power to a civilian government but not before appointing [Ernest Shonekan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Shonekan "Ernest Shonekan") head of an [interim national government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Ernest_Shonekan "Cabinet of Ernest Shonekan").[^95] Babangida's regime has been considered the most corrupt and responsible for creating a culture of corruption in Nigeria.[^96]

Shonekan's interim government, the shortest in the political history of the country, was overthrown in a [coup d'état of 1993](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1993_Nigerian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1993 Nigerian coup d'état") led by General [Sani Abacha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sani_Abacha "Sani Abacha"), who used military force on a wide scale to end the crisis of the Third Republic. In 1995, Abacha's regime hanged environmentalist [Ken Saro-Wiwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ken_Saro-Wiwa "Ken Saro-Wiwa") on trumped-up charges in the deaths of four [Ogoni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ogoni_people "Ogoni people") elders, which caused Nigerian's [suspension from the Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_from_the_Commonwealth_of_Nations "Suspension from the Commonwealth of Nations"). Lawsuits under the American [Alien Tort Statute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alien_Tort_Statute "Alien Tort Statute") against [Royal Dutch Shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell "Royal Dutch Shell") and Brian Anderson, the head of Shell's Nigerian operation, settled out of court.[^97] Several hundred million dollars in accounts traced to Abacha were discovered in 1999.[^98] The regime came to an end in 1998 when the dictator died in the villa. Abacha looted money to offshore accounts in Western European banks and maintained his grip on power through arrests and bribing generals and politicians. His successor, General [Abdulsalami Abubakar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdulsalami_Abubakar "Abdulsalami Abubakar"), adopted a new constitution on 5 May 1999, which provided for multiparty elections.

### 1999–present

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Olusegun_Obasanjo_DD-SC-07-14396-cropped.jpg/250px-Olusegun_Obasanjo_DD-SC-07-14396-cropped.jpg)

Olusegun Obasanjo served as president of Nigeria from 1999 to 2007.

On 29 May 1999, Abubakar handed over power to the winner of the 1999 presidential election, former military ruler General [Olusegun Obasanjo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olusegun_Obasanjo "Olusegun Obasanjo"), as President of Nigeria. Obasanjo had been in prison under the dictatorship of Abacha. Obasanjo's inauguration heralded the beginning of the Fourth Nigerian Republic,[^99] ending a 39-year period of short-lived democracies, civil war and military dictatorship. Although the elections that brought Obasanjo to power and allowed him to run for a second term in the 2003 presidential elections were condemned as unfree and unfair, Nigeria made significant progress in democratisation under Obasanjo.[^100]

In the 2007 general elections, [Umaru Yar'Adua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umaru_Musa_Yar%27Adua "Umaru Musa Yar'Adua") of the People's Democratic Party came to power. The international community, which had observed the Nigerian elections to promote a free and fair process, condemned these elections as seriously flawed.[^101] Yar'Adua died on 5 May 2010, and Vice President [Goodluck Jonathan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodluck_Jonathan "Goodluck Jonathan") had been sworn in by the Senate three months earlier as acting president to succeed Yar'Adua.[^102] [^103] Jonathan won the 2011 presidential election; the polls went smoothly and with relatively little violence or electoral fraud.[^104] Jonathan's tenure saw an economic recovery that made Nigeria the leading economic power in Africa.[^105] [^106] The Jonathan administration also saw an increase in unparalleled corruption, with as many as 20 billion US dollars said to have been lost to the Nigerian state through the national oil company. Above all, however, Jonathan's tenure saw the emergence of a wave of terror by the [Boko Haram insurgency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boko_Haram_insurgency "Boko Haram insurgency"), including the [Gwoza massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gwoza_massacre "Gwoza massacre") and [Chibok schoolgirls kidnapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chibok_schoolgirls_kidnapping "Chibok schoolgirls kidnapping") in 2014.[^107]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Bola_Tinubu_portrait.jpg/250px-Bola_Tinubu_portrait.jpg)

Chief Bola Tinubu is serving as President of Nigeria since 29 May 2023.

Ahead of the [general election of 2015](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015_Nigerian_general_election "2015 Nigerian general election"), a merger of the biggest opposition parties in Nigeria – the [Action Congress of Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_Congress_of_Nigeria "Action Congress of Nigeria"), the [Congress for Progressive Change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress_for_Progressive_Change "Congress for Progressive Change"), the [All Nigeria Peoples Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_Nigeria_Peoples_Party "All Nigeria Peoples Party"), a faction of the [All Progressives Grand Alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_Progressives_Grand_Alliance "All Progressives Grand Alliance") and the new PDP (a faction of serving governors of the ruling People's Democratic Party) – formed the [All Progressives Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_Progressives_Congress "All Progressives Congress") led by current president Bola Ahmed Tinubu. At the time, it was the most expensive election ever to be held on the African continent (being surpassed only by the elections of [2019](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_Nigerian_general_election "2019 Nigerian general election") and [2023](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Nigerian_presidential_election "2023 Nigerian presidential election")). The new mega-opposition party chose as their candidate for the election former military dictator Muhammadu Buhari. Buhari's campaign in 2015 was popular and built around his image as a staunch anti-corruption fighter—he won the election by over two million votes. Observers generally praised the election as being fair.[^108] [^109] [^110] [^111] The election marked the first time an incumbent president had lost re-election in Nigeria. Insecurity heightened drastically under Buhari with [banditry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_bandit_conflict "Nigerian bandit conflict"), [insurgency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boko_Haram_insurgency "Boko Haram insurgency") and [separatist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separatist_movements_of_Nigeria "Separatist movements of Nigeria") agitations increasingly widespread. The economy also experienced two recessions and global oil shocks as a result of the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_Nigeria "COVID-19 pandemic in Nigeria"). Buhari was elected in the [2019 presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_Nigerian_general_election "2019 Nigerian general election"), he died two years after leaving office.[^112]

Four major candidates, amongst other less popular candidates, vied for the presidency in the [2023 presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Nigerian_presidential_election "2023 Nigerian presidential election"). For the first time since the return of democracy, no former military ruler ran for president, marking a strengthening of democracy and faith in the [multiparty constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Nigeria "Constitution of Nigeria"). The election also saw the rise of metonymic supporters of the new candidates, the [Obidient movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obidient_Movement "Obidient Movement") of [Peter Obi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Obi "Peter Obi"), previously governor of Anambra State, widely appealed to young, urban voters and has his core base in the Southeast;[^113] and the Kwankwassiya of [Rabiu Kwankwaso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rabiu_Kwankwaso "Rabiu Kwankwaso"), former governor of [Kano State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kano_State "Kano State") in the Northwest.[^114]

[Bola Tinubu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bola_Tinubu "Bola Tinubu") of the ruling party won the disputed election with 36.61% of the vote,[^115] though both runners-up claimed victory and litigation continued in an election tribunal.[^116] Tinubu was [inaugurated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inauguration_of_Bola_Tinubu "Inauguration of Bola Tinubu") on 29 May 2023.[^117] His government faced ongoing domestic challenges, including widespread [kidnapping in Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidnapping_in_Nigeria "Kidnapping in Nigeria").[^118] On 29 May 2024, Tinubu signed a law reinstating *[Nigeria, We Hail Thee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigeria,_We_Hail_Thee "Nigeria, We Hail Thee")*, the national anthem from 1960 to 1978, replacing *[Arise, O Compatriots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arise,_O_Compatriots "Arise, O Compatriots")*. The [United States carried out a strike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_United_States_strikes_in_Nigeria "2025 United States strikes in Nigeria") against [Islamic State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_State_%E2%80%93_West_Africa_Province "Islamic State – West Africa Province") militants in [northwest Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_West_\(Nigeria\) "North West (Nigeria)") on 25 December 2025 at the request of Tinubu's government, marking the first foreign military intervention in post-independence Nigeria.[^119] U.S. President [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump") stated the strike was to protect against [alleged violence against Christians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_violence_in_Nigeria "Religious violence in Nigeria") in the north.

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Nigeria_Topography.png/250px-Nigeria_Topography.png)

Topography of Nigeria

Nigeria is located in western Africa on the [Gulf of Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea "Gulf of Guinea") and has a total area of 923,768 km <sup>2</sup> (356,669 mi <sup>2</sup>),[^120] making it the world's [32nd-largest country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_area "List of countries and dependencies by area"). Its borders span 4,047 kilometres (2,515 mi), and it shares borders with Benin (773 km or 480 mi), Niger (1,497 km or 930 mi), Chad (87 km or 54 mi), and Cameroon (including the separatist [Ambazonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambazonia "Ambazonia")) 1,690 km or 1,050 mi. Its [coastline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastline "Coastline") is at least 853 km (530 mi).[^121] Nigeria lies between latitudes [4°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4th_parallel_north "4th parallel north") and [14°N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/14th_parallel_north "14th parallel north"), and longitudes [2°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2nd_meridian_east "2nd meridian east") and [15°E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/15th_meridian_east "15th meridian east"). The highest point in Nigeria is [Chappal Waddi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chappal_Waddi "Chappal Waddi") at 2,419 m (7,936 ft). The main rivers are the [Niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger_River "Niger River") and the [Benue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benue_River "Benue River"), which converge and empty into the [Niger Delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger_Delta "Niger Delta"). This is one of the world's largest river deltas and the location of a large area of Central African [mangroves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mangrove "Mangrove").

Nigeria's most expansive topographical region is that of the valleys of the Niger and Benue river valleys (which merge and form a Y-shape).[^122] To the southwest of the Niger is a "rugged" [highland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highland_\(geography\) "Highland (geography)"). To the southeast of the Benue are hills and mountains, which form the [Mambilla Plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mambilla_Plateau "Mambilla Plateau"), the highest plateau in Nigeria. This plateau extends through the border with [Cameroon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameroon "Cameroon"), where the [montane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montane "Montane") land is part of the [Bamenda Highlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamenda_Highlands "Bamenda Highlands") of Cameroon.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Koppen-Geiger_Map_NGA_present.svg/250px-Koppen-Geiger_Map_NGA_present.svg.png)

Climate map of Nigeria

The far south is defined by its [tropical rainforest climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_rainforest_climate "Tropical rainforest climate"), where annual rainfall is 1,500 to 2,000 millimetres (60 to 80 in) per year.[^123] In the southeast stands the [Obudu Plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obudu_Plateau "Obudu Plateau"). [Coastal plains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastal_plain "Coastal plain") are found in both the southwest and the southeast.[^122] [Mangrove swamps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mangrove_swamp "Mangrove swamp") are found along the coast.[^124]

The area near the border with Cameroon close to the coast is rich rainforest and part of the [Cross-Sanaga-Bioko coastal forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross%E2%80%93Sanaga%E2%80%93Bioko_coastal_forests "Cross–Sanaga–Bioko coastal forests") [ecoregion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecoregion "Ecoregion"), an important centre for [biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity "Biodiversity"). It is a habitat for the [drill primate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drill_\(animal\) "Drill (animal)"), which is found in the wild only in this area and across the border in Cameroon. The areas surrounding [Calabar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabar "Calabar"), Cross River State, also in this forest, are believed to contain the world's largest diversity of butterflies. The area of southern Nigeria between the Niger and the [Cross Rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_River_\(Nigeria\) "Cross River (Nigeria)") has lost most of its forest because of development and harvesting by increased population and has been [replaced by grassland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross%E2%80%93Niger_transition_forests "Cross–Niger transition forests").

Everything in between the far south and the far north is [savannah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savanna "Savanna") (insignificant tree cover, with grasses and flowers located between trees). Rainfall is more limited to between 500 and 1,500 millimetres (20 and 60 in) per year.[^123] The savannah zone's three categories are [Guinean forest-savanna mosaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guinean_forest%E2%80%93savanna_mosaic "Guinean forest–savanna mosaic"), Sudan savannah, and Sahel savannah. Guinean forest-savanna mosaic is plains of tall grass interrupted by trees. Sudan savannah is similar but with shorter grasses and shorter trees. Sahel savannah consists of patches of grass and sand, found in the northeast.[^124]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Megachad_en_disappearance_of_lake_chad.jpg/250px-Megachad_en_disappearance_of_lake_chad.jpg)

Shrinking of Lake Chad in north-eastern Nigeria, with the outline of the British Isles for size comparison

### Hydrology

Nigeria is divided into two main catchment areas – that of Lake Chad and that of the Niger. The Niger catchment area covers about 63% of the country. The main tributary of the Niger is the Benue, whose tributaries extend beyond Cameroon into Cameroon into Chad and the Sharie catchment area. In the Sahel region, rain is less than 500 millimetres (20 in) per year, and the [Sahara Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahara "Sahara") is encroaching.[^123] In the dry northeast corner of the country lies [Lake Chad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Chad "Lake Chad"), on a [shared water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Chad_Basin_Commission "Lake Chad Basin Commission") boundary [delimitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_boundary_delimitation "National boundary delimitation") with Niger, Chad and Cameroon.

The Chad Basin is fed from the north-eastern quarter of Nigeria. The Bauchi Plateau forms the watershed between the Niger/Benue and Komadugu Yobe river systems. The flat plains of north-eastern Nigeria are geographically part of the Chad Basin, where the course of the El Beid River forms the border with Cameroon, from the Mandara Mountains to Lake Chad. The Komadugu Yobe river system gives rise to the internationally important Hadejia-Nguru wetlands and Ox-bow lakes around Lake Nguru in the rainy season.[^125] [^126] Other rivers of the northeast include the Ngadda and the Yedseram, both of which flow through the Sambisa swamps, thus forming a river system. The river system of the northeast is also a major river system.[^127] In addition, Nigeria has numerous coastal rivers.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Lake_Chad_from_Apollo_7.jpg/250px-Lake_Chad_from_Apollo_7.jpg)

Photo of Lake Chad from Apollo 7, 1968

Over the last million years, [Lake Chad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Chad "Lake Chad") in the far north-east of Nigeria has dried up several times for a few thousand years and just as often growing to many times its current size. In recent decades its surface area has been reduced considerably, which may also be due to humans taking water from the inlets to irrigate agricultural land.

### Vegetation

Nigeria is covered by three types of vegetation: forests (where there is significant tree cover), [savannahs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savannah "Savannah") (insignificant tree cover, with grasses and flowers located between trees), and [montane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montane "Montane") land (least common and mainly found in the mountains near the Cameroon border). Both the forest zone and the savannah zone are divided into three parts.[^128]

Some of the forest zone's most southerly portion, especially around the Niger River and [Cross River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_River_\(Nigeria\) "Cross River (Nigeria)") deltas, is [mangrove swamp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_African_mangroves "Central African mangroves"). North of this is a freshwater swamp, containing different vegetation from the saltwater mangrove swamps, and north of that is a rainforest.[^128]

The savannah zone's three categories are divided into [Guinean forest-savanna mosaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guinean_forest%E2%80%93savanna_mosaic "Guinean forest–savanna mosaic"), made up of plains of tall grass which are interrupted by trees, the most common across the country; Sudan savannah, with short grasses and short trees; and [Sahel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahel "Sahel") savannah patches of grass and sand, found in the northeast.[^128]

### Environmental issues

![Nigerian deforrestation 1981 – 2020](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Nigerian-deforrestation-b.jpg/250px-Nigerian-deforrestation-b.jpg)

Deforestation in Nigeria 1981–2020 129

[Waste management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waste_management "Waste management") including [sewage treatment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sewage_treatment "Sewage treatment"), the linked processes of [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation "Deforestation") and [soil degradation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_retrogression_and_degradation "Soil retrogression and degradation"), and [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") are the major environmental problems in Nigeria.

**Climate change**

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Lake_Chad%E2%80%99s_shrinking_waters_ESA418540.gif/250px-Lake_Chad%E2%80%99s_shrinking_waters_ESA418540.gif)

Satellite image of Lake Chad, showing it shrinking between 1984 and 2018

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Show_Your_Stripes_change_in_temperature_graphic_for_All_of_Nigeria_with_bars_with_labels.png/250px-Show_Your_Stripes_change_in_temperature_graphic_for_All_of_Nigeria_with_bars_with_labels.png)

Temperature change in Nigeria; each bar represents the average temperature over that year.

[Climate change in Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_Nigeria "Climate change in Nigeria") has caused increasing temperatures and [rainfall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rainfall "Rainfall") variability (increasing in coastal areas and declining in continental areas) resulting in [drought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drought "Drought"), [desertification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desertification "Desertification"), rising sea levels, erosion, floods, [thunderstorms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thunderstorm "Thunderstorm"), [bush fires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildfire "Wildfire"), [landslides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landslides "Landslides"), [land degradation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_degradation "Land degradation") and more frequent, extreme weather conditions.[^130] [Climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") is leading to [biodiversity loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_loss "Biodiversity loss"), reduced food and water security, increasing poverty, conflict, [displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_displacement "Human displacement"), economic instability and negative health outcomes in Nigeria.[^131] Nigeria is highly vulnerable to and not well prepared to deal with the effects of climate change.[^132] The agricultural sector is particularly most vulnerable.[^133]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Climate_and_weather_in_Nigeria_04.jpg/250px-Climate_and_weather_in_Nigeria_04.jpg)

A thick layer of Harmattan fog in Enugu State

Nigeria is in the top 25 highest [greenhouse gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas") emitters, contributing 0.8% of the global total [emissions.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_intensity "Emission intensity")[^134] Nigeria has committed to cut [greenhouse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas") gas emissions by 20% on its own, and by 47% if it receives international support, by 2030. The country has also committed to [net zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net-zero_emissions "Net-zero emissions") by 2060.[^135] [^136] Nigeria's climate change [mitigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation "Climate change mitigation") and [adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation "Climate change adaptation") plans focus on agriculture and food security (through e.g.: [climate-smart agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate-smart_agriculture "Climate-smart agriculture")), forests and [biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity "Biodiversity"), water resources, energy and infrastructure (e.g.: transitioning to [renewable energies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renewable_energy "Renewable energy") like [solar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_energy "Solar energy")), health, human settlement, [industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industry_\(economics\) "Industry (economics)") and [commerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commerce "Commerce"), transportation and communication.[^137] [^138] While there is some discussion about necessary [capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacity_building "Capacity building") building at the individual, group and community level to engage in climate change responses, there is less attention given to higher levels of capacity building at the state and national level.[^139]

**Deforestation**

In 2005, Nigeria had the highest rate of [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation "Deforestation") in the world, according to the [Food and Agriculture Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization "Food and Agriculture Organization") of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations").[^140] That year, 12.2%, the equivalent of 11,089,000 hectares, had been forested in the country. Between 1990 and 2000, Nigeria lost an average of 409,700 hectares of forest every year equal to an average annual deforestation rate of 2.4%. Between 1990 and 2005, in total Nigeria lost 35.7% of its [forest cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_cover "Forest cover") or around 6,145,000 hectares.[^141] Nigeria had a 2019 [Forest Landscape Integrity Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_Landscape_Integrity_Index "Forest Landscape Integrity Index") mean score of 6.2/10, ranking it 82nd globally out of 172 countries.[^142]

**Pollution**

Waste management poses problems in a [megacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megacity "Megacity") like Lagos and other major Nigerian cities, which are linked to economic development, population growth, and the inability of municipal councils to manage the resulting rise in industrial and domestic waste. This waste management problem is also attributable to unsustainable environmental management lifestyles of [Kubwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kubwa,_Abuja "Kubwa, Abuja") community in the Federal Capital Territory, where there are habits of indiscriminate disposal of waste, dumping of waste along or into the canals, sewerage systems that are channels for water flows, and the like. Haphazard industrial planning, increased urbanisation, poverty, and the lack of competence of the municipal government are seen as the major reasons for high levels of waste pollution in the country's major cities. Some of the solutions have been disastrous to the environment, resulting in untreated waste being dumped in places where it can pollute waterways and [groundwater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groundwater_in_Nigeria "Groundwater in Nigeria").[^143]

[Nigeria's Delta region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger_Delta "Niger Delta") is one of the most polluted regions in the world due to serious [oil spills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_spill "Oil spill") and other environmental problems caused by its oil industry.[^144] [^145] The heavy contamination of the air, ground and water with toxic pollutants is often used as an example of [ecocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecocide "Ecocide").[^146] [^147] [^148] [^149] [^150] In additional to the environmental damage it has caused [conflict in the Delta region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conflict_in_the_Niger_Delta "Conflict in the Niger Delta").

Illegal oil refineries, in which local operators convert stolen crude oil into petrol and diesel, are considered particularly "dirty, dangerous and lucrative".[^151] Safety and environmental aspects are usually ignored. Refining petroleum also inevitably produces heavy oil, which is "cracked" into lighter fuel components in regular plants at great technical expense. Illegal refineries do not have these technical capabilities and "dispose" of the heavy oil where it then accumulates. The lighter components of crude oil ([methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") to [butane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butane "Butane"), [isobutane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isobutane "Isobutane")) create a certain risk of explosion, which often leads to disasters at illegal plants.[^152] In 2022, Nigeria suffered 125 deaths from explosions at local, illegal refineries.[^153]

In 2010, thousands of people were inadvertently [exposed to lead-containing soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zamfara_State_lead_poisoning_outbreak "Zamfara State lead poisoning outbreak") from informal [gold mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_mining "Gold mining") within the northern state of Zamfara. While estimates vary, it is thought that upwards of 400 children died of acute [lead poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead_poisoning "Lead poisoning"), making this perhaps the largest lead poisoning fatality outbreak ever encountered.[^154]

## Politics

### Government

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Coat_of_arms_of_Nigeria.svg/250px-Coat_of_arms_of_Nigeria.svg.png)

Coat of arms of Nigeria in current use

Nigeria is a [federal republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_republic "Federal republic") modelled after the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"),[^155] with 36 states and the capital Abuja as an independent unit. The executive power is exercised by the [President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Nigeria "President of Nigeria"). The president is both [head of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_state "Head of state") and [head of the federal government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_government "Head of government"). The president is [elected by popular vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_election "Direct election") to a maximum of two four-year terms.[^156] [State governors](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=State_governors_in_nigeria&action=edit&redlink=1 "State governors in nigeria (page does not exist)"), like the president, are elected for four years and may serve a maximum of two terms. The president's power is limited by a [Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senate_of_Nigeria "Senate of Nigeria") and a [House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Representatives_\(Nigeria\) "House of Representatives (Nigeria)"), which are combined in a [bicameral body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicameralism "Bicameralism") called the [National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_\(Nigeria\) "National Assembly (Nigeria)"). The Senate is a 109-seat body with three members from each state and one from the capital region of Abuja; members are elected by popular vote to four-year terms. The House contains 360 seats, with the number of seats per state determined by population.[^156]

The Nigerian president is elected in a modified two-round system. To be elected in the first round, a candidate must receive a majority of the votes and more than 25% of the votes in at least 24 of the 36 states.[^157] If no candidate reaches this hurdle, a second round of voting takes place between the leading candidate and the next candidate who received the majority of votes in the highest number of states. By convention, presidential candidates take a running mate (candidate for the vice presidency) who is both ethnically and religiously the opposite of themselves. There is no law prescribing this, yet all presidential candidates since the existence of the Fourth Republic until 2023 adhered to this rule.

However, this principle of religious and ethnic diversity in leadership was ignored in the 2023 General Elections, where the candidate for the [All Progressives Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_Progressives_Congress "All Progressives Congress"), [Bola Ahmed Tinubu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bola_Tinubu "Bola Tinubu"), a Muslim, selected another Muslim, [Senator Kashim Shettima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashim_Shettima "Kashim Shettima"), as running mate.[^158] [^159]

### Administrative divisions

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Political_map_of_Nigeria.svg/500px-Political_map_of_Nigeria.svg.png)

Map of Nigeria with administrative divisions

Nigeria is divided into [thirty-six states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_of_Nigeria "States of Nigeria") and one [Federal Capital Territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Capital_Territory,_Nigeria "Federal Capital Territory, Nigeria"), which are further sub-divided into 774 [local government areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_government_areas_of_Nigeria "Local government areas of Nigeria"). In some contexts, the states are aggregated into [six geopolitical zones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geopolitical_zones_of_Nigeria "Geopolitical zones of Nigeria"): [North West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_West_\(Nigeria\) "North West (Nigeria)"), [North East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_East_\(Nigeria\) "North East (Nigeria)"), [North Central](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Central,_Nigeria "North Central, Nigeria"), [South West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_West_\(Nigeria\) "South West (Nigeria)"), [South East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_East_\(Nigeria\) "South East (Nigeria)"), and [South South](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_South "South South").[^160] [^161]

Nigeria has five [cities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Nigerian_cities_by_population "List of Nigerian cities by population") with a population of over a million (from largest to smallest): [Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos "Lagos"), [Kano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kano_\(city\) "Kano (city)"), [Ibadan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibadan "Ibadan"), [Benin City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benin_City "Benin City") and [Port Harcourt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_Harcourt "Port Harcourt"). Lagos is the [largest city in Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_most_populous_cities_in_Africa "List of most populous cities in Africa"), with a population of over [12 million](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_metropolitan_areas_by_population "List of metropolitan areas by population") in its [urban area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urban_area "Urban area").[^162]

The south of the country in particular is characterised by very strong urbanisation and a relatively large number of cities. According to an estimate from 2015,[^163] there are 20 cities in Nigeria with more than 500,000 inhabitants, including ten cities with a population of one million.

### Law

The [Constitution of Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Nigeria "Constitution of Nigeria") is the supreme law of the country. There are four distinct legal systems in Nigeria, which include [English law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_law "English law"), [common law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_law "Common law"), [customary law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customary_law "Customary law"), and [Sharia law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharia "Sharia"):

- English law in Nigeria consists of the collection of British laws from colonial times.
- Common law is the collection of authoritative judicial decisions in the field of civil law (so-called precedents) that have been handed down in the country concerned – in this case Nigeria. (This system is mainly found in Anglo-Saxon countries; in continental Europe, on the other hand, codified and, as far as possible, abstracted civil law predominates, as in the [Napoleonic Code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Code "Napoleonic Code") in France).[^164]
- Customary law is derived from indigenous traditional norms and practices, including the dispute resolution meetings of pre-colonial Yoruba land secret societies and the [Èkpè](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egbo "Egbo") and Okónkò of [Igboland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igboland "Igboland") and [Ibibioland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibibio_people "Ibibio people").[^165]
- [Sharia in Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharia_in_Nigeria "Sharia in Nigeria") *(also known as Islamic Law)* used to be used only in [Northern Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Nigeria "Northern Nigeria"), where [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") is the predominant [religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion "Religion"). It is also being used in [Lagos State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos_State "Lagos State"), Oyo State, Kwara State, Ogun State, and Osun State by Muslims. Muslim penal codes are not the same in every state and they differentiate in punishment and offences according to religious affiliation (for example, alcohol consumption and distribution).

The country has a [judicial branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judicial_branch "Judicial branch"), the highest court of which is the [Supreme Court of Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Nigeria "Supreme Court of Nigeria").[^166]

### Foreign relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/MinForeignAffairs.jpg/250px-MinForeignAffairs.jpg)

The Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Abuja

Upon gaining independence in 1960, Nigeria made African unity the centrepiece of its foreign policy.[^167] One exception to the African focus was Nigeria's close relationship with Israel throughout the 1960s. Israel sponsored and oversaw the construction of Nigeria's parliament buildings.[^168]

Nigeria's foreign policy was put to the test in the 1970s after the country emerged united from its civil war. It supported movements against white minority governments in [Southern Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Africa "Southern Africa"). Nigeria backed the [African National Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_National_Congress "African National Congress") by taking a committed tough line about the South African government. Nigeria was a founding member of the [Organisation for African Unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_of_African_Unity "Organisation of African Unity") (now the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union")) and had tremendous influence in West Africa and Africa on the whole. Nigeria founded regional cooperative efforts in West Africa, functioning as the standard-bearer for the [Economic Community of West African States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_Community_of_West_African_States "Economic Community of West African States") (ECOWAS) and [ECOMOG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECOMOG "ECOMOG") (especially during the Liberia and Sierra Leone civil wars).

With this Africa-centred stance, Nigeria readily [sent troops to the Congo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congo_Crisis "Congo Crisis") at the behest of the United Nations shortly after independence (and has maintained membership since that time). Nigeria also supported several Pan-African and pro-self-government causes in the 1970s, including garnering support for [Angola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angola "Angola") 's [MPLA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_Movement_for_the_Liberation_of_Angola "Popular Movement for the Liberation of Angola"), [SWAPO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SWAPO "SWAPO") in Namibia, and aiding opposition to the minority governments of [Portuguese Mozambique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mozambican_War_of_Independence "Mozambican War of Independence"), and [Rhodesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhodesian_Bush_War "Rhodesian Bush War"). Nigeria retains membership in the [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement"). In late November 2006, it organised an Africa-South America Summit in [Abuja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuja "Abuja") to promote what some attendees termed "South-South" linkages on a variety of fronts.[^169] Nigeria is also a member of the [International Criminal Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Criminal_Court "International Criminal Court") and the [Commonwealth of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations"). It was temporarily expelled from the latter in 1995 when ruled by the [Abacha regime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sani_Abacha "Sani Abacha").

Nigeria has remained a key player in the [international oil industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum_industry "Petroleum industry") since the 1970s and maintains membership in [OPEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OPEC "OPEC"), which it joined in July 1971. Its [status as a major petroleum producer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum_industry_in_Nigeria "Petroleum industry in Nigeria") figures prominently in its sometimes volatile international relations with [developed countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country"), notably the United States, and with developing countries.[^170]

Since 2000, [Chinese–Nigerian trade relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Nigeria_relations "China–Nigeria relations") have risen exponentially. There has been an increase in total trade of over 10.3 billion dollars between the two nations from 2000 to 2016.[^171] However, the structure of the Chinese–Nigerian trade relationship has become a major political issue for the Nigerian state. Chinese exports account for around 80 per cent of total bilateral trade volumes.[^171] This has resulted in a serious [trade imbalance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balance_of_trade "Balance of trade"), with Nigeria importing ten times more than it exports to China.[^171] Subsequently, Nigeria's economy is becoming over-reliant on cheap imports to sustain itself, resulting in a clear decline in Nigerian industry under such arrangements.[^171]

Continuing its Africa-centred foreign policy, Nigeria introduced the idea of a [single currency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Currency_union "Currency union") for West Africa known as the [Eco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eco_\(currency\) "Eco (currency)") under the presumption that it would be led by the [naira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_naira "Nigerian naira"). But on 21 December 2019, [Ivorian President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_heads_of_state_of_Ivory_Coast "List of heads of state of Ivory Coast") [Alassane Ouattara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alassane_Ouattara "Alassane Ouattara"), [Emmanuel Macron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron "Emmanuel Macron"), and multiple other [UEMOA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_Community_of_West_African_States "Economic Community of West African States") states announced that they would merely rename the [CFA franc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CFA_franc "CFA franc") instead of replacing the currency as originally intended.[^172] As of 2021, the Eco currency has been delayed to 2027.[^173]

### Military

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/ZSU-23-4_Shilka_01.jpg/250px-ZSU-23-4_Shilka_01.jpg)

Nigerian Army self-propelled anti-aircraft gun

The [Nigerian Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Armed_Forces "Nigerian Armed Forces") are the [combined military forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military "Military") of Nigeria. The Nigerian military is the largest and widely recognised as the strongest military in [Sub-Saharan-Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa "Sub-Saharan Africa"), ranking third overall on the [African continent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa"), only behind [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") and [Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria "Algeria") [^174] [^175]. It consists of three uniformed service branches: the [Nigerian Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Army "Nigerian Army"), [Nigerian Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Navy "Nigerian Navy"), and [Nigerian Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Air_Force "Nigerian Air Force"). The [President of Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Nigeria "President of Nigeria") functions as the [commander-in-chief](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commander-in-chief "Commander-in-chief") of the armed forces, exercising his [constitutional authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Nigeria "Constitution of Nigeria") through the Ministry of Defence, which is responsible for the management of the military and its personnel. The operational head of the AFN is the [Chief of the Defence Staff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chief_of_the_Defence_Staff_\(Nigeria\) "Chief of the Defence Staff (Nigeria)"), who is subordinate to the [Nigerian Defence Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defence_Minister_of_Nigeria "Defence Minister of Nigeria"). With a force of more than 223,000 active personnel, the Nigerian military is one of the largest uniformed combat services in Africa.[^176]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Nigerian_Air_Force_Mil_Mi-24V_Iwelumo-1.jpg/250px-Nigerian_Air_Force_Mil_Mi-24V_Iwelumo-1.jpg)

Nigerian Air Force Mi-24 attack helicopter

Nigeria has 143,000 troops in the armed forces (army 100,000, navy 25,000, air force 18,000) and another 80,000 personnel for "gendarmerie & paramilitary" in 2020, according to the [International Institute for Strategic Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Institute_for_Strategic_Studies "International Institute for Strategic Studies").[^177] Nigeria spent just under 0.4 per cent of its economic output, or US$1.6 billion, on its armed forces in 2017.[^178] [^179] For 2022, US$2.26 billion has been budgeted for the Nigerian armed forces, which is just over a third of [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium "Belgium") 's [defence budget](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defence_budget "Defence budget") (US$5.99 billion).[^177]

### Communal conflicts

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/BokoHaram_deaths_by_state.jpg/250px-BokoHaram_deaths_by_state.jpg)

Attacks by Boko Haram, 2011 to October 2022. Each figure represents 1,000 deaths. 180

The predominantly [Kanuri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanuri_people "Kanuri people") led [Boko Haram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boko_Haram "Boko Haram") and the [bandit conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_bandit_conflict "Nigerian bandit conflict") have been responsible for numerous serious attacks with thousands of casualties since mid-2010. Since then, according to the Council on Foreign Relations' Nigeria Security Tracker, over 41,600 lives have been lost to this conflict (as of October 2022).[^180] The United Nations refugee agency UNHCR counts about 1.8 million internally displaced persons and about 200,000 Nigerian refugees in neighbouring countries.

The Boko Haram-affected states agreed in February 2015 to establish an 8,700-strong Multinational Joint Task Force to jointly fight Boko Haram. By October 2015, Boko Haram had been driven out of all the cities it controlled and almost all the counties in northeastern Nigeria. In 2016, Boko Haram split and in 2022, 40,000 fighters surrendered.[^181] The splinter group [ISWAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_State_%E2%80%93_West_Africa_Province "Islamic State – West Africa Province") (Islamic State in West Africa) remains active.

The fight against Boko Haram, other sectarians, and criminals, has been accompanied by increasing police attacks. The [Council on Foreign Relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_on_Foreign_Relations "Council on Foreign Relations") ' Nigeria Security Tracker counted 1,086 deaths from Boko Haram attacks and 290 deaths from police violence in the first 12 months of its establishment in May 2011. In the 12 months after October 2021, 2,193 people died from police violence and 498 from Boko Haram and ISWAP,[^180] according to the NST. The Nigerian police are notorious for [vigilante justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vigilantism "Vigilantism").[^180]

The Niger Delta saw intense [attacks on oil infrastructure in 2016](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_Niger_Delta_conflict "2016 Niger Delta conflict") by militant groups such as the *Movement for the Emancipation of the Niger Delta* (MEND), the *Niger Delta People's Volunteer Force* (NDPVF), the *Ijaw National Congress* (INC) and the *Pan Niger Delta Forum* (PANDEF). In response, the new Buhari government pursued a dual strategy of repression and negotiation.

In late 2016, the Nigerian federal government resorted to the gambit of offering the militant groups a 4.5 billion naira (US$144 million) contract to *guard* oil infrastructure. Most accepted. The contract was [renewed in August 2022](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tompolo#Arrest_warrant "Tompolo"), but led to fierce disputes among the above-mentioned groups over the distribution of the funds. Representatives speak of "war" [^182] – against each other. The high propensity for violence and the pettiness of the leaders, as well as the complete absence of social and environmental arguments in this dispute [^182] give rise to fears that the militant groups, despite their lofty names, have discarded responsibility for their region and ethnic groups and have moved into the realm of protection rackets and self-enrichment. In any case, the pipelines in the Niger Delta are not very effectively "guarded" – the pollution of the Niger Delta with stolen crude oil and [illegally produced heavy fuel oil](#Oil_spills) continued unhindered after 2016.[^183]

In central Nigeria, [conflicts between Muslim Fulani herders and indigenous Christian farmers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herder%E2%80%93farmer_conflicts_in_Nigeria "Herder–farmer conflicts in Nigeria") flared up again, especially in Kaduna, Plateau, Taraba and Benue states. In individual cases, these clashes have claimed several hundred lives. Conflict over land and resources is increasing due to the ongoing desertification in northern Nigeria, population growth and the generally tense economic situation.

In June 2022, a massacre took place in the St. Francis Xavier Church, in Owo. The Government blamed ISWAP for the murder of over 50 parishioners, but locals suspect Fulani herdsmen's involvement.[^184]

## Economy

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Lagos_Nigeria_City_View_Climate_change.jpg/330px-Lagos_Nigeria_City_View_Climate_change.jpg)

Over 21 million residents live in Lagos, the financial epicentre of Nigeria and the most populous urban area in Africa.

Nigeria's economy is the [fourth largest in Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_African_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of African countries by GDP (nominal)"), the [31st-largest in the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of countries by GDP (nominal)") by nominal GDP, and [30th-largest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(PPP\) "List of countries by GDP (PPP)") by [PPP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity "Purchasing power parity"). As of 2023, Nigeria's economy is classified as lower-middle-income.[^185]

Before 1999, economic development was hindered by years of military rule, corruption, and mismanagement. In the decades thereafter, the restoration of democracy and subsequent economic reforms led to rapid growth. In 2011, [Citigroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citigroup "Citigroup") projected that Nigeria would have the highest average GDP growth in the world between 2010 and 2050.[^186]

Nigeria is an economic leader in Africa in several industries, including energy, [financial markets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_market "Financial market"), [pharmaceuticals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication "Medication"), and [entertainment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entertainment_industry "Entertainment industry"). Its [financial services sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banking_in_Nigeria "Banking in Nigeria") is well-developed, with a mix of local and international banks, asset management companies, brokerage houses, insurance companies and brokers, private equity funds, and investment banks.[^187] After petroleum, the largest source of foreign exchange earnings for Nigeria are [remittances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remittance "Remittance") sent home by Nigerians living abroad.[^188]

Nigeria also has an abundant supply of under-exploited natural resources, including coal, [bauxite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bauxite "Bauxite"), [tantalite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tantalite "Tantalite"), gold, [tin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tin "Tin"), iron ore, [limestone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limestone "Limestone"), [niobium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niobium "Niobium"), lead and [zinc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc "Zinc").[^189] The country's gold production in 2015 was 8 metric tons.[^190] Despite huge deposits of these natural resources, the mining industry in Nigeria is still in its infancy.

### Agriculture

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Palm_nuts_put_out_to_dry.jpg/250px-Palm_nuts_put_out_to_dry.jpg)

Nigerian palm nuts put out to dry

In 2021, about 23.4% of Nigeria's GDP is contributed by agriculture, forestry and fishing combined.[^191] Nigeria is the world's largest producer of [cassava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassava_production_in_Nigeria "Cassava production in Nigeria").[^192] Additional major crops include [maize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maize "Maize"), [rice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rice "Rice"), [millet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millet "Millet"), [yam beans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yam_\(vegetable\) "Yam (vegetable)"), and [guinea corn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sorghum_bicolor "Sorghum bicolor") (sorghum).[^193] [Cocoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cocoa_bean "Cocoa bean") is the principal agricultural export, and one of the country's most significant non-petroleum products.[^194] [^195] Nigeria is also one of the world's top twenty exporters of [natural rubber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_rubber "Natural rubber"), generating $20.9 million in 2019.[^196]

Before the [Nigerian Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Civil_War "Nigerian Civil War") and the [oil boom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_boom "Oil boom"), Nigeria was self-sufficient in food.[^197] [^198] [^199] Agriculture used to be the principal foreign exchange earner of Nigeria.[^200] Agriculture has failed to keep pace with Nigeria's rapid population growth, and Nigeria now relies upon food imports to sustain itself.[^198] [^201] It spends US$6.7 billion yearly for food imports, four times more than revenues from food export.[^192] The Nigerian government promoted the use of inorganic fertilisers in the 1970s.[^202]

Nigeria's rice production increased by 10% from 2017/18 to 2021/22, reaching 5 million tonnes per year,[^203] but it failed to keep up with rising demand. Consequently, rice imports remained steady at 2 million tonnes annually. In August 2019, Nigeria closed its border with Benin and other neighbouring countries to stop rice smuggling into the country as part of efforts to boost local production.[^204]

Until now, Nigeria exported unhusked rice but had to import husked rice, the country's staple food. The [rice mill in Imota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imota_rice_mill "Imota rice mill"), near Lagos, is designed to handle the processing at home, improve the balance of trade and the labour market, and save transport and middlemen costs. When fully operational at the end of 2022, the plant, the largest south of the Sahara, is expected to employ 250,000 people and produce 2.5 million 50-kg bags of rice annually.[^205]

The plant was inaugurated in 2023 with the commencement of full production.

### Oil and natural gas

Nigeria is the [15th largest producer of petroleum in the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_oil_production "List of countries by oil production"), the [6th largest exporter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_oil_exports "List of countries by oil exports"), and has the [9th largest proven reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_proven_oil_reserves "List of countries by proven oil reserves"). Petroleum plays a large role in the Nigerian economy and politics, accounting for about 80% of government earnings. Nigeria also has the [9th largest proven natural gas reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_natural_gas_proven_reserves "List of countries by natural gas proven reserves") estimated by [OPEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OPEC "OPEC"); the government's value of its about 206.53 trillion cubic feet has been valued at $803.4 trillion.[^206] Natural gas is seen as having the potential to unlock an [economic miracle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_miracle "Economic miracle") on the Niger River.[^207] Nigeria each year loses to [gas flaring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_flare "Gas flare") an estimate of US$2.5 billion,[^208] and over 120,000 barrels of oil per day to [crude theft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_theft_in_Nigeria "Oil theft in Nigeria") in the [Niger Delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger_Delta "Niger Delta"), its main oil-producing region.[^209] [^210] This has led to [piracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piracy_in_the_Gulf_of_Guinea "Piracy in the Gulf of Guinea") and [conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conflict_in_the_Niger_Delta "Conflict in the Niger Delta") for control in the region and has led to disruptions in production preventing the country from meeting its OPEC quota and exporting petroleum at full capability.[^211]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Nigerdelta_NASA.jpg/250px-Nigerdelta_NASA.jpg)

Overflight photo of the creeks of the Niger Delta

Nigeria has a total of 159 [oil fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum_reservoir "Petroleum reservoir") and 1,481 [wells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_well "Oil well") in operation according to the [Department of Petroleum Resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Department_of_Petroleum_Resources "Department of Petroleum Resources").[^212] The most productive region of the nation is the coastal [Niger Delta Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger_Delta_Basin_\(geology\) "Niger Delta Basin (geology)") in the Niger Delta or "south-south" region which encompasses 78 of the 159 oil fields. Most of Nigeria's oil fields are small and scattered, and as of 1990, these small fields accounted for 62.1% of all Nigerian production. This contrasts with the sixteen largest fields which produced 37.9% of Nigeria's petroleum at that time.[^213] Petrol was Nigeria's main import commodity until 2021, accounting for 24% of import volume.[^214]

The Niger Delta Nembe Creek oil field was discovered in 1973 and produces from the middle [Miocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miocene "Miocene") [deltaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/River_delta "River delta") [sandstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandstone "Sandstone") - [shale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shale "Shale") in an [anticline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticline "Anticline") [structural trap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum_trap "Petroleum trap") at a depth of 2 to 4 kilometres (7,000 to 13,000 feet).[^215] In June 2013, Shell announced a strategic review of its operations in Nigeria, hinting that assets could be divested. While many international oil companies have operated there for decades, by 2014 most were making moves to divest their interests, citing a range of issues including oil theft. In August 2014, Shell said it was finalising its interests in four Nigerian oil fields.[^216]

The supply of natural gas to Europe, threatened by the [Russo-Ukrainian war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russo-Ukrainian_war_\(2022%E2%80%93present\) "Russo-Ukrainian war (2022–present)"), pushed projects to transport Nigerian natural gas via pipelines to Morocco or Algeria.[^217] [^218] [^219] As of May 2022, however, there are no results on this yet.

### Energy

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/ASC_Leiden_-_Rietveld_Collection_-_Nigeria_1970_-_1973_-_01_-_083_Kainji_Dam._The_water_flows_from_four_openings.jpg/250px-ASC_Leiden_-_Rietveld_Collection_-_Nigeria_1970_-_1973_-_01_-_083_Kainji_Dam._The_water_flows_from_four_openings.jpg)

Kainji Dam on the Niger River, built in the 1960s

Nigeria's energy consumption is much more than its generation capacity. Most of the energy comes from traditional fossil fuels, which account for 73% of total primary production. The rest is from hydropower (27%). Since independence, Nigeria has tried to develop a domestic nuclear industry for energy. Nigeria opened 2004 a Chinese-origin research reactor at [Ahmadu Bello University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmadu_Bello_University "Ahmadu Bello University") and has sought the support of the [International Atomic Energy Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency "International Atomic Energy Agency") to develop plans for up to 4,000 MWe of nuclear capacity by 2027 according to the National Program for the Deployment of Nuclear Power for Generation of Electricity. In 2007, President [Umaru Yar'Adua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umaru_Musa_Yar%27Adua "Umaru Musa Yar'Adua") urged the country to embrace nuclear power to meet its growing energy needs. In 2017, Nigeria signed the UN [Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Prohibition_of_Nuclear_Weapons "Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons").[^220] In April 2015, Nigeria began talks with Russia's state-owned [Rosatom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosatom "Rosatom") to collaborate on the design, construction and operation of four nuclear power plants by 2035, the first of which will be in operation by 2025. In June 2015, Nigeria selected two sites for the planned construction of the nuclear plants. Neither the Nigerian government nor Rosatom would disclose the specific locations of the sites, but it is believed that the nuclear plants will be sited in [Akwa Ibom State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akwa_Ibom_State "Akwa Ibom State") and [Kogi State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kogi_State "Kogi State"). The sites are planned to house two plants each. In 2017 agreements were signed for the construction of the [Itu nuclear power plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Itu_nuclear_power_plant "Itu nuclear power plant").

#### Electricity

According to the survey, 94% of Nigerians are connected to the national grid, but only 57% have their electricity consumption recorded by an electricity meter.[^221] Only 1% of Nigerians surveyed reported having electricity 24 hours a day. 68% have electricity 1 to 9 hours a day, according to the NIO. Two-thirds of Nigerians, or 66%, pay up to 10,000 Naira (US$13) a month for electricity, which is almost 3% of the average income in Nigeria.[^221] Over two-thirds of respondents, or 67%, were willing to pay more for uninterrupted electricity supply. Power generators are owned by 21% of Nigerians, while 14% use solar energy.[^221]

### Manufacturing and technology

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Nigeria_EduSat-1.jpg/250px-Nigeria_EduSat-1.jpg)

Nigeria EduSat-1, the first satellite built by Nigeria by the Federal University of Technology Akure

Nigeria has a manufacturing industry that includes leather and textiles (centred in [Kano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kano_\(city\) "Kano (city)"), [Abeokuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abeokuta "Abeokuta"), [Onitsha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onitsha "Onitsha"), and Lagos), plastics and processed food. [Ogun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ogun_State "Ogun State") is considered to be Nigeria's current industrial hub, as most factories are located in Ogun and more companies are moving there, followed by Lagos.[^222] [^223] [^224] The city of [Aba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aba,_Abia "Aba, Abia") in the south-eastern part of the country is well known for handicrafts and shoes, known as "Aba made".[^225] Nigeria has a market of 720,000 cars per year, but less than 20% of these are produced domestically.[^226]

In 2016, Nigeria was the [leading cement producer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cement_production_by_country "Cement production by country") south of the Sahara, ahead of South Africa.[^227] [Aliko Dangote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aliko_Dangote "Aliko Dangote"), Nigeria's richest inhabitant, based his wealth on cement production, as well as agricultural commodities.[^228] According to its own information, the [Ajaokuta Steel Company Limited](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajaokuta_Steel_Mill "Ajaokuta Steel Mill") produces 1.3 million tonnes of [steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel "Steel") per year.[^229] However, steel plants in [Katsina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katsina_\(city\) "Katsina (city)"), [Jos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jos "Jos") and [Osogbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osogbo "Osogbo") no longer appear to be active.[^230]

In June 2019, [Nigeria EduSat-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigeria_EduSat-1 "Nigeria EduSat-1") was deployed from the [International Space Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station "International Space Station"). It is the first satellite that was built in Nigeria, which followed [many other Nigerian satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_weather_and_communications_satellites "Nigerian weather and communications satellites") that were built by other countries.[^3] [^231] In 2021, Nigeria hosts about 60 percent of the pharmaceutical production capacity in Africa,[^232] the larger pharmaceutical companies are located in [Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos "Lagos").[^233] The pharmaceutical producer with the most employees in Nigeria is [Emzor Pharmaceutical Industries](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Emzor_Pharmaceutical_Industries&action=edit&redlink=1 "Emzor Pharmaceutical Industries (page does not exist)") Ltd.[^234] Nigeria has a few electronic manufacturers like [Zinox](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Zinox&action=edit&redlink=1 "Zinox (page does not exist)"), the first branded Nigerian computer, and manufacturers of electronic gadgets such as tablet PCs.[^235] As of January 2022, Nigeria is the host to 5 out of the 7 [unicorn companies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicorn_\(company\) "Unicorn (company)") in Africa.[^236]

### Internet and telecommunications

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Wikidata_for_Libraries_and_Librarians_in_Nigeria_69.jpg/250px-Wikidata_for_Libraries_and_Librarians_in_Nigeria_69.jpg)

Nigerian librarians editing the Wikidata database

The Nigerian telecommunications market is one of the fastest-growing in the world, with major emerging market operators (like [MTN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MTN_Group "MTN Group"), [9mobile](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=9mobile&action=edit&redlink=1 "9mobile (page does not exist)"), [Airtel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airtel "Airtel") and [Globacom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globacom "Globacom")) basing their largest and most profitable centres in the country.[^237] Nigeria's ICT sector has experienced much growth, representing 10% of the nation's GDP in 2018 as compared to just 1% in 2001.[^238] Lagos is regarded as one of the largest technology hubs in Africa with its thriving tech ecosystem.[^239] According to a survey by the [GSM Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GSM_Association "GSM Association"), 92% of adult Nigerian men and 88% of women owned a mobile phone.[^240] Using various measures including but not limited to illegal arrest, taking down of websites, passport seizures, and restricted access to bank accounts, the [Nigerian government is punishing citizens for expressing themselves on the internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunications_in_Nigeria#Internet_censorship_and_surveillance "Telecommunications in Nigeria") and working to stifle internet freedom.[^241]

### Tourism

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/%C3%92w%C3%BA_Waterfalls.jpg/250px-%C3%92w%C3%BA_Waterfalls.jpg)

Owu waterfalls, visited by Nigerian undergraduates

Tourism in Nigeria centres largely on events, because of the country's ample amount of ethnic groups, but also includes rain forests, savannahs, waterfalls, and other natural attractions.[^242] Abuja is home to several parks and green areas. The largest, [Millennium Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millennium_Park_\(Abuja\) "Millennium Park (Abuja)"), was designed by architect [Manfredi Nicoletti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manfredi_Nicoletti "Manfredi Nicoletti") and officially opened in December 2003. After the re-modernisation project achieved by the administration of Governor Raji Babatunde Fashola, Lagos is gradually becoming a major tourist destination. Lagos is currently taking steps to become a [global city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_city "Global city"). The 2009 Eyo carnival (a yearly festival originating from [Iperu Remo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iperu,_Ogun "Iperu, Ogun"), Ogun State) was a step toward world city status. Currently, Lagos is primarily known as a business-oriented and fast-paced community.[^243] Lagos has become an important location for African and black cultural identity.[^244]

Lagos has sandy beaches by the Atlantic Ocean, including [Elegushi Beach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elegushi_Beach "Elegushi Beach") and Alpha Beach. Lagos also has many private beach resorts including Inagbe Grand Beach Resort and several others in the outskirts. Lagos has a variety of hotels ranging from three-star to five-star hotels, with a mixture of local hotels such as [Eko Hotels and Suites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eko_Hotels_and_Suites "Eko Hotels and Suites"), [Federal Palace Hotel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Palace_Hotel "Federal Palace Hotel") and franchises of multinational chains such as [Intercontinental Hotel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/InterContinental "InterContinental"), [Sheraton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheraton_Hotels_and_Resorts "Sheraton Hotels and Resorts"), and [Four Points by Sheraton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_Points_by_Sheraton "Four Points by Sheraton"). Other places of interest include the [Tafawa Balewa Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tafawa_Balewa_Square "Tafawa Balewa Square"), Festac town, The [Nike Art Gallery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nike_Art_Gallery "Nike Art Gallery"), [Freedom Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_Park_\(Lagos\) "Freedom Park (Lagos)"), and the [Cathedral Church of Christ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathedral_Church_of_Christ,_Lagos "Cathedral Church of Christ, Lagos").

### Transport

Due to Nigeria's location in the centre of West Africa, [transport plays a major role in the national service sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport_in_Nigeria "Transport in Nigeria"). Government investments have seen an increase in extensive road repairs and new construction has been carried out gradually as states in particular spend their share of increased government allocations. Representative of these improvements is the [Second Niger Bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Niger_bridge "Second Niger bridge") near Onitsha, which was largely completed in 2022.[^245] A 2017 World Bank report on logistics hubs in Africa placed the country in fourth place, behind Côte d'Ivoire, Senegal, and Sao Tome,[^246] but in 2021, Nigeria joined the World Logistics Passport, a private sector group working to increase the effiency of global trade.[^247]

#### Roads

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Map_of_Trans-African_Highways.PNG/330px-Map_of_Trans-African_Highways.PNG)

Map of trans-African automobile routes

Four trans-African automobile routes pass through Nigeria:

- [Lagos-Mombasa Highway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos-Mombasa_Highway "Lagos-Mombasa Highway")
- [Algiers-Lagos Highway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algiers-Lagos_Highway "Algiers-Lagos Highway")
- [Dakar-Lagos Highway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dakar-Lagos_Highway "Dakar-Lagos Highway")
- [Dakar-Ndjamena Highway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dakar-Ndjamena_Highway "Dakar-Ndjamena Highway")

Nigeria has the largest road network in [West Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Africa "West Africa"). It covers about 200,000 km, of which 60,000 km are asphalted. Nigeria's roads and highways handle 90% of all passenger and freight traffic. It contributes N2.4trn ($6.4bn) to GDP in 2020. The federal government is responsible for 35,000 km of the road network. The motorway links of important economic centres such as [Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos "Lagos") - [Ibadan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibadan "Ibadan"), Lagos- [Badagry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Badagry "Badagry") and [Enugu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enugu_\(city\) "Enugu (city)") - [Onitsha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onitsha "Onitsha") have been renovated.[^248]

The rest of the road network is a state matter and therefore in very different shape, depending on the state. Economically strong states such as Lagos, [Anambra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anambra_State "Anambra State") and [Rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rivers_State "Rivers State") receive particularly poor evaluations.[^249] Most roads were built in the 1980s and early 1990s. Poor maintenance and inferior materials have worsened the condition of the roads. Travelling is very difficult. Especially during the rainy season, the use of secondary roads is sometimes almost impossible due to potholes.[^250] Road bandits often take advantage of this situation for their criminal purposes.[^251] [^252]

#### Rail transport

Railways have undergone a massive revamping with projects such as the [Lagos-Kano Standard Gauge Railway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos%E2%80%93Kano_Standard_Gauge_Railway "Lagos–Kano Standard Gauge Railway") being completed connecting cities of [Kano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kano_State "Kano State"), [Kaduna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaduna_\(city\) "Kaduna (city)"), [Abuja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuja "Abuja"), [Ibadan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibadan "Ibadan") and [Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos "Lagos"). [Lagos Rail Mass Transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos_Rail_Mass_Transit "Lagos Rail Mass Transit") controls transport in Lagos with two currently operating services. The [Red Line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Line_\(Lagos_Transit\) "Red Line (Lagos Transit)") and The [Blue Line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_Line_\(Lagos_Transit\) "Blue Line (Lagos Transit)"). [Abuja Light Rail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuja_Light_Rail "Abuja Light Rail") controls rail services in Abuja and currently has one operating service, the Blue Line.

#### Air transport

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Arik_Air_Hifly_A340-500_CS-TFW_in_LHR.jpg/250px-Arik_Air_Hifly_A340-500_CS-TFW_in_LHR.jpg)

An A340-500 of Arik Air

The Nigerian aviation industry generated 198.62 billion naira (€400 million) in 2019, representing a contribution of 0.14% to GDP. It was the fastest-growing sector of the Nigerian economy in 2019. Passenger traffic increased from 9,358,166 in 2020 to 15,886,955 in 2021, a significant increase of over 69%. Aircraft movements increased by more than 46% from 2020 to 2021. Total freight volumes were 191 tonnes in 2020 but increased to 391 tonnes in 2021.[^253] In December 2021, the [Anambra International Cargo Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinua_Achebe_International_Airport "Chinua Achebe International Airport") started its operation.[^254] In April 2022, the second terminal of the [Murtala Muhammed International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murtala_Muhammed_International_Airport "Murtala Muhammed International Airport") has been inaugurated. It will increase the capacity of the airport to 14 million passengers per year.[^255]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Air_Peace%2C_5N-BQP%2C_Boeing_737-33R.jpg/250px-Air_Peace%2C_5N-BQP%2C_Boeing_737-33R.jpg)

B737-300 of Air Peace

There are 54 airports in Nigeria, The principal airports are:

- [Murtala Muhammed International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murtala_Muhammed_International_Airport "Murtala Muhammed International Airport") in Lagos,
- [Nnamdi Azikiwe International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nnamdi_Azikiwe_International_Airport "Nnamdi Azikiwe International Airport") in Abuja,
- [Mallam Aminu Kano International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mallam_Aminu_Kano_International_Airport "Mallam Aminu Kano International Airport") in Kano,
- [Akanu Ibiam International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akanu_Ibiam_International_Airport "Akanu Ibiam International Airport") in Enugu and
- [Port Harcourt International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_Harcourt_International_Airport "Port Harcourt International Airport") in Port Harcourt.

Nigeria had in the past operated a state-owned airline [Nigeria Airways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigeria_Airways "Nigeria Airways") which was over-indebted in 2003 and was bought by the British [Virgin Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgin_Group "Virgin Group"); since 28 June 2005, it has flown under the name [Virgin Nigeria Airways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_Nigeria "Air Nigeria"). At the end of 2008, the Virgin Group announced its withdrawal from the airline; since September 2009 the airline has been operating as Nigerian Eagle Airlines. The largest airline in Nigeria is privately owned [Air Peace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_Peace "Air Peace"), founded in 2012.

## Demographics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Population_density_map_of_Nigerian_states_-_English.png/500px-Population_density_map_of_Nigerian_states_-_English.png)

Population density (persons per square kilometre) in Nigeria

The [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") estimates that the population of Nigeria in 2021 was at 213,401,323 [^256] [^257], distributed as 51.7% rural and 48.3% urban, and with a population density of 167.5 people per square kilometre. Around 42.5% of the population were 14 years or younger, 19.6% were aged 15–24, 30.7% were aged 25–54, 4.0% were aged 55–64, and 3.1% were aged 65 years or older. The median age in 2017 was 18.4 years.[^258] Nigeria is the [world's sixth-most populous country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_population#Sovereign_states_and_dependencies_by_population "List of countries and dependencies by population"). The birth rate is 35.2-births/1,000 population and the death rate is 9.6 deaths/1,000 population as of 2017, while the total fertility rate is 5.07 children born/woman.[^258] Nigeria's population increased by 57 million from 1990 to 2008, a 60% growth rate in less than two decades.[^259] Nigeria is the most populous country in Africa [^260] and accounts for about 17% of the continent's total population as of 2017; however, exactly how populous is a subject of speculation.[^261]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Nigeria-demography.svg/250px-Nigeria-demography.svg.png)

Historical population of Nigeria (1950–2020)

Millions of Nigerians have emigrated during times of economic hardship, primarily to Europe, North America and Australia. It is estimated that over a million Nigerians have emigrated to the United States and constitute the [Nigerian American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_American "Nigerian American") populace. Individuals in many such Diasporic communities have joined the "Egbe Omo Yoruba" society, a national association of Yoruba descendants in North America.[^262] [^263] Nigeria's largest city is [Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos "Lagos"). Lagos has grown from about 300,000 in 1950 [^264] to an estimated 13.4 million in 2017.[^265]

Nigeria has more than 250 ethnic groups, with varying languages and customs, creating a country of rich ethnic diversity. The three largest ethnic groups are the [Hausa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausa_people "Hausa people"), [Yoruba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoruba_people "Yoruba people") and [Igbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igbo_people "Igbo people"), together accounting for more than 60% of the population, while the [Edo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edo_people "Edo people"), [Ijaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ijaw_people "Ijaw people"), [Fulɓe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fula_people "Fula people"), [Kanuri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanuri_people "Kanuri people"), [Urhobo-Isoko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urhobo_people "Urhobo people"), [Ibibio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibibio_people "Ibibio people"), [Ebira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebira_people "Ebira people"), [Nupe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nupe_people "Nupe people"), [Gbagyi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gbagyi_people "Gbagyi people"), [Jukun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jukun_people_\(West_Africa\) "Jukun people (West Africa)"), [Igala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igala_people "Igala people"), [Idoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idoma_people "Idoma people"), [Ogoni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ogoni_people "Ogoni people") and [Tiv](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiv_people "Tiv people") account for between 35 and 40%; other minorities make up the remaining 5%.[^266] The [Middle Belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Belt "Middle Belt") of Nigeria is known for its diversity of ethnic groups, including the [Atyap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atyap_people "Atyap people"), [Berom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berom_people "Berom people"), Goemai, Igala, [Kofyar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kofyar_people "Kofyar people"), Pyem, and Tiv.[^162] [^267] [^268] There are small minorities of British, American, [Indian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-resident_Indian_and_person_of_Indian_origin "Non-resident Indian and person of Indian origin"), [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_people_in_Nigeria "Chinese people in Nigeria") (est. 50,000),[^269] [white Zimbabwean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Zimbabweans "White Zimbabweans"),[^270] Japanese, Greek, Syrian and [Lebanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Nigerians "Lebanese Nigerians") immigrants. Immigrants also include those from other West African or East African nations.

### Languages

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Nigeria_linguistical_map_1979.svg/500px-Nigeria_linguistical_map_1979.svg.png)

Map of Nigeria's linguistic groups

Of the 525 known languages that have been spoken in Nigeria at some time in its history, eight are now extinct.[^271] In some areas of Nigeria, ethnic groups speak more than one language. The official language of Nigeria, English, was chosen to facilitate the cultural and linguistic unity of the country, owing to the influence of British colonisation which ended in 1960. [Nigerian Pidgin English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Pidgin "Nigerian Pidgin"), first used by British and [African slavers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery_in_Africa "Slavery in Africa") to facilitate the [Atlantic slave trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade "Atlantic slave trade") in the late 17th century,[^272] has replaced the native language for many Nigerians. Many French speakers from surrounding countries have influenced the English spoken in the border regions of Nigeria and some Nigerian citizens have become fluent enough in French to work in the surrounding countries.[^273]

With the majority of Nigeria's populace in rural areas, the major languages of communication in the country remain indigenous languages. Some of the largest of these, notably Yoruba and Igbo, have derived standardised languages from several different dialects and are widely spoken by those ethnic groups. [Nigerian Pidgin English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Pidgin "Nigerian Pidgin"), often known simply as " [Pidgin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pidgin "Pidgin") " or "Broken" (Broken English), is also a popular [lingua franca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lingua_franca "Lingua franca"), though with varying regional influences on dialect and slang. The pidgin English or Nigerian English is widely spoken within the Niger Delta Region.[^274]
