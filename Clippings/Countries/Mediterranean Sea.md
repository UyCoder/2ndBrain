The **Mediterranean Sea** ([/ ˌ m ɛ d ɪ t ə ˈ r eɪ n i ən /](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English") [*MED-ih-tə-RAY-nee-ən*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key")) is an intercontinental [sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea "Sea") situated between Europe, Asia, and Africa. It is surrounded by the [Mediterranean basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_basin "Mediterranean basin") and almost completely enclosed by land: on the east by the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant") in [West Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Asia "West Asia"), on the north by [Anatolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolia "Anatolia") in West Asia and [Southern Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Europe "Southern Europe"), and on the south by [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa"). To its west it is connected to the [Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") via the [Strait of Gibraltar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar "Strait of Gibraltar") that separates the [Iberian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula") in [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe") from [Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco "Morocco") in [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa") by only 14 km (9 mi); additionally, it is connected to the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea") through the [Bosporus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosporus "Bosporus") strait that intersects Turkey in the northeast and the [Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea") via the [Suez Canal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Canal "Suez Canal") in the southeast.

The Mediterranean Sea covers an area of about 2,500,000 km <sup>2</sup> (970,000 sq mi),[^12] representing 0.7% of the global [ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean "Ocean") surface; it includes fifteen [marginal seas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginal_sea "Marginal sea"), including the [Aegean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_Sea "Aegean Sea"), [Adriatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adriatic_Sea "Adriatic Sea"), [Tyrrhenian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrrhenian_Sea "Tyrrhenian Sea"), and [Marmara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marmara_sea "Marmara sea"). Geological evidence indicates that around 5.9 million years ago, the Mediterranean was cut off from the Atlantic and was partly or completely [desiccated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desiccation "Desiccation") over a period of some 600,000 years during the [Messinian salinity crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messinian_salinity_crisis "Messinian salinity crisis") before being refilled by the [Zanclean flood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zanclean_flood "Zanclean flood") about 5.3 million years ago.

The [history of the Mediterranean region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Mediterranean_region "History of the Mediterranean region") is crucial to understanding the origins and development of many modern societies; it is sometimes described as an "incubator of [Western civilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_civilization "Western civilization") ". and saw the emergence of some of the earliest and most advanced civilisations, including those of [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt"), [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece"), and the [Fertile Crescent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertile_Crescent "Fertile Crescent").[^13] The Levant in the [Eastern Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean "Eastern Mediterranean") was among the first regions in the world to display permanent human habitation as early as 12,000 BC. The Mediterranean Sea was an important route for [merchants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merchant "Merchant"), travellers, and migrants in antiquity, facilitating trade and cultural exchange between various peoples as well as colonisation and conquest. The [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") maintained [nautical hegemony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mare_Nostrum "Mare Nostrum") over the sea for centuries and is the only state to have ever controlled all of its coast.

The Mediterranean Sea has an average depth of 1,500 m (4,900 ft) and the deepest recorded point is 5,109 ± 1 m (16,762 ± 3 ft) in the [Calypso Deep](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calypso_Deep "Calypso Deep") in the [Ionian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionian_Sea "Ionian Sea"). It lies between latitudes [30°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/30th_parallel_north "30th parallel north") and [46° N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/46th_parallel_north "46th parallel north") and longitudes [6° W](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/6th_meridian_west "6th meridian west") and [36° E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/36th_meridian_east "36th meridian east"). Its west–east length, from the Strait of Gibraltar to the [Gulf of Alexandretta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Alexandretta "Gulf of Alexandretta"), on the southeastern coast of [Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey"), is about 4,000 kilometres (2,500 mi). The north–south length varies greatly between different shorelines and whether only straight routes are considered. Also including longitudinal changes, the shortest shipping route between the multinational [Gulf of Trieste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Trieste "Gulf of Trieste") and the Libyan coastline of the [Gulf of Sidra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Sidra "Gulf of Sidra") is about 1,900 kilometres (1,200 mi). The water temperatures are mild in winter and warm in summer and give name to the [Mediterranean climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_climate "Mediterranean climate") type due to the majority of precipitation falling in the cooler months. Its southern and eastern coastlines are lined with hot deserts not far inland, but the immediate coastline on all sides of the Mediterranean tends to have strong maritime moderation.

The countries surrounding the Mediterranean and its marginal seas in clockwise order are [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain"), [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France"), [Monaco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monaco "Monaco"), [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy"), [Slovenia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovenia "Slovenia"), [Croatia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Croatia "Croatia"), [Bosnia and Herzegovina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina "Bosnia and Herzegovina"), [Montenegro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montenegro "Montenegro"), [Albania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albania "Albania"), [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece"), [Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey"), [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria"), [Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanon "Lebanon"), [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel"), [Palestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestine "Palestine") ([Gaza Strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_Strip "Gaza Strip")), [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt"), [Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya "Libya"), [Tunisia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunisia "Tunisia"), [Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria "Algeria"), and [Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco "Morocco"); [Cyprus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyprus "Cyprus") and [Malta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malta "Malta") are [island countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island_country "Island country") in the sea. In addition, [Northern Cyprus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Cyprus "Northern Cyprus") ([de facto state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_states_with_limited_recognition "List of states with limited recognition")) and two [overseas territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories "British Overseas Territories") of the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") ([Akrotiri and Dhekelia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akrotiri_and_Dhekelia "Akrotiri and Dhekelia"), and [Gibraltar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibraltar "Gibraltar")) also have [coastlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coast "Coast") along the Mediterranean Sea. The drainage basin encompasses a large number of other countries, the [Nile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile "Nile") being the longest river ending in the Mediterranean Sea.[^14] The Mediterranean Sea encompasses [a vast number of islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_islands_in_the_Mediterranean "List of islands in the Mediterranean"), some of them of volcanic origin. The two largest islands, in both area and population, are [Sicily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sicily "Sicily") and [Sardinia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sardinia "Sardinia").

[Wadj-Wer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wadj-Wer "Wadj-Wer"), or Wadj-Ur, ancient Egyptian name of the Mediterranean Sea  
in [hieroglyphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs "Egyptian hieroglyphs")

- [Romans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_people "Roman people") called the Mediterranean *Mare Magnum* ("Great Sea") or *Mare Internum* ("Internal Sea") and, starting with the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire"), *[Mare Nostrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mare_Nostrum "Mare Nostrum")* ("Our Sea"). The term *Mare Mediterrāneum* appears later: [Solinus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaius_Julius_Solinus "Gaius Julius Solinus") apparently used this in the 3rd century, but the earliest extant witness to it is in the 6th century,[^15] in [Isidore of Seville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isidore_of_Seville "Isidore of Seville").[^16] [^17] It means 'in the middle of land, inland' in [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language"), a compound of *medius* ('middle'), *terra* ('land, earth'), and *\-āneus* ('having the nature of'). The Latin word is a [calque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calque "Calque") of Greek μεσόγειος (*mesógeios*; 'inland'), from μέσος (*mésos*, 'in the middle') and γήινος (*gḗinos*, 'of the earth'), from γῆ (*gê*, 'land, earth'). The original meaning may have been 'the sea in the middle of the earth', rather than 'the sea enclosed by land'.[^18] [^19]
- In [Modern Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic "Modern Standard Arabic"), it is known as *al-Baḥr \[al-Abyaḍ\] al-Mutawassiṭ* (البحر \[الأبيض\] المتوسط) 'the \[White\] Middle Sea'. In Islamic and older Arabic literature, it was *Baḥr al-Rūm(ī)* (بحر الروم or بحر الرومي) 'the Sea of the Romans' or 'the Roman Sea'. At first, that name referred to only the Eastern Mediterranean, but it was later extended to the whole Mediterranean. Other Arabic names were *Baḥr al-šām(ī)* (بحر الشام) ("the Sea of Syria") and *Baḥr al-Maghrib* (بحر المغرب) ("the Sea of the West").[^20] [^17]
- The [Ancient Egyptians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptians "Ancient Egyptians") called the Mediterranean [*Wadj-wr* / *Wadj-Wer* / *Wadj-Ur*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wadj-Wer "Wadj-Wer"). This term (lit. 'great green') was the name given by the Ancient Egyptians to the semi-solid, semi-aquatic region characterized by papyrus forests to the north of the cultivated [Nile Delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile_Delta "Nile Delta"), and, by extension, the sea beyond.[^21]
- The [Carthaginians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carthaginians "Carthaginians") called it the "Syrian Sea". In ancient [Syriac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syriac_language "Syriac language") texts, [Phoenician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenician_language "Phoenician language") epics and in the Hebrew [Bible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bible "Bible"), it was primarily known as the "Great Sea", הים הגדול, *HaYam HaGadol*, ([Numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Numbers "Book of Numbers"); [Book of Joshua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Joshua "Book of Joshua"); [Ezekiel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Ezekiel "Book of Ezekiel")) or simply as "The Sea" ([1 Kings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Books_of_Kings "Books of Kings")). However, it has also been called the "Hinder Sea" because of its location on the west coast of the [region of Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria_\(region\) "Syria (region)") or the [Holy Land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Land "Holy Land") (and therefore behind a person facing the east), which is sometimes translated as "Western Sea". Another name was the "Sea of the [Philistines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philistines "Philistines") ", ([Book of Exodus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Exodus "Book of Exodus")). In [Modern Hebrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_Hebrew "Modern Hebrew"), it is called הים התיכון *HaYam HaTikhon*, 'the Middle Sea'.[^22]
- The [Ancient Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") called the Mediterranean simply ἡ θάλασσα ('the Sea') or sometimes ἡ μεγάλη θάλασσα ('the Great Sea'), ἡ ἡμετέρα θάλασσα ('Our Sea'), or ἡ θάλασσα ἡ καθ'ἡμᾶς ('the sea around us').[^23] [^24] According to Johann Knobloch, in [classical antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_antiquity "Classical antiquity"), cultures in the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant") used colors to refer to the cardinal points: black referred to the north (explaining the name [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea")), yellow or blue to east, red to south (e.g., the [Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea")), and white to west. This would explain the Greek *Áspri Thálassa*, the [Bulgarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulgarian_language "Bulgarian language") *Byalo More*, the Turkish *Akdeniz*, and the Arab nomenclature described above, lit. "White Sea".[^25]
- The [Old English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_English "Old English") name for the Mediterranean was the *Wendel-sæ*, or "Vandal Sea", after the [Vandals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vandals "Vandals") who had occupied the shores of North Africa in the [Migration Period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Migration_Period "Migration Period").[^26]
- One name for the Mediterranean in [Old (West) Norse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Norse "Old Norse") appears to have been *Jórsalahaf*, the "Sea of Jerusalem".[^27] The "Hreiðsea", *Hreiðmarar*, mentioned on the [Rök runestone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%C3%B6k_runestone "Rök runestone") might also refer to the Mediterranean.
- [Ancient Iranians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Iranian_peoples "Ancient Iranian peoples") called it the "Roman Sea", in Classic Persian texts was called *Daryāy-e Rōm* (دریای روم) which may be from [Middle Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Persian "Middle Persian") form, *Zrēh ī Hrōm* (𐭦𐭫𐭩𐭤 𐭩 𐭤𐭫𐭥𐭬).[^28] In [Classic Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_language "Persian language") texts it was called *Daryāy-e Šām* دریای شام) "The Western Sea" or "Syrian Sea".[^29]
- In [Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_language "Turkish language"), it is the *Akdeniz* 'the White Sea'; in Ottoman, ﺁق دكز, which sometimes means only the [Aegean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_Sea "Aegean Sea").[^30] The name is thought to be coming from the [colors associated with the cardinal directions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_direction#Northern_Eurasia "Cardinal direction") in [Turkic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_peoples "Turkic peoples") tradition, where "Ak" (white) stands for the west. It may be to contrast with the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea").[^20] [^22] [^31] In Persian, the name was translated as *Baḥr-i Safīd*, which was also used in later [Ottoman Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language "Ottoman Turkish language"). It is probably the origin of the colloquial Greek phrase Άσπρη Θάλασσα, *Áspri Thálassa*, 'White Sea'.[^20]

## History

### Ancient civilizations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/AntikeGriechen1.jpg/500px-AntikeGriechen1.jpg)

Greek (red) and Phoenician (yellow) colonies in antiquity c. the 6th century BC

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Roman_Empire_Trajan_117AD.png/500px-Roman_Empire_Trajan_117AD.png)

The Roman Empire in 117 AD, the end of the reign of Trajan

Major ancient civilizations were located around the Mediterranean. The sea provided routes for trade, colonization, and war, as well as food (from fishing and the gathering of other seafood) for numerous communities throughout the ages.[^32] The earliest advanced civilizations in the Mediterranean were the [Egyptians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") and the [Minoans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minoan_civilization "Minoan civilization"), who traded extensively with each other. Around 1200 BC the eastern Mediterranean was greatly affected by the [Bronze Age Collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age_Collapse "Bronze Age Collapse"), which resulted in the destruction of many cities and trade routes.[^33]

Two other notable Mediterranean civilizations in classical antiquity are the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece") [city-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City-state "City-state") and [Phoenicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia"), both of whom extensively colonized the coastlines of the Mediterranean.[^34]

[Darius I of Persia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darius_I_of_Persia "Darius I of Persia"), who conquered Ancient Egypt, built a canal linking the [Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea") to the Nile, and thus the Mediterranean. Darius's canal was wide enough for two [triremes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trireme "Trireme") to pass each other with oars extended and required four days to traverse.[^35]

Following the [Punic Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punic_Wars "Punic Wars") in the 3rd and 2nd centuries BC, the [Roman Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic "Roman Republic") defeated the [Carthaginians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carthaginians "Carthaginians") to become the preeminent power in the Western Mediterranean region.[^36] When [Augustus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustus "Augustus") founded the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire"), the Romans referred to the Mediterranean as *[Mare Nostrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mare_Nostrum "Mare Nostrum")* ("Our Sea").[^37] For the next 400 years, the Roman Empire completely controlled the Mediterranean Sea and virtually all its coastal regions from Gibraltar to the Levant,[^34] giving the lake the nickname "Roman Lake".[^38]

### Middle Ages and empires

The [Western Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire "Western Roman Empire") collapsed around 476 AD.[^39] The east was again dominant as Roman power still existed on in the [Byzantine Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire") formed in the 4th century from the eastern half of the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire").[^40] During that time, another power arose in the 7th century, and with it the religion of [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam"), which soon swept across from the east; at its greatest extent,[^41] the Arabs, under the [Umayyads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate"), controlled the [Iberian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula"), making a new phase of art and culture in the region.[^42]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/The_port_and_fleet_of_Genoa%2C_early_14th_century.jpg/250px-The_port_and_fleet_of_Genoa%2C_early_14th_century.jpg)

The port and fleet of Genoa in the early 14th century, by Quinto Cenni

A variety of foodstuffs, spices and crops were introduced to the western Mediterranean's Spain and [Sicily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirate_of_Sicily "Emirate of Sicily") during Arab rule, via the commercial networks of the Islamic world. These include sugarcane,[^43] rice,[^44] cotton, alfalfa, oranges,[^45] lemons,[^46] apricots,[^47] spinach,[^48] eggplants,[^49] carrots,[^50] [saffron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saffron "Saffron") [^51] and bananas.[^52] The Arabs also continued extensive cultivation and production of olive oil (the Spanish words for 'oil' and 'olive'— *aceite* and *aceituna*, respectively—are derived from the Arabic *al-zait*, meaning 'olive juice'),[^53] and pomegranates (the heraldic symbol of Granada) from classical [Greco-Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Roman_world "Greco-Roman world") times.

The [Arab invasions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Muslim_conquests "Early Muslim conquests") disrupted the trade relations between Western and Eastern Europe while disrupting trade routes with Eastern Asian Empires. This, however, had the indirect effect of promoting trade across the [Caspian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caspian_Sea "Caspian Sea"). The export of grains from [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") was re-routed towards the [Eastern world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_world "Eastern world"). Products from East Asian empires, like silk and spices, were carried from Egypt to ports like [Venice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Venice "Republic of Venice") and [Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantinople "Constantinople") by sailors and Jewish merchants. The [Viking raids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking_expansion "Viking expansion") further disrupted the trade in western Europe and brought it to a halt. However, the [Norsemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norsemen "Norsemen") developed the trade from Norway to the [White Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Sea "White Sea"), while also trading in luxury goods from [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Andalus "Al-Andalus") and the Mediterranean. The [Byzantines in the mid-8th century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire_under_the_Isaurian_dynasty "Byzantine Empire under the Isaurian dynasty") retook control of the area around the north-eastern part of the Mediterranean. Venetian ships from the 9th century armed themselves to counter the harassment by Arabs while concentrating trade of Asian goods in Venice.[^54]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Battle_of_Lepanto_1571.jpg/250px-Battle_of_Lepanto_1571.jpg)

The Battle of Lepanto, 1571, ended in victory for the European Holy League against the Ottoman Turks.

The [Fatimids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimids "Fatimids") maintained trade relations with the [Italian city-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_city-states "Italian city-states") like [Amalfi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_Amalfi "Duchy of Amalfi") and [Genoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa "Republic of Genoa") before the Crusades, according to the [Cairo Geniza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cairo_Geniza "Cairo Geniza") documents. A document dated 996 mentions Amalfian merchants living in [Cairo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cairo "Cairo"). Another letter states that the Genoese had traded with [Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandria "Alexandria"). The caliph [al-Mustansir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ma%27ad_al-Mustansir_Billah "Ma'ad al-Mustansir Billah") had allowed Amalfian merchants to reside in [Jerusalem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerusalem "Jerusalem") about 1060 in place of the Latin [hospice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospice "Hospice").[^55]

The [Crusades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusades "Crusades") led to the flourishing of trade between Europe and the *[outremer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outremer "Outremer")* region.[^56] Genoa, Venice and [Pisa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Pisa "Republic of Pisa") created colonies in regions controlled by the Crusaders and came to control the trade with the Orient. These colonies also allowed them to trade with the Eastern world. Though the fall of the Crusader states and attempts at banning of trade relations with Muslim states by the Popes temporarily disrupted the trade with the Orient, it however continued.[^57] Europe started to revive, however, when there was gradual centralization of state power in the [Renaissance of the 12th century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance_of_the_12th_century "Renaissance of the 12th century").[^58]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/De_Engels-Nederlandse_vloot_in_de_Baai_van_Algiers_ter_ondersteuning_van_het_ultimatum_tot_vrijlating_van_blanke_slaven%2C_26_augustus_1816._Rijksmuseum_SK-A-1377.jpeg/250px-thumbnail.jpeg)

The bombardment of Algiers by the Anglo-Dutch fleet in support of an ultimatum to release European slaves, August 1816

[Ottoman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") power based in Anatolia continued to grow, and in 1453 extinguished the Byzantine Empire with the [Conquest of Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conquest_of_Constantinople "Conquest of Constantinople").[^59] [Hayreddin Barbarossa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hayreddin_Barbarossa "Hayreddin Barbarossa"), the Ottoman captain is a symbol of this domination with the victory of the [Battle of Preveza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Preveza "Battle of Preveza") (1538), opening up [Tripoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripoli,_Libya "Tripoli, Libya") and the eastern Mediterranean to Ottoman rule.[^60] As the naval prowess of the European powers increased, they confronted Ottoman expansion in the region when the [Battle of Lepanto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Lepanto "Battle of Lepanto") (1571) damaging the power of the [Ottoman Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Navy "Ottoman Navy"). This was the last naval battle to be fought primarily between [galleys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galley "Galley").[^61]

The [Barbary pirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary_pirates "Barbary pirates") of [Northwest Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northwest_Africa "Northwest Africa") preyed on Christian shipping and coastlines in the Western Mediterranean Sea.[^62] According to Robert Davis, from the 16th to 19th centuries, pirates captured 1 million to 1.25 million Europeans as slaves.[^63]

The development of oceanic shipping began to affect the entire Mediterranean. Once, most of the trade between Western Europe and the East was [passing through the region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Mediterranean "Indo-Mediterranean"), but after the 1490s the development of a sea route to the Indian Ocean allowed the importation of Asian [spices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spice_trade "Spice trade") and other goods through the Atlantic ports of western Europe.[^64] [^65] [^66]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cena_da_Batalha_do_Nilo%2C_1_a_3_de_Agosto_de_1798_%28escola_europeia%2C_s%C3%A9c._XIX%29.png/250px-Cena_da_Batalha_do_Nilo%2C_1_a_3_de_Agosto_de_1798_%28escola_europeia%2C_s%C3%A9c._XIX%29.png)

Battle of the Nile during the French campaign in Egypt, August 1798

The sea remained strategically important. British mastery of [Gibraltar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibraltar "Gibraltar") ensured their influence in Africa and Southwest Asia. Especially after the naval battles of Abukir (1799, [Battle of the Nile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Nile "Battle of the Nile")) and [Trafalgar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar "Battle of Trafalgar") (1805), the British had for a long time strengthened their dominance in the Mediterranean.[^67] Wars included [Naval warfare in the Mediterranean during World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naval_warfare_in_the_Mediterranean_during_World_War_I "Naval warfare in the Mediterranean during World War I") [^68] and [Mediterranean theatre of World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_theatre_of_World_War_II "Mediterranean theatre of World War II").[^69]

With the opening of the lockless [Suez Canal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Canal "Suez Canal") in 1869, the flow of trade between Europe and Asia changed fundamentally. The fastest route now led through the Mediterranean towards East Africa and Asia. This led to a preference for the Mediterranean countries and their ports like [Trieste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trieste "Trieste") with direct connections to Central and Eastern Europe experienced a rapid economic rise. In the 20th century, the 1st and 2nd World Wars as well as the [Suez Crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Crisis "Suez Crisis") and the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") led to a shift of trade routes to the European northern ports, which changed again towards the southern ports through European integration, the activation of the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road") and free world trade.[^70]

### 21st century and migrations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/BlackMarble20161km.jpg/3840px-BlackMarble20161km.jpg)

[Satellite image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_imagery "Satellite imagery") of the Mediterranean Sea at night

In 2013, the [Maltese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malta "Malta") president described the Mediterranean Sea as a "cemetery" due to the large number of migrants who drowned there after their boats capsized.[^71] [European Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Parliament "European Parliament") president [Martin Schulz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Schulz "Martin Schulz") said in 2014 that Europe's migration policy "turned the Mediterranean into a graveyard", referring to the number of drowned refugees in the region as a direct result of the policies.[^72] An Azerbaijani official described the sea as "a burial ground... where people die".[^73]

Following the [2013 Lampedusa migrant shipwreck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2013_Lampedusa_migrant_shipwreck "2013 Lampedusa migrant shipwreck"), the [Italian government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Letta_Cabinet "Letta Cabinet") decided to strengthen the national system for the patrolling of the Mediterranean Sea by authorizing " [Operation Mare Nostrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Mare_Nostrum "Operation Mare Nostrum") ", a military and humanitarian mission in order to rescue the migrants and arrest the traffickers of immigrants. In 2015, more than one million migrants crossed the Mediterranean Sea into Europe.[^74]

Italy was particularly affected by the [European migrant crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_migrant_crisis "European migrant crisis"). Between 2013 and 2018, over 700,000 migrants landed in Italy,[^75] mainly sub-Saharan Africans.[^76]
