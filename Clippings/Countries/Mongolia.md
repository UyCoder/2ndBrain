**Mongolia** [^2] [^3] is a [landlocked country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landlocked_country "Landlocked country") in the [East Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asia "East Asia") region. It is bordered by [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia") to the north and [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") to the south, and covers an area of 1,564,116 square kilometers (603,909 square miles), making it the [18th-largest country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_area "List of countries and dependencies by area") in the world and the second largest landlocked country after [Kazakhstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan "Kazakhstan"), as well as the largest landlocked country that does not border an [inland sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endorheic_basin "Endorheic basin"). With a population of 3.5 million, Mongolia is the world's [most sparsely populated sovereign state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_population_density "List of countries and dependencies by population density"), excluding partially unrecognized [Western Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic "Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic"). The country constitutes a significant portion of the [Mongolian Plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_Plateau "Mongolian Plateau"), and its natural environment is characterized by grassy [steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steppe "Steppe"), mountains to the north and west, and the [Gobi Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gobi_Desert "Gobi Desert") to the south. [Ulaanbaatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulaanbaatar "Ulaanbaatar"), the capital and [largest city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Mongolia "List of cities in Mongolia"), is home to roughly half of the country's population.

The territory of modern-day Mongolia has been ruled by various [nomadic empires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomadic_empire "Nomadic empire"), including the [Xiongnu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiongnu "Xiongnu"), the [Xianbei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xianbei "Xianbei"), the [Rouran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rouran "Rouran"), the [First Turkic Khaganate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Turkic_Khaganate "First Turkic Khaganate"), the [Second Turkic Khaganate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Turkic_Khaganate "Second Turkic Khaganate"), the [Uyghur Khaganate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghur_Khaganate "Uyghur Khaganate") and others. In 1206, [Genghis Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genghis_Khan "Genghis Khan") founded the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire"), which became the largest [contiguous land empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_largest_empires "List of largest empires") in history. His grandson [Kublai Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kublai_Khan "Kublai Khan") conquered [China proper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_proper "China proper") and established the [Yuan dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_dynasty "Yuan dynasty"). After the collapse of the Yuan, the [Mongols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongols "Mongols") retreated to Mongolia and resumed their earlier pattern of factional conflict, except during the era of [Dayan Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dayan_Khan "Dayan Khan") and [Tumen Zasagt Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tumen_Zasagt_Khan "Tumen Zasagt Khan").

In the 16th century, [Tibetan Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism_in_Mongolia "Buddhism in Mongolia") spread to Mongolia, being further led by the [Manchu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchu_people "Manchu people") -founded [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty"), which absorbed the country in the 17th century. By the early 20th century, almost one-third of the adult male population were Buddhist monks.[^13] [^14] After the [collapse of the Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1911_Revolution "1911 Revolution") in 1911, Mongolia [declared independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_Revolution_of_1911 "Mongolian Revolution of 1911"), and achieved actual [independence from the Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_Revolution_of_1921 "Mongolian Revolution of 1921") in 1921. Shortly thereafter, the country became a [satellite state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_state "Satellite state") of the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"). In 1924, the [Mongolian People's Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Republic "Mongolian People's Republic") was founded as a [socialist state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_state "Socialist state").[^15] After the anti-communist [revolutions of 1989](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989 "Revolutions of 1989"), Mongolia conducted its own peaceful [democratic revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1990_Democratic_Revolution_in_Mongolia "1990 Democratic Revolution in Mongolia") in early 1990. This led to a [multi-party system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-party_system "Multi-party system"), a new [constitution of 1992](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Mongolia "Constitution of Mongolia"), and transition to a [market economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_economy "Market economy").

Approximately 30% of the population is [nomadic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomad "Nomad") or semi-nomadic; [horse culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horse_culture_in_Mongolia "Horse culture in Mongolia") remains integral. [Mahayana Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahayana_Buddhism "Mahayana Buddhism") is the majority religion (51.7%), with the irreligious being the second-largest group (40.6%). [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") is the third-largest religious identification (3.2%), concentrated among ethnic [Kazakhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhs "Kazakhs"). The vast majority of citizens are ethnic Mongols, with roughly 5% of the population being [Kazakhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhs "Kazakhs"), [Tuvans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuvans "Tuvans"), and other ethnic minorities, who are especially concentrated in the western regions. Mongolia is a member of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), [Asia Cooperation Dialogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia_Cooperation_Dialogue "Asia Cooperation Dialogue"), [G77](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_of_77 "Group of 77"), [Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_Infrastructure_Investment_Bank "Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank"), [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement") and a [NATO global partner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_NATO#Mongolia "Foreign relations of NATO"). Mongolia joined the [World Trade Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Trade_Organization "World Trade Organization") in 1997 and seeks to expand its participation in regional economic and trade groups.[^7]

## Etymology

The name Mongolia means the "Land of the Mongols" in Latin. The Mongolian word *"Mongol"* ([монгол](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BC%D0%BE%D0%BD%D0%B3%D0%BE%D0%BB#Mongolian "wikt:монгол")) is of uncertain etymology. Sükhbataar (1992) and de la Vaissière (2021) proposed it being a derivation from [Mugulü](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mugul%C3%BC "Mugulü"), the 4th-century founder of the [Rouran Khaganate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rouran_Khaganate "Rouran Khaganate"),[^16] first attested as the 'Mungu',[^17] ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"): [蒙兀](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%92%99%E5%85%80#Chinese "wikt:蒙兀"), Modern Chinese *Měngwù*, [Middle Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Chinese "Middle Chinese") *Muwngu*),[^18] a branch of the [Shiwei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiwei_people "Shiwei people") in an 8th-century [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty") list of northern tribes, presumably related to the [Liao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liao_dynasty "Liao dynasty") -era *Mungku* [^17] (Chinese: [蒙古](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%92%99%E5%8F%A4#Chinese "wikt:蒙古"), [Modern Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin "Pinyin") *Měnggǔ*, [Middle Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Chinese "Middle Chinese") *MuwngkuX*).[^19]

After the fall of the [Liao dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liao_dynasty "Liao dynasty") in 1125, the [Khamag Mongols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khamag_Mongol "Khamag Mongol") became a leading tribe on the [Mongolian Plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_Plateau "Mongolian Plateau"). However, their wars with the [Jurchen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jurchen_people "Jurchen people") -ruled [Jin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jin_dynasty_\(1115%E2%80%931234\) "Jin dynasty (1115–1234)") and the [Tatar confederation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatar_confederation "Tatar confederation") had weakened them. The last head of the tribe was [Yesügei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yesugei "Yesugei"), whose son [Temüjin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genghis_Khan "Genghis Khan") (Genghis Khan) eventually united all the [Shiwei tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiwei_people "Shiwei people") as the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire") (*Yekhe Monggol Ulus*). In the thirteenth century, the word Mongol grew into an umbrella term for a large group of [Mongolic-speaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolic_languages "Mongolic languages") tribes united under the rule of [Genghis Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genghis_Khan "Genghis Khan").[^20]

Since the adoption of the new [Constitution of Mongolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Mongolia "Constitution of Mongolia") on 13 February 1992, the official name of the state is "Mongolia" (*Mongol Uls*).[^21] [^22]

## History

### Prehistory and antiquity

The *[Khoit Tsenkher Cave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khoit_Tsenkher_Cave_Rock_Art "Khoit Tsenkher Cave Rock Art")* [^23] in [Khovd Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khovd_Province "Khovd Province") shows lively pink, brown, and red ocher paintings (dated to 20,000 years ago) of [mammoths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mammoths "Mammoths"), [lynx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lynx "Lynx"), [bactrian camels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bactrian_camel "Bactrian camel"), and [ostriches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostrich "Ostrich"), earning it the nickname "the [Lascaux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lascaux "Lascaux") of Mongolia". The [Venus figurines of Mal'ta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus_figurines_of_Mal%27ta "Venus figurines of Mal'ta") (21,000 years ago) testify to the level of Upper Paleolithic art in northern Mongolia; Mal'ta is now part of Russia. Neolithic agricultural settlements (c. 5500–3500 BC), such as those at Norovlin, Tamsagbulag, Bayanzag, and Rashaan Khad, predated the introduction of horse-riding nomadism, a pivotal event in the history of Mongolia which became the dominant culture. The [ethnogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_history_of_East_Asians "Genetic history of East Asians") of [Mongolic peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolic_peoples "Mongolic peoples") is largely linked with the expansion of [Ancient Northeast Asians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Northeast_Asian "Ancient Northeast Asian"). The Mongolian pastoralist lifestyle may in part be derived from the [Western Steppe Herders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Steppe_Herders "Western Steppe Herders"), but without much geneflow between these two groups, suggesting cultural transmission.[^24] [^25]

Horse-riding nomadism has been documented by archeological evidence in Mongolia during the Copper and Bronze Age [Afanasevo culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afanasevo_culture "Afanasevo culture") (3500–2500 BC);[^26] this [Indo-European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-European_migrations "Indo-European migrations") culture was active to the [Khangai Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khangai_Mountains "Khangai Mountains") in Central Mongolia. The wheeled vehicles found in the burials of the Afanasevans have been dated to before 2200 BC.[^27] Pastoral nomadism and metalworking became more developed with the later [Okunev culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Okunev_culture "Okunev culture") (2nd millennium BC), [Andronovo culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andronovo_culture "Andronovo culture") (2300–1000 BC) and [Karasuk culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karasuk_culture "Karasuk culture") (1500–300 BC), culminating with the Iron Age [Xiongnu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiongnu "Xiongnu") Empire in 209 BC. Monuments of the pre-Xiongnu Bronze Age include [deer stones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deer_stone "Deer stone"), keregsur [kurgans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurgans "Kurgans"), [square slab tombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slab_Grave_Culture "Slab Grave Culture"), and rock paintings.

Although cultivation of crops has continued since the Neolithic period, agriculture has always remained small in scale compared to pastoral nomadism. Agriculture may have first been introduced from the west or arose independently in the region. The population during the [Copper Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper_Age "Copper Age") has been described as mongoloid in the east of what is now Mongolia, and as europoid in the west.[^23] Tocharians ([Yuezhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuezhi "Yuezhi")) and [Scythians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scythians "Scythians") inhabited western Mongolia during the Bronze Age. The mummy of a Scythian warrior, which is believed to be about 2,500 years old, was a 30- to 40-year-old man with blond hair; it was found in [the Altai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altai_Mountains "Altai Mountains"), Mongolia.[^28] As equine nomadism was introduced into Mongolia, the political center of the [Eurasian Steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Steppe "Eurasian Steppe") also shifted to Mongolia, where it remained until the 18th century. The intrusions of northern pastoralists (e.g. the Guifang, Shanrong, and [Donghu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donghu_people "Donghu people")) into China during the [Shang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shang_dynasty "Shang dynasty") (1600–1046 BC) and [Zhou dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_dynasty "Zhou dynasty") (1046–256 BC) presaged the age of [nomadic empires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomadic_empire "Nomadic empire").

### Early states

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/ZaamarTomb.jpg/250px-ZaamarTomb.jpg)

7th-century Göktürk artifacts found 180 km (112 mi) from Ulaanbaatar

Since prehistoric times, Mongolia has been inhabited by nomads who, from time to time, formed great confederations that rose to power and prominence. Common institutions were the office of the [Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khan_\(title\) "Khan (title)"), the [Kurultai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurultai "Kurultai") (Supreme Council), left and right wings, imperial army ([Keshig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keshig "Keshig")) and the decimal military system. The first of these empires, the [Xiongnu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiongnu "Xiongnu") of undetermined ethnicity, were brought together by [Modu Shanyu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modu_Shanyu "Modu Shanyu") to form a confederation in 209 BC. Soon they emerged as the greatest threat to the [Qin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_dynasty "Qin dynasty"), forcing the latter to construct the [Great Wall of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China "Great Wall of China"). It was guarded by up to almost 300,000 soldiers during Marshal [Meng Tian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meng_Tian "Meng Tian") 's tenure, as a means of defense against the destructive Xiongnu raids. The vast Xiongnu empire (209 BC–93 AD) was followed by the Mongolic [Xianbei empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xianbei_state "Xianbei state") (93–234 AD), which also ruled more than the entirety of present-day Mongolia. The Mongolic Rouran Khaganate (330–555), of [Xianbei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xianbei "Xianbei") provenance was the first to use "Khagan" as an imperial title. It ruled a massive empire before being defeated by the [Göktürks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6kt%C3%BCrks "Göktürks") (555–745), an even larger empire.

The Göktürks laid siege to [Panticapaeum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panticapaeum "Panticapaeum"), present-day [Kerch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerch "Kerch"), in 576. They were succeeded by the [Uyghur Khaganate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghur_Khaganate "Uyghur Khaganate") (745–840) who were defeated by the Kyrgyz. The Mongolic [Khitans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khitan_people "Khitan people"), descendants of the Xianbei, ruled Mongolia during the Liao dynasty (907–1125), after which the [Khamag Mongol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khamag_Mongol "Khamag Mongol") (1125–1206) rose to prominence.

Lines 3–5 of the memorial inscription of [Bilge Khagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilge_Khagan "Bilge Khagan") (684–737) in central Mongolia summarizes the time of the [Khagans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khagan "Khagan"):

> In battles they subdued the nations of all four sides of the world and suppressed them. They made those who had heads bow their heads, and who had knees genuflect them. In the east up to the Kadyrkhan common people, in the west up to the Iron Gate they conquered... These Khagans were wise. These Khagans were great. Their servants were wise and great too. Officials were honest and direct with people. They ruled the nation this way. This way they held sway over them. When they died ambassadors from Bokuli Cholug (Baekje Korea), Tabgach (Tang China), Tibet (Tibetan Empire), [Avar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pannonian_Avars "Pannonian Avars") (Avar Khaganate), Rome (Byzantine Empire), [Kirgiz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yenisei_Kirghiz "Yenisei Kirghiz"), Uch-Kurykan, Otuz-Tatars, [Khitans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khitan_people "Khitan people"), [Tatabis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kumo_Xi "Kumo Xi") came to the funerals. So many people came to mourn over the great Khagans. They were famous Khagans.[^29]

### Mongol empire to early 20th century

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Yuen_Dynasty_1294_-_Goryeo_as_vassal.png/250px-Yuen_Dynasty_1294_-_Goryeo_as_vassal.png)

Yuan dynasty, c. 1294

In the chaos of the late 12th century, a chieftain named Temüjin finally succeeded in uniting the Mongol tribes between [Manchuria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchuria "Manchuria") and the [Altai Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altai_Mountains "Altai Mountains"). In 1206, he took the title [Genghis Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genghis_Khan#Name_and_title "Genghis Khan"), and waged a series of military campaigns – renowned for their brutality and ferocity – sweeping through much of Asia, and forming the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire"), the largest contiguous land empire in world history. Under his successors it stretched from present-day [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland "Poland") in the west to [Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korea "Korea") in the east, and from parts of [Siberia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberia "Siberia") in the north to the [Gulf of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman "Gulf of Oman") and [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam") in the south, covering some 33,000,000 square kilometers (13,000,000 sq mi),[^30] (22% of Earth's total land area) and had a population of over 100 million people (about a quarter of Earth's total population at the time). The emergence of [Pax Mongolica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pax_Mongolica "Pax Mongolica") also significantly eased trade and commerce across Asia during its height.[^31] [^32]

After Genghis Khan's death, the empire was subdivided into four kingdoms or [Khanates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khanate "Khanate"). These eventually became quasi-independent after the [Toluid Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toluid_Civil_War "Toluid Civil War") (1260–1264), which broke out in a battle for power following [Möngke Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6ngke_Khan "Möngke Khan") 's death in 1259. One of the khanates, the "Great Khaanate", consisting of the Mongol homeland and most of modern-day China, became known as the Yuan dynasty under [Kublai Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kublai_Khan "Kublai Khan"), the grandson of Genghis Khan. He set up his capital in present-day Beijing. After more than a century of power, the Yuan dynasty was overthrown by the Ming dynasty in 1368, and the Yuan court fled to the north, thus becoming the [Northern Yuan dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Yuan "Northern Yuan"). As the Ming armies pursued the Mongols into their homeland, they successfully sacked and destroyed the Mongol capital [Karakorum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakorum "Karakorum") and other cities. Some of these attacks were repelled by the Mongols under [Ayushridar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biligt%C3%BC_Khan_Ayushiridara "Biligtü Khan Ayushiridara") and his general [Köke Temür](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%B6ke_Tem%C3%BCr "Köke Temür").[^33]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/YuanEmperorAlbumKhubilaiPortrait.jpg/250px-YuanEmperorAlbumKhubilaiPortrait.jpg)

A portrait of Kublai Khan by Araniko (1245–1306)

After the expulsion of the Yuan rulers from [China proper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_proper "China proper"), the Mongols continued to rule their homeland, known in historiography as the Northern Yuan dynasty. With the division of the Mongol tribes, it was subsequently also known as "The Forty and the Four" (Döčin dörben) among them.[^34] The next centuries were marked by violent power struggles among various factions, notably the Genghisids and the non-Genghisid [Oirats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oirats "Oirats"), as well as by several Ming invasions (such as the [five expeditions led by the Yongle Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yongle_Emperor%27s_campaigns_against_the_Mongols "Yongle Emperor's campaigns against the Mongols")).

In the early 16th century, [Dayan Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dayan_Khan "Dayan Khan") and his [khatun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khatun "Khatun") [Mandukhai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandukhai "Mandukhai") reunited all Mongol groups under the Genghisids. In the mid-16th century, [Altan Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altan_Khan "Altan Khan") of the Tümed, a grandson of Dayan Khan – but not a hereditary or legitimate Khan – became powerful. He founded [Hohhot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hohhot "Hohhot") in 1557. After he met with the [Dalai Lama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3rd_Dalai_Lama "3rd Dalai Lama") in 1578, he ordered the introduction of [Tibetan Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism "Tibetan Buddhism") to Mongolia. (It was the second time this had occurred.) Abtai Khan of the [Khalkha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalkha_Mongols "Khalkha Mongols") converted to Buddhism and founded the [Erdene Zuu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erdene_Zuu_Monastery "Erdene Zuu Monastery") monastery in 1585. His grandson [Zanabazar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zanabazar "Zanabazar") became the first [Jebtsundamba Khutughtu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jebtsundamba_Khutuktu "Jebtsundamba Khutuktu") in 1640. Following the leaders, the entire Mongolian population embraced Buddhism. Each family kept scriptures and Buddha statues on an altar at the north side of their [yurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yurt "Yurt"). Mongolian nobles donated land, money and herders to the monasteries. As was typical in states with established religions, the top religious institutions, the monasteries, wielded significant temporal power in addition to spiritual power.[^35]

The last Khagan of the Northern Yuan was Ligden Khan in the early 17th century. He came into conflict with the Manchus over the looting of Chinese cities and alienated most Mongol tribes. After his death in 1634, most Inner Mongolian tribes had submitted to the Manchus by 1636, when they founded the Qing dynasty. The Khalkha followed in 1691, bringing all of present-day Mongolia under Manchu rule, as the Northern Yuan Mongols, unable to resist the Dzungars, also submitted in the late 17th century.[^36]

After several [Dzungar–Qing Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dzungar%E2%80%93Qing_Wars "Dzungar–Qing Wars"), the [Dzungars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dzungars "Dzungars") were virtually annihilated during the Qing conquest of Dzungaria in 1755 and 1758.[^37]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/76/Altan_Khan.jpg)

Altan Khan (1507–1582) founded the city of Hohhot, helped introduce Buddhism and originated the title of Dalai Lama.

Some scholars estimate that about 80% of the 600,000 or more [Dzungar were killed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dzungar_genocide "Dzungar genocide") by a combination of disease and warfare.[^38] Outer Mongolia was given relative autonomy, being administered by the hereditary Genghisid khanates of Tusheet Khan, Setsen Khan, Zasagt Khan and Sain Noyon Khan. The [Jebtsundamba Khutuktu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jebtsundamba_Khutuktu "Jebtsundamba Khutuktu") of Mongolia had immense *de facto* authority. The Manchu forbade mass Chinese immigration into the area, which allowed the Mongols to keep their culture. The Oirats who migrated to the [Volga steppes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pontic%E2%80%93Caspian_steppe "Pontic–Caspian steppe") in Russia became known as [Kalmyks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalmyks "Kalmyks").

The main trade route during this period was the [Tea Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberian_Route "Siberian Route") through Siberia; it had permanent stations located every 25 to 30 kilometers (16 to 19 mi), each of which was staffed by 5–30 chosen families.

Until 1911, the Qing dynasty maintained control of Mongolia with a series of alliances and intermarriages, as well as military and economic measures. [Ambans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amban "Amban"), Manchu "high officials", were installed in [Khüree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulaanbaatar "Ulaanbaatar"), [Uliastai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uliastai "Uliastai"), and [Khovd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khovd_\(city\) "Khovd (city)"), and the country was divided into numerous feudal and ecclesiastical fiefdoms (which also placed people in power with loyalty to the Qing). Over the course of the 19th century, the feudal lords attached more importance to representation and less importance to the responsibilities towards their subjects. The behavior of Mongolia's nobility, together with [usurious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usury "Usury") practices by Chinese traders and the collection of imperial taxes in silver instead of animals, resulted in widespread poverty among the nomads. By 1911 there were 700 large and small monasteries in Outer Mongolia; their 115,000 monks made up 21% of the population. Apart from the Jebtsundamba Khutuktu, there were 13 other reincarnating high lamas, called 'seal-holding saints' (*tamgatai khutuktu*), in Outer Mongolia.

### Modern history

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/BogdKhan.jpg/250px-BogdKhan.jpg)

The eighth Jebtsundamba Khutuktu, Bogd Khaan

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Map-of-Unified-Mongolia-1917.jpg/250px-Map-of-Unified-Mongolia-1917.jpg)

Map of unified Mongolia in 1917

With the fall of the Qing dynasty in 1911, Mongolia under the [Bogd Khaan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bogd_Khaan "Bogd Khaan") declared its independence. However, the newly established [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beiyang_government "Beiyang government") considered Mongolia to be part of its own territory. [Yuan Shikai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_Shikai "Yuan Shikai"), the [President of the Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_the_Republic_of_China "President of the Republic of China"), considered the new republic to be the [successor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Successor_state "Successor state") of the Qing. Bogd Khaan said that [both Mongolia and China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty_in_Inner_Asia "Qing dynasty in Inner Asia") had been administered by the Manchu during the Qing, and after the fall of the Qing dynasty in 1911, the contract of Mongolian submission to the Manchu had become invalid.[^39]

The area controlled by the Bogd Khaan was approximately that of the former [Outer Mongolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Mongolia "Outer Mongolia") during the Qing period. In 1919, after the [October Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/October_Revolution "October Revolution") in Russia, Chinese troops led by warlord [Xu Shuzheng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xu_Shuzheng "Xu Shuzheng") occupied Mongolia. Warfare erupted on the northern border. As a result of the [Russian Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Civil_War "Russian Civil War"), the [White](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_movement "White movement") Russian Lieutenant General [Baron Ungern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Ungern_von_Sternberg "Roman Ungern von Sternberg") led his troops into Mongolia in October 1920, defeating the Chinese forces in [Niislel Khüree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulaanbaatar "Ulaanbaatar") (now Ulaanbaatar) in early February 1921 with Mongol support.

To eliminate the threat posed by Ungern, [Bolshevik Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_SFSR "Russian SFSR") decided to support the establishment of a communist Mongolian government and army. This Mongolian army took the [Mongolian part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altanbulag,_Selenge "Altanbulag, Selenge") of [Kyakhta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyakhta "Kyakhta") from Chinese forces on 18 March 1921, and on 6 July, Russian and Mongolian troops arrived in Khüree. Mongolia declared its independence again on 11 July 1921.[^40] As a result, Mongolia was closely aligned with the Soviet Union over the next seven decades.

#### Mongolian People's Republic

In 1924, after the [Bogd Khaan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bogd_Khaan "Bogd Khaan") died of [laryngeal cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laryngeal_cancer "Laryngeal cancer") [^41] or, as some sources claim, at the hands of Russian spies,[^42] the country's [political system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_system "Political system") was changed. The [Mongolian People's Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Republic "Mongolian People's Republic") was established. In 1928, [Khorloogiin Choibalsan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khorloogiin_Choibalsan "Khorloogiin Choibalsan") rose to power. The early leaders of the Mongolian People's Republic (1921–1952) included many with [Pan-Mongolist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan-Mongolism "Pan-Mongolism") ideals. However, changing global politics and increased Soviet pressure led to the decline of Pan-Mongol aspirations in the following period.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Horloogiyn_Choybalsan.jpg/250px-Horloogiyn_Choybalsan.jpg)

Khorloogiin Choibalsan led Mongolia during the Stalinist era, and presided over an environment of intense political persecution.

[Khorloogiin Choibalsan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khorloogiin_Choibalsan "Khorloogiin Choibalsan") instituted collectivization of livestock, began the destruction of the Buddhist monasteries, and carried out [Stalinist purges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stalinist_repressions_in_Mongolia "Stalinist repressions in Mongolia"), which resulted in the murders of numerous monks and other leaders. In Mongolia during the 1920s, approximately one-third of the male population were monks. By the beginning of the 20th century, 750 monasteries were functioning in Mongolia and by the end of the 1930s almost all had been looted or razed.[^43]

In 1930, the Soviet Union stopped [Buryat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buryats "Buryats") migration to the [Mongolian People's Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Republic "Mongolian People's Republic") to prevent Mongolian reunification. All leaders of Mongolia who did not fulfill Stalin's demands to perform [Red Terror](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Terror "Red Terror") against Mongolians were executed, including [Peljidiin Genden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peljidiin_Genden "Peljidiin Genden") and [Anandyn Amar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anandyn_Amar "Anandyn Amar"). The [Stalinist purges in Mongolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Purge "Great Purge"), which began in 1937, killed more than 30,000 people. Under Stalinist influence in the [Mongolian People's Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Republic "Mongolian People's Republic"), an estimated 17,000 monks were killed, official figures show.[^44] [Choibalsan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khorloogiin_Choibalsan "Khorloogiin Choibalsan"), who led a dictatorship and organized [Stalinist purges in Mongolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stalinist_repressions_in_Mongolia "Stalinist repressions in Mongolia") between 1937 and 1939, died suspiciously in the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") in 1952. [Comintern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comintern "Comintern") leader [Bohumír Šmeral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohum%C3%ADr_%C5%A0meral "Bohumír Šmeral") said, "People of Mongolia are not important, the land is important. Mongolian land is larger than England, France and Germany".[^45]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/MNRA_soldiers_1939.jpg/250px-MNRA_soldiers_1939.jpg)

Mongolian troops fight against the Japanese counterattack at Khalkhin Gol, 1939.

After the [Japanese invasion of neighboring Manchuria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_invasion_of_Manchuria "Japanese invasion of Manchuria") in 1931, Mongolia was threatened on this front. During the [Soviet-Japanese Border War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battles_of_Khalkhin_Gol "Battles of Khalkhin Gol") of 1939, the Soviet Union successfully defended Mongolia against Japanese expansionism. Mongolia fought against Japan during the [Battles of Khalkhin Gol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battles_of_Khalkhin_Gol "Battles of Khalkhin Gol") in 1939 and during the [Soviet–Japanese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet%E2%80%93Japanese_War "Soviet–Japanese War") in August 1945 to liberate Inner Mongolia from Japan and [Mengjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mengjiang "Mengjiang").[^46]

#### Cold War

The February 1945 [Yalta Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yalta_Conference "Yalta Conference") provided for the Soviet Union's participation in the [Pacific War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_War "Pacific War"). One of the Soviet conditions for its participation, put forward at Yalta, was that after the war Outer Mongolia would retain its independence. [The referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1945_Mongolian_independence_referendum "1945 Mongolian independence referendum") took place on 20 October 1945, with (according to official numbers) 100% of the electorate voting for independence.[^47]

After the establishment of the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China"), both countries confirmed their mutual recognition on 6 October 1949. However, the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China") used its [Security Council veto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_veto_power "United Nations Security Council veto power") in 1955, to stop the admission of the [Mongolian People's Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Republic "Mongolian People's Republic") to the United Nations on the grounds it recognized all of Mongolia —including Outer Mongolia— as part of China. This was the only time the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_and_the_United_Nations "Republic of China and the United Nations") ever used its veto. Hence, and because of the repeated threats to veto by the ROC, Mongolia did not join the UN until 1961 when the Soviet Union agreed to lift its veto on the admission of [Mauritania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauritania "Mauritania") (and any other newly independent African state), in return for the admission of Mongolia. Faced with pressure from nearly all the other African countries, the ROC relented under protest. Mongolia and Mauritania were both admitted to the UN on 27 October 1961.[^48] [^49] [^50] (see [China and the United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_and_the_United_Nations "China and the United Nations"))

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Yumjaagiin_Tsedenbal_Berlin%2C_VIII.jpg/250px-Yumjaagiin_Tsedenbal_Berlin%2C_VIII.jpg)

Mongolian Premier Yumjaagiin Tsedenbal was the longest-serving leader in the Eastern Bloc, with over 44 years in office.

On 26 January 1952, [Yumjaagiin Tsedenbal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yumjaagiin_Tsedenbal "Yumjaagiin Tsedenbal") took power in Mongolia after the death of Choibalsan. Tsedenbal was the leading political figure in Mongolia for more than 30 years.[^51] While Tsedenbal was visiting Moscow in August 1984, his severe illness prompted the parliament to announce his retirement and replace him with [Jambyn Batmönkh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jambyn_Batm%C3%B6nkh "Jambyn Batmönkh").

#### Post-Cold War

The [dissolution of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union "Dissolution of the Soviet Union") in 1991 strongly influenced Mongolian politics and [youth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Youth_in_Mongolia "Youth in Mongolia"). Its people undertook the [peaceful Democratic Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_Revolution_of_1990 "Mongolian Revolution of 1990") in [January 1990](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989 "Revolutions of 1989") and the introduction of a [multi-party system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-party_system "Multi-party system") and a market economy.[^52] At the same time, the transformation of the former [Marxist-Leninist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxist-Leninist "Marxist-Leninist") [Mongolian People's Revolutionary Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Party "Mongolian People's Party") to the current social democratic [Mongolian People's Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Party "Mongolian People's Party") reshaped the country's political landscape.

A [new constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Mongolia "Constitution of Mongolia") was introduced in 1992, and the term "People's Republic" was dropped from the country's name.[^53] The [transition to a market economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Mongolia#Transition_to_a_market_economy "Economy of Mongolia") was often rocky; during the early 1990s the country had to deal with high inflation and food shortages.[^54] The first election victories for non-communist parties came in 1993 (presidential elections) and 1996 (parliamentary elections). China has supported Mongolia's application for membership in the [Asia Cooperation Dialogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia_Cooperation_Dialogue "Asia Cooperation Dialogue") (ACD), [Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia-Pacific_Economic_Cooperation "Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation") (APEC) and granting it observer status in the [Shanghai Cooperation Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Cooperation_Organisation "Shanghai Cooperation Organisation").[^55]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Mongolia_1996_CIA_map.jpg/250px-Mongolia_1996_CIA_map.jpg)

The southern portion of Mongolia is taken up by the Gobi Desert, while the northern and western portions are mountainous.

At 1,564,116 km <sup>2</sup> (603,909 mi <sup>2</sup>), Mongolia is the world's [18th-largest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states_and_dependencies_by_area "List of sovereign states and dependencies by area") country.[^56] It is significantly larger than the next-largest country, Peru. It mostly lies between latitudes [41°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/41st_parallel_north "41st parallel north") and [52°N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/52nd_parallel_north "52nd parallel north") (a small area is north of 52°), and longitudes [87°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/87th_meridian_east "87th meridian east") and [120°E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/120th_meridian_east "120th meridian east"). As a point of reference the northernmost part of Mongolia is on roughly the same latitude as Berlin (Germany) and Saskatoon (Canada), while the southernmost part is on roughly the same latitude as Rome (Italy) and Chicago (US). The westernmost part of Mongolia is on roughly the same longitude as Kolkata in India, while the easternmost part is on the same longitude as Qinhuangdao and Hangzhou in China, as well as the western edge of Taiwan. Although Mongolia does not share a border with Kazakhstan, its westernmost point is only 36.76 kilometers (22.84 mi) from Kazakhstan, nearly making a [quadripoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadripoint "Quadripoint").

The geography of Mongolia is varied, with the [Gobi Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gobi_Desert "Gobi Desert") to the south and cold, mountainous regions to the north and west. Much of Mongolia consists of the [Mongolian-Manchurian grassland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian-Manchurian_grassland "Mongolian-Manchurian grassland"), with forested areas accounting for 11.2% of the total land area.[^57] The whole of Mongolia is considered to be part of the Mongolian Plateau. The highest point in Mongolia is the [Khüiten Peak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kh%C3%BCiten_Peak "Khüiten Peak") in the [Tavan bogd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tavan_bogd "Tavan bogd") massif in the far west at 4,374 m (14,350 ft). The basin of the [Uvs Lake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uvs_Lake "Uvs Lake"), shared with [Tuva Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuva_Republic "Tuva Republic") in Russia, is a natural [World Heritage Site](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Site "World Heritage Site").

### Climate

Mongolia is known as the "Land of the Eternal Blue Sky" or "Country of Blue Sky" (Mongolian: "Mönkh khökh tengeriin oron") because it has over 250 sunny days a year.[^58] [^59] [^60] [^61]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Koppen-Geiger_Map_MNG_present.svg/250px-Koppen-Geiger_Map_MNG_present.svg.png)

Köppen climate classification zones in Mongolia

Most of the country is hot in the summer and extremely cold in the winter, with January averages dropping as low as −30 °C (−22 °F).[^62] A vast front of cold, heavy, shallow air comes in from Siberia in winter and collects in river valleys and low basins, causing very cold temperatures, while slopes of mountains are much warmer due to the effects of [temperature inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature_inversion "Temperature inversion") (temperature increases with altitude).

In winter the whole of Mongolia comes under the influence of the [Siberian Anticyclone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberian_Anticyclone "Siberian Anticyclone"). The localities most severely affected by this cold weather are Uvs province ([Ulaangom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulaangom "Ulaangom")), western Khovsgol ([Rinchinlhumbe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rinchinlkhumbe,_Khovsgol "Rinchinlkhumbe, Khovsgol")), eastern Zavkhan ([Tosontsengel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tosontsengel,_Zavkhan "Tosontsengel, Zavkhan")), northern Bulgan (Hutag) and eastern Dornod province (Khalkhiin Gol). Ulaanbaatar is strongly, but less severely, affected. The cold gets less severe as one goes south, reaching the warmest January temperatures in Omnogovi Province ([Dalanzadgad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalanzadgad "Dalanzadgad"), [Khanbogd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khanbogd,_%C3%96mn%C3%B6govi "Khanbogd, Ömnögovi")) and the region of the Altai mountains bordering China. A unique microclimate is the fertile grassland-forest region of central and eastern Arkhangai Province ([Tsetserleg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsetserleg_\(city\) "Tsetserleg (city)")) and northern Ovorkhangai Province ([Arvaikheer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arvaikheer "Arvaikheer")) where January temperatures are on average the same and often higher than the warmest desert regions to the south in addition to being more stable. The [Khangai Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khangai_Mountains "Khangai Mountains") play a certain role in forming this microclimate. In Tsetserleg, the warmest town in this microclimate, nighttime January temperatures rarely go under −30 °C (−22 °F) while daytime January temperatures often reach 0 °C (32 °F) to 5 °C (41 °F).[^63] [^64]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Gorkhi-Terelj_National_Park.jpg/250px-Gorkhi-Terelj_National_Park.jpg)

The Khentii Mountains in Terelj, close to the birthplace of Genghis Khan

The country is subject to occasional harsh climatic conditions known as *[zud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zud "Zud").* It results in large proportions of the country's livestock dying from starvation or freezing temperatures or both, resulting in economic upheaval for the largely pastoral population. The annual average temperature in Ulaanbaatar is −1.3 °C (29.7 °F), making it the world's coldest capital city.[^62] Mongolia is high, cold and windy.[^65] It has an extreme continental climate with long, cold winters and short summers, during which most of its annual precipitation falls.[^65] The country averages 257 cloudless days a year, and it is usually at the center of a region of high atmospheric pressure.[^65] Precipitation is highest in the north (average of 200 to 350 millimeters (8 to 14 in) per year) and lowest in the south, which receives 100 to 200 millimeters (4 to 8 in) annually.[^65] The highest annual precipitation of 622.297 mm (24.500 in) occurred in the forests of [Bulgan Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulgan_Province "Bulgan Province") near the border with Russia and the lowest of 41.735 mm (1.643 in) occurred in the Gobi Desert (period 1961–1990).[^66] The sparsely populated far north of Bulgan Province averages 600 mm (24 in) in annual precipitation which means it receives more precipitation than Beijing (571.8 mm or 22.51 in) or Berlin (571 mm or 22.5 in).

### Environmental issues

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/A_landscape_picture_of_the_Tost_Local_Protected_Area_in_South_Gobi%2C_Mongolia_02.JPG/250px-A_landscape_picture_of_the_Tost_Local_Protected_Area_in_South_Gobi%2C_Mongolia_02.JPG)

The Gobi Desert, Ömnögovi Province. Desertification is a key issue in Mongolia.

There are many pressing [environmental issues in Mongolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_issues_in_Mongolia "Environmental issues in Mongolia") that are detrimental to both human and environmental wellness. These problems have arisen in part due to natural factors, but increasingly because of human actions. One of these issues is [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change"), which will be responsible for an increase in [desertification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desertification "Desertification"), natural disasters, and [land degradation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_degradation "Land degradation").[^67] Another is [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation "Deforestation"), which is expanding due to human activity, pests, disease, and fires. Mongolian lands are becoming more arid through desertification, a process that is being exacerbated due to irresponsible [land use](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_use "Land use"). Additionally, more and more species are disappearing and at risk for extinction. Moreover, especially in population centers, Mongolians deal with [air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_pollution "Air pollution") and [water pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_pollution "Water pollution") caused by [industrialization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrialization "Industrialization").

### Wildlife

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/KhongorynElsCamels.jpg/250px-KhongorynElsCamels.jpg)

Bactrian camels by sand dunes in Gobi Desert

The name "Gobi" is a Mongol term for a desert steppe, which usually refers to a category of arid rangeland with insufficient vegetation to support [marmots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marmot "Marmot") but with enough to support camels.[^65] Mongols distinguish Gobi from desert proper, although the distinction is not always apparent to outsiders unfamiliar with the Mongolian landscape.[^65]

Gobi rangelands are fragile and easily destroyed by overgrazing, which results in expansion of the true desert, a stony waste where not even [Bactrian camels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bactrian_camel "Bactrian camel") can survive.[^65] The arid conditions in the Gobi are attributed to the [rain shadow effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain_shadow_effect "Rain shadow effect") caused by the Himalayas. Before the Himalayas were formed by the collision of the Indo-Australian plate with the Eurasian plate 10 million years ago, Mongolia was a flourishing habitat for major fauna but still somewhat arid and cold due to distance from sources of evaporation. Sea turtle and mollusk fossils have been found in the Gobi, apart from well-known dinosaur fossils. [Tadpole shrimps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tadpole_shrimps "Tadpole shrimps") are still found in the Gobi today. The eastern part of Mongolia including the [Onon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onon_River "Onon River") and [Kherlen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kherlen_River "Kherlen River") rivers and [Lake Buir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Buir "Lake Buir") form part of the [Amur river](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amur_river "Amur river") basin draining to the Pacific Ocean. It hosts some unique species like the Eastern brook lamprey, Daurian crayfish (*cambaroides dauricus*) and Daurian pearl oyster (*dahurinaia dahurica*) in the Onon/Kherlen rivers as well as Siberian prawn (*exopalaemon modestus*) in Lake Buir.
