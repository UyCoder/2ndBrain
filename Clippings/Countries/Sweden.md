**Sweden**,[^5] formally the **Kingdom of Sweden**,[^6] [^7] is a [Nordic country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nordic_countries "Nordic countries") located on the [Scandinavian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scandinavian_Peninsula "Scandinavian Peninsula") in [Northern Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Europe "Northern Europe"). It borders [Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway "Norway") to the west and north, and [Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland "Finland") to the east, and shares a maritime border with [Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark "Denmark") to the south. At 450,295 square kilometres (173,860 sq mi) [^13] and with a population of 10.6 million, Sweden is the largest and most populous Nordic country, and is the [fifth-largest country in Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_European_countries_by_area "List of European countries by area"). Its capital and largest city is [Stockholm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stockholm "Stockholm"). The population density is 25.5 inhabitants per square kilometre (66/sq mi), and 88% of [Swedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedes "Swedes") reside in urban areas,[^20] mostly in the southern and central portions of the country. Sweden's urban areas together cover 1.5% of its land area. Sweden has a diverse [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_of_Sweden "Climate of Sweden") owing to the length of the country, which ranges from [55°N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/55th_parallel_north "55th parallel north") to [69°N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/69th_parallel_north "69th parallel north").

Sweden has been inhabited since [prehistoric times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prehistoric_Sweden "Prehistoric Sweden") around 12,000 BC. The inhabitants emerged as the [Geats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geats "Geats") (Swedish: *Götar*) and [Swedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedes_\(tribe\) "Swedes (tribe)") (*Svear*), who formed part of the sea-faring peoples known as the [Norsemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norsemen "Norsemen"). A unified Swedish state was established during the late 10th century. In 1397, Sweden joined Norway and Denmark to form the Scandinavian [Kalmar Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalmar_Union "Kalmar Union"),[^21] which Sweden left in 1523. When Sweden became involved in the [Thirty Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War "Thirty Years' War") on the Protestant side, an expansion of its territories began, forming the [Swedish Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_Empire "Swedish Empire"), which remained one of the [great powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_power "Great power") of Europe until the early 18th century. During this era Sweden [controlled much of the Baltic Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominium_maris_baltici "Dominium maris baltici"). Most of the conquered territories outside the Scandinavian Peninsula were lost during the 18th and 19th centuries. The eastern half of Sweden, present-day Finland, was lost to [Imperial Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") in 1809. The last war in which Sweden was directly involved was in 1814, when Sweden by military means forced Norway into a [personal union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_between_Sweden_and_Norway "Union between Sweden and Norway"), a union which lasted until 1905.

Sweden is a highly developed country ranked fifth in the [Human Development Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index").[^22] It is a [constitutional monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy") and a [parliamentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system") democracy, with [legislative power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature "Legislature") vested in the 349-member [unicameral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicameral "Unicameral") *[Riksdag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riksdag "Riksdag")*. It is a [unitary state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_state "Unitary state"), divided into [21 counties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counties_of_Sweden "Counties of Sweden") and [290 municipalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Municipalities_of_Sweden "Municipalities of Sweden"). Sweden maintains a [Nordic social welfare system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nordic_model "Nordic model") that provides [universal health care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_health_care "Universal health care") and [tertiary education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tertiary_education "Tertiary education") for its citizens. It has the [world's 14th highest GDP per capita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\)_per_capita "List of countries by GDP (nominal) per capita") and [ranks very highly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_rankings_of_Sweden "International rankings of Sweden") in [quality of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quality_of_life "Quality of life"), [health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_in_Sweden "Health in Sweden"), [education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Education_Index "Education Index"), protection of [civil liberties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_in_the_World "Freedom in the World"), economic competitiveness, [income equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gini_coefficient "Gini coefficient"), [gender equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender_equality "Gender equality") and [prosperity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legatum_Prosperity_Index "Legatum Prosperity Index").[^23] [^24] Sweden joined the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") in 1995, and in response to the [2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine"), [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") in 2024. It is also a [member](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_states_of_the_United_Nations "Member states of the United Nations") of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), the [Schengen Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schengen_Area "Schengen Area"), the [Council of Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Europe "Council of Europe"), the [Nordic Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nordic_Council "Nordic Council"), the [World Trade Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Trade_Organization "World Trade Organization") and the [Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development "Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development") (OECD).

## Etymology

The name for Sweden is generally agreed to derive from the [Proto-Indo-European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language "Proto-Indo-European language") root \* *[s(w)e](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/sw%C3%A9 "wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/swé")*, meaning 'one's own', referring to one's own tribe from the tribal period.[^25] [^26] [^27] The native Swedish name, *Sverige* (a compound of the words *Svea* and *rike*, first recorded in the cognate *Swēorice* in *[Beowulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beowulf "Beowulf")*),[^28] translates as "realm of the [Swedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedes_\(tribe\) "Swedes (tribe)") ", which excluded the [Geats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geats "Geats") in [Götaland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6taland "Götaland").

The contemporary English variation was derived in the 17th century from [Middle Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Dutch "Middle Dutch") and [Middle Low German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Low_German "Middle Low German"). As early as 1287, references are found in Middle Dutch referring to a *lande van sweden* ("land of \[the\] Swedes"), with *swede* as the singular form.[^29] In [Old English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_English "Old English") the country was known as *Swéoland* or *Swíoríce*, and in [Early Modern English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Modern_English "Early Modern English") as *Swedeland*.[^30] Some [Finnic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnic_languages "Finnic languages"), such as [Finnish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_language "Finnish language") and [Estonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estonian_language "Estonian language"), use the terms *Ruotsi* and *Rootsi*; these variations refer to the [Rus' people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rus%27_people "Rus' people") who inhabited the coastal areas of [Roslagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roslagen "Roslagen") in [Uppland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uppland "Uppland") and who gave their name to Russia.[^31]

## History

### Prehistory

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Vendel_I_helmet_456057.jpg/250px-Vendel_I_helmet_456057.jpg)

A Vendel-era helmet, at the Swedish Museum of National Antiquities

Sweden's prehistory begins in the [Allerød oscillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aller%C3%B8d_oscillation "Allerød oscillation"), a warm period around 12,000 BC,[^32] with Late [Palaeolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palaeolithic "Palaeolithic") [reindeer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reindeer "Reindeer") -hunting camps of the [Bromme culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromme_culture "Bromme culture") at the edge of the ice in what is now the country's southernmost province, [Scania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scania "Scania"). This period was characterised by small clans of [hunter-gatherers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hunter-gatherer "Hunter-gatherer") who relied on [flint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flint "Flint") technology.[^33]

Sweden and its people were first described by the Roman historian [Publius Cornelius Tacitus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Publius_Cornelius_Tacitus "Publius Cornelius Tacitus") in his *[Germania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germania_\(book\) "Germania (book)")* (98 AD).[^34] In [*Germania* 44 and 45](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Germania#XLIV "s:Germania") he mentions the Swedes (*Suiones*) as a powerful tribe with ships that had a [prow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prow "Prow") at each end ([longships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longship "Longship")).[^35] Which kings (\* *kuningaz*) ruled these Suiones is unknown, but [Norse mythology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norse_mythology "Norse mythology") presents a long line of legendary and semi-legendary kings going back to the last centuries BC. The [runic script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Runes "Runes") was in use among the south Scandinavian elite by at least the second century AD, but all that has survived from the Roman Period is curt inscriptions demonstrating that the people of south Scandinavia spoke [Proto-Norse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Norse "Proto-Norse") at the time, a language ancestral to Swedish and other [North Germanic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Germanic_languages "North Germanic languages").[^36]

In the sixth century, the Byzantine historian [Jordanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordanes "Jordanes") names two tribes living in [Scandza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scandza "Scandza"), both of which are now considered to be synonymous with the Swedes: the *Suetidi* and *Suehans*.[^37] The *Suehans* were known to the Roman world as suppliers of black fox skins and, according to Jordanes, had very fine horses, similar to those of the *[Thyringi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thuringians "Thuringians")* of *Germania* (" *alia vero gens ibi moratur Suehans, quae velud Thyringi equis utuntur eximiis* ").

### Viking Age

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Vikings-Voyages.png/330px-Vikings-Voyages.png)

Viking expeditions (blue lines) and settlements (bright green)

The Swedish [Viking Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking_Age "Viking Age") lasted roughly from the eighth century to the 11th century. It is believed that Swedish Vikings and [Gutar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gutes "Gutes") mainly travelled east and south, going to Finland, Estonia, the [Baltic countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_countries "Baltic countries"), Russia, Belarus, Ukraine, the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea") and even as far as [Baghdad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baghdad "Baghdad"). Their routes passed [through the Dnieper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Trade_Route_from_the_Varangians_to_the_Greeks "The Trade Route from the Varangians to the Greeks") south to [Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantinople "Constantinople"), on which they carried out numerous raids. The [Byzantine emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Emperor "Byzantine Emperor") [Theophilos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theophilos_\(emperor\) "Theophilos (emperor)") noticed their great skills in war and invited them to serve as his personal bodyguard, known as the [Varangian Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varangian_Guard "Varangian Guard"). The Swedish Vikings, called [Rus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rus_\(people\) "Rus (people)"), are believed to be the founders of [Kievan Rus'](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27 "Kievan Rus'").[^38] The Vikings were described by many outside sources, such as the Arab traveller [Ibn Fadlan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Fadlan "Ibn Fadlan").[^39] The actions of these [Swedish Vikings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vikings "Vikings") are commemorated on many [runestones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Runestone "Runestone") in Sweden, such as the [Greece runestones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece_runestones "Greece runestones") and the [Varangian runestones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varangian_runestones "Varangian runestones"). There was also considerable participation in expeditions westwards, which are commemorated on stones such as the [England runestones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/England_runestones "England runestones"). The last major Swedish Viking expedition appears to have been the ill-fated expedition of [Ingvar the Far-Travelled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ingvar_the_Far-Travelled "Ingvar the Far-Travelled") to [Serkland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serkland "Serkland"), the region south-east of the [Caspian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caspian_Sea "Caspian Sea"). Its members are commemorated on the [Ingvar runestones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ingvar_runestones "Ingvar runestones"), none of which mentions any survivor.[^40]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Bildsten_med_skepp_800-1099_Tj%C3%A4ngvide%2C_Gotland.jpg/250px-Bildsten_med_skepp_800-1099_Tj%C3%A4ngvide%2C_Gotland.jpg)

The Tjängvide image stone dating from 800 to 1099, example of Viking art

During the early stages of the Viking Age, a centre of trade in [Northern Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Europe "Northern Europe") developed at [Birka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birka "Birka") on the island of [Björkö](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bj%C3%B6rk%C3%B6,_Eker%C3%B6 "Björkö, Ekerö"), not far from where Stockholm was later constructed, in mid-latitude Sweden. Birka was founded around 750 AD as a trading port by a king or merchants trying to control trade.[^41] Birka was the [Baltic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_Sea "Baltic Sea") link in the [Dnieper trade route](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dnieper_trade_route "Dnieper trade route") through [Ladoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staraya_Ladoga "Staraya Ladoga") (*Aldeigja*) and [Novgorod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Novgorod_Land "Novgorod Land") (*Holmsgard*) to the [Byzantine Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire") and the [Abbasid Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate "Abbasid Caliphate").[^42] It was abandoned around 975, at about the same time [Sigtuna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigtuna "Sigtuna") was founded as a town some 35 km to the northeast. It has been estimated that the population in Viking Age Birka was between 500 and 1000 people.[^41] Archaeological finds indicate that Birka still was wealthy in the 9th and 10th centuries. Thousands of graves, coins, jewellery and other luxury items have been found there.[^43]

### Kingdom of Sweden

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Map_of_Sweden_ca_1280.png/250px-Map_of_Sweden_ca_1280.png)

A rough map of the extent of Swedish rule, c. 1280

The exact age of the kingdom of Sweden is unknown.[^44] Determining its age depends mostly on whether Sweden is considered a nation when the *[Svear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Svear "Svear")* (Swedes) ruled Svealand or when the *Svear* and the *[Götar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6tar "Götar")* (Geats) of Götaland were united under a single ruler. Svealand was described by [Tacitus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tacitus "Tacitus") in 98 AD as being under a single ruler, though it is unknown how long this had been the case. The epic poem *Beowulf* describes semi-legendary [Swedish-Geatish wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish-Geatish_wars "Swedish-Geatish wars") in the sixth century.

However, historians typically start the line of [Swedish monarchs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_monarchs "Swedish monarchs") from when Svealand and Götaland were ruled under the same king, namely [Erik the Victorious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erik_the_Victorious "Erik the Victorious") and his son [Olof Skötkonung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olof_Sk%C3%B6tkonung "Olof Skötkonung") in the tenth century. These events are often described as the [consolidation of Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consolidation_of_Sweden "Consolidation of Sweden"), although substantial areas were conquered and incorporated later. In this context, "Götaland" primarily refers to the provinces of [Östergötland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%96sterg%C3%B6tland "Östergötland") and [Västergötland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V%C3%A4sterg%C3%B6tland "Västergötland"). [Småland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sm%C3%A5land "Småland") was of little interest at the time due to its deep pine forests, with only the city of [Kalmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalmar "Kalmar") and its castle holding any significant importance. There were also Swedish settlements along the southern coastline of [Norrland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norrland "Norrland"), one of the four [lands of Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lands_of_Sweden "Lands of Sweden").[^45]

[Saint Ansgar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ansgar "Ansgar") is traditionally credited with introducing Christianity to Sweden in 829, but the new religion did not begin to fully replace [paganism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paganism "Paganism") until the 12th century. During that century, Sweden was undergoing dynastic struggles between the [Erik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Eric "House of Eric") and [Sverker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Sverker "House of Sverker") clans. The conflict ended when a third clan married into the Erik clan, founding the [Bjälbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Bj%C3%A4lbo "House of Bjälbo") dynasty, which gradually consolidated Sweden into a stronger state. According to the *Legend of [Saint Erik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Erik "Saint Erik")* and the *[Erik's Chronicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erik%27s_Chronicle "Erik's Chronicle")*, Swedish kings conducted [a series of Crusades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_Crusades_\(disambiguation\) "Swedish Crusades (disambiguation)") to pagan Finland and started conflicts with the [Rus'](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27 "Kievan Rus'"), who by then had no further connections with Sweden.[^46] As a result of the Crusades, mostly with the Second Swedish Crusade led by [Birger Jarl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birger_Jarl "Birger Jarl") [^47] Finland gradually became part of the kingdom of Sweden and the sphere of influence of the [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church").[^48] The Swedes built fortresses in [Tavastland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%A4me "Häme") and [Åbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%85bo "Åbo"), while a Swedish royal council was instituted, an administrative structure and fiscal apparatus was created, and law codes were codified during the reigns of [Magnus Ladulås](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnus_Ladul%C3%A5s "Magnus Ladulås") (1275–1290) and [Magnus Eriksson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnus_Eriksson "Magnus Eriksson") (1319–1364).[^49] As a result, the Finnish lands were firmly integrated into the Swedish realm.[^50]

Except for the provinces of Scania, Blekinge, and Halland in the southwest of the Scandinavian peninsula, which were part of the Kingdom of Denmark during this period, [feudalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism "Feudalism") never developed in Sweden as it did in much of Europe.[^51] As a result, the peasantry remained largely a class of free farmers throughout most of Swedish history. [Slavery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery "Slavery"), also known as [thralldom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrall "Thrall"), was not common in Sweden,[^52] and the institution gradually diminished due to the spread of Christianity, the difficulty of obtaining slaves from lands east of the Baltic Sea, and by the development of cities before the 16th century.[^53] Indeed, both slavery and [serfdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serfdom "Serfdom") were abolished altogether by a decree of King [Magnus Eriksson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnus_Eriksson "Magnus Eriksson") in 1335. Sweden remained a poor and economically underdeveloped country, where barter was the primary means of exchange.[^54]

In 1319, Sweden and Norway were united in a [personal union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_union "Personal union") under King Magnus Eriksson, the grandson of King [Magnus Ladulås](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnus_Ladul%C3%A5s "Magnus Ladulås") of Sweden and of King [Haakon V](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haakon_V_of_Norway "Haakon V of Norway") of Norway. Magnus Eriksson also ruled Scania from 1332 to 1360. In the mid-14th century, Sweden was struck by the [Black Death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Death "Black Death").[^55] The population of Sweden and most of Europe was decimated. The population did not reach its pre-1348 levels until the beginning of the 19th century, with one third of the population dying between 1349 and 1351. During this period, the cities began to acquire greater rights and were heavily influenced by German merchants of the [Hanseatic League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanseatic_League "Hanseatic League"), active especially at [Visby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visby "Visby"). In 1397, Queen [Margaret I of Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_I_of_Denmark "Margaret I of Denmark") (the former daughter-in-law of Magnus Eriksson) established the personal union of Sweden, Norway, and Denmark through the [Kalmar Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalmar_Union "Kalmar Union"). However, Margaret's successors, whose rule was centred in Denmark, were unable to control the Swedish nobility.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Gustav_Vasa.jpg/250px-Gustav_Vasa.jpg)

Gustav I liberated Sweden from Christian II of Denmark, ending the Kalmar Union. He established the House of Vasa which ruled Sweden and Poland until the 17th century.

In 1520, King [Christian II of Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_II_of_Denmark "Christian II of Denmark"), who attempted to restore the Union of Kalmar through military force, ordered the massacre of Swedish nobles in Stockholm, an event known as the " [Stockholm Bloodbath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stockholm_Bloodbath "Stockholm Bloodbath")." This atrocity incited the Swedish nobility to renew their resistance, and on 6 June 1523 (now celebrated as [Sweden's National Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Day_of_Sweden "National Day of Sweden")), they made [Gustav Vasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Vasa "Gustav Vasa") their king.[^56] This is sometimes considered as the [foundation of modern Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundation_of_modern_Sweden "Foundation of modern Sweden"). Shortly afterwards the new king rejected Catholicism and led Sweden into the [Protestant Reformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestant_Reformation "Protestant Reformation").[^56] The term *riksdag* was used for the first time in the 1540s, although the first meeting where representatives of different social groups were called to discuss and determine affairs affecting the country as a whole took place as early as 1435, in the town of [Arboga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arboga "Arboga").[^57] During the [Riksdag assemblies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riksdag_of_the_Estates "Riksdag of the Estates") of 1527 and 1544, under King Gustav Vasa, representatives of all four [estates of the realm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm "Estates of the realm") (clergy, [nobility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_nobility "Swedish nobility"), [townsmen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bourgeoisie "Bourgeoisie") and [peasants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peasant "Peasant")) were called on to participate for the first time.[^57] The monarchy became hereditary in 1544.[^58] When Gustav Vasa broke the monopoly power of the [Hanseatic League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanseatic_League "Hanseatic League"), he was regarded as a hero by the Swedish people.[^59] Furthermore, when Sweden became more developed, after breaking the trade monopoly of the Hanseatic League, and entered its golden age, the fact that the peasantry had traditionally been free meant that a greater share of the economic benefits went to them rather than to a feudal landowning class.[^60]

The end of the 16th century was marked by a final phase of rivalry between the remaining Catholics and the new Protestant communities. In 1592, Gustav Vasa's Catholic grandson and [king of Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_of_Poland "King of Poland"), [Sigismund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigismund_III_Vasa "Sigismund III Vasa"), ascended the Swedish throne.[^61] He pursued to strengthen [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_See "Holy See") 's influence by initiating [Counter-Reformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counter-Reformation "Counter-Reformation") and created a dual monarchy that temporarily became known as the [Polish-Swedish Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish-Swedish_Union "Polish-Swedish Union"). His despotic rule, strongly characterised by intolerance towards the Protestants, sparked a [civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_against_Sigismund "War against Sigismund") that plunged Sweden into poverty.[^62] In opposition, Sigismund's uncle and successor, [Charles Vasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_IX_of_Sweden "Charles IX of Sweden"), summoned the [Uppsala Synod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uppsala_Synod "Uppsala Synod") in 1593 which officially confirmed the modern [Church of Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_of_Sweden "Church of Sweden") as [Lutheran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lutheranism "Lutheranism"). Following his [deposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deposition_\(politics\) "Deposition (politics)") in 1599, Sigismund attempted to reclaim the throne sparing no expense, and hostilities between [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth "Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth") and Sweden continued for the next hundred years.[^63]

### Swedish Empire

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Gustave_Adolphe_at_Breitenfeld-Johann_Walter-f3706497.jpg/250px-Gustave_Adolphe_at_Breitenfeld-Johann_Walter-f3706497.jpg)

Gustavus Adolphus at the Battle of Breitenfeld in 1631

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Swedish_Empire.svg/250px-Swedish_Empire.svg.png)

The Swedish Empire between 1560 and 1815; its peak was between 1658 and 1660

Sweden rose to prominence on a continental scale during the reign of king [Gustavus Adolphus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden "Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden") (1611-1632), seizing territories from [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsardom_of_Russia "Tsardom of Russia") and the [Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth "Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth") in multiple conflicts.[^64] During the Thirty Years' War, Sweden conquered approximately half of the Holy Roman states and defeated the Imperial army at the [Battle of Breitenfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Breitenfeld_\(1631\) "Battle of Breitenfeld (1631)") in 1631.[^65] Gustavus Adolphus planned to become the new [Holy Roman Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor "Holy Roman Emperor"), ruling over a united Scandinavia and the Holy Roman states, but he was killed at the [Battle of Lützen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_L%C3%BCtzen_\(1632\) "Battle of Lützen (1632)") in 1632. After the [Battle of Nördlingen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_N%C3%B6rdlingen_\(1634\) "Battle of Nördlingen (1634)") in 1634 – Sweden's only significant military defeat of the war – pro-Swedish sentiment among the German states faded.[^65] These German provinces broke away from Swedish power one by one, leaving Sweden with only a few northern German territories: [Swedish Pomerania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_Pomerania "Swedish Pomerania"), [Bremen-Verden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bremen-Verden "Bremen-Verden") and [Wismar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wismar "Wismar"). From 1643 to 1645, during the last years of the war, Sweden and [Denmark-Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark-Norway "Denmark-Norway") fought the [Torstenson War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torstenson_War "Torstenson War"). The result of that conflict and the conclusion of the Thirty Years' War helped establish postwar Sweden as a major force in Europe.[^65] The [Peace of Westphalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia "Peace of Westphalia") in 1648 granted Sweden territories in northern Germany.

In the middle of the 17th century, Sweden was the third-largest country in Europe by land area. Sweden reached its largest territorial extent under the rule of [Charles X](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_X_of_Sweden "Charles X of Sweden") after the [treaty of Roskilde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Roskilde "Treaty of Roskilde") in 1658, following Charles X's [crossing of the Danish Belts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_Across_the_Belts "March Across the Belts").[^66] The foundation of Sweden's success during this period is credited to Gustav I's major changes to the [Swedish economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Sweden "Economy of Sweden") in the 16th century, and his introduction of [Protestantism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestantism "Protestantism").[^67] One-third of the Finnish population died in the devastating [Great Famine of 1695–1697](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Famine_of_1695%E2%80%931697 "Great Famine of 1695–1697") that struck the country.[^68] Famine also hit Sweden, killing roughly 10% of Sweden's population.[^69]

In the 17th century, Sweden was engaged in many wars, for example with Poland–Lithuania, with both sides competing for territories of today's [Baltic states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_states "Baltic states"). The Polish–Swedish War (1626–1629) ended with a ceasefire in [Stary Targ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stary_Targ "Stary Targ") ([Truce of Altmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truce_of_Altmark "Truce of Altmark")) on 26 September 1629 that was in favour of the Swedes, to whom Poland ceded the larger part of [Livonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livonia "Livonia") together with its important port of [Riga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riga "Riga"). The Swedes also gained the right to tax Poland’s Baltic trade (3.5% on the value of goods), and kept control of several cities in [Royal Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Prussia "Royal Prussia") and [Ducal Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ducal_Prussia "Ducal Prussia") (including [Pillau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pillau "Pillau") (Baltiysk), [Memel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klaip%C4%97da "Klaipėda") (Klaipėda) and [Elbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elbing "Elbing") (Elbląg)). The Swedes later conducted a series of invasions into the Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth, known as the [Deluge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deluge_\(history\) "Deluge (history)").[^70] After more than half a century of almost constant warfare, the Swedish economy had deteriorated. It became the lifetime task of Charles X's son, [Charles XI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_XI_of_Sweden "Charles XI of Sweden"), to rebuild the economy and refit the army.[^71] His legacy to his son, the coming ruler of Sweden, [Charles XII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_XII_of_Sweden "Charles XII of Sweden"), was one of the finest arsenals in the world, a large standing army and a great fleet.[^72] Russia, the most serious threat to Sweden at this time, had a larger army but lagged far behind in both equipment and training.[^73]

After the [Battle of Narva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Narva_\(1700\) "Battle of Narva (1700)") in 1700, one of the first battles of the [Great Northern War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Northern_War "Great Northern War"), the Russian army was so severely devastated that Sweden had an open chance to invade Russia.[^74] However, Charles XII did not pursue the Russian army, instead [turning against Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_invasion_of_Poland_\(1701%E2%80%931706\) "Swedish invasion of Poland (1701–1706)") and defeating the Polish king, [Augustus II the Strong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustus_II_the_Strong "Augustus II the Strong"), and his Saxon allies at the [Battle of Kliszów](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Klisz%C3%B3w "Battle of Kliszów") in 1702.[^75] This gave Russia time to rebuild and modernise its army.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Marten%27s_Poltava.jpg/250px-Marten%27s_Poltava.jpg)

The Battle of Poltava in 1709. In the following years, Russia and her allies occupied all Swedish dominions on the Baltic coast and even Finland.

After the success of invading Poland, Charles XII decided to make an attempt at [invading Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_invasion_of_Russia "Swedish invasion of Russia"), but this ended in a decisive Russian victory at the [Battle of Poltava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Poltava "Battle of Poltava") in 1709.[^76] After a long march exposed to [Cossack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cossack "Cossack") raids, the Russian Tsar [Peter the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_the_Great "Peter the Great") 's [scorched-earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scorched-earth "Scorched-earth") techniques and [the extremely cold winter of 1709](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Frost_of_1709 "Great Frost of 1709"), the Swedes stood weakened with a shattered morale and were enormously outnumbered against the Russian army at Poltava.[^77] The defeat meant the beginning of the end for the Swedish Empire. In addition, [the plague raging in East Central Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_plague_during_the_Great_Northern_War "The plague during the Great Northern War") devastated the [Swedish dominions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_dominions "Swedish dominions") and reached Central Sweden in 1710.[^78] [^79] Returning to Sweden in 1715, Charles XII launched [two campaigns against Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Northern_War_and_Norway "Great Northern War and Norway") in 1716 and 1718, respectively. During the second attempt, he was shot to death during the [siege of Fredriksten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Fredriksten "Siege of Fredriksten") fortress.[^80] The Swedes were not militarily defeated at Fredriksten, but the whole structure and organisation of the campaign fell apart with the king's death. Forced to cede large areas of land in the [Treaty of Nystad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Nystad "Treaty of Nystad") in 1721, Sweden also lost its place as an empire and as the dominant state on the Baltic Sea.[^81] With Sweden's lost influence, Russia emerged as an empire and became one of [Europe's dominant nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_balance_of_power "European balance of power"). As the war finally ended in 1721, Sweden had lost an estimated 200,000 men, 150,000 of those from the area of present-day Sweden and 50,000 from the [Finnish part of Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland_under_Swedish_rule "Finland under Swedish rule").[^82] Executive power was historically shared between the King and an aristocratic [Privy council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privy_council "Privy council") until 1680, followed by the King's [autocratic rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy") initiated by the commoner estates of the [Riksdag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riksdag "Riksdag"). As a reaction to the failed Great Northern War, a parliamentary system was introduced in 1719, followed by three different flavours of constitutional monarchy in 1772, 1789 and 1809, [the latter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_constitution_of_1809 "Swedish constitution of 1809") granting several civil liberties. Already during the first of those three periods, the 'Era of Liberty' (1719–72) the Swedish Riksdag had developed into a very active parliament, and this tradition continued into the nineteenth century, laying the basis for the transition towards modern democracy at the end of that century.[^83] In the 18th century, Sweden did not have enough resources to maintain its territories outside Scandinavia, and most of them were lost, culminating with [the loss in 1809](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_War "Finnish War") of eastern Sweden to Russia, which became the highly autonomous [Grand Principality of Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Finland "Grand Duchy of Finland") in [Imperial Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire").[^84]

In interest of re-establishing Swedish dominance in the Baltic Sea, Sweden allied itself against its traditional ally and benefactor, France, in the [Napoleonic Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars "Napoleonic Wars"). However, in 1810, a French Marshal, [Jean-Baptiste Bernadotte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Bernadotte "Jean-Baptiste Bernadotte"), was chosen as heir presumptive to [Charles XIII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_XIII "Charles XIII"); in 1818, he established the [House of Bernadotte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Bernadotte "House of Bernadotte"), taking the [regnal name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regnal_name "Regnal name") of Charles XIV. Sweden's role in the [Battle of Leipzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Leipzig "Battle of Leipzig") gave it the authority to force Denmark–Norway, an ally of France, to cede Norway to the King of Sweden on 14 January 1814 in exchange for the northern German provinces, at the [Treaty of Kiel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Kiel "Treaty of Kiel").[^85] The Norwegian attempts to keep their status as a sovereign state were rejected by the Swedish king, Charles XIII. He launched a [military campaign against Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish%E2%80%93Norwegian_War_\(1814\) "Swedish–Norwegian War (1814)") on 27 July 1814, ending in the [Convention of Moss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_of_Moss "Convention of Moss"), which forced Norway into a [personal union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_between_Sweden_and_Norway "Union between Sweden and Norway") with Sweden under the Swedish crown, which lasted until 1905.[^86] [^87] The 1814 campaign was the last time Sweden was at war.[^88]

### Modern history

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Starvation_image_from_F%C3%A4derneslandet_1867.jpg/250px-Starvation_image_from_F%C3%A4derneslandet_1867.jpg)

Starvation in northern Sweden depicted in an illustration of the Famine of 1867–1869

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Farewell_to_home%2C_G%C3%B6teborg%2C_1905_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Farewell_to_home%2C_G%C3%B6teborg%2C_1905_%28cropped%29.jpg)

Swedish emigrants boarding a ship in Gothenburg in 1905

The [Swedish East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_East_India_Company "Swedish East India Company") began in 1731. The obvious choice of home port was [Gothenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gothenburg "Gothenburg") ([Swedish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_language "Swedish language"): *Göteborg*) at Sweden's west coast, as the mouth of [Göta älv river](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6ta_%C3%A4lv "Göta älv") was very wide and had the county's largest and best harbour for high-seas journeys. The trade continued into the 19th century, and caused the little town to become Sweden's second city.[^89] Between 1750 and 1850, the population in Sweden doubled. According to some scholars, mass emigration to America became the only way to prevent famine and rebellion; over 1% of the population emigrated annually during the 1880s.[^90] It is thought that between 1850 and 1910 more than one million [Swedes moved to the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_emigration_to_the_United_States "Swedish emigration to the United States").[^91] Nevertheless, Sweden remained poor, retaining a nearly entirely agricultural economy even as Western European countries began to industrialise.[^90] [^92]

Despite the slow rate of industrialisation into the 19th century, many important changes were taking place in the agrarian economy due to constant innovations and a rapid population growth.[^93] These innovations included government-sponsored programmes of [enclosure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enclosure "Enclosure"), aggressive exploitation of agricultural lands, and the introduction of new crops such as the potato.[^93] The Swedish farming culture began to take on a critical role in Swedish politics, which has continued through modern times with modern Agrarian party (now called the Centre Party).[^94] Between 1870 and 1914, Sweden began developing the industrialised economy that exists today.[^95]

Strong grassroots movements sprang up in Sweden during the latter half of the 19th century (trade unions, [temperance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperance_movement "Temperance movement") groups, and independent religious groups), creating a strong foundation of democratic principles. These movements precipitated Sweden's migration into a modern parliamentary democracy, achieved by the time of World War I. As the [Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution "Industrial Revolution") progressed during the 20th century, people gradually moved into cities to work in factories and became involved in socialist unions. A communist revolution was avoided in 1917, following the re-introduction of [parliamentarism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentarism "Parliamentarism"), and the country was [democratised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy").

### World War I and World War II

Sweden was officially neutral during [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"). However, under pressure from the [German Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Empire "German Empire"), they did take steps which were detrimental to the [Allied powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I "Allies of World War I") – most notably, mining the [Öresund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%96resund "Öresund") channel, thus closing it to Allied shipping, and allowing the Germans to use Swedish facilities and the Swedish [cipher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cipher "Cipher") to transmit secret messages to their overseas embassies.[^96] Sweden also allowed volunteers to fight alongside the Germans for the [White Guards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Guard_\(Finland\) "White Guard (Finland)") against the [Red Guards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Guards_\(Finland\) "Red Guards (Finland)") and Russians in the [Finnish Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_Civil_War "Finnish Civil War"), and briefly occupied [Åland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%85land "Åland") in cooperation with the German Empire.

As in the First World War, Sweden remained officially neutral during [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), although its neutrality has been disputed.[^97] [^98] Sweden was under German influence for much of the war, as ties to the rest of the world were cut off through blockades.[^97] The Swedish government unofficially supported Finland in the [Winter War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter_War "Winter War") and the [Continuation War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuation_War "Continuation War") by allowing volunteers and [materiel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materiel "Materiel") to be shipped to Finland. However, Sweden supported Norwegian resistance against Germany, and in 1943 helped [rescue Danish Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rescue_of_the_Danish_Jews "Rescue of the Danish Jews") from deportation to [Nazi concentration camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps "Nazi concentration camps").

During the last year of the war, Sweden began to play a role in humanitarian efforts, and many refugees, among them several thousand Jews from Nazi-occupied Europe, were rescued thanks to the [Swedish rescue missions to internment camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Buses "White Buses") and partly because Sweden served as a haven for refugees.[^99] The Swedish diplomat [Raoul Wallenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raoul_Wallenberg "Raoul Wallenberg") and his colleagues ensured the safety of tens of thousands of Hungarian Jews.[^100] Nevertheless, both Swedes and others have argued that Sweden could have done more to oppose the Nazis' war efforts.[^99]

### Post-war era

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Olivecrona_erlander_380.jpg/250px-Olivecrona_erlander_380.jpg)

Tage Erlander (left), Prime Minister under the ruling Swedish Social Democratic Party from 1946 to 1969

Sweden was officially a neutral country and remained outside [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") and [Warsaw Pact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warsaw_Pact "Warsaw Pact") membership during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), but privately Sweden's leadership had strong ties with the United States and other western governments. Following the war, Sweden took advantage of an intact industrial base, social stability and its natural resources to expand its industry to supply the rebuilding of Europe.[^101] Sweden received aid under the [Marshall Plan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Plan "Marshall Plan") and participated in the OECD. During most of the post-war era, the country was governed by the [Swedish Social Democratic Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_Social_Democratic_Party "Swedish Social Democratic Party") largely in co-operation with [trade unions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_Trade_Union_Confederation "Swedish Trade Union Confederation") and industry. The government actively pursued an internationally competitive manufacturing sector of primarily large corporations.[^102]

Sweden was one of the founding states of the [European Free Trade Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Free_Trade_Area "European Free Trade Area") (EFTA). During the 1960s the EFTA countries were often referred to as the Outer Seven, as opposed to the [Inner Six](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_Six "Inner Six") of the then- [European Economic Community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Economic_Community "European Economic Community") (EEC).[^103]

Like many industrialised countries, Sweden entered a period of economic decline and upheaval following the oil embargoes of 1973–74 and 1978–79.[^104] In the 1980s several key Swedish industries were significantly restructured. Shipbuilding was discontinued, wood pulp was integrated into modernised paper production, the steel industry was concentrated and specialised, and mechanical engineering was robotised.[^105] Swedish GDP per capita ranking declined during this time.[^102]

### Recent history

A bursting real estate bubble caused by inadequate controls on lending combined with an international recession and a policy switch from anti-unemployment policies to anti-inflationary policies resulted in a [fiscal crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden_financial_crisis_1990-1994 "Sweden financial crisis 1990-1994") in the early 1990s.[^106] Sweden's GDP declined by around 5%. In 1992, a run on the currency caused the central bank to briefly increase interest rates to 500%.[^107] [^108]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Tratado_de_Lisboa_13_12_2007_%28081%29.jpg/250px-Tratado_de_Lisboa_13_12_2007_%28081%29.jpg)

Sweden joined the European Union in 1995, and signed the Lisbon Treaty in 2007.

The response of the government was to cut spending and institute a multitude of reforms to improve Sweden's competitiveness, among them reducing the [welfare state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welfare_state "Welfare state") and [privatising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privatisation "Privatisation") public services and goods. [A referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1994_Swedish_European_Union_membership_referendum "1994 Swedish European Union membership referendum") passed with 52.3% in favour of joining the EU on 13 November 1994. Sweden joined the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") on 1 January 1995. In a 2003 referendum the Swedish electorate [voted against](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2003_Swedish_euro_referendum "2003 Swedish euro referendum") joining the [Euro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Monetary_Union "European Monetary Union") currency. Sweden held the chair of the European Union from 1 July to 31 December 2009.

On 28 September 1994, the [MS Estonia sank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinking_of_the_MS_Estonia "Sinking of the MS Estonia") as the ship was crossing the Baltic Sea, en route from Tallinn, Estonia, to Stockholm, Sweden. The disaster claimed the lives of 852 people (501 of them were Swedes [^109]), being one of the worst maritime disasters of the 20th century.[^110]

Until 2022, Sweden generally remained non-aligned militarily, although it participated in some joint military exercises with the [North Atlantic Treaty Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization "North Atlantic Treaty Organization") (NATO) and some other countries, stationed its troops under NATO command in [Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Republic_of_Afghanistan "Islamic Republic of Afghanistan"), took part in EU-sponsored [peacekeeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peacekeeping "Peacekeeping") operations in [Kosovo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kosovo "Kosovo"), [Bosnia and Herzegovina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina "Bosnia and Herzegovina"), and [Cyprus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyprus "Cyprus"), and [helped enforce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_military_intervention_in_Libya "2011 military intervention in Libya") a [UN-mandated no-fly zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_1973 "United Nations Security Council Resolution 1973") over Libya during the [Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring "Arab Spring"). In addition, there was extensive cooperation with other European countries in the area of defence technology and defence industry; some Swedish-made weaponry was used by [Coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-National_Force_%E2%80%93_Iraq "Multi-National Force – Iraq") militaries in Iraq.[^111] In response to the [2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine"), Sweden moved to formally join NATO, alongside Finland.[^112] After many months of delays caused by the objections of Turkey and Hungary, Sweden became a NATO member on 7 March 2024.[^113] [^114] [^115]

In recent decades Sweden has become a more culturally diverse nation due to significant immigration; in 2013, it was estimated that 15% of the population was foreign-born, and an additional 5% of the population were born to two immigrant parents. The influx of immigrants has brought new social challenges. Violent incidents have [periodically occurred](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roseng%C3%A5rd#Violence "Rosengård") [^116] [^117] including the [2013 Stockholm riots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/May_2013_Stockholm_riots "May 2013 Stockholm riots").[^118] In response to these violent events, the [anti-immigration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far-right_politics "Far-right politics") opposition party, the [Sweden Democrats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden_Democrats "Sweden Democrats"), promoted their anti-immigration policies, while the [left-wing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-wing_politics "Left-wing politics") opposition blamed growing inequality caused by the [centre-right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre-right_politics "Centre-right politics") government's socioeconomic policies.[^119]

Since the postwar period, [LGBTQ rights in Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LGBTQ_rights_in_Sweden "LGBTQ rights in Sweden") have gradually expanded through the steady extension of legal and social protections for LGBTQ+ people. Over subsequent decades, anti-discrimination laws, adoption rights, and the ability to change legal gender were progressively strengthened, contributing to high levels of public support for LGBTQ+ equality. In the mid-2010s, however, Sweden also saw the rise of [anti-gender mobilizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-gender_movement_in_Sweden "Anti-gender movement in Sweden"), which gender studies scholars situate within a broader process of [democratic backsliding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_backsliding "Democratic backsliding").[^120]

Sweden was heavily affected by the [2015 European migrant crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015_European_migrant_crisis "2015 European migrant crisis"), eventually forcing the government to tighten regulations of entry to the country.[^121] Some of the asylum restrictions were relaxed again later.[^122]

On 30 November 2021, [Magdalena Andersson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magdalena_Andersson "Magdalena Andersson") became Sweden's first female prime minister.[^123] [^124] The September 2022 general [election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Swedish_general_election "2022 Swedish general election") ended in a narrow win to a bloc of right-wing parties.[^125] On 18 October 2022, [Ulf Kristersson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulf_Kristersson "Ulf Kristersson") of the Moderate Party became the new Prime Minister.[^126]

## Geography

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Vy_mot_Stora_Sj%C3%B6fallet_fr%C3%A5n_Saltoluokta.jpg/250px-Vy_mot_Stora_Sj%C3%B6fallet_fr%C3%A5n_Saltoluokta.jpg)

View of the Stora Sjöfallet National Park

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Sand%C3%B6n_-_KMB_-_16001000454384.jpg/250px-Sand%C3%B6n_-_KMB_-_16001000454384.jpg)

Sandhamn island, Stockholm archipelago

Situated in Northern Europe, Sweden lies west of the [Baltic Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_Sea "Baltic Sea") and [Gulf of Bothnia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Bothnia "Gulf of Bothnia"), providing a long coastline, and forms the eastern part of the [Scandinavian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scandinavian_Peninsula "Scandinavian Peninsula"). To the west is the [Scandinavian mountain chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scandinavian_mountain_chain "Scandinavian mountain chain") (*Skanderna*), a range that separates Sweden from [Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway "Norway"). [Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland "Finland") is located to its north-east. It has maritime borders with [Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark "Denmark"), [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany"), [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland "Poland"), [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia"), [Lithuania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithuania "Lithuania"), [Latvia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latvia "Latvia") and [Estonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estonia "Estonia"), and it is also linked to Denmark (south-west) by the [Öresund Bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%96resund_Bridge "Öresund Bridge"). Its border with Norway ([1,619 km](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_and_territories_by_land_borders "List of countries and territories by land borders") long) is the longest uninterrupted border within Europe.

Sweden lies between latitudes [55°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/55th_parallel_north "55th parallel north") and [70° N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/70th_parallel_north "70th parallel north"), and mostly between longitudes [11°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/11th_meridian_east "11th meridian east") and [25° E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/25th_meridian_east "25th meridian east") (part of [Stora Drammen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stora_Drammen "Stora Drammen") island is just west of 11°).

At 449,964 km <sup>2</sup> (173,732 sq mi), Sweden is the 55th-largest country in the world,[^127] the [fifth-largest country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_and_population_of_European_countries "Area and population of European countries") in Europe, and the largest country in Northern Europe. The lowest elevation in Sweden is in the bay of Lake Hammarsjön, near [Kristianstad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kristianstad "Kristianstad"), at −2.41 m (−7.91 ft) below sea level. The highest point is [Kebnekaise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kebnekaise "Kebnekaise") at 2,096.8 m (6,879 ft) [above sea level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level "Above mean sea level").[^128]

Sweden has 25 [provinces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provinces_of_Sweden "Provinces of Sweden") or *landskap*. While these provinces serve no political or administrative purpose, they play an important role in people's [self-identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-concept "Self-concept"). The provinces are usually grouped together in three large *[lands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lands_of_Sweden "Lands of Sweden")*, parts, the northern Norrland, the central Svealand and southern Götaland. The sparsely populated Norrland encompasses almost 60% of the country. Sweden also has the [Vindelfjällen Nature Reserve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vindelfj%C3%A4llens_Nature_Reserve "Vindelfjällens Nature Reserve"), one of the largest protected areas in Europe, totalling 562,772 ha (approx. 5,628 km <sup>2</sup>).

About 15% of Sweden lies north of the [Arctic Circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_Circle "Arctic Circle"). Southern Sweden is predominantly agricultural, with increasing forest coverage northward. Around 65% of Sweden's total land area is covered with forests. The highest population density is in the [valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%A4laren_Valley "Mälaren Valley") of lake [Mälaren](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%A4laren "Mälaren") and Stockholm, southern Scania along the [Öresund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%96resund "Öresund") coast and along the [western coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Region_V%C3%A4stra_G%C3%B6taland "Region Västra Götaland") up to central Bohuslän. Gotland and [Öland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%96land "Öland") are Sweden's largest [islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islands_of_Sweden "Islands of Sweden"); [Vänern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V%C3%A4nern "Vänern") and [Vättern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V%C3%A4ttern "Vättern") are its largest lakes. Vänern is the third largest in Europe, after [Lake Ladoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Ladoga "Lake Ladoga") and [Lake Onega](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Onega "Lake Onega") in Russia. Combined with the third- and fourth-largest lakes Mälaren and [Hjälmaren](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hj%C3%A4lmaren "Hjälmaren"), these lakes take up a significant part of southern Sweden's area. Sweden's extensive waterway availability throughout the south was exploited with the building of the [Göta Canal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6ta_Canal "Göta Canal") in the 19th century, shortening the potential distance between the Baltic Sea south of [Norrköping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norrk%C3%B6ping "Norrköping") and [Gothenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gothenburg "Gothenburg") by using the lake and river network to facilitate the canal.[^129]

Sweden also has [plenty of long rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_rivers_of_Sweden "List of rivers of Sweden") draining the lakes. Northern and central Sweden have several wide rivers known as *älvar*, commonly sourced within the Scandinavian Mountains. The longest river is [Klarälven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klar%C3%A4lven "Klarälven") - [Göta älv](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6ta_%C3%A4lv "Göta älv"), which originates in [Trøndelag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tr%C3%B8ndelag "Trøndelag") in central Norway, running 1,160 kilometres (720 mi) before it enters the sea at [Gothenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gothenburg "Gothenburg"). In southern Sweden, narrower rivers known as *åar* are also common. The vast majority of municipal seats are situated either along the sea, a river, or a lake, and most of the country's population resides in coastal municipalities.

### Climate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Koppen-Geiger_Map_v2_SWE_1991%E2%80%932020.svg/250px-Koppen-Geiger_Map_v2_SWE_1991%E2%80%932020.svg.png)

Köppen-Geiger climate classification map of Sweden

Most of Sweden has a [temperate climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperate_climate "Temperate climate"), despite its northern [latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude"), with largely four distinct seasons and mild temperatures throughout the year. The winter in the far south is usually weak and is manifested only through some shorter periods with snow and sub-zero temperatures; autumn may well turn into spring there, without a distinct period of winter. The northern parts of the country have a [subarctic climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subarctic_climate "Subarctic climate") while the central parts have a [humid continental climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humid_continental_climate "Humid continental climate"). The coastal south can be defined as having either a humid continental climate using the 0 °C isotherm, or an [oceanic climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceanic_climate "Oceanic climate") using the -3 °C isotherm.

Due to the increased maritime moderation in the peninsular south, summer differences between the coastlines of the southernmost and northernmost regions are about 2 °C (4 °F) in summer and 10 °C (18 °F) in winter. This grows further when comparing areas in the northern interior where the winter difference in the far north is about 15 °C (27 °F) throughout the country. The warmest summers usually happen in the [Mälaren Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%A4laren_Valley "Mälaren Valley") around [Stockholm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stockholm "Stockholm") [^130] due to the vast landmass shielding the middle east coast from Atlantic low-pressure systems in July. Daytime highs in Sweden's municipal seats vary from 19 °C (66 °F) to 24 °C (75 °F) in July and −9 °C (16 °F) to 3 °C (37 °F) in January. The colder temperatures are influenced by the higher elevation in the northern interior. At sea level, the coldest average highs range from 21 °C (70 °F) to −6 °C (21 °F). As a result of the mild summers, the arctic region of [Norrbotten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norrbotten_County "Norrbotten County") has some of the northernmost [agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture") in the world.[^131]

Sweden is much warmer and drier than other places at a similar latitude, and even somewhat farther south, mainly because of the combination of the [Gulf Stream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Stream "Gulf Stream") [^132] [^133] and the general west wind drift, caused by the direction of Earth's rotation.[^134] Because of Sweden's high latitude, the length of daylight varies greatly over the course of a year. North of the Arctic Circle, the [sun never sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midnight_sun "Midnight sun") for part of each summer, and [it never rises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_night "Polar night") for part of each winter. In the capital, [Stockholm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stockholm "Stockholm"), the sun is up for more than 18 hours in late June but only around 6 hours in late December. Sweden receives between 1,100 and 1,900 hours of sunshine annually.[^135]

The highest temperature ever recorded in Sweden was 38 °C (100 °F) in [Målilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%A5lilla "Målilla") in 1947,[^136] while the coldest temperature ever recorded was −52.6 °C (−62.7 °F) in [Vuoggatjålme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vuoggatj%C3%A5lme "Vuoggatjålme") on 2 February 1966.[^137]

On average, most of Sweden receives between 500 and 800 mm (20 and 31 in) of precipitation each year, making it considerably drier than the [global average](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation_\(meteorology\) "Precipitation (meteorology)"). The south-western part of the country receives more precipitation, between 1,000 and 1,200 mm (39 and 47 in), and some mountain areas in the north are estimated to receive up to 2,000 mm (79 in). Despite northerly locations, southern and central Sweden may have almost no snow in some winters. Most of Sweden is located in the [rain shadow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain_shadow "Rain shadow") of the Scandinavian Mountains through Norway and north-west Sweden. It is predicted that as the [Barents Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barents_Sea "Barents Sea") gets less frozen in the coming winters, becoming thus "Atlantified", additional evaporation will increase future snowfalls in Sweden and much of continental Europe.[^138]

### Vegetation

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Sweden_Vegetation_Zones.jpg/500px-Sweden_Vegetation_Zones.jpg)

Map of Sweden's five major vegetation zones

Sweden has a considerable south to north distance which causes large climatic difference, especially during the winter. The related matter of the length and strength of the four seasons plays a role in which plants that *naturally* can grow at various places. Sweden is divided into five major vegetation zones. These are:

- The southern deciduous forest zone
- The southern coniferous forest zone
- The northern coniferous forest zone, or the [Taiga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiga "Taiga")
- The alpine-birch zone
- The bare mountain zone

Southern deciduous forest zone, also known as the nemoral region, the southern deciduous forest zone is a part of a larger vegetation zone which also includes Denmark and large parts of Central Europe. It has to a rather large degree become agricultural areas, but larger and smaller forests still exist. The region is characterised by a large wealth of trees and shrubs. The [beech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beech "Beech") is the most dominant tree, but [oak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oak "Oak") can also form smaller forests. [Elm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elm "Elm") at one time formed forests, but have been heavily reduced due to [Dutch Elm disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_Elm_disease "Dutch Elm disease"). Other important trees and shrubs in this zone include [hornbeam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hornbeam "Hornbeam"), [elder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sambucus_nigra "Sambucus nigra"), [hazel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corylus_avellana "Corylus avellana"), [fly honeysuckle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lonicera_xylosteum "Lonicera xylosteum"), [linden (lime)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tilia "Tilia"), [spindle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euonymus_europaeus "Euonymus europaeus"), [yew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxus_baccata "Taxus baccata"), [alder buckthorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alder_buckthorn "Alder buckthorn"), [blackthorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackthorn "Blackthorn"), [aspen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Populus_tremula "Populus tremula"), [European rowan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_rowan "European rowan"), [Swedish whitebeam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_whitebeam "Swedish whitebeam"), [juniper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juniperus_communis "Juniperus communis"), [European holly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_holly "European holly"), [ivy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hedera_helix "Hedera helix"), [dogwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornus_sanguinea "Cornus sanguinea"), [goat willow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goat_willow "Goat willow"), [larch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larix_decidua "Larix decidua"), [bird cherry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prunus_padus "Prunus padus"), [wild cherry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prunus_avium "Prunus avium"), [maple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maple "Maple"), [ash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraxinus_excelsior "Fraxinus excelsior"), [alder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alder "Alder") along creeks, and in sandy soil [birch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch "Birch") compete with [pine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pine "Pine").[^139] [Spruce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spruce "Spruce") is not native but between approximately 1870 and 1980, large areas were planted with it.[^140] During the last 40–50 years large areas of former spruce plantings have been replanted with deciduous forest.[^141]

Southern coniferous forest zone, also known as the boreo-nemoral region, is delimited by the [oak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oak "Oak") 's northern natural limit (*limes norrlandicus*) and the [Spruce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spruce "Spruce") 's southern natural limit,[^142] between the southern deciduous zone and the [Taiga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiga "Taiga") farther north. In the southern parts of this zone the coniferous species are found, mainly spruce and [pine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pine "Pine"), mixed with various deciduous trees. [Birch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch "Birch") grows largely everywhere. The [beech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beech "Beech") 's northern boundary crosses this zone. Although in its natural area, also *planted* Spruce are common, and such woods are very dense, as the spruces can grow very tight, especially in this vegetation zone's southern areas.

The northern coniferous forest zone or the [Taiga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiga "Taiga") begins north of the natural boundary of the [oak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oak "Oak"). Of deciduous species the [birch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch "Birch") is the only one of significance. [Pine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pine "Pine") and [spruce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spruce "Spruce") are dominant, but the forests are slowly but surely more sparsely grown the farther towards the north it gets. In the extreme north is it difficult to state the trees forms true forests at all, due to the large distances between the trees.[^143]

The alpine-birch zone, in the Scandinavian mountains, depending on both latitude and altitude, is an area where only a smaller kind of [birch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch "Birch") (*Betula pubescens* or *B.tortuosa*) can grow. Where this vegetation zone ends, no trees grow at all: the bare mountain zone.[^144]

Sweden had a 2019 [Forest Landscape Integrity Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_Landscape_Integrity_Index "Forest Landscape Integrity Index") mean score of 5.35/10, ranking it 103rd globally out of 172 countries.[^145] Sweden was ranked sixth in the [Environmental Performance Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_Performance_Index "Environmental Performance Index") in 2024. The index combines various indicators around known issues around the world, and measures how good they fit in among each countries on a scale. Sweden scores well in parameters like air pollution, air quality, waste management, sanitation and drinking water etc.[^146]
