The Shaksgam Valley—also referred to as the Trans-Karakoram Tract—is a sparsely populated region north of the Siachen Glacier disputed by China, India, and Pakistan. India considers the territory part of the former princely state of Jammu and Kashmir and therefore asserts sovereignty over it, while Pakistan maintains its right to dictate its administration as a former piece of its broader Kashmir claim.

In 1963, Pakistan and China signed the Sino-Pakistan Boundary Agreement, under which Pakistan provisionally ceded control of the Shaksgam Valley to China, pending a final resolution of the Kashmir dispute. The 1962 Sino-Indian War over disputed border territory motivated Pakistan to seek peaceful negotiations to settle its own nondelineated border with China in the contested Kashmir region. The agreement enabled Pakistan to gain the upper hand against India in Kashmir while securing a stronger partnership with China in the aftermath of India’s 1962 defeat. India rejected the agreement as illegal, arguing that Pakistan lacked authority to transfer territory it did not sovereignly control. Today, China administers the area in practice, while India continues to contest both the boundary agreement and China’s presence there, making the Shaksgam Valley a politically sensitive flashpoint at the intersection of the China-India and India-Pakistan disputes.

In the early weeks of 2026, Indian media reported swift and significant infrastructure development by China in the Shaksgam Valley, including extensions to roads that potentially strengthen China’s capacity for rapid military mobilization near the Line of Actual Control, the de facto border between India and China. Satellite imagery also suggests that China is potentially building new construction support sites and cement production units, while pursuing ongoing tunneling activity in the valley. In this Q&A, Daniel Markey discusses the importance of the Shaksgam Valley in the region and how control over the area aligns with China’s interests in strengthening its influence and position in its southern borderlands.

**The Shaksgam Valley is a sparsely populated, high-altitude region bordering Xinjiang to the north, Pakistan-administered Gilgit-Baltistan to the south and west, and the India-administered Siachen area to the east. What significance does this area hold for India’s, China’s, and Pakistan’s broader strategic and political interests?**

There are a lot of moving parts here. Starting with India, New Delhi claims all of Kashmir dating back to independence and its first war with Pakistan, which was fought largely over their differences about territorial control of the region. As a result, India considers the Shaksgam Valley—located within Kashmir—an undisputed piece of its territory. This is meaningful because while Pakistan and China claim that the Shaksgam Valley was transferred to China in the 1960s, India never accepted that decision. This disagreement distinguishes this border territory from others that are more settled—those that India has admitted to be a part of China. Whenever China tries to construct infrastructure in Pakistan-administered Kashmir, including in Gilgit-Baltistan, India considers this to be a direct threat undermining its core claim to all that territory. China-Pakistan Economic Corridor projects and the Karakoram Highway cutting through this territory are perceived by India as entirely illegal.

Strategically, it is clear from an Indian perspective that China is seizing advantages along the entire stretch of its contested border, and the events in the Shaksgam Valley support that interpretation. India views China as undertaking activities to extend practical control over the territory in a way that resembles “salami slicing” tactics to incrementally alter the status quo. These activities also demonstrate to New Delhi that China possesses the capability and resources to build impressive infrastructure in difficult terrain. As a result, India feels constrained by China across all of their disputed border. The Shaksgam Valley is a continuation of the activities seen in Ladakh in 2020 and further to the east near Bhutan. More generally, Indian experts believe China is attempting to show India who is boss in Asia. Chinese experts have described this behavior as Beijing establishing a “new equilibrium” on their border, which India will have to live with.

On the Indian domestic side, the Shaksgam Valley news places Prime Minister Narendra Modi and his ruling Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) in a bind because these Chinese activities undermine the tough image that Modi has cultivated over many years. Opposition parties are highlighting the loss of Indian territory, the loss of practical control over that territory, and China advancing its infrastructure along their contested border. These are all areas of specific political sensitivity inside India, which is why there has been such a proliferation of media attention on this issue recently, even though China’s road-building activities have been ongoing for many years.

Shifting gears, although Pakistan no longer lays claim to the Shaksgam Valley, it does claim nearby Gilgit-Baltistan. Due to those claims, Pakistan wants to ensure that China’s hold over the valley is tight and cannot be tested by India. If China’s claims to the Shaksgam Valley are reinforced by infrastructure development, then that indirectly reinforces Pakistan’s own claims to Gilgit-Baltistan. Factoring in its historical hostilities and animosity toward India, Islamabad prefers to see China take steps to exert greater pressure on India to distract Indian forces away from the Pakistan front and toward the China front. Pakistan welcomes Chinese activities that undermine Indian pretensions of regional hegemony or give Pakistan more space to push its own narrative about its control over contested territory.

From China’s perspective, the Shaksgam Valley relates to a broader story of bolstering control over Xinjiang and Tibet and making significant investments in transit infrastructure all across that territory. Beijing has been physically transforming this incredibly remote and difficult terrain to ensure that its control over every inch of that territory cannot be contested by India. This physical construction of infrastructure allows China to reject India’s claims in a very tangible manner. A physical road further stamps China’s claims to the Shaksgam Valley and rejects the possibility of negotiation over the territory. China has even gone so far as to say publicly that this activity is beyond reproach.[^1]

**China has developed road and transport infrastructure in the Shaksgam Valley since the 1970s. What practical or strategic advantages might this latest road construction provide China and Pakistan, and how could it alter India’s operational or diplomatic posture in the region?**

By building a road in the Shaksgam Valley, China enhances its access to areas that are close to the main pass for the Karakoram Highway connecting China and Pakistan. This strengthens ground lines of communication between the two countries. In a military context, it would enable China and Pakistan to coordinate more effectively against India.

To the south is the Siachen Glacier, which is geostrategically important because it stretches north from Indian-controlled territory into areas claimed by Pakistan. India’s high-altitude military positions are already exceptionally difficult to access and hold due to the unforgiving terrain and weather. As China extends its ground lines into the Shaksgam Valley, it places more pressure on Siachen, where Indian troops are flanked by Chinese forces to the north and east and by Pakistani forces to the west.

Even though Siachen is a remote territory, it holds operational significance for all three countries. Pakistan considers Siachen to be one of its most significant military losses dating back to the 1980s. Siachen’s relatively high elevation in the area gives India the vantage point to monitor sections of Pakistan-controlled Gilgit-Baltistan, including areas near the Karakoram Highway. China wishes to secure the highway from Indian attacks or reconnaissance in the event of a conflict. If Pakistan were to take the Siachen Glacier, it would reciprocally give Pakistan greater visibility over Indian-administered Kashmir. Pakistan has attempted to retake Siachen on numerous occasions and has always failed. One can imagine a conflict situation since the May 2025 India-Pakistan clashes in which Pakistan feels more confident about its military balance with India and in which China or Pakistan could escalate to force India out of Siachen. This would be a major setback and major loss from India’s perspective, so the nearby Shaksgam Valley does have a practical military consequence for the region.

**From the Indian side, how is infrastructure development in the Shaksgam Valley assessed, particularly when compared with China’s village construction and dual-use infrastructure development in the Tibetan Autonomous Region?**

There is a big difference between what China might build in Tibet and what it might build in the Shaksgam Valley or Gilgit-Baltistan because these latter territories are still claimed by India. So there are legal, historical, and geopolitical distinctions that India is inclined to draw. Indian observers believe that China’s efforts to build new roads and infrastructure in the Shaksgam Valley have no nonmilitary purpose, and thus can only be interpreted as a means to place greater military pressure on India or shore up China’s control over this contested territory. Compared to the dual-use village projects on the contested border in Tibet, the Shaksgam Valley development can only be interpreted as a military endeavor. This makes it harder for China to mask its infrastructure development in the valley as anything else and harder for India to perceive it as anything else.

**To what extent does the enduring tension between India and Pakistan factor into China’s strategic calculations in its southern border activities, including those in the Shaksgam Valley? Does Beijing view this rivalry as a risk to manage, as structural conditions to navigate, or even as something to benefit from?**

The answer is all three: a risk to manage, structural conditions to navigate, and something to benefit from.

There is a sense in China that it would prefer to prevent the risk of India-Pakistan conflict from spiraling out of hand. China has no interest in an outright war between those two countries, and certainly not a nuclear war. However, when unpacking China’s motivations, it becomes evident that Beijing is more concerned about the greater risk that Pakistan becomes so weak that it cannot defend itself against India. Therefore, the primary risk that China intends to mitigate is India outpacing Pakistan. This has been a particular concern for China as the U.S.-India relationship has improved and as Pakistan over the past decade has been faltering economically and militarily. To mitigate that risk, China has taken great efforts to provide Pakistan with the military capabilities it needs to defend itself against India. I think that China putting pressure on India’s border is probably among the single most important actions that it could undertake to improve Pakistan’s position with respect to India. It is an enormous strategic gift to Pakistan.

China cannot imagine a realistic scenario involving peace between India and Pakistan; the conflict is fixed, or structural, and not resolvable. Still, it could be better managed; there were instances about ten years ago in which experts believed China might be helpful with the United States in normalizing India-Pakistan relations. There was hope that if they could not be brought to peace, they could at least be brought to a “cold peace.”

Yet China also has strategic reasons not to favor India-Pakistan peace. For China, Pakistan is a useful means to balance or distract India. Most recently, the clashes between India and Pakistan in May 2025 enabled China to test its weapons against India through a regional proxy force in Pakistan.[^2] In this way, the continued competition between India and Pakistan serves Chinese purposes.

**Heightened scrutiny in Indian media of China’s activities in the Shaksgam Valley coincided with a visit by a Chinese Communist Party delegation on January 13 to New Delhi for talks with the BJP. How might observers interpret the timing of these developments, and do they reflect a broader pattern in how Beijing manages its most sensitive relationships with neighboring states where competing interests are at play?**

In terms of its relationship with India, China pushes its infrastructure development and its border defense efforts without giving an inch, even while simultaneously attempting to normalize its relationship. It holds firm on its territorial claims and instead looks for ways to change facts on the ground and force India to adjust to a new equilibrium condition. That is exactly how some Chinese experts have described Beijing’s recent behavior.

Almost immediately after the 2020 Galwan clashes, Chinese diplomats were seeking a diplomatic approach that would separate the border issue from broader bilateral relations. Up until the past year, India had rejected that parallel approach. But now it has effectively had to come around. The domestic pressures within India to normalize aspects of its commercial relationship with China mounted over time. In addition, India has been less confident about its relationship with the United States, compelling New Delhi to relent a little to avoid being left in a really difficult position without Washington’s support. India’s leaders also recognized that their hard-line rejection of China’s parallel process plan was yielding diminishing returns. China was not making concessions, leading only to stalemate. To their credit, India’s leaders made their stance plain, holding firm against Chinese pressure and accepting the costly consequences for years. That, New Delhi believes, will have some deterrent value.

The recent Shaksgam Valley developments, however, gave India’s opposition Congress Party opportunities to undermine Modi’s claims about being tough on China and to ask more critical questions in Parliament about India’s gains and losses in its conflict with China under his leadership. The Congress Party is scrutinizing the BJP to suggest that it is caving to China, which would harm the BJP’s relatively strong domestic political position.

The Congress Party is justified in promoting transparency about the India-China relationship. However, that does not mean India has any great options to solve its China problem. India should debate this problem more publicly as a democracy, but that will not easily translate into more effective solutions to counter China’s tens of billions of dollars allocated to border infrastructure or to balance the clear military and technological advantages China already enjoys.

**What indicators should regional analysts watch going forward to assess whether China’s activities in the Shaksgam Valley represent a localized development or are part of a more systematic effort to expand influence and control across its strategic borderlands?**

When you are looking for indicators, you want to observe Chinese statements and planning. The April 2025 Central Conference on Work Related to Countries of the Periphery publicly showed that China has a clear commitment to prioritize what it describes as its periphery. That is an indicator of top-level guidance from Beijing about its intentions. Another indicator is to follow the money, which leads us to conclude that China has likely already spent tens of billions of dollars on building out physical infrastructure in difficult places along the contested border with India in Tibet and Xinjiang. Finally, it is important to follow the actions and note the responses of China’s bordering countries to compare outcomes across various regions.
