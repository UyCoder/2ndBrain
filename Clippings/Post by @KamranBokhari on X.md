The U.S. geostrategy of burden-shifting is creating space for regional powers like Turkey to build their own architectures across Eurasia.

Ankara’s push to consolidate the Turkic world reflects Washington’s preference for partners that can help balance Russia and Iran without direct U.S. intervention.

But Central Asia’s strategic future will be shaped less by Turkic solidarity than by China’s entrenched economic dominance through trade, infrastructure and connectivity.

The result is a Eurasia where security balancing may become more regionalized even as Beijing remains the decisive economic center of gravity.
