[Aliens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alien_\(law\) "Alien (law)") entering the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") must obtain a [visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Travel_visa "Travel visa") from one of the [U.S. diplomatic missions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_diplomatic_missions_of_the_United_States "List of diplomatic missions of the United States"). Visitors may be exempt if they are citizens of one of the visa-exempt or [Visa Waiver Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visa_Waiver_Program "Visa Waiver Program") countries.

The same rules apply for travel to all [U.S. states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._state "U.S. state"), [Washington, D.C.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington,_D.C. "Washington, D.C."), [Puerto Rico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puerto_Rico "Puerto Rico") and the [U.S. Virgin Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Virgin_Islands "U.S. Virgin Islands"), as well as to [Guam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guam "Guam") and the [Northern Mariana Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Mariana_Islands "Northern Mariana Islands") with additional waivers, while similar but separate rules apply to [American Samoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Samoa "American Samoa").

## Travel documents

The U.S. government requires all individuals entering or departing the United States by air, or entering the United States by sea from outside the [Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americas "Americas"), to hold one of the following documents:[^32]

- [U.S. passport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._passport "U.S. passport")
- Foreign passport; for entry, a U.S. visa is also required except for:
	- Citizens of the [freely associated states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_of_Free_Association "Compact of Free Association") ([Marshall Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Islands "Marshall Islands"), [Micronesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federated_States_of_Micronesia "Federated States of Micronesia") and [Palau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palau "Palau")) [^33]
		- Nationals of certain neighboring jurisdictions (Canada and [Bermuda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda "Bermuda") generally; [Bahamas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bahamas "Bahamas"), [British Virgin Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands "British Virgin Islands"), [Cayman Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayman_Islands "Cayman Islands") and [Turks and Caicos Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands "Turks and Caicos Islands") under certain conditions; and Mexico under limited categories)
		- Nationals of countries in the [Visa Waiver Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visa_Waiver_Program "Visa Waiver Program") who possess a valid [ESTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_System_for_Travel_Authorization "Electronic System for Travel Authorization") (or of certain additional countries only for [Guam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guam "Guam") and the [Northern Mariana Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Mariana_Islands "Northern Mariana Islands"))
- [U.S. permanent resident card](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_card "Green card") (Form I-551) or temporary I-551 stamp
- U.S. travel document serving as a [re-entry permit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._re-entry_permit "U.S. re-entry permit") (Form I-327) or [refugee travel document](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refugee_travel_document "Refugee travel document") (Form I-571)
- [U.S. advance parole authorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parole_\(United_States_immigration\) "Parole (United States immigration)") (Form I-512), [temporary protected status](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temporary_protected_status "Temporary protected status") document (Form I-512T), or [employment authorization document](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Employment_authorization_document "Employment authorization document") (Form I-766) annotated "valid for re-entry to U.S." or "serves as I-512 advance parole"
- [U.S. military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._military "U.S. military") or [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") identification with official travel order
- [U.S. merchant mariner credential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merchant_Mariner_Credential "Merchant Mariner Credential") indicating [U.S. citizenship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._citizenship "U.S. citizenship")
- [NEXUS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NEXUS "NEXUS") card indicating U.S. or [Canadian citizenship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_citizenship "Canadian citizenship") (only to or from [Canadian airports](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_airports_in_Canada "List of airports in Canada") with [preclearance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_border_preclearance "United States border preclearance"))
- U.S. government-issued transportation letter or boarding foil (for entry only)
- Foreign emergency travel document or U.S. removal order (for departure only)

For entry by land or sea from the Americas, individuals must present one of the documents acceptable for entry by air or one of the following:[^32]

- [U.S. passport card](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Passport_Card "United States Passport Card")
- [NEXUS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NEXUS "NEXUS"), [SENTRI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SENTRI "SENTRI"), [FAST](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_and_Secure_Trade "Free and Secure Trade") or [Global Entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Entry "Global Entry") card indicating U.S. or Canadian citizenship
- U.S. or Canadian [enhanced driver's license](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enhanced_driver%27s_license "Enhanced driver's license")
- Enhanced tribal card, [Native American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States "Native Americans in the United States") photo identification card, or [Canadian Indian status](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_register "Indian register") card
- [Border Crossing Card](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Border_Crossing_Card "Border Crossing Card"), only for entry from Mexico by land, [pleasure vessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boating "Boating") or [ferry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferry "Ferry") [^34]
- U.S. or Canadian [birth certificate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birth_certificate "Birth certificate"), U.S. Consular Report of Birth Abroad, U.S. naturalization certificate or Canadian citizenship certificate, only for children under age 16, or under age 19 in a supervised group [^35]
- Government-issued photo identification along with U.S. birth certificate, Consular Report of Birth Abroad or naturalization certificate, only for travel by [cruise ship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cruise_ship "Cruise ship") returning to the same place of departure in the United States [^36]

Children born to a U.S. permanent resident mother during a temporary visit abroad do not need a passport or visa at the mother's first re-entry to the United States within two years after birth. Children born abroad to a parent with a U.S. immigrant visa after its issuance do not need a visa if holding a passport and a birth certificate.[^32]

## Visas

While there are about 185 different types of U.S. visas,[^37] there are two main categories:

- **Nonimmigrant visa**, for temporary stays such as for [tourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourism "Tourism"), [business](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business "Business"), family visits, study, [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Employment "Employment"), or transit;
- **Immigrant visa**: for permanent residence in the United States. At the port of entry, upon endorsement with an I-551 admission stamp, the visa serves as evidence of permanent residence for one year, and the visa holder is processed for a [green card](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_card "Green card"). A child with an IR-3 or IH-3 visa automatically [becomes a U.S. citizen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naturalization "Naturalization") upon admission and is processed for a certificate of citizenship (N-560).

A U.S. visa does not authorize entry into the United States or a stay in a particular status, but only serves as a preliminary permission to travel to the United States and to seek admission at a port of entry. The final admission to the United States is made at the port of entry by a [Customs and Border Protection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Customs_and_Border_Protection "U.S. Customs and Border Protection") (CBP) officer. For those entering in a nonimmigrant visa status, the admission details are recorded by the CBP officer on a [Form I-94](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Form_I-94 "Form I-94"), which serves as the official document authorizing the stay in the United States in a particular status and for a particular period of time. When entering by land, a Form I-94 must be obtained in advance or at the border, and a [US$](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_dollar "United States dollar") 30 fee is charged for its issuance.[^38] In order to immigrate, one should either have an immigrant visa or have a [dual intent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_intent "Dual intent") visa, which is one that is compatible with making a concurrent application for nonimmigrant and immigrant status.

Entering the United States on an employment visa may be described as a three-step process in most cases. First, the employer files an application with [Citizenship and Immigration Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Citizenship_and_Immigration_Services "United States Citizenship and Immigration Services") requesting a particular type of category visa for a specific individual. If the employer's application is approved, it only authorizes the individual to apply for a visa; the approved application is not actually a visa. The individual then applies for a visa and is usually interviewed at a [U.S. embassy or consulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_diplomatic_missions_of_the_United_States "List of diplomatic missions of the United States") in the native country. If the embassy or consulate grants the visa, the individual is then allowed to travel to the United States. At the airport, border crossing or other point of entry, the individual speaks with an officer from the [Customs and Border Protection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Customs_and_Border_Protection "U.S. Customs and Border Protection") to request admission, and if approved, the individual may then enter the United States.[^37]

In addition to immigration sponsored by a U.S. family member or employer, about 55,000 immigrant visas are available each year to natives of certain countries under the [Diversity Immigrant Visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diversity_Immigrant_Visa "Diversity Immigrant Visa") program, also known as the green card lottery.

### Citizens of freely associated states

- [Marshall Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Islands "Marshall Islands")
- [Micronesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federated_States_of_Micronesia "Federated States of Micronesia")
- [Palau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palau "Palau")

Under [Compacts of Free Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_of_Free_Association "Compact of Free Association"), citizens of the Marshall Islands, Micronesia and Palau may enter, reside, study and work in the United States indefinitely without a visa. These benefits are granted to citizens from birth or [independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trust_Territory_of_the_Pacific_Islands "Trust Territory of the Pacific Islands"), and to [naturalized citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naturalization "Naturalization") who have resided in the respective country for at least five years, excluding those who acquired citizenship by investment.[^45] [^46]

### Nationals of neighboring jurisdictions

The United States grants visa-free entry to nationals of two neighboring jurisdictions under most circumstances:[^34]

- [Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada "Canada") – Citizens of Canada do not need a visa to visit the United States under most circumstances.[^48] In addition, under the [USMCA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States%E2%80%93Mexico%E2%80%93Canada_Agreement "United States–Mexico–Canada Agreement") (and previously the [NAFTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_Free_Trade_Agreement "North American Free Trade Agreement")), they may obtain authorization to work under a [simplified procedure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TN_status "TN status").
- [Bermuda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda "Bermuda") – [British Overseas Territories citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories_citizen "British Overseas Territories citizen") of Bermuda do not need a visa to visit the United States under most circumstances for up to 180 days.[^48] They may also enter to study there without a visa.[^49] To qualify for the visa exemption, they must present a [British passport with "Government of Bermuda" on the cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_passport_\(Bermuda\) "British passport (Bermuda)"), with the nationality listed as "British Overseas Territories Citizen" or "British Dependent Territories Citizen", and containing an endorsement stamp of "Holder is registered as a Bermudian", "Holder possesses Bermudian status" or "Holder is deemed to possess Bermudian status".[^49]

The United States also grants visa-free entry to nationals of some other neighboring jurisdictions under certain conditions:[^34]

- [Bahamas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Bahamas "The Bahamas") – Nationals of the Bahamas do not need a visa to the United States if they apply for admission at a [U.S. preclearance facility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_border_preclearance "United States border preclearance") located in the Bahamas. In addition to a [Bahamian passport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bahamian_passport "Bahamian passport"), applicants 14 years of age or older must present a police certificate issued by the [Royal Bahamas Police Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Bahamas_Police_Force "Royal Bahamas Police Force") in the previous six months indicating no criminal record.[^50]
- [British Virgin Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands "British Virgin Islands") – [British Overseas Territories citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories_citizen "British Overseas Territories citizen") of the British Virgin Islands may travel without a visa to the [U.S. Virgin Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Virgin_Islands "U.S. Virgin Islands") with their [British Virgin Islands passport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands_passport "British Virgin Islands passport"). They may also continue travel to other parts of the United States if they present a Certificate of Good Conduct issued by the [Royal Virgin Islands Police Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Virgin_Islands_Police_Force "Royal Virgin Islands Police Force") indicating no criminal record.[^34] [^51]
- [Cayman Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayman_Islands "Cayman Islands") – British Overseas Territories citizens of the Cayman Islands may travel without a visa to the United States. To qualify, they must receive a visa waiver from the Cayman Islands Passport and Corporate Services Office, for which they must present a [Cayman Islands passport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayman_Islands_passport "Cayman Islands passport") valid for at least six months beyond their intended departure from the United States, a fee of 25 [Cayman Islands dollars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayman_Islands_dollar "Cayman Islands dollar"), and a police clearance certificate for applicants age 13 or older.[^52] The visa waiver is valid for only one entry and for travel directly from the Cayman Islands to the United States.[^53]
- [Turks and Caicos Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands "Turks and Caicos Islands") – British Overseas Territories citizens of the [Turks and Caicos Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands "Turks and Caicos Islands") may travel to the United States without a visa for short stays for business or pleasure. To qualify, they must travel directly from the territory to the United States, present a [Turks and Caicos Islands passport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands_passport "Turks and Caicos Islands passport") or another travel document stating that they are British Overseas Territory citizens with the right of abode in the Turks and Caicos Islands, and applicants 14 years of age or older must also present a police certificate issued by the [Royal Turks and Caicos Islands Police Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Turks_and_Caicos_Islands_Police_Force "Royal Turks and Caicos Islands Police Force") in the previous six months indicating no criminal record.[^54]

Visa-free entry is also granted to limited categories of nationals of another neighboring country:

- [Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexico "Mexico") – Some nationals of Mexico do not need a visa to travel to the United States: government officials not permanently assigned to the United States and their accompanying family members, holding diplomatic or official passports, for stays of up to 6 months; members of the [Kickapoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kickapoo_people "Kickapoo people") tribes of Texas or Oklahoma, holding Form I-872, American Indian Card; and crew members of [Mexican airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_airlines_of_Mexico "List of airlines of Mexico") operating in the United States.[^34] Other nationals of Mexico may travel to the United States with a [Border Crossing Card](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Border_Crossing_Card "Border Crossing Card"), which functions as a visa and has similar requirements.[^55] Under the [USMCA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USMCA "USMCA") (and earlier [NAFTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NAFTA "NAFTA")), they may also obtain authorization to work under a [simplified procedure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TN_status "TN status").

### Visa Waiver Program

As of 2026, 42 countries [^15] have been selected by the U.S. government for inclusion in the [Visa Waiver Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visa_Waiver_Program "Visa Waiver Program") (VWP).[^70] [^72] Their nationals do not need a U.S. visa for short stays, but they are required to obtain an [Electronic System for Travel Authorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_System_for_Travel_Authorization "Electronic System for Travel Authorization") (ESTA) prior to arrival.[^73] Visitors may stay for up to 90 days in the United States, which also includes time spent in Canada, Mexico, [Bermuda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda "Bermuda") or the islands in the [Caribbean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caribbean "Caribbean") if the arrival was through the United States. Nationals of the following countries can only apply for an ESTA if they hold a [biometric passport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biometric_passport "Biometric passport"):[^32]

Program countries:
- [European Union member states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union "Member state of the European Union") (except [Bulgaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulgaria "Bulgaria"), [Cyprus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyprus "Cyprus") and [Romania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romania "Romania")) [^9]
- All [European Free Trade Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Free_Trade_Association "European Free Trade Association") member states

- [Andorra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andorra "Andorra")
- [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia")
- [Brunei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brunei "Brunei") [^2]
- [Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile "Chile")
- [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel") [^10]
- [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan")
- [Monaco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monaco "Monaco")
- [New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand "New Zealand")
- [Qatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar "Qatar")
- [San Marino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Marino "San Marino")
- [Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore "Singapore")
- [South Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Korea "South Korea")
- [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") [^16]
- [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") [^5]

The [Electronic System for Travel Authorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_System_for_Travel_Authorization "Electronic System for Travel Authorization") (ESTA) is not considered a visa,[^76] but a prerequisite to traveling to the United States under the Visa Waiver Program. ESTA has an application fee of [US$](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_dollar "United States dollar") 10.27, and if approved, an additional fee of $30 is charged, for a total of $40.27.[^77] Once obtained, the authorization is valid for up to two years or until the traveler's passport expires, whichever comes first, and is valid for multiple entries into the United States.[^2] Passengers are advised to apply for ESTA at least 72 hours before departure.[^78] [^39]

Travel by air or sea with ESTA must be made on a participating commercial carrier. The VWP does not apply at all if arriving by air or sea on an unapproved carrier (e.g. a private ship or plane), in which case a standard visa is required.[^39] ESTA is also required for entry by land.[^79]

When entering the United States by land, travelers must, apart from their ESTA, also have an I-94 (Arrival/Departure Record) as their official record of admission and authorized stay. Unlike air or sea travel, where the I-94 is created automatically, land travelers must usually apply for the I-94, either at the border or in advance online using the official CBP website. The I-94 costs an additional $30.[^80]

As of 2026, those who have previously been in [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran "Iran"), [Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq "Iraq"), [Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya "Libya"), [North Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Korea "North Korea"), [Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalia "Somalia"), [Sudan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudan "Sudan"), [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria") or [Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemen "Yemen") on or after March 1, 2011, or in [Cuba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuba "Cuba") on or after January 12, 2021, or who are [dual nationals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_citizenship "Multiple citizenship") of Cuba, Iran, Iraq, North Korea, Sudan or Syria, are not eligible to travel under the VWP and must obtain a standard visa.[^70] However, those who traveled to such countries for diplomatic, military, humanitarian, reporting or legitimate business purposes may have this ineligibility waived.[^81]

### Visa waiver program of Guam and the Northern Mariana Islands

Although the visa policy of the United States also applies to the [U.S. territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States "Territories of the United States") of [Guam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guam "Guam") and the [Northern Mariana Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Mariana_Islands "Northern Mariana Islands"), both territories have an additional visa waiver program for certain nationalities. The Guam–CNMI Visa Waiver Program, first enacted in October 1988 and periodically amended, permits nationals of 12 countries to visit Guam and the Northern Mariana Islands for up to 45 days, and nationals of China to visit the Northern Mariana Islands for up to 14 days, for tourism or business, without the need to obtain a U.S. visa.[^34]

Program countries:

- [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia")
- [Brunei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brunei "Brunei")
- [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") [^17]
- [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong") [^14]
- [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan")
- [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia")
- [Nauru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nauru "Nauru")
- [New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand "New Zealand")
- [Papua New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea "Papua New Guinea")
- [Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore "Singapore")
- [South Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Korea "South Korea")
- [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") [^18]
- [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") [^6]

Travelers with a visa or ESTA are admitted to the territories in accordance with the terms of the visa or ESTA.

Travelers using the Guam–CNMI Visa Waiver Program are required to obtain a Guam–CNMI Electronic Travel Authorization (G-CNMI ETA), similar to ESTA. This authorization is valid for up to two years (one year for nationals of China), or until the traveler's passport expires (six months before the passport expires, for nationals of Brunei, China and Nauru), whichever comes first, and it is valid for multiple entries during this period. This authorization does not require a fee.[^93] [^94] [^95]

Travelers using this program must also hold a machine-readable passport and nonrefundable return ticket, and are not permitted to travel to other parts of the United States. Because of work visas and waivers specific to the Northern Mariana Islands, traveling between Guam and the Northern Mariana Islands still requires a full immigration inspection, and all visitors departing Guam or Northern Mariana Islands are inspected regardless of final destination.[^96]

### Alaska

Residents of the [Chukotka Autonomous Okrug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chukotka_Autonomous_Okrug "Chukotka Autonomous Okrug") in Russia who are members of the indigenous population do not need a visa to visit [Alaska](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaska "Alaska") if they have relatives (blood relatives, members of the same tribe, native people who have similar language and cultural heritage) in Alaska. Entry points are in [Gambell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gambell,_Alaska "Gambell, Alaska") and [Nome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nome,_Alaska "Nome, Alaska"). Individuals must be invited by a relative in Alaska, must notify local authorities at least ten days before traveling to Alaska, and must leave Alaska within 90 days.

The agreement establishing this policy was signed by Russia (then the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union")) and the United States on September 23, 1989. The United States made it effective as of July 17, 2015.[^115] [^116]

### American Indians born in Canada

[Members of certain indigenous peoples born in Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Nations_in_Canada "First Nations in Canada") may enter and remain in the United States indefinitely "for the purpose of employment, study, retirement, investing, and/or immigration" or any other reason by virtue of the [Jay Treaty of 1794](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jay_Treaty_of_1794 "Jay Treaty of 1794"), as codified in Section 289 of the Immigration and Naturalization Act.[^117]

In order to qualify, an individual must possess "at least 50 per centum blood of the American Indian Race".[^117] [^118] Tribal membership alone does not qualify an individual.[^117] The individual bears the burden of proof in establishing eligibility, typically by way of presenting identification based on reliable tribal records, birth certificates, and other documents establishing the percentage of indigenous ancestry.[^119] A Canadian Certificate of Indian Status is insufficient proof because it does not indicate the percentage of indigenous ancestry.[^119]

This provision does not extend to family members unless they qualify in their own right.[^117] [^119] [^120] However, qualifying American Indians residing in the United States are considered to be lawfully admitted for U.S. permanent residence and therefore may file a petition for their spouse and dependent children, subject to statutory numerical limitations and a potential backlog of applications.[^117] [^121] [^122]

### Outlying islands

Visits to the [United States Minor Outlying Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Minor_Outlying_Islands "United States Minor Outlying Islands") – [Baker Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baker_Island "Baker Island"),[^130] [Howland Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Howland_Island "Howland Island"),[^131] [Jarvis Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jarvis_Island "Jarvis Island"),[^132] [Johnston Atoll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnston_Atoll "Johnston Atoll"),[^133] [Kingman Reef](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingman_Reef "Kingman Reef"),[^134] [Midway Atoll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midway_Atoll "Midway Atoll"),[^135] [Navassa Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navassa_Island "Navassa Island"),[^136] [Palmyra Atoll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palmyra_Atoll "Palmyra Atoll") [^137] and [Wake Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wake_Island "Wake Island") [^138] – are severely restricted. The islands are not accessible to the general public, and all visits require special permits from the [U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Fish_and_Wildlife_Service "U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service"), or from [The Nature Conservancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Nature_Conservancy "The Nature Conservancy") for Palmyra Atoll,[^137] or from the [U.S. Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Air_Force "U.S. Air Force") for Johnston Atoll [^133] and Wake Island.[^139]

## Qualification process

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Visas_Mantis.png/250px-Visas_Mantis.png)

The typical process for issuing a United States visa, possibly including a Visas Mantis check

Applicants for visitor visas must show that they qualify under provisions of the [Immigration and Nationality Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immigration_and_Nationality_Act_of_1965 "Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965"). The presumption in the law is that every nonimmigrant visa applicant (except certain employment-related applicants, who are exempt) is an intending immigrant unless otherwise proven. Therefore, applicants for most nonimmigrant visas must overcome this presumption by demonstrating that:

- The purpose of their trip is to enter the U.S. for a specific, intended purpose;
- They plan to remain for a specific, limited period; and
- They have a residence outside the U.S. as well as other binding ties which will ensure their return at the end of their stay.

All visit, business, transit, student, and exchange visitor visa applicants must pay an application fee of [US$](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_dollar "United States dollar") 185 [^140] to the consular section at a U.S. embassy or consulate in order to be interviewed by a consular officer who will determine if the applicant is qualified to receive a visa to travel to the United States (additionally, the officer may also ask the [United States Department of State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State "United States Department of State") for a [Security Advisory Opinion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Security_Advisory_Opinion "Security Advisory Opinion"), which can take several weeks to resolve). The application fee is increased to $205 for most work visas and can be even higher for certain categories.[^140]

Amongst the items included in the qualification decision are financial independence, adequate employment, material assets, and lack of a criminal record and other security risks. If the applicant is rejected, the application fee is not refunded. If the application is approved, nationals of certain countries must also pay a visa issuance fee, based on reciprocity.[^141]

The [One Big Beautiful Bill Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_Big_Beautiful_Bill_Act "One Big Beautiful Bill Act"), enacted in July 2025, would require nonimmigrants to pay a $250 "visa integrity fee", which would be refunded after the expiration of the visa if the person has complied with its conditions.[^142]

### Visa bonds

#### A visa

A visas are issued to representatives of a foreign government traveling to the United States to engage in official activities for that government. A visas are granted to foreign government [ambassadors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambassador "Ambassador"), [ministers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_\(government\) "Minister (government)"), [diplomats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomat "Diplomat"), as well as other foreign government officials or employees traveling on official business (A-1 visa). Certain foreign officials require an A visa regardless of the purpose of their trip.

The A visa is also granted to immediate family members of such foreign government officials, defined as "the principal applicant's spouse and unmarried sons and daughters of any age who are not members of some other household and who will reside regularly in the household of the principal alien" (A-2 Visa) and which "may also include close relatives of the principal alien or spouse who are related by blood, marriage, or [adoption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adoption "Adoption") who are not members of some other household; who will reside regularly in the household of the principal alien; and who are recognized as [dependents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependents "Dependents") by the sending government (A-3 Visa).[^161]

#### B visa

The most common non-immigrant visa is the multiple-purpose B-1/B-2 visa, also known as the "visa for temporary visitors for business or pleasure." Visa applicants sometimes receive either a B-1 (temporary visitor for business) or a B-2 (temporary visitor for pleasure) visa, if their reason for travel is specific enough that the consular officer does not feel they qualify for combined B-1/B-2 status.[^162]

Holders of B visas may also attend short non-credit courses. Nationals of Mexico are eligible for [Border Crossing Cards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Border_Crossing_Card "Border Crossing Card").[^163]

From November 29, 2016, all holders of [Chinese passports](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_passport "Chinese passport") who also hold 10-year B visas are required to enroll in the Electronic Visa Update System (EVUS) before traveling to the United States. This requirement may be extended to other nationalities in the future.[^164] [^165]

Effective January 24, 2020, B visas are not issued to individuals expected to [give birth during their stay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birth_tourism "Birth tourism"), unless they demonstrate that the primary purpose of their visit is not to [obtain U.S. citizenship for the child](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birthright_citizenship_in_the_United_States "Birthright citizenship in the United States"). In addition, B visa applicants [seeking medical treatment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_tourism "Medical tourism") in the United States must demonstrate their arrangements for the medical treatment and sufficiently establish their ability to pay for it.[^166]

#### C visa

The [C-1 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-1_visa "C-1 visa") is a transit visa issued to individuals who are traveling in "immediate and continuous transit through the United States en-route to another country". The only reason to enter the United States must be for transit purposes.

A subtype [C-2 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-2_visa "C-2 visa") is issued to diplomats transiting to and from the [Headquarters of the United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headquarters_of_the_United_Nations "Headquarters of the United Nations") and is limited to the vicinity of [New York City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City "New York City"). A subtype C-3 visa is issued to diplomats and their dependents transiting to and from their posted country.[^167]

#### D visa

D visa is issued to crew members of sea-vessels and international airlines in the United States. This includes commercial airline pilots and flight attendants, captain, engineer, or deckhand of a sea vessel, service staff on a cruise ship and trainees on board a training vessel. Usually a combination of a [C-1 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-1_visa "C-1 visa") and D visa is required.[^168]

There are two types of D visa: [D-1 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D-1_visa "D-1 visa") for people departing on the same vessel and [D-2 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D-2_visa "D-2 visa") for people departing on a different vessel.

#### E visa

Treaty Trader ([E-1 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-1_visa "E-1 visa")) and Treaty Investor ([E-2 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-2_visa "E-2 visa")) visas are issued to citizens of countries that have signed treaties of commerce and navigation with the United States.[^169]

They are issued to individuals working in businesses engaged in substantial international trade or to investors (and their employees) who have made a 'substantial investment' in a business in the United States.[^170]

The variant visa issued only to citizens of Australia is the [E-3 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-3_visa "E-3 visa") (E-3D visa is issued to spouse or child of E-3 visa holder and E-3R to a returning E-3 holder).[^171]

#### F visa

These visas are issued for foreign students enrolled at accredited U.S. institutions.

F-1 visas are for full-time students, F2 visas are for spouses and children of F-1 visa holders and F-3 visas are for "border commuters" who reside in their country of origin while attending school in the United States.[^172] They are managed through SEVIS.[^173]

#### G visa

G visas are issued to diplomats, government officials, and employees who will work for international organizations in the United States. The international organization must be officially designated as such.[^174]

The [G-1 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-1_visa "G-1 visa") is issued to permanent mission members; the [G-2 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-2_visa "G-2 visa") is issued to representatives of a recognized government traveling temporarily to attend meetings of a designated international organization; the [G-3 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-3_visa "G-3 visa") is issued to persons who represent a non-recognized government; the [G-4 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-4_visa "G-4 visa") is for those who are taking up an appointment; and the [G-5 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-5_visa "G-5 visa") is issued to personal employees or [domestic workers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domestic_worker "Domestic worker") of G1–G4 visa holders.[^175] G1–G4 visas are also issued to immediate family members of the principal visa holder, if they meet certain criteria.[^175]

#### NATO visa

Officials who work for the [North Atlantic Treaty Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization "North Atlantic Treaty Organization") require a NATO visa.

The [NATO-1 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO-1_visa "NATO-1 visa") is issued to permanent representatives of NATO and their staff members. The NATO-2 visa is issued to a representative of a member state to NATO or its subsidiary bodies, an advisor or technical expert of the NATO delegation visiting the United States, a member of the NATO military forces component, or a staff member of the NATO representative. The NATO-3 visa is granted to official clerical staff accompanying a representative of a NATO member state. The NATO-4 visa is issued to a foreign national recognized as a NATO official, while the NATO-5 visa is for a foreign national recognized as a NATO expert. The NATO-6 visa is issued to a member of the civilian component of NATO. All NATO visas are also issued to immediate family members. NATO-7 visas are issued to personal employees or domestic workers of NATO-1 to NATO-6 visa holders.[^175]

#### H visa

H visas are issued to [temporary workers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temporary_worker "Temporary worker") in the United States.

##### Discontinued visa types

The discontinued [H-1A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H-1A_visa "H-1A visa") and [H-1C visas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H-1C_visa "H-1C visa") existed during periods when the United States experienced a shortage of [nurses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing "Nursing") from 1989. The H-1A classification was created by the Nursing Relief Act of 1989 and ended in 1995.

The H-1C visa was created by the Nursing Relief for Disadvantaged Area Act of 1999 and expired in 2005. Currently nurses must apply for H-1B visas.[^176]

##### H-1B visas

The H-1B classification is for professional-level jobs that require a minimum of a bachelor's degree in a specific academic field. In addition, the employee must have the degree or the equivalence of such a degree through education and experience.

There is a required wage, which is at least equal to the wage paid by the employer to similarly qualified workers or a [prevailing wage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prevailing_wage "Prevailing wage") for such positions in the geographic regions where the jobs are located.

This visa also covers fashion models of distinguished merit and ability.[^177] [^178] The [H-1B1 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H-1B1_visa "H-1B1 visa") is the variant issued to citizens of [Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore "Singapore") and [Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile "Chile").

##### Temporary agricultural workers

The H-2A visa allows a foreign national entry into the United States for temporary or seasonal [agricultural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agricultural "Agricultural") work for eligible employers under certain conditions (seasonal job, no available U.S. workers).[^179]

##### Temporary nonagricultural workers

The H-2B visa allows a foreign national entry into the United States for temporary or seasonal non-agricultural work for eligible employers under certain conditions (seasonal job, no available U.S. workers).[^180]

##### Nonimmigrant trainee or special education exchange Visitor

The H-3 visa is available to those foreign nationals looking to "receive training in any field of endeavor, other than graduate medical education or training, that is not available in the foreign national's home country" or "participate in a special education exchange visitor training program that provides for practical training and experience in the education of children with physical, mental, or emotional disabilities".[^181]

##### Family members

H-4 visas are issued to immediate family members of H visa holders. In some cases, they are eligible for employment.[^182]

#### I visa

The [I-1 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I-1_visa "I-1 visa") is issued to representatives of the foreign media, including members of the press, radio, film, and print industries travelling to temporarily work in the United States in the profession.[^183]

#### J visa

The J-1 visa is issued to participants of work-and study-based exchange visitor programs.[^184]

The Exchange Visitor Program is carried out under the provisions of the [Fulbright-Hays Act of 1961](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fulbright-Hays_Act_of_1961 "Fulbright-Hays Act of 1961"), officially known as the Mutual Educational and Cultural Exchange Act of 1961 ([Pub. L.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Act_of_Congress#Public_law,_private_law,_designation "Act of Congress") [87–256](https://uslaw.link/citation/us-law/public/87/256), 75 [Stat.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Statutes_at_Large "United States Statutes at Large") [527](https://legislink.org/us/stat-75-527)). The purpose of the act is to increase mutual understanding between the people of the United States and the people of other countries by means of educational and cultural exchanges. The Exchange Visitor Program is administered by the Office of Exchange Coordination and Designation in the [Bureau of Educational and Cultural Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureau_of_Educational_and_Cultural_Affairs "Bureau of Educational and Cultural Affairs").

In carrying out the responsibilities of the Exchange Visitor Program, the department designates public and private entities to act as exchange sponsors. Spouses and dependents of J-1 exchange visitors are issued a [J-2 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J-2_visa "J-2 visa").[^185]

Exchange visa categories are:

- Au pair and EduCare [^186]
- Camp counselor [^187]
- College and university student [^188]
- Government visitor [^189]
- Intern [^190]
- International visitor [^191]
- Physician [^192]
- Professor and research scholar [^193]
- Secondary school student [^194]
- Short-term scholar [^195]
- Specialist [^196]
- Summer Work Travel Program participant [^197]
- Teacher [^198]
- Trainee [^199]

Exchange Visitor Pilot Programs exist for citizens of Australia,[^200] Ireland,[^201] New Zealand [^202] and South Korea.[^203]

#### K visa

A K-1 visa is a visa issued to the [fiancé or fiancée](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engagement "Engagement") of a [United States citizen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_citizen "United States citizen") to enter the United States. A K-1 visa requires a foreigner to [marry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marriage "Marriage") his or her U.S. citizen petitioner within 90 days of entry, or depart the United States. Once the couple marries, the foreign citizen can adjust status to become a [lawful permanent resident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permanent_residence_\(United_States\) "Permanent residence (United States)") of the United States (Green Card holder).[^204]

A K-2 visa is issued to unmarried children under the age of 21. Foreign same-sex partners of United States citizens are currently recognized by [United States Citizenship and Immigration Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Citizenship_and_Immigration_Services "United States Citizenship and Immigration Services") (USCIS) and accordingly can be sponsored for K-1 visas and for permanent resident status.[^205]

[K-3/K-4 visas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_Immigration_Family_Equity_Act#K-3/K-4_visas "Legal Immigration Family Equity Act") are issued to foreign spouses and children of U.S. citizens.[^206]

#### L visa

The L-1 classification is for international transferees who have worked for a related organization abroad for at least one continuous year in the past three years and who will be coming to the United States to work in an executive or managerial (L-1A) or specialized knowledge capacity (L-1B).[^207] [^208] [^209] The [L-2 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L-2_visa "L-2 visa") is issued to dependent spouse and unmarried children under 21 years of age of qualified L-1 visa holders.

#### M visa

The M-1 visa is a type of student visa reserved for [vocational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocational_school "Vocational school") and technical schools.

Students in M-1 status may not work on or off campus while studying, and they may not change their status to F-1. The M-2 visa permits the spouse and minor children of an M-1 vocational student to accompany him or her to the United States.[^172]

#### O visa

The O visa is a classification of non-immigrant [temporary worker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temporary_worker "Temporary worker") visa granted to an [alien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alien_\(law\) "Alien (law)") "who possesses extraordinary ability in the [sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sciences "Sciences"), [arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arts "Arts"), [education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Education "Education"), [business](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business "Business"), or [athletics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport "Sport") (O-1A visa), or who has a demonstrated record of extraordinary achievement in the [motion picture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_picture_industry "Motion picture industry") or [television industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Television_industry "Television industry") and has been recognized nationally or internationally for those achievements," (O-1B visa) and to certain assistants (O-2 visa) and immediate family members of such aliens (O-3 visa).[^210]

#### P visa

P visas are issued to individuals or team athletes, or member of an entertainment group including persons providing essential support services (P-1 visa), artists or entertainers (individual or group) under a reciprocal exchange program (P-2 visa) and artists or entertainers (individual or group) visiting to perform, teach or coach under a program that is culturally unique (P-3 Visa).[^211]

P-4 visas are issued to spouses, or children under the age of 21, of a P-1, P-2, or P-3 alien and who is accompanying, or following to join.

#### Q visa

The Q visa is issued to participants in an international cultural exchange program.[^211]

#### R visa

The [R-1 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R-1_visa "R-1 visa") is issued to temporary religious workers. They must have been a member of a religious denomination having a bona fide non-profit religious organization in the United States for at least 2 years.[^212] R-2 visa is issued to dependent family members.[^213]

#### S visa

S visas are nonimmigrant visas issued to individuals who have assisted law enforcement as a witness or informant. There is a limit of 200 S visas a year.[^214]

A law enforcement agency can then submit an application for resident alien status, i.e. a [green card](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawful_permanent_residents_\(United_States\) "Lawful permanent residents (United States)") on behalf of the witness or informant once the individual has completed the terms and conditions of his or her S visa.[^215]

#### TN visa

[NAFTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_Free_Trade_Agreement "North American Free Trade Agreement") Professional (TN) visa allows citizens of Canada and Mexico whose profession is on the NAFTA list [^216] and who must hold a bachelor's degree to work in the United States on a prearranged job. Canadian citizens usually do not need a visa to work under the TN status (unless they live outside Canada with non-Canadian family members) while Mexican citizens require a TN visa.

Spouse and dependent children of a TN professional can be admitted into the United States in the TD status.[^217]

#### T and U visas

The [T-1 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_visa "T visa") is issued to victims of severe forms of human trafficking.

Holders may adjust their status to permanent resident status.[^218]

Subtypes of this visa are T-2 (issued to spouses of T-1), T-3 (issued to children of T-1), T-4 (issued to parents of T-1 under the age of 21), and T-5 (issued to unmarried siblings under the age of 18 of T-1 who is under 21).

The [U-1 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U_visa "U visa") is a nonimmigrant visa which is set aside for victims of [crimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crime_in_the_United_States "Crime in the United States") (and their immediate family members) who have suffered substantial mental or physical abuse and are willing to assist law enforcement and government officials in the investigation or prosecution of the criminal activity.[^219]

Subtypes of this visa are U-2 issued to spouses of U-1, U-3 issued to children of U-1, U-4 issued to parents of U-1 under the age of 21 and U-5 issued to unmarried siblings under the age of 18 of U-1 who is under 21.

#### V visa

The V visa was a temporary visa available to spouses and minor children (unmarried, under 21) of [U.S.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_of_America "United States of America") [lawful permanent residents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permanent_residence_\(United_States\) "Permanent residence (United States)") (LPR, also known as [green card](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Permanent_Resident_Card "United States Permanent Resident Card") holders).

It allowed permanent residents to achieve family unity with their spouses and children while the immigration process takes its course. It was created by the Legal Immigration Family Equity Act of 2000.[^220]

The Act is to relieve those who applied for immigrant visas on or before December 21, 2000. Practically, the V visa is currently not available to spouses and minor children of LPRs who have applied after December 21, 2000.[^221]

### Dual-intent visas

The concept of the [dual intent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_intent "Dual intent") visa is to grant legal status to certain types of visa applicants when they are in the process of applying for a visa with the intent to obtain a permanent residency/green card. Generally, nonimmigrant visas require the applicant to show they plan to return to their home country after their stay.

There are a certain number of U.S. visa categories that grant permission for dual intent, or to get a temporary visa status while having an intention to get a green card and stay permanently in the [United States of America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States").

## Digital Visa Authorization (DVA)

The State Department's [Bureau of Consular Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureau_of_Consular_Affairs "Bureau of Consular Affairs") began developing and trialling an [electronic visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_visa "Electronic visa") system envisioned to eventually replace traditional sticker visas on passports. In 2023, this was trialled with [K-1 visas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-1_visa "K-1 visa") issued at the [Embassy of the United States, Dublin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embassy_of_the_United_States,_Dublin "Embassy of the United States, Dublin"), [Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ireland "Ireland").[^224]

As this will only apply to individuals requiring a visa, this is different to the established [ESTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_System_for_Travel_Authorization "Electronic System for Travel Authorization") system for [visa-waived nationals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visa_Waiver_Program "Visa Waiver Program").

## Visa denial

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Mock-up_United_States_Nonimmigrant_Visa_Denial_Letter_INA_214b_Shanghai.pdf/page1-250px-Mock-up_United_States_Nonimmigrant_Visa_Denial_Letter_INA_214b_Shanghai.pdf.jpg)

A mock-up of the letter issued by the United States Consulate-general in Shanghai to inform nonimmigrant visa denials under Section 214(b) of the Immigration and Nationality Act.

Section 221(g) of the Immigration and Nationality Act defined several classes of aliens ineligible to receive visas.

Grounds for denial may include, but are not limited to:

- Risk of visa overstay
- Financial insecurity
- Having low or middle class income
- Having an informal job
- Unemployment
- Not having children and/or being single
- Relatives living in the United States
- Political, economic or social instability in the country of origin
- Incomplete education
- Health grounds
- Criminal history
- Security fears
- Public charge (charge means burden in this context)
- Illegal entrants or immigration violators
- Failure to produce requested documents
- Ineligible for citizenship
- Previously removed from the U.S.
- The spouse of a U.S. Citizen is almost always denied a visitor's (B1/B2) visa on the grounds that the spouse might want to stay in the United States.

Section 214(b) of the Immigration and Nationality Act (also cited as 8 United States Code § 1184(b)) [^225] states that most aliens must be presumed to be intending to remain in the U.S., until and unless they are able to show that they are entitled to non-immigrant status. This means there are two sides to a 214(b) denial. Denials occur when applicants do not convince the consular officer of their intent to stay in the U.S. temporarily, or were qualified for the visa.

An example of a denial based upon the first ground would be an applicant for an F-1 student visa who the consular officer felt was secretly intending to remain in the U.S. permanently.

An example of a denial based upon the second ground would be an H-1B applicant who couldn't prove he possessed the equivalent of a U.S. bachelor's degree in a specialty field—such an equivalency being a requirement for obtaining an H-1B visa.

In order to thereafter obtain a visa applicants are recommended to objectively evaluate their situation, see in what way they fell short of the visa requirements, and then reapply.[^226]

In rare cases, Section 212(d)(3) of the Immigration and Nationality Act allows for the temporary entry of certain aliens who would otherwise be prohibited from entering the United States.

The person applies for a Hranka waiver and pays the filing fee. When deciding whether to approve the waiver, the [Board of Immigration Appeals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Board_of_Immigration_Appeals "Board of Immigration Appeals") considers whether there would be harm to society if the applicant were admitted to the United States, the seriousness of the applicant's prior violations, and the nature of the applicant's reasons for wishing to enter the United States.

If approved for a Hranka waiver, the applicant would need to have this documentation when requesting entry to the United States.[^227]

## Exceptions

There are cases when a U.S. visa has been granted to aliens who were technically ineligible.

[Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_people "Japanese people") mafia ([yakuza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yakuza "Yakuza")) leader [Tadamasa Goto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tadamasa_Goto "Tadamasa Goto") and three others were issued visas for travel between 2000 and 2004 to undergo [liver transplant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver_transplant "Liver transplant") surgery at [UCLA Medical Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UCLA_Medical_Center "UCLA Medical Center").[^228] The [FBI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FBI "FBI") had aided the men in the visa application process hoping that they would [provide information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informant "Informant") regarding yakuza activities in the U.S.[^228]

In 2005, Indian Prime Minister [Narendra Modi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narendra_Modi "Narendra Modi") (then Chief Minister of Gujarat) was denied a [diplomatic visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-1_visa "G-1 visa") to the United States. The [B-1/B-2 visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B_visa "B visa") that had previously been granted to him was also revoked, under a section of the [Immigration and Nationality Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immigration_and_Nationality_Act_of_1965 "Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965") which makes any foreign government official who was responsible or "directly carried out, at any time, particularly severe violations of religious freedom" ineligible for the visa (the violations of religious freedom in question being the [2002 Gujarat riots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2002_Gujarat_riots "2002 Gujarat riots")).[^229] Modi is the only person ever denied a visa to the U.S. under this provision.[^230] In 2014, after Modi's [BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bharatiya_Janata_Party "Bharatiya Janata Party") political party won the [2014 Indian general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Indian_general_election "2014 Indian general election"), U.S. President [Barack Obama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barack_Obama "Barack Obama") ended the visa issue by calling Modi to congratulate him on his victory, and invited him to the White House. On June 8, 2016, Modi addressed a joint meeting of the U.S. Congress.[^231]
