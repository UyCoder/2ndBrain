A **parliamentary system** is a form of [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government") based on the [fusion of powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fusion_of_powers "Fusion of powers"). In this system the [head of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_government "Head of government") (chief executive) derives their [democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") legitimacy from their ability to command the support ("confidence") of a majority of the [parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament "Parliament"), to which they are held accountable. This head of government is usually, but not always, distinct from a ceremonial [head of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_state "Head of state"). This is in contrast to a [presidential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_system "Presidential system") or [assembly-independent system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assembly-independent "Assembly-independent"), which features a president whose power is [balanced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_powers#Checks_and_balances "Separation of powers") against the [legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature "Legislature") and the [judiciary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judiciary "Judiciary"), and cannot be replaced by a simple [majority vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majority_rule "Majority rule").

Countries with parliamentary systems may be [constitutional monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchies "Constitutional monarchies"), where a [monarch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarch "Monarch") is the head of state while the head of government is almost always a [member of parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_of_parliament "Member of parliament"), or [parliamentary republics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_republic "Parliamentary republic"), where a mostly ceremonial president is the head of state while the head of government is from the legislature. In some countries, the head of government is also head of state but is [elected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elected_monarchy "Elected monarchy") by the legislature. In [bicameral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicameral_legislature "Bicameral legislature") parliaments, the head of government is generally, though not always, a member of the [lower house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_house "Lower house"). Typically, the head of state in a parliamentary republic is elected by popular vote; however, in some cases they are elected by an electoral college (e.g. [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Germany "President of Germany")) or by members of parliament (e.g. [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Italy "President of Italy")) in a special process.

Parliamentary democracy is the predominant [form of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Form_of_government "Form of government") in the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union"), [Oceania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceania "Oceania"), and throughout the former [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire"), with other users scattered throughout [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa") and [Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia "Asia"). A similar system, called a [council–manager government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council%E2%80%93manager_government "Council–manager government"), is used by many local governments in the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States").

## History

The first [parliaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament "Parliament") date back to Europe in the Middle Ages. The earliest example of a parliament is disputed, especially depending how the term is defined. For example, the Icelandic [Althing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Althing "Althing") consisting of prominent individuals among the free landowners of the various districts of the [Icelandic Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icelandic_Commonwealth "Icelandic Commonwealth") first gathered around the year 930 (it conducted its business orally, with no written record allowing an exact date). The first written record of a parliament, in particular in the sense of an assembly separate from the population called in presence of a king was in 1188, when Alfonso IX, King of Leon (Spain) convened the three states in the [Cortes of León](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cortes_of_Le%C3%B3n "Cortes of León").[^1] [^2] The [Corts of Catalonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_Courts "Catalan Courts") were the first parliament of Europe that officially obtained the power to pass legislation, apart from the custom.[^3] An early example of parliamentary government also occurred in today's Netherlands and Belgium during the [Dutch revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_revolt "Dutch revolt") (1581), when the sovereign, legislative and executive powers were taken over by the [States General of the Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_General_of_the_Netherlands "States General of the Netherlands") from the monarch, [King Philip II of Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Philip_II_of_Spain "King Philip II of Spain").

In England, [Simon de Montfort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort,_6th_Earl_of_Leicester "Simon de Montfort, 6th Earl of Leicester") is remembered as one of the figures relevant later for convening two famous parliaments.[^4] [^5] [^6] [The first](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford_Parliament_\(1258\) "Oxford Parliament (1258)"), in 1258, stripped the king of unlimited authority and the second, in 1265, included [ordinary citizens from the towns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort%27s_Parliament "Simon de Montfort's Parliament").[^7] Later, in the 17th century, the [Parliament of England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_England "Parliament of England") pioneered some of the ideas and systems of [liberal democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_democracy "Liberal democracy") culminating in the [Glorious Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glorious_Revolution "Glorious Revolution") and passage of the [Bill of Rights 1689](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_of_Rights_1689 "Bill of Rights 1689").[^8] [^9] In the [Kingdom of Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain "Kingdom of Great Britain"), the monarch in theory chaired the cabinet and chose ministers. In practice, [King George I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_I_of_Great_Britain "George I of Great Britain") 's inability to speak English led to the responsibility for chairing cabinet to go to the leading minister, literally the *prime* or first minister, [Robert Walpole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Walpole "Robert Walpole"). The gradual democratisation of Parliament with the broadening of the voting franchise increased Parliament's role in controlling government, and in deciding whom the king could ask to form a government. By the 19th century, the [Great Reform Act 1832](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Reform_Act_1832 "Great Reform Act 1832") led to parliamentary dominance, with its choice deciding who was prime minister and the complexion of the government.[^10] [^11]

Other countries gradually adopted what came to be called the [Westminster system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westminster_system "Westminster system") of government,[^12] with an executive answerable to the lower house of a bicameral parliament, and exercising, in the name of the head of state, powers nominally vested in the head of state – hence the use of phrases such as [*His Majesty's government*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/His_Majesty%27s_Government_\(term\) "His Majesty's Government (term)") (in constitutional monarchies) or *His Excellency's government* (in parliamentary republics).[^13] Such a system became particularly prevalent in older British dominions, many of which had their constitutions enacted by the British parliament; such as Australia, New Zealand, Canada, the [Irish Free State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irish_Free_State "Irish Free State") and the [Union of South Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_of_South_Africa "Union of South Africa").[^14] [^15] [^16] Some of these parliaments were reformed from, or were initially developed as distinct from their original British model: the [Australian Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_Senate "Australian Senate"), for instance, has since its inception more closely reflected the [US Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Senate "United States Senate") than the British [House of Lords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Lords "House of Lords"). Many of these countries, such as [Trinidad and Tobago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago "Trinidad and Tobago") and [Barbados](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbados "Barbados"), have severed institutional ties to Great Britain by becoming republics with their own ceremonial presidents, but still retaining the Westminster system. The idea of parliamentary accountability and [responsible government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Responsible_government "Responsible government") spread with these systems.[^17]

Democracy and parliamentarianism became increasingly prevalent in Europe in the years after [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), partially imposed by the democratic victors, the United States, Great Britain and France, on the defeated countries and their successors, notably [Germany's Weimar Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_Germany "Weimar Germany") and the [First Austrian Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Austrian_Republic "First Austrian Republic"). Nineteenth-century [urbanisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urbanisation "Urbanisation"), the [Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution "Industrial Revolution") and [modernism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernism "Modernism") had already made the parliamentarist demands of the [radicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_radicalism "Classical radicalism") and the emerging movement of [social democrats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_democracy "Social democracy") increasingly difficult to ignore; these forces came to dominate many states that transitioned to parliamentarism, particularly in the [French Third Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Third_Republic "French Third Republic") where the [Radical Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_Party_\(France\) "Radical Party (France)") and its centre-left allies dominated the government for several decades. The rise of [fascism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fascism "Fascism") in the 1930s put an end to parliamentary democracy in Italy and Germany, among others. After the [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), the defeated [Axis powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_powers "Axis powers") were occupied by the victorious [Allies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II "Allies of World War II"). In those countries occupied by the Allied democracies (the United States, United Kingdom, and France) parliamentary constitutions were implemented, resulting in the Constitutions of [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Italy "Constitution of Italy"), [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_Law_for_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany "Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany"), and [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Japan "Constitution of Japan"). The experiences of the war in the occupied nations where the legitimate democratic governments were allowed to return strengthened the public commitment to parliamentary principles; in [Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark "Denmark"), a new constitution was written in 1953, while a long and acrimonious debate in Norway resulted in no changes being made to the [Constitution of Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Norway "Constitution of Norway"), a strongly entrenched democratic constitution.

## Characteristics

A parliamentary system may be either [bicameral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicameralism "Bicameralism"), with two [chambers of parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chambers_of_parliament "Chambers of parliament") (or houses) or [unicameral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicameralism "Unicameralism"), with just one parliamentary chamber. A bicameral parliament usually consists of a directly elected [lower house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_house "Lower house") with the power to determine the executive government, and an [upper house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_house "Upper house") which may be appointed or elected through a different mechanism from the lower house. A 2019 peer-reviewed [meta-analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meta-analysis "Meta-analysis") based on 1,037 regressions in 46 studies finds that presidential systems generally seem to favor revenue cuts, while parliamentary systems would rely more on fiscal expansion characterized by a higher level of spending before an election.[^18]

### Types

Scholars of democracy such as [Arend Lijphart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arend_Lijphart "Arend Lijphart") distinguish two types of parliamentary democracies: the Westminster and Consensus systems.[^19] Other variations exist, which scholars occasionally treat as variations of parliamentary systems, hybrids or [sui generis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sui_generis "Sui generis") forms of government.

#### Westminster system

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Houses.of.parliament.overall.arp.jpg/250px-Houses.of.parliament.overall.arp.jpg)

The Palace of Westminster in London, United Kingdom. The Westminster system originates from the British Houses of Parliament.

The [Westminster system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westminster_system "Westminster system") is usually found in the [Commonwealth of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations") and countries which were influenced by the British political tradition.[^20] [^21] [^22] These parliaments tend to have a more adversarial style of debate and the [plenary session](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plenary_session "Plenary session") of parliament is more important than committees. Some parliaments in this model are elected using a [plurality voting system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plurality_voting_system "Plurality voting system"), such as the United Kingdom, Canada, India and Malaysia, while others use some form of [proportional representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportional_representation "Proportional representation"), such as Ireland and New Zealand. The [Australian House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_House_of_Representatives "Australian House of Representatives") is elected using [instant-runoff voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instant-runoff_voting "Instant-runoff voting"), while the [Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_Senate "Australian Senate") is elected using proportional representation through [single transferable vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_transferable_vote "Single transferable vote"). Regardless of which system is used, the voting systems tend to allow the voter to vote for a named candidate rather than a [closed list](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_list "Closed list"). Most Westminster systems employ strict monism, where ministers must be members of parliament simultaneously; while some Westminster systems, such as [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh"),[^23] permit the appointment of extra-parliamentary ministers, and others (such as [Jamaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamaica "Jamaica")) allow outsiders to be appointed to the ministry through an appointed upper house, although a majority of ministers (which, by necessity, includes the prime minister) must come from within (the lower house of) the parliament.

#### Consensus system

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Berlin_reichstag_CP.jpg/250px-Berlin_reichstag_CP.jpg)

The Reichstag Building in Berlin, Germany. The Consensus system is used in most Western European countries.

The Western European parliamentary model (e.g., Spain, Germany) tends to have a more consensual debating system and usually has semi-circular debating chambers. Consensus systems have more of a tendency to use [proportional representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportional_representation "Proportional representation") with [open party lists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_party_list "Open party list") than the Westminster Model legislatures. The committees of these parliaments tend to be more important than the [plenary chamber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plenary_chamber "Plenary chamber"). Most Western European countries do not employ strict monism, and allow extra-parliamentary ministers as a matter of course. The Netherlands, Slovakia and Sweden outright implement the principle of dualism, where Members of Parliament have to resign their place in Parliament upon being appointed (or elected) minister, as a form of [separation of powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_powers "Separation of powers").

### Atypical parliamentary systems

The [semi-presidential system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-presidential_system "Semi-presidential system") is the most common variant of the parliamentary system. Widely adopted after the 1950s, it is modelled after the [Fifth Republic of France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fifth_Republic_of_France "Fifth Republic of France"). The French model (termed "premier-presidential") [^24] retains the principle that the cabinet is [de jure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_jure "De jure") only politically responsible to parliament, however by also placing the president of the republic in the executive, who, unlike the prime minister, is not subject to parliamentary confidence (dual executive) it creates a hybrid of presidential and parliamentary models. Another model of semi-presidentialism is the "president-parliamentary" model, where the prime-minister and cabinet may also be dismissed be the president. Semi-presidential countries pose a challenge to political scientists as practice often differs from constitutional text. For example the French Republic is considered more presidentialized (president-parliamentary) in practice as presidents have been able to dismiss prime ministers at will.[^25]

In the [semi-parliamentary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-parliamentary_system "Semi-parliamentary system"), the legislature is split into two parts that are both directly elected, but only one has the power to select and remove the members of the executive by a vote of no confidence. This exists in [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia") and [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan").

In the (elected) [prime-ministerial system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime-ministerial_system "Prime-ministerial system") [^26] voters vote for both members of legislature and the [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_minister "Prime minister"). It is possible under a constitutional monarchy, but has only existed in [republics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic "Republic").

A [parliamentary republic with an executive presidency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_republics_with_an_executive_presidency "Parliamentary republics with an executive presidency") is a form of parliamentary republic in which the executive derives its democratic legitimacy from its ability to command the confidence of the legislature to which it is held accountable, but is characterized by a combined head of state–head of government office in the form of an [executive president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_president "Executive president") who carries out both functions.

### Appointment of the head of government

Implementations of the parliamentary system can also differ as to how the prime minister and government are appointed and whether the government needs the explicit approval of the parliament, rather than just the absence of its disapproval. While most parliamentary systems such as India require the prime minister and other ministers to be a member of the legislature, in other countries like Canada and the United Kingdom this only exists as a convention, some other countries including Norway, Sweden and the Benelux countries require a sitting member of the legislature to resign such positions upon being appointed to the executive.

- **The head of state appoints a prime minister who will likely have majority support in parliament**. While in the majority of cases prime ministers in the [Westminster system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westminster_system "Westminster system") are the leaders of the largest party in parliament, technically the appointment of the prime minister is a prerogative exercised by the head of state (be it the monarch, the governor-general, or the president). This system is used in:
	- [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia")
		- [Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada "Canada")
		- [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India")
		- [Jamaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamaica "Jamaica")
		- [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia")
		- [New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand "New Zealand")
		- [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom")
		- [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portugal "Portugal") [^27]
		- [Czech Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_Republic "Czech Republic")
		- [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland "Poland") [^28]
- **The head of state appoints a prime minister who must gain a vote of confidence within a set time.** This system is used in:
	- [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy")
- **The head of state appoints the leader of the political party holding a plurality of seats in parliament as prime minister**. For example, in Greece, if no party has a majority, the leader of the party with a plurality of seats is given an *exploratory mandate* to receive the confidence of the parliament within three days. If said leader fails to obtain the confidence of parliament, then the leader of the *second* -largest party is given the *exploratory mandate*. If that fails, then the leader of the *third* -largest political party is given the *exploratory mandate*, and so on. This system is used in:
	- [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece")
- **The head of state *nominates* a candidate for prime minister who then tries to form a government that doesn't have a majority against them in parliament.** In practice all leaders of political parties meets with the head of state and secretly nominates favorite candidates, and who they would reject. If one candidate fails for form a government another may be appointed, or a new election called.
	- [Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark "Denmark") [^29]
- **The head of state *nominates* a candidate for prime minister who is then submitted to parliament for approval before appointment.** Example: Spain, where the King sends a proposal to the [Congress of Deputies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress_of_Deputies "Congress of Deputies") for approval. Also, Germany where under the [German Basic Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_Law_of_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany "Basic Law of the Federal Republic of Germany") (constitution) the [Bundestag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bundestag "Bundestag") votes on a candidate nominated by the federal president. In these cases, parliament can choose another candidate who then would be appointed by the head of state. This system is used in:
	- [Estonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estonia "Estonia")
		- [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany")
		- [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain")
- **Parliament *nominates* a candidate whom the head of state is constitutionally obliged to appoint as prime minister.** Example: Japan, where the [Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan "Emperor of Japan") appoints the [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Japan "Prime Minister of Japan") on the nomination of the [National Diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Diet "National Diet"). Also Ireland, where the [President of Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Ireland "President of Ireland") appoints the [Taoiseach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoiseach "Taoiseach") on the nomination of [Dáil Éireann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann "Dáil Éireann"). This system is used in:
	- [Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland "Republic of Ireland")
		- [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan")
		- [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand")
- **A public officeholder (other than the head of state or their representative) *nominates* a candidate, who, if approved by parliament, is appointed as prime minister.** Example: Under the Swedish [Instrument of Government (1974)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instrument_of_Government_\(1974\) "Instrument of Government (1974)"), the power to appoint someone to form a government has been moved from the monarch to the Speaker of Parliament and the parliament itself. The speaker nominates a candidate, who is then elected to prime minister (*statsminister*) by the parliament if an absolute majority of the members of parliament does not vote against the candidate (i.e. they can be elected even if more members of parliament vote *No* than *Yes).* This system is used in:
	- [Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden "Sweden")
- **Direct election by popular vote.** Example: Israel, 1996–2001, where the prime minister was elected in a general election, with no regard to political affiliation, and whose procedure can also be described as an [elected prime-ministerial system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime-ministerial_system "Prime-ministerial system").[^30] [^31] [^32] This system was used in:
	- [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel") (1996–2001)

### Head of state

Parliamentary systems vary also vary in how the head of state is elected or selected. [Parliamentary monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy") operate under [hereditary succession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_succession "Order of succession"). Parliamentary republics most commonly elect the head of state directly by popular vote, typically via a [two-round system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-round_system "Two-round system"), therefore a [majority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majority_rule "Majority rule") or plurality principle.

- **The head of state is elected directly**, usually via a two-round system, rarely by [first-past-the-post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting "First-past-the-post voting") voting. This system is used in:
	- [Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austria "Austria")
		- [Croatia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Croatia "Croatia")
		- [Czech Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_Republic "Czech Republic")
		- [Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland "Finland")
		- [Iceland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceland "Iceland")
		- [Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland "Republic of Ireland")
		- [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland "Poland")
		- [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portugal "Portugal") [^27]
		- [Slovakia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovakia "Slovakia")
		- [Slovenia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovenia "Slovenia")
- **The head of state is a constitutional monarch**. This system is used in:
	- [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia")
		- [Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada "Canada")
		- [Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark "Denmark")
		- [Jamaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamaica "Jamaica")
		- [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan")
		- [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia")
		- [New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand "New Zealand")
		- [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain")
		- [Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden "Sweden")
		- [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom")
- **The head of state is elected by members of parliament under a special voting procedure.** This usually involves supermajority rules in the first rounds, and simple majority rule in subsequent rounds. This system is used in:
	- [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy")
		- [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece")

### Power of dissolution and call for election

Furthermore, there are variations as to what conditions exist (if any) for the government to have the right to dissolve the parliament:

- In some countries, especially those operating under a [Westminster system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westminster_system "Westminster system"), such as the United Kingdom, Denmark, Malaysia, Australia and New Zealand, the prime minister has the *de facto* power to call an election, at will. In Spain, the prime minister is the only person with the *de jure* power to call an election, granted by Article 115 of the [Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Spain "Constitution of Spain").
- In Israel, parliament may vote to dissolve itself in order to call an election, or the prime minister may call a snap election with presidential consent if his government is deadlocked. A non-passage of the budget automatically calls a snap election.
- Other countries only permit an election to be called in the event of a [vote of no confidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vote_of_no_confidence "Vote of no confidence") against the government, a supermajority vote in favour of an early election or a prolonged deadlock in parliament. These requirements can still be circumvented. For example, in Germany in 2005, [Gerhard Schröder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerhard_Schr%C3%B6der "Gerhard Schröder") deliberately allowed his government to lose a confidence motion, in order to call an early election.
- In Sweden, the government may call a snap election at will, but the newly elected [Riksdag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riksdag "Riksdag") is only elected to fill out the previous Riksdag's term. The last time this option was used was in [1958](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1958_Swedish_general_election "1958 Swedish general election").
- In [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece"), before 2019, a general election was called if the [Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenic_Parliament "Hellenic Parliament") failed to elect a new [head of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Greece "President of Greece") when his or her term ended. In January 2015, [this constitutional provision was exploited](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014%E2%80%932015_Greek_presidential_election "2014–2015 Greek presidential election") by [Syriza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syriza "Syriza") to [trigger a snap election, win it](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/January_2015_Greek_legislative_election "January 2015 Greek legislative election") and oust rivals [New Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Democracy_\(Greece\) "New Democracy (Greece)") from power. After the [2019 constitutional revision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_constitutional_amendment_of_2019 "Greek constitutional amendment of 2019"), the election of the President can no longer trigger a general election.
- In Italy the government has no power to call a snap election. A snap election can only be called by the [head of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Italy "President of Italy"), following a consultation with the presidents of both houses of parliament.
- Norway is unique among parliamentary systems in that the [Storting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Storting "Storting") always serves the whole of its four-year term. In the event of a [vote of no confidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vote_of_no_confidence "Vote of no confidence"), neither the [head of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_of_Norway "King of Norway") nor the outgoing [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_minister_of_Norway "Prime minister of Norway") has the legal authority to call a snap election.
- In Australia, under certain, unique conditions, the [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Australia "Prime Minister of Australia") can request the [Governor General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governor-General_of_Australia "Governor-General of Australia") to call for a [double dissolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_dissolution "Double dissolution"), whereby all rather than only half of the [Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_Senate "Australian Senate"), is dissolved – in effect electing all of the Parliament simultaneously.

The parliamentary system can be contrasted with a [presidential system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_system "Presidential system") which operates under a stricter separation of powers, whereby the executive does not form part of—nor is appointed by—the parliamentary or legislative body. In such a system, parliaments or congresses do not select or dismiss heads of government, and governments cannot request an early dissolution as may be the case for parliaments (although the parliament may still be able to dissolve itself, as in the case of [Cyprus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyprus "Cyprus")). There also exists the [semi-presidential system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-presidential_system "Semi-presidential system") that draws on both presidential systems and parliamentary systems by combining a powerful president with an executive responsible to parliament: for example, the [French Fifth Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Fifth_Republic "French Fifth Republic").

Parliamentarianism may also apply to [regional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_government "Regional government") and [local governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_government "Local government"). An example is [Oslo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oslo "Oslo") which has an executive council (Byråd) as a part of the parliamentary system. The [devolved nations of the United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom "Devolution in the United Kingdom") are also parliamentary and which, as with the [UK Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom "Parliament of the United Kingdom"), may hold early elections – this has only occurred with regards to the [Northern Ireland Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly "Northern Ireland Assembly") in [2017](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2017_Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election "2017 Northern Ireland Assembly election") and [2022](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Next_Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election "Next Northern Ireland Assembly election").

### Anti-defection law

A few parliamentary democratic nations such as [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-defection_law_\(India\) "Anti-defection law (India)"), Pakistan and Bangladesh have enacted laws that prohibit floor crossing or switching parties after the election. Under these laws, elected representatives will lose their seat in the parliament if they go against their party in votes.[^33] [^34] [^35]

In the UK parliament, a member is free to cross over to a different party. In Canada and Australia, there are no restraints on legislators switching sides.[^36] In New Zealand, [waka-jumping legislation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waka-jumping#Legislation "Waka-jumping") provides that MPs who switch parties or are expelled from their party may be expelled from Parliament at the request of their former party's leader.

### Parliamentary sovereignty

A few parliamentary democracies such as the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") and [New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand "New Zealand") have weak or non-existent checks on the legislative power of their Parliaments,[^37] [^38] where any newly approved Act shall take precedence over all prior Acts. All laws are equally unentrenched, wherein [judicial review](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judicial_review "Judicial review") may not outright annul nor amend them, as frequently occurs in other parliamentary systems like [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_review_in_Germany "Constitutional review in Germany"). Whilst the head of state for both nations ([Monarch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom "Monarchy of the United Kingdom"), and or [Governor General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governor-General_of_New_Zealand "Governor-General of New Zealand")) has the de jure power to withhold [assent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_assent "Royal assent") to any bill passed by their Parliament, this check has not been exercised in Britain since the [1708 Scottish Militia Bill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scottish_Militia_Bill "Scottish Militia Bill").

Whilst both the UK and New Zealand have some Acts or parliamentary rules establishing [supermajorities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermajority "Supermajority") or additional legislative procedures for certain legislation, such as previously with the [Fixed-term Parliaments Act 2011](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed-term_Parliaments_Act_2011 "Fixed-term Parliaments Act 2011") (FTPA), these can be bypassed through the enactment of another that amends or ignores these supermajorities away, such as with the [Early Parliamentary General Election Act 2019](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Parliamentary_General_Election_Act_2019 "Early Parliamentary General Election Act 2019") – bypassing the 2/3rd supermajority required for an early dissolution under the FTPA [^39] -, which enabled the early dissolution for the [2019 general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_United_Kingdom_general_election "2019 United Kingdom general election").

### Metrics

Parliamentarism metrics allow a quantitative comparison of the strength of parliamentary systems for individual countries. One parliamentarism metric is the Parliamentary Powers Index.[^40]

## Advantages

### Adaptability

Parliamentary systems like that found in the United Kingdom are widely considered to be more flexible, allowing a rapid change in legislation and policy as long as there is a stable majority or coalition in parliament, allowing the government to have 'few legal limits on what it can do' [^41] When combined with [first-past-the-post voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting "First-past-the-post voting"), this system produces the classic "Westminster model" with the twin virtues of strong but responsive party government.[^42] This electoral system providing a strong majority in the House of Commons, paired with the [fused power system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fusion_of_powers "Fusion of powers") results in a particularly powerful government able to provide change and 'innovate'.[^41]

### Scrutiny and accountability

The United Kingdom's fused power system is often noted to be advantageous with regard to accountability. The centralised government allows for more transparency as to where decisions originate from, this contrasts with the American system with Treasury Secretary [C. Douglas Dillon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C._Douglas_Dillon "C. Douglas Dillon") saying "the president blames Congress, the Congress blames the president, and the public remains confused and disgusted with government in Washington".[^43] Furthermore, ministers of the UK cabinet are subject to weekly Question Periods in which their actions/policies are scrutinised; no such regular check on the government exists in the U.S. system.

### Distribution of power

A 2001 [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank") study found that parliamentary systems are associated with less corruption.[^44]

### Calling of elections

In his 1867 book *[The English Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_English_Constitution "The English Constitution")*, [Walter Bagehot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Bagehot "Walter Bagehot") praised parliamentary governments for producing serious debates, for allowing for a change in power without an election, and for allowing elections at any time. Bagehot considered [fixed-term elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed-term_election "Fixed-term election") such as the four-year election rule for [presidents of the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_the_United_States "President of the United States") to be unnatural, as it can potentially allow a president who has disappointed the public with a dismal performance in the second year of their term to continue on until the end of their four-year term. Under a parliamentary system, a prime minister that has lost support in the middle of their term can be easily replaced by their own peers with a more popular alternative, as the Conservative Party in the UK did with successive prime ministers [David Cameron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Cameron "David Cameron"), [Theresa May](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theresa_May "Theresa May"), [Boris Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boris_Johnson "Boris Johnson"), [Liz Truss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liz_Truss "Liz Truss"), and [Rishi Sunak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rishi_Sunak "Rishi Sunak").

Although Bagehot praised parliamentary governments for allowing an election to take place at any time, the lack of a definite election calendar can be abused. Under some systems, such as the British, a ruling party can schedule elections when it believes that it is likely to retain power, and so avoid elections at times of unpopularity. (From 2011, election timing in the UK was partially fixed under the [Fixed-term Parliaments Act 2011](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed-term_Parliaments_Act_2011 "Fixed-term Parliaments Act 2011"), which was repealed by the [Dissolution and Calling of Parliament Act 2022](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_and_Calling_of_Parliament_Act_2022 "Dissolution and Calling of Parliament Act 2022").) Thus, by a shrewd timing of elections, in a parliamentary system, a party can extend its rule for longer than is feasible in a presidential system. This problem can be alleviated somewhat by setting fixed dates for parliamentary elections, as is the case in several of Australia's state parliaments. In other systems, such as the Dutch and the Belgian, the ruling party or coalition has some flexibility in determining the election date. Conversely, flexibility in the timing of parliamentary elections can avoid periods of legislative gridlock that can occur in a fixed period presidential system. In any case, voters ultimately have the power to choose whether to vote for the ruling party or someone else.

## Disadvantages

### Incomplete separation of powers

According to [Arturo Fontaine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arturo_Fontaine_Talavera "Arturo Fontaine Talavera"), parliamentary systems in Europe have yielded very powerful heads of government which is rather what is often criticized about presidential systems. Fontaine compares United Kingdom's [Margaret Thatcher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher "Margaret Thatcher") to the United States' [Ronald Reagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Reagan "Ronald Reagan") noting the former head of government was much more powerful despite governing under a parliamentary system.[^45] The rise to power of [Viktor Orbán](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viktor_Orb%C3%A1n "Viktor Orbán") in Hungary has been claimed to show how parliamentary systems can be subverted.[^45] The situation in Hungary was according to Fontaine allowed by the deficient separation of powers that characterises parliamentary and semi-presidential systems.[^45] Once [Orbán's party got two-thirds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Hungarian_parliamentary_election "2010 Hungarian parliamentary election") of the seats in Parliament in a single election, a [supermajority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermajority "Supermajority") large enough to amend the Hungarian constitution, there was no institution that was able to balance the concentration of power.[^45] In a presidential system it would require at least two separate elections to create the same effect; the presidential election, and the legislative election, and that the president's party has the legislative supermajority required for constitutional amendments. Safeguards against this situation implementable in both systems include the establishment of an upper house or a requirement for external ratification of constitutional amendments such as a [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Referendum "Referendum"). Fontaine also notes as a warning example of the flaws of parliamentary systems that if the United States had a parliamentary system, [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump"), as head of government, could have dissolved the [United States Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Congress "United States Congress").[^45]

### Legislative flip-flopping

The ability for strong parliamentary governments to push legislation through with the ease of fused power systems such as in the United Kingdom, whilst positive in allowing rapid adaptation when necessary e.g. the nationalisation of services during the world wars, in the opinion of some commentators does have its drawbacks. For instance, the flip-flopping of legislation back and forth as the majority in parliament changed between the Conservatives and Labour over the period 1940–1980, contesting over the nationalisation and privatisation of the British Steel Industry resulted in major instability for the British steel sector.[^41]

### Political fragmentation

In R. Kent Weaver's book *Are Parliamentary Systems Better?*, he writes that an advantage of presidential systems is their ability to allow and accommodate more diverse viewpoints. He states that because "legislators are not compelled to vote against their constituents on matters of local concern, parties can serve as organizational and roll-call cuing vehicles without forcing out dissidents".[^41]

### Democratic unaccountability

All current parliamentary democracies see the indirect election or appointment of their head of government. As a result, the electorate has limited power to remove or install the person or party wielding the most power. Although [strategic voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_voting "Strategic voting") may enable the party of the prime minister to be removed or empowered, this can be at the expense of voters first preferences in the many parliamentary systems utilising [first past the post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting "First-past-the-post voting"), or having no effect in dislodging those parties who consistently form part of a coalition government, as with then [Dutch prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_Netherlands "Prime Minister of the Netherlands") [Mark Rutte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Rutte "Mark Rutte") and his party the [VVD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Party_for_Freedom_and_Democracy "People's Party for Freedom and Democracy") 's 4 terms in office, despite their peak support reaching only [26.6% in 2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Dutch_general_election "2012 Dutch general election").[^46]

| Bangladesh | [Jatiya Sangsad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jatiya_Sangsad "Jatiya Sangsad") approves the [Cabinet of Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Bangladesh "Cabinet of Bangladesh") |
| Bhutan | [Parliament of Bhutan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Bhutan "Parliament of Bhutan") approves the [Lhengye Zhungtshog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lhengye_Zhungtshog "Lhengye Zhungtshog") |
| Cambodia | [Parliament of Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Cambodia "Parliament of Cambodia") approves the [Council of Ministers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_\(Cambodia\) "Council of Ministers (Cambodia)") |
| Republic of China (Taiwan) | - 1947 Constitution: The [Legislative Yuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislative_Yuan "Legislative Yuan") approves the [Executive Yuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_Yuan "Executive Yuan") in which the premier is nominated and appointed by the president, with the consent of the Legislative Yuan. - 2005 Amendments: The [Legislative Yuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislative_Yuan "Legislative Yuan") approves the [Executive Yuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_Yuan "Executive Yuan") in which the premier is appointed by the president. The Legislative Yuan may vote for motion of no confidence. |
| Georgia | The [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Georgia "Prime Minister of Georgia") is nominated by a political party that has secured the best results in the parliamentary election. The nominee must be approved by the [Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Georgia_\(country\) "Parliament of Georgia (country)") and formally by the [President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Georgia "President of Georgia"). The Prime Minister then appoints the [Cabinet of Ministers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Georgia_\(country\) "Government of Georgia (country)"). |
| India | [President of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_India "President of India") appoints the leader of the political party or [alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alliance "Alliance") that has the support of a majority in the [Lok Sabha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lok_Sabha "Lok Sabha") as [Prime Minister of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_India "Prime Minister of India"), who then forms the [Union Council of Ministers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_Council_of_Ministers "Union Council of Ministers") |
| Iraq | [Council of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Representatives_of_Iraq "Council of Representatives of Iraq") approves the [Cabinet of Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Iraq "Cabinet of Iraq") |
| Israel | A member of the [Knesset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knesset "Knesset") that has the best chance of forming a coalition is given a mandate to do so by the [President of Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Israel "President of Israel"). On success, they are appointed as the [Prime Minister of Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Israel "Prime Minister of Israel"). The Prime Minister then appoints the [Cabinet of Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Israel "Cabinet of Israel"). |
| Japan | [National Diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Diet "National Diet") nominates the [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Japan "Prime Minister of Japan") who appoints the [Cabinet of Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Japan "Cabinet of Japan") |
| Kuwait | [National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_\(Kuwait\) "National Assembly (Kuwait)") approves the Crown Prince who appoints the [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Kuwait "Prime Minister of Kuwait") who appoints the [Cabinet of Kuwait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Kuwait "Cabinet of Kuwait") |
| Laos | [National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_\(Laos\) "National Assembly (Laos)") elects the [President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Laos "President of Laos") who nominates the [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Laos "Prime Minister of Laos") |
| Lebanon | Maronite Christian president is elected by the [Parliament of Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Lebanon "Parliament of Lebanon"). He appoints the Prime Minister (a Sunni Muslim) and the cabinet. The Parliament thereafter approves the [Cabinet of Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Lebanon "Cabinet of Lebanon") through a vote of confidence (a simple majority). |
| Malaysia | Leader of the political party that has the support of a majority in the [Dewan Rakyat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dewan_Rakyat "Dewan Rakyat") is appointed [Prime Minister of Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Malaysia "Prime Minister of Malaysia") by the [Yang di-Pertuan Agong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_di-Pertuan_Agong "Yang di-Pertuan Agong"), who then appoints the [Cabinet of Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Malaysia "Cabinet of Malaysia") on the advice of the Prime Minister. |
| Myanmar | [Assembly of the Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Union "Assembly of the Union"), by an [electoral college](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_Electoral_College "Presidential Electoral College"), elects the [President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Myanmar "President of Myanmar") who forms the [Cabinet of Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Myanmar "Cabinet of Myanmar"). However, Myanmar is currently under the rule of the [State Administration Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Administration_Council "State Administration Council"), which assumed power by [coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Myanmar_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "2021 Myanmar coup d'état") |
| Nepal | [Parliament of Nepal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Nepal "Parliament of Nepal") elects the [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Nepal "Prime Minister of Nepal") who, by turn, appoints the [Cabinet of Nepal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Nepal "Cabinet of Nepal") |
| Pakistan | [Parliament of Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Pakistan "Parliament of Pakistan") elects the [Prime Minister of Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Pakistan "Prime Minister of Pakistan") who has majority support of members of [National Assembly of Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Pakistan "National Assembly of Pakistan"). [Cabinet of Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Pakistan "Cabinet of Pakistan") is appointed by [President of Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Pakistan "President of Pakistan") on advice of Prime Minister. |
| Singapore | Leader of the political party that has the support of a majority in the [Parliament of Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Singapore "Parliament of Singapore") is appointed [Prime Minister of Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Singapore "Prime Minister of Singapore") by the [President of Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Singapore "President of Singapore"), who then appoints the [Cabinet of Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Singapore "Cabinet of Singapore") on the advice of the Prime Minister. |
| Thailand | The [Monarch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_of_Thailand "Monarchy of Thailand") appoints the MP or individual nominated by in the [House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Representatives_\(Thailand\) "House of Representatives (Thailand)") (usually the leader of the largest party or coalition) as [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Thailand "Prime Minister of Thailand"), who forms the [Cabinet of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Thailand "Cabinet of Thailand"). |
| Vietnam | [National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_\(Vietnam\) "National Assembly (Vietnam)") elects the [President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Vietnam "President of Vietnam") and [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Vietnam "Prime Minister of Vietnam") who forms the [Cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Vietnam "Government of Vietnam"). |

The [Government of the Republic of Estonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_the_Republic_of_Estonia "Government of the Republic of Estonia") is later appointed by the President of the Republic under proposal of the approved Prime Minister candidate. The Riigikogu may remove the Prime Minister and any other member of the government through a motion of no confidence. |
| Finland | [Parliament of Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Finland "Parliament of Finland") appoints the [Cabinet of Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Finland "Cabinet of Finland") |
| Germany | [Bundestag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bundestag "Bundestag") elects the [Federal Chancellor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany_\(Federal_Republic\) "Chancellor of Germany (Federal Republic)") (after nomination from the [President of Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Germany "President of Germany")), who forms the [Cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Germany "Cabinet of Germany") |
| Greece | [Hellenic Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenic_Parliament "Hellenic Parliament") approves the [Cabinet of Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Greece "Cabinet of Greece") |
| Hungary | [National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Hungary "National Assembly of Hungary") approves the [Cabinet of Hungary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Hungary "Cabinet of Hungary") |
| Iceland | The [President of Iceland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Iceland "President of Iceland") appoints and discharges the [Cabinet of Iceland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Iceland "Cabinet of Iceland"). Ministers can not even resign without being discharged by [presidential decree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decree "Decree"). |
| Ireland | [Dáil Éireann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann "Dáil Éireann") nominates the [Taoiseach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoiseach "Taoiseach"), who is then appointed by the [President of Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Ireland "President of Ireland") |

| Kosovo | [Assembly of Kosovo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assembly_of_Kosovo "Assembly of Kosovo") appoints the [Government of Kosovo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Kosovo "Government of Kosovo") |

| Luxembourg | [Chamber of Deputies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies_of_Luxembourg "Chamber of Deputies of Luxembourg") appoints the [Cabinet of Luxembourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Luxembourg "Cabinet of Luxembourg") |
| Malta | [House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Representatives_of_Malta "House of Representatives of Malta") appoints the [Cabinet of Malta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Malta "Cabinet of Malta") |
| Moldova | [Parliament of Moldova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Moldova "Parliament of Moldova") appoints the [Cabinet of Moldova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Moldova "Cabinet of Moldova") |
| Montenegro | [Parliament of Montenegro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Montenegro "Parliament of Montenegro") appoints the [Government of Montenegro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Montenegro "Government of Montenegro") |
| Netherlands | [Second Chamber of the States-General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_General_of_the_Netherlands "States General of the Netherlands") can dismiss the [Cabinet of the Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_Netherlands "Cabinet of the Netherlands") through a [motion of no confidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_of_no_confidence "Motion of no confidence") |
| North Macedonia | [Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assembly_of_North_Macedonia "Assembly of North Macedonia") approves the [Government of North Macedonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_North_Macedonia#Executive_branch "Politics of North Macedonia") |
| Norway | The [Monarch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_of_Norway "Monarchy of Norway") appoints the MP leading the largest party or coalition in [Stortinget](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stortinget "Stortinget") as [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Norway "Prime Minister of Norway"), who forms the [Cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Norway "Cabinet of Norway") |
| Poland | The [President of Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Poland "President of Poland") appoints the [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Poland "Prime Minister of Poland") and the [Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Poland "Cabinet of Poland"), subject to a vote of confidence by the [Sejm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sejm "Sejm") within two weeks. If this fails, the Sejm can approve its own candidate with an absolute majority of votes within two weeks. |
| Portugal | After the [elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elections_in_Portugal "Elections in Portugal") for the [Assembly of the Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Republic_\(Portugal\) "Assembly of the Republic (Portugal)") or the resignation of the previous government, the [president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Portugal "President of Portugal") listens to the parties in the Assembly of the Republic and invites someone to form a government, usually the leader of the biggest party. | San Marino |  |

| Slovenia | [National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_\(Slovenia\) "National Assembly (Slovenia)") appoints the [Government of Slovenia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Slovenia "Government of Slovenia") |
