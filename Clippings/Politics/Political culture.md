**Political culture** describes how [culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture "Culture") impacts [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics"). Every [political system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_system "Political system") is embedded in a particular political culture.[^1] Political culture is what the people, the voters, the electorates believe and do based on their understanding of the political system in which they have found themselves. These may be regarded as good or bad when compared with global best practices or norms.

## Definition

[Gabriel Almond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabriel_Almond "Gabriel Almond") defines it as "the particular pattern of orientations toward political actions in which every political system is embedded".[^1]

[Lucian Pye's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucian_Pye "Lucian Pye") definition is that "Political culture is the set of attitudes, beliefs, and sentiments, which give order and meaning to a political process and which provide the underlying assumptions and rules that govern behavior in the political system".[^1]

[María Eugenia Vázquez Semadeni](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mar%C3%ADa_Eugenia_V%C3%A1zquez_Semadeni&action=edit&redlink=1 "María Eugenia Vázquez Semadeni (page does not exist)") defines political culture as "the set of discourses and symbolic practices by means of which both individuals and groups articulate their relationship to power, elaborate their political demands and put them at stake." [^2]

## Analysis

The limits of a particular political culture are based on subjective [identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_\(social_science\) "Identity (social science)").[^1] The most common form of such identity today is the [national identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_identity "National identity"), and hence [nation states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation_state "Nation state") set the typical limits of political cultures.[^1] The [sociocultural system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociocultural_system "Sociocultural system"), in turn, gives meaning to a political culture through shared symbols and rituals (such as a national [independence day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_national_independence_days "List of national independence days")) which reflect common values.[^1] This may develop into a [civil religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_religion "Civil religion"). The values themselves can be more [hierarchical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hierarchy "Hierarchy") or [egalitarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egalitarianism "Egalitarianism"), and will set the limits to [political participation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_participation_\(decision_making\) "Public participation (decision making)"), thereby creating a basis for [legitimacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legitimacy_\(political\) "Legitimacy (political)").[^1] They are transmitted through [socialization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialization "Socialization"), and shaped by shared historical experiences which form the [collective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_memory "Collective memory") or [national memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_memory "National memory").[^1] [Intellectuals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellectual "Intellectual") will continue to interpret the political culture through political [discourse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discourse "Discourse") in the [public sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_sphere "Public sphere").[^1] Indeed, [elite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elite "Elite") political culture is more consequential than [mass-level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_politics "Mass politics").[^3]

### Elements

[Trust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trust_\(social_science\) "Trust (social science)") is a major factor in political culture, as its level determines the state's capacity to function.[^3] [Postmaterialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmaterialism "Postmaterialism") is the degree to which political culture is concerned with issues which are not of immediate physical or material concern, such as [human rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights "Human rights") and [environmentalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmentalism "Environmentalism").[^1] [Religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion "Religion") has also an impact on political culture.[^3]

## Classifications

Different [typologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") of political culture have been proposed.

### Almond & Verba

[Gabriel Almond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabriel_Almond "Gabriel Almond") and [Sidney Verba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidney_Verba "Sidney Verba") in *[The Civic Culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Civic_Culture "The Civic Culture")* outlined three pure types of political culture based on level and type of [political participation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_participation "Political participation") and the nature of people's attitudes toward politics:

- [Parochial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parochial_political_culture "Parochial political culture") – Where citizens are only remotely aware of the presence of central government, and live their lives near enough regardless of the decisions taken by the state, distant and unaware of political phenomena. They have neither knowledge nor interest in politics. This type of political culture is generally congruent with a traditional political structure.
- [Subject](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commoner "Commoner") – Where citizens are aware of [central government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_government "Central government"), and are heavily subjected to its decisions with little scope for dissent. The individual is aware of politics, its actors, and institutions. It is effectively oriented towards politics, yet it is on the "downward flow" side of politics. In general, it is congruent with a centralized authoritarian structure.
- [Participant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_participation "Civic participation") – [Citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizen "Citizen") can influence the government in various ways, and they are affected by it. The individual is oriented toward the system as a whole, to both the political and administrative structures and processes (to both the input and output aspects). In general, it is congruent with a democratic political structure.

Almond and Verba wrote that these types of political culture can combine to create the [civic culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_culture "Civic culture"), which mixes the best elements of each.

### Elazar

[Daniel J. Elazar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_J._Elazar "Daniel J. Elazar") identified three kinds of political culture:[^3]

- [Individualistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individualistic "Individualistic") culture – In which politics is a marketplace between individuals seeking to maximize their [self-interest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-interest "Self-interest"), with minimal community involvement and opposition to the government, as well as a high degree of [patronage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patronage "Patronage"). *See also:* [Neopatrimonialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neopatrimonialism "Neopatrimonialism").
- [Moralistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moralism "Moralism") culture – Whereby government is seen as important and as a way to improve people's lives.
- [Traditionalistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tradition "Tradition") culture – One which seeks to preserve the status quo under which elites have all the power and citizen participation is not expected.

### Huntington

[Samuel P. Huntington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_P._Huntington "Samuel P. Huntington") classified political cultures according to [civilizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civilization "Civilization") on the basis of [geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography "Geography") and [history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History "History"):[^3]

- [Western](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_world "Western world") civilization
- [Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan") civilization
- [Islamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") civilization
- [Hindu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism") civilization
- [Slavic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavs "Slavs") - [Orthodox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church "Eastern Orthodox Church") civilization
- [Latin American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_America "Latin America") civilization
- [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") civilization
- [African](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa") civilization

### Inglehart

[Ronald Inglehart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Inglehart "Ronald Inglehart") proposes that political culture can dictate political systems, and points out a correlation between Protestantism (or more generally societies with high Secular-Rational values and high Self Expression values on the Inglehart-Weltzel values map) and stable democratization.[^4] However, the recurrent post-elections clashes in largely protestant Sub-Saharan countries, such as Kenya or Uganda, shows that religious affiliations seem to affect the political behaviour of populations poorly.

## National political cultures

### Russia

[Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia") is a [low-trust society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-trust_society "Low-trust society"), with even the highest trusted institutions of [church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church "Russian Orthodox Church") and the [military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Armed_Forces "Russian Armed Forces") having more distrustful than trusting citizens, and with low participation in [civil society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_society "Civil society").[^3] [^5] This means that Russia has a weak [civic political culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_political_culture "Civic political culture"). Furthermore, the authoritarian traditions of Russia mean that there is little support for democratic norms such as tolerance of dissent and [pluralism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluralism_\(political_philosophy\) "Pluralism (political philosophy)").[^6] Russia has a history of authoritarian rulers from [Ivan the Terrible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivan_the_Terrible "Ivan the Terrible") to [Joseph Stalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Stalin "Joseph Stalin"), who have engaged in massive repression of all potential political competitors, from the [oprichnina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oprichnina "Oprichnina") to the [Great Purge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Purge "Great Purge"). The resulting political systems of [Tsarist autocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsarist_autocracy "Tsarist autocracy") and Soviet communism had no space for independent institutions.

### United States

The background of its early immigrants heavily influenced the political culture of the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), as it is a [settler society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Settler_society "Settler society"). [Samuel P. Huntington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_P._Huntington "Samuel P. Huntington") identified American politics as having a " [Tudor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tudor_period "Tudor period") " character, with elements of English political culture of that period, such as [common law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_law "Common law"), strong [courts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Court "Court"), local [self-rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-governance "Self-governance"), decentralized [sovereignty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereignty "Sovereignty") across institutions, and reliance on popular [militias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Militia_\(United_States\) "Militia (United States)") instead of a [standing army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standing_army "Standing army"), having been imported by early settlers.[^7] Another source of political culture was the arrival of [Scotch-Irish Americans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scotch-Irish_Americans "Scotch-Irish Americans"), who came from a violent region of Britain, and brought with them a strong sense of [individualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individualism "Individualism") and support for the [right to bear arms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_to_keep_and_bear_arms "Right to keep and bear arms").[^8] These settlers provided the support for [Jacksonian democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacksonian_democracy "Jacksonian democracy"), which was a revolution of its time against the established elites, and remnants of which can still be seen in modern American [populism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Populism "Populism").[^8]

### China

The [political culture of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_culture_of_China "Political culture of China") is tied closely to political [socialization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialization "Socialization"), as children are [indoctrinated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indoctrinated "Indoctrinated") into the [collectivist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureaucratic_collectivism "Bureaucratic collectivism") perspective of the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party"). This inculcation is theorized to explain the delayed growth of [secularism in Chinese culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreligion_in_China "Irreligion in China"), especially during the [Cultural Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_Revolution "Cultural Revolution").[^9] Chinese political culture perceives the relationship between [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government") and [individuals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individual "Individual") to be a [hierarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hierarchy "Hierarchy"). Because of this, there is little pushback from individuals during policy and regulation changes. The political culture also shows a trend against [confrontationality](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Confrontationality&action=edit&redlink=1 "Confrontationality (page does not exist)"), which decreases the quantity and frequency of [social conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_conflict "Social conflict").[^10] Both of these qualities stem from traditional [Chinese values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_values "Chinese values") embedded during the [age of Confucianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Age_of_Confucianism&action=edit&redlink=1 "Age of Confucianism (page does not exist)"). When the Chinese Communist Party took power in 1948, [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") unsuccessfully attempted to remove these traits from the culture, instead prioritizing revolutionary values.[^11]

### India

Due to [India's colonization by the British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_India "Colonial India"), the contemporary political culture has been [influenced by Western ideas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westernization "Westernization") that were not present before, so we also follow Western political culture. These influences include [democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") and [parliamentary systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system"), two institutions that stood ideologically opposed to the [caste system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caste_system "Caste system") that had previously dictated society.[^12] Because of India's [multicultural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicultural "Multicultural") demography, the political culture varies by group and region. [India's successful democratization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy_in_India "Democracy in India") lead to power being given to both the [urbanized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urbanization "Urbanization") and well-educated class who focused on national appeal, as well as more traditional, rural, and lower class political actors.[^13] In the [modern era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_era "Modern era"), the [class system of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caste_system_in_India "Caste system in India") has begun to break down, and members of lower classes are now entering higher political and economic positions. This is especially true for lower-class women, who historically have been excluded from such activities.[^14]
