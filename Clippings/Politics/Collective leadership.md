In [communist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist "Communist") and [socialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist "Socialist") theory, **collective leadership** is a shared distribution of power within an organizational structure. A single officer, such as the [general secretary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_secretary "General secretary") or [party chair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Party_chair "Party chair"), is supposed to be only *[primus inter pares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primus_inter_pares "Primus inter pares")* on the relevant leadership body.

## Communist states

### China

Official post-Mao historiography portrays 1966 as the year when [Mao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") wrongly abandoned the organizational principle of collective leadership ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters "Simplified Chinese characters"): 集体领导; [pinyin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin "Pinyin"): *jiti lingdao*).[^1] Collective leadership in the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC) and the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) is generally considered to have then begun with reformist [Deng Xiaoping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping "Deng Xiaoping") in the late 1970s during the same time period as the [reform and opening up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reform_and_opening_up "Reform and opening up"), who tried to encourage the [CCP Politburo Standing Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Politburo Standing Committee of the Chinese Communist Party") to rule by consensus in order to prevent a resurgence of [autocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy") under [Maoist rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maoism "Maoism"). CCP general secretary [Jiang Zemin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiang_Zemin "Jiang Zemin") formally established himself as the " [first among equals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primus_inter_pares "Primus inter pares") ". Some political analysts has alleged that this era of collective leadership has been said to end with [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping"), following the abolition of [term limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Term_limits "Term limits") in 2018 under his tenure.[^2] Academic Joseph Torigian, however, has argued that the notion of collective leadership under Deng is a "myth", stating that Deng used collective leadership to politically damage [Hua Guofeng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hua_Guofeng "Hua Guofeng"), who he believes adhered to collective leadership far more.[^3]

[Brookings Institution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brookings_Institution "Brookings Institution") fellow and former [US National Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_National_Security_Council "United States National Security Council") China director Jonathan A. Czin argues Hu Jintao's adherence to collective leadership "prevented him and others from acting decisively", meaning "Hu’s attempts to reorient policy—including efforts to address the glaring inequalities he saw emerging from China’s modernization—largely failed to gain traction" and that corruption became endemic. Czin argues this led Xi to believe collective leadership was the "source of many of the party’s maladies" and led to Xi's centralization of power.[^4]

Xi has taken deliberate steps to establish his personal dominance within the Chinese political system, effectively rising above his peers in the Politburo Standing Committee. He has done so by creating key bodies such as the National Security Commission, which holds sway over party, state, and military organizations. Furthermore, Xi heads the Small Leadership Group on Comprehensively Deepening Reform, a pivotal entity responsible for designing and executing various reform initiatives. His leadership of this group underscores his intention to personally oversee institutional reforms.

Xi has made it clear that he will have the final say in economic and financial matters, foregoing the tradition of shared responsibility with the Premier. Consequently, Xi's purview now extends to encompass military affairs, security, foreign policy, economic reform, state-building, economic policymaking, and social governance.[^5] This concentration of power has led to concerns that Xi's actions might be undermining essential party norms and pushing China toward a more personalistic dictatorship, a notion reinforced by the party machine and state media's vigorous promotion of his image and authority through various channels such as publishing his speeches and writings, public appearances, and the creation of cartoons portraying him as a strong leader.[^6]

The central authority of the [Chinese government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_government "Chinese government") and CCP is concentrated in the CCP Politburo Standing Committee, which is composed of seven members of the Communist Party and headed by the CCP general secretary.[^7] Nevertheless, while the CCP *[de jure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_jure "De jure")* maintains collective leadership of government, the position of the CCP general secretary has palpably become more powerful under [Xi's administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping_Administration "Xi Jinping Administration"), with him being the most powerful paramount leader since [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong").[^8]

### Vietnam

In the [Socialist Republic of Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_Republic_of_Vietnam "Socialist Republic of Vietnam") (SRV) under the [Communist Party of Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Vietnam "Communist Party of Vietnam") (CPV), when the country was ruled by [Lê Duẩn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%AA_Du%E1%BA%A9n "Lê Duẩn"), collective leadership involved powers being distributed from the office of [General Secretary of the Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam "General Secretary of the Communist Party of Vietnam") and shared with the Politburo Standing Committee while still retaining one ruler. Power is shared between the General Secretary, [President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Vietnam "President of Vietnam"), the [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Vietnam "Prime Minister of Vietnam") as well as the [National Assembly Chairmain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_National_Assembly_of_Vietnam "Chairman of the National Assembly of Vietnam") along with collegial bodies such as the [Politburo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam "Politburo of the Communist Party of Vietnam"), [Secretariat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secretariat_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam "Secretariat of the Communist Party of Vietnam") and the [Central Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam "Central Committee of the Communist Party of Vietnam").

### Soviet Union

Collective leadership ([Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language "Russian language"): коллективное руководство, **kollektivnoye rukovodstvo**) or Collectivity of leadership ([Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language "Russian language"): коллективность руководства, **kollektivnost rukovodstva**), was considered an ideal form of governance in the [Union of Soviet Socialist Republics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_of_Soviet_Socialist_Republics "Union of Soviet Socialist Republics") (USSR) under the [Communist Party of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union "Communist Party of the Soviet Union") (CPSU). Its main task was to distribute powers and functions among the [Politburo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union "Politburo of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union"), the [Central Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union "Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union"), and the [Council of Ministers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_of_the_Soviet_Union "Council of Ministers of the Soviet Union") to hinder any attempts to create a [one-man dominance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy") over the Soviet political system by a Soviet leader, such as that seen under [Joseph Stalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Stalin "Joseph Stalin") 's [rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stalin_era "Stalin era"). On the national level, the heart of the collective leadership was officially the Central Committee of the [Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union "Communist Party of the Soviet Union"), but in practice, was the Politburo. Collective leadership is characterized by limiting the powers of the [General Secretary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union "General Secretary of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union") and the [Chairman of the Council of Ministers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_the_Soviet_Union "Premier of the Soviet Union") (Premier) as related to other offices by enhancing the powers of collective bodies, such as the Politburo.

According to [Soviet literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_literature#20th_century "Russian literature"), [Vladimir Lenin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin "Vladimir Lenin") was the perfect example of a leader ruling in favour of the collective. Stalin was also claimed to embody this style of ruling, with most major policy decisions involving lengthy discussion and debate in the Politburo and/or Central Committee; after [his death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stalin%27s_death "Stalin's death") in 1953, [Nikita Khrushchev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev "Nikita Khrushchev") [accused Stalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_the_Cult_of_Personality_and_Its_Consequences "On the Cult of Personality and Its Consequences") of [one-man dominance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_personality "Cult of personality"), leading to controversy surrounding the period of his rule. At the [20th Party Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/20th_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union "20th Congress of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union"), Stalin's reign was criticized by Khrushchev as a "personality cult". As Stalin's successor, Khrushchev supported the ideal of collective leadership but [increasingly ruled in an autocratic fashion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khrushchev%27s_USSR "Khrushchev's USSR"), his anti-Stalin accusations followed by much the same behaviour which led to accusations of hypocrisy. In 1964, Khrushchev was ousted and replaced by [Leonid Brezhnev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev "Leonid Brezhnev") as General Secretary and by [Alexei Kosygin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexei_Kosygin "Alexei Kosygin") as Premier. Collective leadership was strengthened during the [Brezhnev years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brezhnev%27s_USSR "Brezhnev's USSR") and the [later reigns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Soviet_Union_\(1982%E2%80%931991\)#Leadership_transition "History of the Soviet Union (1982–1991)") of [Yuri Andropov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuri_Andropov "Yuri Andropov") and [Konstantin Chernenko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konstantin_Chernenko "Konstantin Chernenko"). [Mikhail Gorbachev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev "Mikhail Gorbachev") 's [reforms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Soviet_Union_\(1982%E2%80%931991\)#Ousting_the_old_guard "History of the Soviet Union (1982–1991)") helped spawn factionalism within the Soviet leadership, and members of Gorbachev's faction openly disagreed with him on key issues. The factions usually disagreed on how little or how much reform was needed to rejuvenate the [Soviet system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_the_Soviet_Union "Politics of the Soviet Union").

## Other left-wing parties

Aside from [communist states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_states "Communist states"), [left-wing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-wing "Left-wing") to [far-left](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far-left "Far-left") [green](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_party "Green party") and [socialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialism "Socialism") parties in [liberal democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_democracies "Liberal democracies") often also practice collective leadership under socialist ideals, either through male and female co-leaders or through several co-spokespersons. For green parties, this practice is often justified by their movement's emphasis on [consensus decision making](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consensus_decision_making "Consensus decision making") and [gender equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender_equality "Gender equality").

- [Alliance 90/The Greens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alliance_90/The_Greens "Alliance 90/The Greens"): the Federal Executive is divided between two co-equal spokespersons, a political director, treasurer, and two vice-chairs.
- [Québec solidaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qu%C3%A9bec_solidaire "Québec solidaire") divides its leadership among its president, secretary-general, and male and female spokespersons
- [Green Party of England and Wales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales "Green Party of England and Wales"): from 1990 to 1991, the GPE&W practiced co-leadership among six spokespersons, and from 1991 to 2008, the GPEW practiced it through a male and female spokesperson. After [Caroline Lucas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caroline_Lucas "Caroline Lucas") was elected the party's first sole leader and deputy leader in 2008, collective leadership was in hiatus until 2016, when the party leadership was once again divided between male and female co-leaders [Adrian Ramsay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrian_Ramsay "Adrian Ramsay") and [Carla Denyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carla_Denyer "Carla Denyer") under a job-share agreement while retaining a deputy leader. As of 2025, the party only has one leader, [Zack Polanski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zack_Polanski "Zack Polanski").
- [Green Party of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada "Green Party of Canada"): adopted a co-leadership model for its party leaders in 2025.[^9] [^10] [^11]
- [Left Bloc (Portugal)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_Bloc "Left Bloc"): formally, it has always had a collective leadership as provided for in its by-laws and no one-person office has ever been recognised. In practice, there has always been a single prominent figure (*coordinator*, 1999-2012 and 2016-; *spokesperson*, 2014–2016), except for between 2012 and 2014, when it had a de facto co-leadership between a male and a female apart from the legal structures. From 2014 to 2016, there was an informal collective and gender-balanced leadership of six people on top of the existing bodies, with a member serving as the party's spokesperson.
- [Scottish Green Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party "Scottish Green Party"): began to practice collective leadership in 2004 with the election of a male and a female co-convenor.
- [New Popular Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Popular_Front "New Popular Front"): A broad left-wing electoral alliance of political parties in France.
- [Green Party of the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States "Green Party of the United States"): the [Green National Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_National_Committee "Green National Committee") 's steering committee is a collective leadership of seven co-chairs, as well as a secretary and a treasurer.
- [The Left (Germany)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Die_Linke "Die Linke"): the party executive consists of an elected 44-member committee, headed by a 12-member executive board comprising two party chairpeople, four deputy chairs, a national secretary, treasurer, and four other members.
- [International Socialist Alternative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Socialist_Alternative "International Socialist Alternative"): The leading body of the ISA is the World Congress, which elects an International Committee (IC) to govern between congresses. The IC then appoints an International Executive (IE) body which is responsible for the day-to-day work of the International.
- [Your Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Your_Party_\(UK\) "Your Party (UK)"), led by a panel of its members in the form of a 24-member Central Executive Committee (CEC), chosen by its membership.[^12] [^13]
- Several Irish left-wing parties have collective leadership:
	- [People Before Profit–Solidarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People_Before_Profit%E2%80%93Solidarity "People Before Profit–Solidarity") [^14] [^15]
		- [Solidarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solidarity_\(Ireland\) "Solidarity (Ireland)")
		- [Socialist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_Party_\(Ireland\) "Socialist Party (Ireland)") [^16]
		- [Independents 4 Change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independents_4_Change "Independents 4 Change") [^17]
