A **monarchy** is a hereditary [form of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Form_of_government "Form of government") in which political power is legally passed on to the family members of the [monarch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarch "Monarch"), a head of state who rules for life.[^1] [^2] While monarchs gain their power depending on specific succession laws, they can also gain their authority via [election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elective_monarchy "Elective monarchy").[^3]

Monarchies have historically been a common form of government. Nearly half of all independent states at the start of the 19th century were monarchies.[^4] After reaching a peak in the middle of the 19th century, the proportion of monarchies in the world has steadily declined.[^4] Republics [replaced many monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abolition_of_monarchy "Abolition of monarchy"), notably at the end of [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") and World War II.[^4] [^5] [^6]

There are conventionally two types of monarchy: absolute monarchy and constitutional monarchy.[^4] [Absolute monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy "Absolute monarchy"), of which there are approximately twelve, are governed as [autocracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy").[^4] Most of the modern monarchies are [constitutional monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchies "Constitutional monarchies"), retaining under a [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution "Constitution") unique legal and ceremonial roles for monarchs exercising limited or no political power, similar to heads of state in a [parliamentary republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_republic "Parliamentary republic").[^6]

As of 2025, [forty-three sovereign nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_current_monarchs_of_sovereign_states "List of current monarchs of sovereign states") in the world have a monarch, including fifteen [Commonwealth realms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_realm "Commonwealth realm") that share [King Charles III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_III "Charles III") as their head of state. Other than that, there is a range of [sub-national monarchical entities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_current_non-sovereign_monarchs "List of current non-sovereign monarchs").

## Etymology

The word *monarch* first appeared in English in the mid-15th century as monark, meaning "a supreme governor for life, a sole or autocratic ruler of a state." It comes from the Old French monarche (14th century, Modern French monarque) and directly from the Late Latin monarcha, which in turn derives from the Greek monarkhēs, meaning "one who rules alone" (see monarchy).[^7] The term *monarchy* dates back to the mid-14th century, when it referred to a kingdom or territory ruled by a monarch, and by the late 14th century it also meant rule by a single person with supreme power. It comes from Old French monarchie (13th century), meaning "sovereignty" or "absolute power," which was borrowed from Late Latin monarchia and ultimately from Greek monarkhia, "absolute rule," literally "ruling of one," from monos ("alone") and arkhein ("to rule").[^8]

## History

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Sumerian_King_List%2C_1800_BC%2C_Larsa%2C_Iraq.jpg/250px-Sumerian_King_List%2C_1800_BC%2C_Larsa%2C_Iraq.jpg)

The Weld-Blundell Prism, inscribed with the Sumerian King List

The similar form of societal hierarchy known as [chiefdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiefdom "Chiefdom") or [tribal kingship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribal_chief "Tribal chief") is prehistoric. Chiefdoms provided the concept of state formation, which started with civilizations such as [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia"), [Ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") and the [Indus Valley civilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilisation "Indus Valley Civilisation").[^9] In some parts of the world, chiefdoms became monarchies.[^10] Some of the oldest recorded and evidenced monarchies were those of [Narmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narmer "Narmer"), [Pharaoh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharaoh "Pharaoh") of Ancient Egypt c. 3100 BCE, and [Enmebaragesi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enmebaragesi "Enmebaragesi"), a [Sumerian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumer "Sumer") King of [Kish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kish_\(Sumer\) "Kish (Sumer)") c. 2600 BCE.[^11] [^12]

From the earliest records, monarchs could be directly hereditary, while others were elected from among eligible members. With the [Egyptian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharaoh "Pharaoh"), [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outline_of_ancient_China "Outline of ancient China"), [Indian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outline_of_ancient_India "Outline of ancient India"),[^13] [Mesopotamian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lugal "Lugal"), [Sudanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudanic_languages "Sudanic languages"),[^14] reconstructed [Proto-Indo-European religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_mythology "Proto-Indo-European mythology"), but not others, the monarch held [sacral functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacred_king "Sacred king") directly connected to [sacrifice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacrifice "Sacrifice") and was sometimes identified with having [divine ancestry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_cult "Imperial cult"), possibly establishing a notion of the [divine right of kings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings "Divine right of kings").

[Polybius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polybius "Polybius") identified monarchy as one of three "benign" basic forms of government (monarchy, [aristocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristocracy "Aristocracy"), and [democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy#Origins "Democracy")), opposed to the three "malignant" basic forms of government ([tyranny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrant "Tyrant"), [oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy "Oligarchy"), and [ochlocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mob_rule "Mob rule")). The monarch in classical antiquity is often identified as " [king](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King "King") " or "ruler" (translating *[archon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archon "Archon")*, *[basileus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basileus "Basileus")*, *[rex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rex_\(title\) "Rex (title)")*, *[tyrannos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrant "Tyrant")*, etc.) or as " [queen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queen_regnant "Queen regnant") " (*[basilinna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basilinna "Basilinna")*, *basilissa*, *basileia* or *basilis*;[^15] *regina*). Polybius originally understood monarchy as a component of [republics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic "Republic"), but since antiquity monarchy has contrasted with forms of republic, where executive power is wielded by free citizens and their assemblies.[^16] The 4th-century BCE Hindu text *[Arthasastra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthashastra "Arthashastra")* laid out the ethics of monarchism.[^17] In antiquity, some monarchies were [abolished](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abolition_of_monarchy "Abolition of monarchy") in favour of such assemblies in [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") ([Roman Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic "Roman Republic"), 509 BCE), and [Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Athens "History of Athens") ([Athenian democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athenian_democracy "Athenian democracy"), 500 BCE).

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Europe_map_1648.PNG/250px-Europe_map_1648.PNG)

Map of monarchies and republics in Europe, 1648

By the 17th century, monarchy was challenged by evolving [parliamentarism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system") e.g. through regional assemblies (such as the [Icelandic Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icelandic_Commonwealth "Icelandic Commonwealth"), the Swiss *[Landsgemeinde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landsgemeinde "Landsgemeinde")* and later *[Tagsatzung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tagsatzung "Tagsatzung")*, and the High Medieval [communal movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_commune "Medieval commune") linked to the rise of medieval [town privileges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Town_privileges "Town privileges")) and by modern anti-monarchism e.g. of the temporary overthrow of the [English monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom "Monarchy of the United Kingdom") by the [Parliament of England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_England "Parliament of England") in 1649, the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution "American Revolution") of 1776 and the [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution") of 1789. One of many opponents of that trend was [Elizabeth Dawbarn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elizabeth_Dawbarn "Elizabeth Dawbarn"), whose anonymous *Dialogue between Clara Neville and Louisa Mills, on Loyalty* (1794) features "silly Louisa, who admires liberty, [Tom Paine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Paine "Thomas Paine") and the US, \[who is\] lectured by Clara on God's approval of monarchy" and on the influence women can exert on men.[^18]

Since then advocacy of the abolition of a monarchy or respectively of [republics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic "Republic") has been called [republicanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republicanism "Republicanism"), while the advocacy of monarchies is called [monarchism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchism "Monarchism"). As such republics have become the opposing and alternative form of government to monarchy,[^19] [^20] [^21] despite some having seen [infringements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_for_life#Similarity_to_a_monarch "President for life") through lifelong or even hereditary heads of state, such as in [North Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Korea "North Korea").[^22] Historically there have also been monarchical republics, such as the [Dutch Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_Republic "Dutch Republic"), and republican monarchies, such as the [Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth "Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth") and [Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden "Sweden") during the [Age of Liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Liberty "Age of Liberty") (1719–1772).[^23]

With the rise of republicanism, a diverse division between republicanism developed in the 19th-century politics (such as anti-monarchist [radicalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_radicalism "Classical radicalism")) and [conservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservatism "Conservatism") or even [reactionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactionary "Reactionary") [monarchism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchism "Monarchism"). In the following 20th century many countries abolished the monarchy and became republics, especially in the wake of [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") and [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II").

Today forty-three sovereign nations in the world have a [monarch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_current_monarchs_of_sovereign_states "List of current monarchs of sovereign states"), including fifteen [Commonwealth realms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_realm "Commonwealth realm") that have [Charles III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_III "Charles III") as the head of state. Most modern monarchs are [constitutional monarchs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy"), who retain a unique legal and ceremonial role but exercise limited or no political power under a constitution. Many are so-called [crowned republics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crowned_republic "Crowned republic"), surviving particularly in small states.[^24]

In some nations, however, such as [Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco "Morocco"), [Qatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar "Qatar"), [Liechtenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liechtenstein "Liechtenstein"), and [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand"), the hereditary monarch has more political influence than any other single source of authority in the state.[^25] [^26] [^27] [^28]

According to a 2020 study, monarchy arose as a system of governance because of an efficiency in governing large populations and expansive territories during periods when coordinating such populations was difficult. The authors argue that monarchy declined as an efficient regime type with innovations in communications and transportation technology, as the efficiency of monarchy relative to other regime types declined.[^29]

According to a 2023 study, monarchy has persisted as a regime type because it can accommodate demands for [democratization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratization "Democratization") better than other forms of autocratic rule: "Monarchies can democratize without destabilizing the leadership through transitioning to a democratic [constitutional monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy"). The prospect of retaining the ruler appeals to opposition groups who value both democracy and stability, but it also has implications for their ability to organize and sustain mass protest." [^30]

## Characteristics and role

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Allan_Ramsay_-_King_George_III_in_coronation_robes_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/250px-Allan_Ramsay_-_King_George_III_in_coronation_robes_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg)

King George III of the United Kingdom, coronation portrait by Allan Ramsay, 1762

Monarchies are associated with [hereditary reign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hereditary_monarchy "Hereditary monarchy"), in which monarchs reign for life and the responsibilities and power of the position pass to their child or another member of their family when they die. Most monarchs, both historically and in the modern-day, have been born and brought up within a [royal family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_family "Royal family"), the centre of the [royal household](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_household "Royal household") and [court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_court "Royal court"). Growing up in a royal family (called a [dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynasty "Dynasty") when it continues for several [generations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generation "Generation")), [future monarchs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heir_apparent "Heir apparent") are often trained for their expected future responsibilities as monarch.[^31]

Different systems of hereditary [succession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_succession "Order of succession") have been used, such as [proximity of blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proximity_of_blood "Proximity of blood"), [primogeniture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primogeniture "Primogeniture"), and [agnatic seniority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agnatic_seniority "Agnatic seniority") ([Salic law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salic_law "Salic law")). While most monarchs in history have been male, many female monarchs also have reigned.[^32] The term " [queen regnant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queen_regnant "Queen regnant") " refers to a ruling monarch, while " [queen consort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queen_consort "Queen consort") " refers to the wife of a reigning king. Rule may be hereditary in practice without being considered a monarchy: there have been some family dictatorships (and also [political families](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_family "Political family")) in many [democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy").

Some monarchies are not hereditary. In an [elective monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elective_monarchy "Elective monarchy"), monarchs are [elected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election "Election") or appointed by some body (an [electoral college](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_college "Electoral college")) for life or a defined period. Four elective monarchies exist today: [Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia "Cambodia"), [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia") and the [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates") are 20th-century creations, while one (the [papacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope "Pope")) is ancient.[^33]

A [self-proclaimed monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-proclaimed_monarchy "Self-proclaimed monarchy") is established when a person claims the monarchy without any historical ties to a previous dynasty. There are examples of republican leaders who have proclaimed themselves monarchs: [Napoleon I of France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon "Napoleon") declared himself [Emperor of the French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs "List of French monarchs") and ruled the [First French Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_French_Empire "First French Empire") after having held the title of [First Consul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Consulate "French Consulate") of the [French Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_First_Republic "French First Republic") for five years from his seizing power in the coup of [18 Brumaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_of_18_Brumaire "Coup of 18 Brumaire"). President [Jean-Bédel Bokassa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-B%C3%A9del_Bokassa "Jean-Bédel Bokassa") of the [Central African Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_African_Republic "Central African Republic") declared himself Emperor of the [Central African Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_African_Empire "Central African Empire") in 1976.[^34] [Yuan Shikai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_Shikai "Yuan Shikai"), the first formal President of the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beiyang_government "Beiyang government"), crowned himself Emperor of the short-lived " [Empire of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_China_\(1915%E2%80%931916\) "Empire of China (1915–1916)") " a few years after the Republic of China was founded.[^35]

### Powers of the monarch

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Salman_of_Saudi_Arabia_-_2020_%2849563590728%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Salman_of_Saudi_Arabia_-_2020_%2849563590728%29_%28cropped%29.jpg)

King Salman of Saudi Arabia is an absolute monarch.

- In an [absolute monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy "Absolute monarchy"), the monarch rules with absolute power over the state and government.[^36]
- In a [constitutional monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy"), the monarch's power is subject to a [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution "Constitution"). In most current constitutional monarchies, the monarch is mainly a ceremonial [figurehead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figurehead "Figurehead") symbol of national unity and state continuity.
	- [Semi-constitutional monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy") exhibit fewer parliamentary powers or simply monarchs with more authority.[^37] The term "parliamentary monarchy" may be used to differentiate from semi-constitutional monarchies.

## Succession

### Hereditary monarchies

![Political Map of Europe with Monarchical states colour-coded](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/European_monarchies_by_succession.svg/250px-European_monarchies_by_succession.svg.png)

Current European monarchies by succession method: Absolute primogeniture Male-preference cognatic primogeniture, to be changed to absolute primogeniture Agnatic primogeniture Elective

In a [hereditary monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hereditary_monarchy "Hereditary monarchy"), the position of monarch is inherited according to a statutory or customary [order of succession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_succession "Order of succession"), usually within one [royal family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_family "Royal family") tracing its origin through a historical [dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynasty "Dynasty") or bloodline. This usually means that the heir to the throne is known well in advance of becoming monarch to ensure a smooth succession.[^38] [^39]

[Primogeniture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primogeniture "Primogeniture"), in which the eldest child of the monarch is first in line to become monarch, is the most common system in hereditary monarchy. The order of succession is usually affected by rules on gender. Historically "agnatic primogeniture" or "patrilineal primogeniture" was favoured, that is inheritance according to seniority of birth among the sons of a monarch or [head of family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pater_familias "Pater familias"), with sons and their male issue inheriting before brothers and their male issue, to the total exclusion of females and descendants through females from succession.[^40] This complete exclusion of females from dynastic succession is commonly referred to as application of the [Salic law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salic_law "Salic law"). Another variation on agnatic primogeniture was the so-called semi-Salic law, or "agnatic-cognatic primogeniture", which allowed women to succeed only at the extinction of all the male descendants in the male line of the particular legislator.[^40] [^41]

Before primogeniture was enshrined in European law and tradition, kings would often secure the succession by having their successor (usually their eldest son) crowned during their own lifetime, so for a time there would be two kings in [coregency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coregency "Coregency") —a senior king and a junior king. Examples were [Henry the Young King](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_the_Young_King "Henry the Young King") of England and the early [Direct Capetians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Capet "House of Capet") in France. Sometimes, however, primogeniture can operate through the female line.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/NICAISE_Leopold_ANV.jpg/250px-NICAISE_Leopold_ANV.jpg)

King Leopold I, an elected founder of the hereditary monarchy of Belgium

In 1980, [Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden "Sweden") became the first monarchy to declare equal (full cognatic) primogeniture, meaning that the eldest child of the monarch, whether female or male, ascends to the throne.[^42] Other kingdoms (such as the [Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands") in 1983, [Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway "Norway") in 1990, [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium "Belgium") in 1991, [Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark "Denmark") in 2009, and [Luxembourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luxembourg "Luxembourg") [^43] in 2011) have since followed suit. The [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") adopted absolute (equal) primogeniture (subject to the claims of existing heirs) on April 25, 2013, following [agreement by the prime ministers of the sixteen Commonwealth Realms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perth_Agreement "Perth Agreement") at the 22nd Commonwealth Heads of Government Meeting.[^44]

Other hereditary systems of succession included [tanistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanistry "Tanistry"), which is semi-elective and gives weight to merit and [Agnatic seniority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agnatic_seniority "Agnatic seniority"). In some monarchies, such as [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia"), succession to the throne first passes to the monarch's next eldest brother, and only after that to the monarch's children (agnatic seniority). On June 21, 2017, King Salman of Saudi Arabia revolted against this style of monarchy and elected his son to inherit the throne.[^45] [^46]

### Elective monarchies

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Pope_Francis_Korea_Haemi_Castle_19.jpg/250px-Pope_Francis_Korea_Haemi_Castle_19.jpg)

Pope Francis, Sovereign of the Vatican City State from 2013 to 2025

In an [elective monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elective_monarchy "Elective monarchy"), monarchs are [elected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election "Election") or appointed by somebody (an [electoral college](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_college "Electoral college")) for life or a defined period, but then reign like any other monarch. There is no popular vote involved in elective monarchies, as the elective body usually consists of a small number of eligible people. Historical examples of elective monarchy are the [Holy Roman Emperors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor "Holy Roman Emperor") (chosen by [prince-electors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince-elector "Prince-elector") but often coming from the same dynasty) and the [free election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_elections_in_Poland "Royal elections in Poland") of kings of the [Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth "Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth"). For example, [Pepin the Short](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pepin_the_Short "Pepin the Short") (father of [Charlemagne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlemagne "Charlemagne")) was elected [King of the Franks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Frankish_kings "List of Frankish kings") by an assembly of Frankish leading men;[^47] nobleman [Stanisław August Poniatowski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_August_Poniatowski "Stanisław August Poniatowski") of Poland was an elected king, as was [Frederick I of Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_I_of_Denmark "Frederick I of Denmark"). [Gallic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauls "Gauls") and [Germanic peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanic_peoples "Germanic peoples") also had elective monarchies.

The [Pope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope "Pope") of the [Roman Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church") (who rules as [Sovereign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign "Sovereign") of the [Vatican City State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vatican_City "Vatican City")) is [elected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papal_conclave "Papal conclave") for life by the [College of Cardinals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/College_of_Cardinals "College of Cardinals").[^48] In the [Sovereign Military Order of Malta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_Military_Order_of_Malta "Sovereign Military Order of Malta"), the [Prince and Grand Master](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_grand_masters_of_the_Knights_Hospitaller "List of grand masters of the Knights Hospitaller") is elected for life tenure by the Council Complete of State from within its members. In [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia"), the federal king, called the [Yang di-Pertuan Agong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_di-Pertuan_Agong "Yang di-Pertuan Agong") or Paramount Ruler, is elected for a five-year term from among and by the hereditary rulers (mostly [sultans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan "Sultan")) of nine of the federation's constitutive [states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_and_federal_territories_of_Malaysia "States and federal territories of Malaysia"), all on the [Malay peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malay_Peninsula "Malay Peninsula").[^49] The [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates") also chooses its federal leaders from among emirs of the federated states. Furthermore, [Andorra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andorra "Andorra") has a unique constitutional arrangement as one of its heads of state is the President of the [French Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France") in the form of a [Co-Prince](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-Princes_of_Andorra "Co-Princes of Andorra").[^50] In New Zealand, the Maori King, head of the Kingitanga Movement, is elected by a council of Maori elders at the funeral of their predecessor, which is also where their coronation takes place. All of the Heads of the Maori King Movement have been descendants of the first Maori King, Potatau Te Wherowhero, who was elected and became King in June 1858.

### Usurpation

Another way monarchs have historically gained royal power is by seizing it, either by force or other illegitimate measures. Historically usurpation has usually happened via a [coup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_detat "Coup detat") or by fraudulently claiming be a descendant of a ruler that they may or may not be related to. According to [Herodotus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus"), this was done by someone impersonating [Smerdis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smerdis "Smerdis") in order to seize the throne of [Cyrus the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great "Cyrus the Great") after his death.[^51]

### Other ways of succession

#### By accession

The legitimacy and authorities of monarchs are often [proclaimed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation "Proclamation") and recognized through occupying and being [invested](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investiture "Investiture") with [insignia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insignia "Insignia"), seats, [deeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deed "Deed") and [titles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Title "Title"), like in the course of [coronations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronation "Coronation").

This is especially employed to legitimize and settle disputed successions, changes in ways of succession, status of a monarch (e.g. as in the case of the [*privilegium maius*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privilegium_Maius "Privilegium Maius") deed) or new monarchies altogether (e.g. as in the case of the [coronation of Napoleon I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronation_of_Napoleon "Coronation of Napoleon")).

#### Succession crisis

In cases of succession challenges, it can be instrumental for [pretenders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pretender "Pretender") to secure or install [legitimacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legitimacy_\(political\) "Legitimacy (political)") through the above, for example proof of accession like insignia, through treaties or a claim of a divine mandate to rule (e.g. by [Hong Xiuquan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Xiuquan "Hong Xiuquan") and his [Taiping Heavenly Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiping_Heavenly_Kingdom "Taiping Heavenly Kingdom")).

Currently, there are several countries in the world with a monarch as head of state. They fall roughly into the following categories:

### Commonwealth realms

[King Charles III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_III "Charles III") is, separately, monarch of fifteen [Commonwealth realms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_realm "Commonwealth realm") ([Antigua and Barbuda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigua_and_Barbuda "Antigua and Barbuda"), the [Commonwealth of Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia"), the [Commonwealth of the Bahamas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Bahamas "The Bahamas"), [Belize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belize "Belize"), [Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada "Canada"), [Grenada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grenada "Grenada"), [Jamaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamaica "Jamaica"), [New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand "New Zealand"), the [Independent State of Papua New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea "Papua New Guinea"), the [Federation of Saint Christopher and Nevis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Kitts_and_Nevis "Saint Kitts and Nevis"), [Saint Lucia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Lucia "Saint Lucia"), [Saint Vincent and the Grenadines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines "Saint Vincent and the Grenadines"), the [Solomon Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon_Islands "Solomon Islands"), [Tuvalu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuvalu "Tuvalu") and the [United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom")). They evolved out of the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") into fully independent states within the [Commonwealth of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations") that retain the King as head of state.[^52]

### Other European constitutional monarchies

The [Principality of Andorra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andorra "Andorra"), the [Kingdom of Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium "Belgium"), the [Kingdom of Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danish_Realm "Danish Realm"), the [Grand Duchy of Luxembourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luxembourg "Luxembourg"), the [Kingdom of the Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands "Kingdom of the Netherlands"), the [Kingdom of Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway "Norway"), the [Kingdom of Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain"), and the [Kingdom of Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden "Sweden") are fully democratic states in which the monarch has a limited or largely ceremonial role.[^53] [^54] [^55] [^56]

Andorra is unique among all existing monarchies, as it is a [diarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diarchy "Diarchy"), with the co-princes being shared by the [president of France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_France "President of France") and the [bishop of Urgell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Urgell "Roman Catholic Diocese of Urgell").[^53]

### European semi-constitutional monarchies

A [semi-constitutional monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy") is a monarchy where the monarch rules according to a democratic constitution but still retains substantial powers. The [Principality of Liechtenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liechtenstein "Liechtenstein") and the [Principality of Monaco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monaco "Monaco") are European semi-constitutional monarchies.[^57] For example, the [2003 Constitution referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2003_Liechtenstein_constitutional_referendum "2003 Liechtenstein constitutional referendum") gave the [Prince of Liechtenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_of_Liechtenstein "Monarchy of Liechtenstein") the power to veto any law that the *[Landtag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landtag "Landtag")* (parliament) proposes, while the Landtag can veto any law that the Prince tries to pass.[^58] The prince can appoint or dismiss any elective member or government employee. The [prince of Monaco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_of_Monaco "Monarchy of Monaco") has simpler powers; he cannot appoint or dismiss any elective member or government employee to or from his or her post, but he can elect the [minister of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_State_\(Monaco\) "Minister of State (Monaco)"), [government council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Monaco "Politics of Monaco") and judges.[^59]

### Monarchies in the Muslim world

The monarchies of the [Kingdom of Bahrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bahrain "Bahrain"), the [Brunei Darussalam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brunei "Brunei"), the [Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan "Jordan"), the [State of Kuwait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuwait "Kuwait"), [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia"), the [Kingdom of Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco "Morocco"), the [Sultanate of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman "Oman"), the [State of Qatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar "Qatar"), the [Kingdom of Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia"), and the [United Arab Emirates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates") generally retain far more powers than their European or Commonwealth counterparts.[^60] Brunei, Oman, and Saudi Arabia are absolute monarchies;[^61] [^62] Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar and United Arab Emirates are classified as mixed, meaning there are representative bodies but the monarch retains most of his powers. Jordan, Malaysia, and Morocco are constitutional monarchies.

### East and Southeast Asian constitutional monarchies

The [Kingdom of Bhutan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhutan "Bhutan"), the [Kingdom of Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia "Cambodia"), the [Kingdom of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand"), and [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan") are constitutional monarchies where the monarch has a limited or merely ceremonial role. Bhutan made the change in 2008.[^63] Cambodia had its own monarchy after independence from the [French colonial empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_colonial_empire "French colonial empire"), but it was deposed after the [Khmer Rouge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Rouge "Khmer Rouge") came into power. The monarchy was subsequently restored in the peace agreement of 1993. Thailand transitioned into a constitutional monarchy over the course of the 20th century. Japan has had a monarchy, an [emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan "Emperor of Japan"), according to legend, since Emperor Jimmu (reigned 660–585 BCE), making it the world's oldest existing monarchy.[^64] After their defeat in the [Second World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), Japan was forced into limiting the power of the Emperor, giving almost all of it to the [National Diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Diet "National Diet").

### Other monarchies

Eswatini is unique among these monarchies, often being considered a [diarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diarchy "Diarchy"): the King, or [Ngwenyama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ngwenyama "Ngwenyama"), rules alongside his mother, the [Ndlovukati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ndlovukati "Ndlovukati"), as dual heads of state.[^65] [^66] This was originally intended to provide a check on political power. The Ngwenyama, however, is considered the administrative head of state, while the Ndlovukati is considered the spiritual and national head of state, a position which more or less has become symbolic in recent years.[^67]

The [Pope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope "Pope") is the absolute monarch of the Vatican City State (a separate entity from the [Holy See](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_See "Holy See")) by virtue of his position as head of the [Roman Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church") and Bishop of Rome; he is an elected rather than a hereditary ruler, and does not have to be a citizen of the territory prior to his election by the cardinals.[^68] [^69]

In [Samoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samoa "Samoa"), the position of head of state is described in Part III of the 1960 [Samoan constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Samoa "Constitution of Samoa"). At the time the constitution was adopted, it was anticipated that future heads of state would be chosen from among the four Tama a 'Aiga "royal" paramount chiefs. However, this is not required by the constitution, and, for this reason, Samoa can be considered a [parliamentary republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_republic "Parliamentary republic") rather than a [constitutional monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy"). However, each member of the [Samoan parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Samoa "Legislative Assembly of Samoa"), except for the two seats reserved for non-Samoans, must be a *matai*, a member of the hereditary political system known as the [Faʻamatai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fa%CA%BBamatai "Faʻamatai").[^70]

The [ruling Kim family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kim_family_\(North_Korea\) "Kim family (North Korea)") in [North Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Korea "North Korea") ([Kim Il Sung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kim_Il_Sung "Kim Il Sung"), [Kim Jong Il](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kim_Jong_Il "Kim Jong Il") and [Kim Jong Un](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kim_Jong_Un "Kim Jong Un")) has been described as a *de facto* [absolute monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy "Absolute monarchy") [^71] [^72] [^73] or a "hereditary dictatorship".[^74] In 2013, Clause 2 of Article 10 of the new edited [Ten Fundamental Principles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ten_Principles_for_the_Establishment_of_a_Monolithic_Ideological_System "Ten Principles for the Establishment of a Monolithic Ideological System") of the [Korean Workers' Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Workers%27_Party_of_Korea "Workers' Party of Korea") states that the party and revolution must be carried "eternally" by the " [Baekdu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paektu_Mountain "Paektu Mountain") (Kim's) bloodline".[^75] This though does not mean it is a *de jure* absolute monarchy, as the country's official name is the Democratic People's Republic of Korea. The [al-Assad family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Assad_family "Al-Assad family"), which ruled [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria") from 1971 to 2024, was similarly categorised as such.[^74]
