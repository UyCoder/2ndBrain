An **electoral system** is a set of rules and mechanisms used to determine the results of an [election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election "Election"). Electoral systems are used in politics to elect governments and also in non-political settings such as business, [nonprofit organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonprofit_organization "Nonprofit organization") and informal organisations. These rules govern all aspects of the voting process: when elections occur, [who is allowed to vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suffrage "Suffrage"), [who can stand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomination_rules "Nomination rules") as a [candidate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candidate "Candidate"), how many votes are cast by each voter, [how ballots are marked and cast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voting_method "Voting method"), how the ballots are counted or weighed, how votes translate into the election outcome (seats), limits on [campaign spending](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campaign_finance "Campaign finance"), and other factors affect the process. Political elections are defined by constitutions or electoral laws, are typically conducted by [election commissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election_commission "Election commission"), and may use one or more electoral systems for different offices.

Some electoral systems elect a single winner to a position, such as prime minister, president or governor, while others elect multiple winners, such as members of parliament or boards of directors. When electing a [legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature "Legislature"), areas may be divided into constituencies with one or more representatives or the electorate may elect representatives as a single unit. Voters may vote directly for an individual candidate or for a list of candidates put forward by a [political party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_party "Political party") or [alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_alliance "Electoral alliance"). There are many variations in electoral systems.

The [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") and [normative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normative_economics "Normative economics") study of voting rules falls under the branches of [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics") called [social choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_choice_theory "Social choice theory") and [mechanism design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanism_design "Mechanism design"), but the question has also engendered substantial contributions from [political scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science"), [analytic philosophers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_philosophy "Analytic philosophy"), [computer scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science"), and [mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"). The field has produced several major results, including [Arrow's impossibility theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrow%27s_impossibility_theorem "Arrow's impossibility theorem") (showing that [ranked voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ranked_voting "Ranked voting") cannot eliminate the [spoiler effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spoiler_effect "Spoiler effect")) and [Gibbard's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibbard%27s_theorem "Gibbard's theorem") (showing it is impossible to design a [straightforward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategyproofness "Strategyproofness") voting system, i.e. one where it is always obvious to a [strategic voter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_voting "Strategic voting") which ballot they should cast).

## Types

The most common categorizations of electoral systems are: single-winner vs. multi-winner systems; at-large contests versus district contests; and [proportional representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportional_representation "Proportional representation") vs. [winner-take-all systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winner-take-all_system "Winner-take-all system") vs. [mixed systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_electoral_system "Mixed electoral system"), which may combine a variety of election systems. As well, the formula used to determine the winners is critical - [plurality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plurality_voting "Plurality voting") (relative standings), majority of votes (50 percent plus 1), quota (a mathematically determined amount equal to less than the majority, such as used in [single transferable voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_transferable_voting "Single transferable voting")), or some super-majority such as 60 percent.[^1]

### Single-winner and winner-take-all systems

Where only a single winner is elected, the electoral system is winner-take-all. The same can be said for elections where only one person is elected per district. When district elections are winner-take-all, the electoral system as a whole may produce dis-proportional results. Where multiple winners are elected at once (in the same district), [plurality block voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plurality_block_voting "Plurality block voting") may elect members of just one party so may be considered winner-take-all.

In [party block voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_ticket "General ticket"), voters can only vote for the list of candidates of a single party, with the party receiving the most votes winning all seats, even if that party receives only a minority of votes. This is also described as winner-take-all. This is used in five countries as part of mixed systems.[^2]

#### Plurality voting - first past the post and block voting

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Lower_House_FPTP.svg/250px-Lower_House_FPTP.svg.png)

Countries that use exclusively first-past-the-post for lower house or unicameral legislatures

[Plurality voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plurality_voting_method "Plurality voting method") is a system in which the candidate(s) with the largest number of votes wins, with no requirement to get a majority of votes. In cases where there is a single position to be filled, it is known as [first-past-the-post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting "First-past-the-post voting"). This is the second-most-common electoral system for national legislatures (after [proportional representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportional_representation "Proportional representation")). Altogether at least 58 countries use FPTP and single-member districts to elect all or some of the members of a national-level legislative chamber,[^2] the vast majority of which are current or former British colonies or U.S. territories. It is also the second-most-common system used for presidential elections, being used in 19 countries. The [two-round system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-round_system "Two-round system") is the most common system used to elect a president.[^2]

In cases where there are multiple positions to be filled, most commonly in cases of multi-member constituencies, there are several types of plurality electoral systems. Under [block voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plurality-at-large_voting "Plurality-at-large voting") (also known as multiple non-transferable vote or plurality-at-large), voters have as many votes as there are seats and can vote for any candidate, regardless of party, but give only one vote to each preferred candidate. The most-popular candidates are declared elected, whether they have a majority of votes or not and whether or not that result is proportional to the way votes were cast. Eight countries use this system.[^2]

[Cumulative voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumulative_voting "Cumulative voting") allows a voter to cast more than one vote for the same candidate, in multi-member districts. Its effect may be proportional to the same degree that [single non-transferable voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_non-transferable_vote "Single non-transferable vote") or [limited voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_voting "Limited voting") is, thus it is often called semi-proportional.

[Approval voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Approval_voting "Approval voting") is a choose-all-you-like voting system that aims to increase the number of candidates that win with majority support.[^3] Voters are free to pick as many candidates as they like and each choice has equal weight, independent of the number of candidates a voter supports. The candidate with the most votes wins.[^4]

#### Runoff systems

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Electoral_systems_for_heads_of_state_map.svg/250px-Electoral_systems_for_heads_of_state_map.svg.png)

Countries by electoral system used to (directly) elect their Head of State (President): Two-round system Election by Legislature Election by electoral college First-past-the-post Instant-runoff voting Not elected (mostly monarchies)

A runoff system is one in which a successful candidate is certain to receive a majority of votes to be elected, either in a runoff round with only two candidates or in a final round of vote counting if [ranked votes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preferential_voting "Preferential voting") are used. Runoff systems may ensure that a winner must have a majority of votes, but in some systems three or more candidates move on to the runoff election, in which case only a plurality is required in the last round.

Runoff systems are often called majoritarian systems, but in social choice theory, runoff systems are not called majority voting, as this term refers to [Condorcet-methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condorcet_method "Condorcet method").

There are two main groups of runoff systems, those in one group use a single round of voting achieved by voters casting [ranked votes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ranked_voting_system "Ranked voting system") and then in successive rounds of counting, using vote transfers if necessary to establish a majority. Those in the other group use two or more rounds of voting, to narrow the field of candidates and to determine a winner who has a majority of the votes. Both are primarily used for single-member constituencies or election of a single position such as mayor.

If a candidate receives a majority of the vote in the first round, then the system is simple [first past the post voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_past_the_post_voting "First past the post voting"). But if no one has a majority of votes in first round, runoff systems respond in different ways.

Under [instant-runoff voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instant-runoff_voting "Instant-runoff voting") (IRV), when no one wins a majority in first round, runoff is achieved through vote transfers made possible by voters having ranked candidates in order of preference, with lower preferences used as back-up preferences. This system is used for parliamentary elections in [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia") and [Papua New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea "Papua New Guinea"). If no candidate receives a majority of the vote in the first round, the votes of the least-popular candidate are transferred as per marked second preferences and added to the totals of surviving candidates. This is repeated until a candidate achieves a majority. The count ends any time one candidate has a majority of votes. It may continue until only two candidates remain, at which point one or other of the candidates will take a majority of votes still in play.

A different form of single-winner preferential voting is the [contingent vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contingent_vote "Contingent vote") where voters do not rank all candidates, but rank just two or three. The first preferences are counted. If no candidate has a majority in the first round, all candidates except the two with the most votes are excluded. If the voter gave first preference to one of the excluded candidates, the vote is transferred to the next usable back-up preferences if possible, or otherwise put in the exhausted pile. The canadidate who holds most of the resulting votes is the winner. With only two in the contest at the end, the winner holds a majority of the votes that are still in play. This system is used in [Sri Lankan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sri_Lanka "Sri Lanka") presidential elections, with voters allowed to mark three preferences.[^5]

The other main form of runoff system is the [two-round system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-round_system "Two-round system"), which is the most common system used for presidential elections around the world, being used in 88 countries. It is also used, in conjunction with single-member districts, in 20 countries for electing members of the legislature.[^2] If no candidate achieves a majority of votes in the first round of voting, a second round is held to determine the winner. In most cases the second round is limited to the top two candidates from the first round, although in some elections more than two candidates may choose to contest the second round; in these cases the second-round winner is not required to have a majority of votes, but may be elected by having a plurality of votes.

Some countries use a modified form of the two-round system, so going to a second round happens less often. In [Ecuador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecuador "Ecuador") a candidate in the presidential election is declared the winner if they receive more than 50 percent of the vote or 40% of the vote and are 10% ahead of their nearest rival,[^6] In Argentina, where the system is known as [ballotage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ballotage_in_Argentina "Ballotage in Argentina"), election is achieved by those with majority or if they have 45% and a 10% lead.

In some cases, where a certain level of support is required, a runoff may be held using a different system. In [U.S. presidential elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_presidential_election "United States presidential election"), when no candidate wins a majority of the [United States Electoral College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_College_\(United_States\) "Electoral College (United States)") (using seat count, not votes cast, as is used in the winner-take-all systems described above), a [contingent election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contingent_election "Contingent election") is held by the House of Representatives, not the voters themselves. The House contingency election sees three candidates go on to the last round and the Representatives of each state vote as a single unit, not as individuals, with the state's votes going to the plurality winner of the State members' votes.

An [exhaustive ballot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exhaustive_ballot "Exhaustive ballot") sees multiple rounds of voting (where no one has majority in first round). The number of rounds is not limited to two rounds, but sees the last-placed candidate eliminated in each round of voting, repeated until one candidate has majority of votes. Due to the potentially large number of rounds, this system is not used in any major popular elections, but is used to elect the Speakers of parliament in several countries and members of the [Swiss Federal Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Council "Swiss Federal Council").

In some systems, such as election of the speaker of the United States House of Representatives, there may be multiple rounds held without any candidates being eliminated (unless by a candidate's own resignation) until a candidate achieves a majority.

#### Positional systems

[Positional systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positional_voting "Positional voting") like the [Borda Count](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borda_Count "Borda Count") are ranked voting systems that assign a certain number of points to each candidate, weighted by position. The most popular such system is [first-preference plurality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-preference_plurality "First-preference plurality"). This system is intended to elect broadly acceptable options or candidates, rather than those preferred by a majority.[^7] This system is used to elect the ethnic minority representatives seats in the Slovenian parliament.[^8] [^9]

The Dowdall system is used in [Nauru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nauru "Nauru") for parliamentary elections and sees voters rank the candidates. First preference votes are counted as whole numbers, the second preferences by two, third preferences by three, and so on; this continues to the lowest possible ranking.[^10] The totals for each candidate determine the winners.[^11]

### Multi-winner systems

Multi-winner systems include both proportional systems and non-proportional multi-winner systems, such as [party block voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_ticket "General ticket") and plurality block voting. A voter can cast one vote, as many votes as the number of seats to fill, or something in between ([limited voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_voting "Limited voting")).

#### Proportional systems

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Proportional_voting_systems.svg/250px-Proportional_voting_systems.svg.png)

Countries by proportional electoral system (lower house or unicameral legislature): Party list (closed list) Party list (open list) Party list (partly-open list) Panachage party list (open list) Mixed-member proportional (seat linkage) type compensatory, some additional compensation for overhang seats (New Zealand) Mixed-member proportional (seat linkage) type compensatory, no additional compensation for overhang seats Personalized proportional (Germany) Single transferable vote

[Proportional representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportional_representation "Proportional representation") is the most widely used type of electoral system to elect national legislatures. All or some members of the parliaments of over a hundred countries are elected by a form of PR.[^12] These systems elect multiple members in one contest, whether that is at-large, as in a city-wide election at the city level or state-wide or nation-wide at those levels, or in multi-member districts at any level.

[Party-list proportional representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Party-list_proportional_representation "Party-list proportional representation") is the single most common electoral system and is used by 80 countries, and involves seats being allocated to parties based on party vote share.

In [closed list](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_list "Closed list") systems voters do not have any influence over which candidates are elected to fill the party seats, but in [open list](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_list "Open list") systems voters are able to both vote for the party list and for candidates (or only for candidates). Voters thus have means to sometimes influence the order in which party candidates will be assigned seats. In some countries, notably [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel") and the [Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands"), elections are carried out using 'pure' proportional representation, with the votes tallied on a national level before assigning seats to parties. (There are no district seats, only at-large.) However, in most cases several multi-member constituencies are used rather than a single nationwide constituency, giving an element of geographical or local representation. Such may result in the distribution of seats not reflecting the national vote totals of parties. As a result, some countries that use districts have [leveling seats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leveling_seat "Leveling seat") that are awarded to some of the parties whose seat proportion is lower than their proportion of the vote. Levelling seats are either used at the regional level or at the national level. Such [mixed member proportional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation "Mixed-member proportional representation") systems are used in New Zealand and in Scotland.

List PR systems usually set an [electoral threshold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_threshold "Electoral threshold"), the minimum percentage of the vote that a party must obtain to win levelling seats or to win seats at all. Some systems allow a go around of this rule. For instance, if a party takes a district seat, the party may be eligible for top-up seats even if its percentage of the votes is below the threshold.

Different methods are used to allocate seats in proportional representation systems. Party-list systems use two main methods: [highest average](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highest_averages_method "Highest averages method") and [largest remainder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Largest_remainder_method "Largest remainder method"). Highest average systems involve dividing the votes received by each party by a *divisor* or *vote average* that represents an idealized [seats-to-votes ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seats-to-votes_ratio "Seats-to-votes ratio"), then rounding normally. In the largest remainder system, parties' vote shares are divided by an [electoral quota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_quota "Electoral quota"). This usually leaves some seats unallocated, which are awarded to parties based on which parties have the largest number of "leftover" votes.

[Single transferable vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_transferable_vote "Single transferable vote") (STV) elects multiple winners in a single contest using multi-member districts. Each voter casts a ballot with first preference and can [rank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ranked_voting_system "Ranked voting system") other candidates, rather than voting for a party list. The secondary preferences are contingency votes used if needed. STV is used in [Malta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malta "Malta"), the [Republic of Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland "Republic of Ireland") and Australia (partially). To be certain of being elected, a candidate must achieve a quota (the [Droop quota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Droop_quota "Droop quota") being the most common). Candidates that achieve the quota are elected. If necessary to fill seats, the candidate with the fewest votes is eliminated and the votes cast for them are redistributed to the second preferences on the ballots in question. Surplus votes held by elected candidates may also be transferred. Eventually all seats are filled by candidates who have passed the quota or by those still in the running when there are only as many remaining candidates as the number of remaining open seats.[^11]

Under [single non-transferable vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_non-transferable_vote "Single non-transferable vote") (SNTV), multi-member districts are used. Each voter can vote for only one candidate, with the candidates receiving the most votes declared the winners, whether any of them have a majority of votes or not. This system, often described as semi-proportional, is used in [Kuwait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuwait "Kuwait"), the [Pitcairn Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitcairn_Islands "Pitcairn Islands") and [Vanuatu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanuatu "Vanuatu") and formerly in Japan.[^2]

#### Mixed systems

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Electoral_systems_map_mixed.svg/250px-Electoral_systems_map_mixed.svg.png)

Countries using a mixed electoral system (lower house or unicameral legislature): Compensatory Mixed-member proportional ( seat linkage ) type compensatory Vote linkage type partially compensatory (supermixed) Majority jackpot Two round majority jackpot Non-compensatory Parallel voting (Party list + FPTP) Parallel voting (Party list + TRS) Parallel voting (Party list + BV/PBV) Majority bonus (fusion)

In several countries, [mixed systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_electoral_system "Mixed electoral system") are used to elect the legislature. These include [parallel voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_voting "Parallel voting"), [mixed-member majoritarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed-member_majoritarian_representation "Mixed-member majoritarian representation"), and [mixed-member proportional representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation "Mixed-member proportional representation").

In non-compensatory, parallel voting systems, which are used in 20 countries,[^2] members of a legislature are elected by two different methods; part of the membership is elected by a winner-take-all election system in single-member constituencies and the other part by proportional representation. The results of the constituency contests have no effect on the outcome of the proportional vote.[^11]

In compensatory [mixed-member systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation "Mixed-member proportional representation") levelling seats are allocated to balance nation-wide or regional disproportionality produced by the way seats are won in constituency contests. The [mixed-member proportional systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation "Mixed-member proportional representation"), in use in eight countries, provide enough compensatory seats to ensure that many parties have a share of seats approximately proportional to their vote share.[^2] Most of the MMP countries use a PR system at the district level, thus lowering the number of levelling seats that are needed to produce proportional results. Of the MMP countries, only New Zealand and Lesotho use single-winner [first-past-the-post voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting "First-past-the-post voting") in their districts. Scotland uses a regionalized MMP system where levelling seats are allocated in each region to balance the disproportionality produced in single-winner districts within the region. Variations of this include the [Additional Member System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additional_Member_System "Additional Member System"), and [Alternative Vote Plus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternative_Vote_Plus "Alternative Vote Plus"), in which voters cast votes for both single-member constituencies and multi-member constituencies; the allocation of seats in the multi-member constituencies is adjusted to achieve an overall seat allocation proportional to parties' vote share by taking into account the number of seats won by parties in the single-member constituencies.

Some MMP systems are insufficiently compensatory, and this may result in [overhang seats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overhang_seat "Overhang seat"), where parties win more seats in the constituency system than they would be entitled to based on their vote share. Some MMP systems have mechanism (another form of top-up) where additional seats are awarded to the other parties to balance out the effect of the overhang. Germany in 2024 passed a new election law where district overhang seats may be denied, over-riding the district result in the pursuit of overall proportionality.[^13]

[Vote linkage mixed systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vote_linkage_mixed_system "Vote linkage mixed system") are also compensatory, however they usually use different mechanism than seat linkage (top-up) method of MMP and usually aren't able to achieve proportional representation.

Some electoral systems feature a [majority bonus system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majority_bonus_system "Majority bonus system") to either ensure one party or coalition gains a majority in the legislature, or to give the party receiving the most votes a clear advantage in terms of the number of seats. [San Marino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Marino "San Marino") has a modified two-round system, which sees a second round of voting featuring the top two parties or coalitions if no party takes a majority of votes in the first round. The winner of the second round is guaranteed 35 seats in the 60-seat [Grand and General Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_and_General_Council "Grand and General Council").[^14] In [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece") the party receiving the most votes was given an additional 50 seats,[^15] a system which was abolished following the [2019 elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_Greek_legislative_election "2019 Greek legislative election").

### Primary elections

[Primary elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partisan_primary "Partisan primary") are a feature of some electoral systems, either as a formal part of the electoral system or informally by choice of individual political parties as a method of selecting candidates, as is the case in [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_elections_in_Italy "Primary elections in Italy"). Primary elections limit the possible adverse effect of [vote splitting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vote_splitting "Vote splitting") by ensuring that a party puts forward only one party candidate. In [Argentina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_elections_in_Argentina "Primary elections in Argentina") they are a formal part of the electoral system and take place two months before the main elections; any party receiving less than 1.5% of the vote is not permitted to contest the main elections.In the United States, there are both partisan and non-partisan [primary elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_elections_in_the_United_States "Primary elections in the United States"). In non-partisan primaries, the most-popular nominees, even if only one party, are put forward to the election.

### Indirect elections

Some elections feature an [indirect electoral system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indirect_election "Indirect election"), whereby there is either no popular vote, or the popular vote is only one stage of the election; in these systems the final vote is usually taken by an [electoral college](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_college "Electoral college"). In several countries, such as [Mauritius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauritius "Mauritius") or [Trinidad and Tobago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago "Trinidad and Tobago"), the post of President is elected by the legislature. In others like [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"), the vote is taken by an electoral college consisting of the national legislature and state legislatures. In the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), the president is indirectly elected using a two-stage process; a popular vote in each state elects members to the [electoral college](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_College_\(United_States\) "Electoral College (United States)") that in turn elects the President. This can result in a situation where a candidate who receives the most votes nationwide does not win the electoral college vote, as most recently happened in [2000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2000_United_States_presidential_election "2000 United States presidential election") and [2016](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_United_States_presidential_election "2016 United States presidential election").

### Proposed and lesser-used systems

In addition to the current electoral systems used for political elections, there are numerous other systems that have been used in the past, are currently used only in private organizations (such as electing board members of corporations or student organizations), or have never been fully implemented.

#### Ranked voting systems

[Ranked vote systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ranked_voting "Ranked voting") include [Bucklin voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bucklin_voting "Bucklin voting"), the various [Condorcet methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condorcet_method "Condorcet method") ([Copeland's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copeland%27s_method "Copeland's method"), [Dodgson's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodgson%27s_method "Dodgson's method"), [Kemeny-Young](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kemeny%E2%80%93Young_method "Kemeny–Young method"), [Maximal lotteries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximal_lotteries "Maximal lotteries"), [Minimax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimax_Condorcet "Minimax Condorcet"), [Nanson's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanson%27s_method "Nanson's method"), [Ranked pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ranked_pairs "Ranked pairs"), [Schulze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schulze_method "Schulze method")), the [Coombs' method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coombs%27_method "Coombs' method") and [positional voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positional_voting_system "Positional voting system").

#### Cardinal voting systems

Among the [Cardinal electoral systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_voting_methods "Cardinal voting methods"), the best known is [range voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Range_voting "Range voting"), where any number of candidates are scored from a set range of numbers. A very common example of range voting are the 5-star ratings used for many customer satisfaction surveys and reviews. Other cardinal systems include [satisfaction approval voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satisfaction_approval_voting "Satisfaction approval voting"), [highest median rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highest_median_voting_rules "Highest median voting rules") (including the [majority judgment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majority_judgment "Majority judgment")), and the [D21 – Janeček method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D21_%E2%80%93_Jane%C4%8Dek_method "D21 – Janeček method") where voters can cast positive and negative votes.

#### Weighted votes

Historically, some countries used [weighted voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weighted_voting "Weighted voting") systems. These allocated a greater weight to the votes of some voters than others. This could be done indirectly by allocating more seats to a certain group of a similar size, or the same number of seats to differently sized groups (such as the [Prussian three-class franchise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussian_three-class_franchise "Prussian three-class franchise")), which are both breaks from the one person one vote rule.

Differential weighting can also be achieved directly by differently weighting the votes cast. This was used in colonial [Rhodesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhodesia "Rhodesia") for the [1962](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1962_Southern_Rhodesian_general_election "1962 Southern Rhodesian general election") and [1965 elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1965_Rhodesian_general_election "1965 Rhodesian general election"). The elections featured two voter rolls (the 'A' roll being largely European and the 'B' roll largely African); the seats of the House Assembly were divided into 50 constituency seats and 15 district seats. Although all voters could vote for both types of seats, 'A' roll votes were given greater weight for the numerous constituency seats and 'B' roll votes greater weight for the fewer district seats.

Weighted systems are used in corporate elections, with votes weighted to reflect stock ownership. Conversely the [Rochdale principles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rochdale_principles "Rochdale principles") used by that pioneer consumer cooperative called for one person one vote, irrespective of the holdings of each member.

#### Unusual Proportional Representation systems

Most countries in the world use proportional representation but some PR systems are relatively scarce.

Unusual proportional systems that use [ranked votes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ranked_voting "Ranked voting") include [single transferable voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_transferable_voting "Single transferable voting") variants, such as [CPO-STV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CPO-STV "CPO-STV"), [Schulze STV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schulze_STV "Schulze STV") and the [Wright system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wright_system "Wright system"). [Single non-transferable voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_non-transferable_voting "Single non-transferable voting"), [limited voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_voting "Limited voting") and [cumulative voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumulative_voting "Cumulative voting") ensure that both most-popular party and at least one other party elect representatives. They are described as semi-proportional.

[Dual-member proportional representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual-member_proportional_representation "Dual-member proportional representation") is a proposed system with two members elected to represent each constituency, one with the most votes cast in the district and one to ensure proportionality of the combined results using votes cast in the district and elsewhere. [Biproportional apportionment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biproportional_apportionment "Biproportional apportionment") is a system where the total number of votes is used to calculate the number of seats each party is due, followed by a calculation of the constituencies in which the seats should be awarded in order to achieve the total due to them.

Some proportional voting systems use rating. They include [Thiele's voting rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiele%27s_voting_rules "Thiele's voting rules") and [Phragmen's voting rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phragmen%27s_voting_rules "Phragmen's voting rules"). A special case of [Thiele's voting rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiele%27s_voting_rules "Thiele's voting rules") is [Proportional Approval Voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportional_approval_voting "Proportional approval voting"). Some proportional systems that may be used with either ranking or rating include the [Method of Equal Shares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_equal_shares "Method of equal shares") and the [Expanding Approvals Rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expanding_approvals_rule "Expanding approvals rule").

## Rules and regulations

In addition to the specific method of electing candidates, electoral systems are also characterised by their wider rules and regulations, which are usually set out in a country's [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution "Constitution") or [electoral law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election_law "Election law"). Participatory rules determine [candidate nomination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomination_rules "Nomination rules") and [voter registration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voter_registration "Voter registration"), in addition to the location of [polling places](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polling_places "Polling places") and the availability of [online voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Online_voting "Online voting"), [postal voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postal_voting "Postal voting"), and [absentee voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absentee_ballot "Absentee ballot"). Other regulations include the selection of voting devices such as paper [ballots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ballots "Ballots"), [machine voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_voting "Machine voting") or [open ballot systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_ballot_system "Open ballot system"), and consequently the type of [vote counting systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vote_counting_systems "Vote counting systems"), verification and [auditing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election_audits "Election audits") used.

Electoral rules place limits on suffrage and candidacy. Most countries's electorates are characterised by [universal suffrage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_suffrage "Universal suffrage"), but there are differences on the [age at which people are allowed to vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voting_age "Voting age"), with the youngest being 16 and the oldest 21. People may be disenfranchised for a range of reasons, such as being a serving prisoner, being declared bankrupt, having committed certain crimes or being a serving member of the armed forces. Similar limits are placed on candidacy (also known as passive suffrage), and in many cases the age limit for candidates is higher than the voting age. A total of 21 countries have [compulsory voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compulsory_voting "Compulsory voting"), although in some there is an upper age limit on enforcement of the law.[^16] Many countries also have the [none of the above](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/None_of_the_above "None of the above") option on their ballot papers.

In systems that use [constituencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_district "Electoral district"), [apportionment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apportionment_\(politics\) "Apportionment (politics)") or districting defines the area covered by each constituency. Where constituency boundaries are drawn has a strong influence on the likely outcome of elections in the constituency due to the geographic distribution of voters. Political parties may seek to gain an advantage during [redistricting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redistricting "Redistricting") by ensuring their voter base has a majority in as many constituencies as possible, a process known as [gerrymandering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerrymandering "Gerrymandering"). Historically [rotten and pocket boroughs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotten_and_pocket_boroughs "Rotten and pocket boroughs"), constituencies with unusually small populations, were used by wealthy families to gain parliamentary representation.

Some countries have minimum turnout requirements for elections to be valid. In Serbia this rule caused multiple re-runs of presidential elections, with the 1997 election re-run once and the 2002 elections re-run three times due insufficient turnout in the [first](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serbian_presidential_election,_September%E2%80%93October_2002 "Serbian presidential election, September–October 2002"), [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/December_2002_Serbian_presidential_election "December 2002 Serbian presidential election") and [third](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2003_Serbian_presidential_election "2003 Serbian presidential election") attempts to run the election. The turnout requirement was scrapped prior to the [fourth vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2004_Serbian_presidential_election "2004 Serbian presidential election") in 2004.[^17] Similar problems in [Belarus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belarus "Belarus") led to the [1995 parliamentary elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1995_Belarusian_parliamentary_election "1995 Belarusian parliamentary election") going to a fourth round of voting before enough parliamentarians were elected to make a [quorum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quorum "Quorum").[^18]

[Reserved seats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reserved_political_positions "Reserved political positions") are used in many countries to ensure representation for ethnic minorities, women, young people or the disabled. These seats are separate from general seats, and may be elected separately (such as in Morocco where a separate ballot is used to elect the 60 seats reserved for women and 30 seats reserved for young people in the House of Representatives), or be allocated to parties based on the results of the election; in [Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan "Jordan") the reserved seats for women are given to the female candidates who failed to win constituency seats but with the highest number of votes, whilst in [Kenya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenya "Kenya") the Senate seats reserved for women, young people and the disabled are allocated to parties based on how many seats they won in the general vote. Some countries achieve minority representation by other means, including requirements for a certain proportion of candidates to be women, or by exempting minority parties from the electoral threshold, as is done in [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland "Poland"),[^19] [Romania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romania "Romania") and [Serbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serbia "Serbia").[^20]

## History

### Pre-democratic

In [ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") and [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Italy "History of Italy"), the institution of suffrage already existed in a rudimentary form at the outset of the historical period. In the early [monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy#History "Monarchy") it was customary for the king to invite pronouncements of his people on matters in which it was prudent to secure its assent beforehand. In these assemblies the people recorded their opinion by clamouring (a method which survived in [Sparta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sparta "Sparta") as late as the 4th century BCE), or by the clashing of [spears](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spear "Spear") on [shields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shield "Shield").[^21]

### Early democracy

Voting has been used as a feature of democracy since the 6th century BCE, when democracy was introduced by the [Athenian democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athenian_democracy "Athenian democracy"). However, in Athenian democracy, voting was seen as the least democratic among methods used for selecting public officials, and was little used, because elections were believed to inherently favor the wealthy and well-known over average citizens. Viewed as more democratic were assemblies open to all citizens, and [selection by lot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selection_by_lot "Selection by lot"), as well as rotation of office.

Generally, the taking of votes was effected in the form of a poll. The practice of the Athenians, which is shown by inscriptions to have been widely followed in the other states of Greece, was to hold a show of hands, except on questions affecting the status of individuals: these latter, which included all [lawsuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawsuit "Lawsuit") and proposals of [ostracism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostracism "Ostracism"), in which voters chose the citizen they most wanted to exile for ten years, were determined by secret ballot (one of the earliest recorded elections in Athens was a [plurality vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plurality_voting "Plurality voting") that it was undesirable to win, namely an ostracism vote). At [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") the method which prevailed up to the 2nd century BCE was that of division (*discessio*). But the system became subject to intimidation and corruption. Hence a series of laws enacted between 139 and 107 BCE prescribed the use of the ballot (*tabella*), a slip of wood coated with wax, for all business done in the assemblies of the people. For the purpose of carrying resolutions a simple majority of votes was deemed sufficient. As a general rule equal value was made to attach to each vote; but in the popular assemblies at Rome a system of voting by groups was in force until the middle of the 3rd century BCE by which the richer classes secured a decisive preponderance.[^21]

Most elections in the early [history of democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_democracy "History of democracy") were held using plurality voting or some variant, but as an exception, the state of [Venice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venice "Venice") in the 13th century adopted approval voting to elect their Great Council.[^22]

The Venetians' method for [electing the Doge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doge_of_Venice#Selection_of_the_Doge "Doge of Venice") was a particularly convoluted process, consisting of five rounds of drawing lots ([sortition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sortition "Sortition")) and five rounds of approval voting. By drawing lots, a body of 30 electors was chosen, which was further reduced to nine electors by drawing lots again. An [electoral college](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_college "Electoral college") of nine members elected 40 people by approval voting; those 40 were reduced to form a second electoral college of 12 members by drawing lots again. The second electoral college elected 25 people by approval voting, which were reduced to form a third electoral college of nine members by drawing lots. The third electoral college elected 45 people, which were reduced to form a fourth electoral college of 11 by drawing lots. They in turn elected a final electoral body of 41 members, who ultimately elected the Doge. Despite its complexity, the method had certain desirable properties such as being hard to game and ensuring that the winner reflected the opinions of both majority and minority factions.[^23] This process, with slight modifications, was central to the politics of the [Republic of Venice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Venice "Republic of Venice") throughout its remarkable lifespan of over 500 years, from 1268 to 1797.

### Development of new systems

[Jean-Charles de Borda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Charles_de_Borda "Jean-Charles de Borda") proposed the [Borda count](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borda_count "Borda count") in 1770 as a method for electing members to the [French Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences "French Academy of Sciences"). His method was opposed by the [Marquis de Condorcet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marquis_de_Condorcet "Marquis de Condorcet"), who proposed instead the method of pairwise comparison that he had devised. Implementations of this method are known as [Condorcet methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condorcet_method "Condorcet method"). He also wrote about the [Condorcet paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condorcet_paradox "Condorcet paradox"), which he called the *intransitivity of majority preferences*. However, recent research has shown that the philosopher [Ramon Llull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramon_Llull "Ramon Llull") devised both the Borda count and a pairwise method that satisfied the Condorcet criterion in the 13th century. The manuscripts in which he described these methods had been lost to history until they were rediscovered in 2001.[^24]

Later in the 18th century, [apportionment methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apportionment_\(politics\) "Apportionment (politics)") came to prominence due to the [United States Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Constitution "United States Constitution"), which mandated that seats in the [United States House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives "United States House of Representatives") had to be allocated among the states proportionally to their population, but did not specify how to do so.[^25] A variety of methods were proposed by statesmen such as [Alexander Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton "Alexander Hamilton"), [Thomas Jefferson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson "Thomas Jefferson"), and [Daniel Webster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Webster "Daniel Webster"). Some of the apportionment methods devised in the United States were in a sense rediscovered in Europe in the 19th century, as seat allocation methods for the newly proposed method of party-list proportional representation. The result is that many apportionment methods have two names; *Jefferson's method* is equivalent to the [D'Hondt method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Hondt_method "D'Hondt method"), *Webster's method* is the [Sainte-Laguë method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sainte-Lagu%C3%AB_method "Sainte-Laguë method"), and *Hamilton's method* is identical to the Hare largest remainder method.[^25]

The [single transferable vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_transferable_vote "Single transferable vote") (STV) election system was devised by [Carl Andræ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Andr%C3%A6 "Carl Andræ") in [Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark "Denmark") in 1855 and in the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") by [Thomas Hare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Hare_\(political_scientist\) "Thomas Hare (political scientist)") in 1857. STV elections were first held in Denmark in 1856. An STV variant (under the name [Hare-Clark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hare-Clark "Hare-Clark")) in [Tasmania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tasmania "Tasmania") was adopted temporarily in 1896 after its use was promoted by [Andrew Inglis Clark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Inglis_Clark "Andrew Inglis Clark"). Over the course of the 20th century, STV was subsequently adopted by Ireland and Malta for their national elections, in Australia for [Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_Senate "Australian Senate") elections and state elections, as well as by many municipalities around the world.[^26]

Party-list proportional representation began to be used to elect European legislatures in the early 20th century, starting in Switzerland, with [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium "Belgium") the first to implement it at the national level for its [1900 general elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1900_Belgian_general_election "1900 Belgian general election"). Since then, proportional and semi-proportional methods have come to be used in almost all democratic countries, with most exceptions being former [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") and [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_colonial_empire "French colonial empire") colonies.

### Single-winner innovations

Perhaps influenced by the rapid development of multiple-winner STV, theorists published new findings about single-winner methods in the late 19th century. Around 1870, [William Robert Ware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Robert_Ware "William Robert Ware") proposed applying STV to single-winner elections, yielding [instant-runoff voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instant-runoff_voting "Instant-runoff voting") (IRV). Soon, mathematicians began to revisit Condorcet's ideas and invent new methods for Condorcet completion; [Edward J. Nanson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_J._Nanson "Edward J. Nanson") combined the newly described instant-runoff voting with the Borda count to yield a new Condorcet method called [Nanson's method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanson%27s_method "Nanson's method").

Charles Dodgson, better known as [Lewis Carroll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_Carroll "Lewis Carroll"), proposed the straightforward Condorcet method known as [Dodgson's method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodgson%27s_method "Dodgson's method"). He also proposed a proportional representation system based on multi-member districts, quotas as minimum requirements to take seats, and votes transferable by candidates through [proxy voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxy_voting "Proxy voting").[^27]

Ranked voting electoral systems eventually gathered enough support to be adopted for use in government elections. In [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia"), IRV was first adopted in 1893 and STV in 1896 (Tasmania). IRV continues to be used along with STV today.

In the United States, during the early 20th-century [progressive era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Progressive_era "Progressive era") some municipalities began to use [supplementary voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supplementary_voting_system "Supplementary voting system") and [Bucklin voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bucklin_voting "Bucklin voting"). However, a series of court decisions ruled Bucklin to be unconstitutional, while supplementary voting was soon repealed in every city that had implemented it.[^28]

The use of [game theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_theory "Game theory") to analyze electoral systems led to discoveries about the effects of certain methods. Earlier developments such as [Arrow's impossibility theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrow%27s_impossibility_theorem "Arrow's impossibility theorem") had already shown the issues with [ranked voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ranked_voting "Ranked voting") systems. Research led [Steven Brams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_Brams "Steven Brams") and [Peter Fishburn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_C._Fishburn "Peter C. Fishburn") to formally define and promote the use of [approval voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Approval_voting "Approval voting") in 1977.[^29] Political scientists of the 20th century published many studies on the effects that the electoral systems have on voters' choices and political parties,[^30] [^31] [^32] and on political stability.[^33] [^34] A few scholars also studied which effects caused a nation to switch to a particular electoral system.[^35] [^36] [^37] [^38] [^39]

### Recent reform efforts

A new push for [electoral reform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_reform "Electoral reform") occurred in the 1990s and early 2000s. Proposals were made to replace plurality-based systems in government elections with other methods. [New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand "New Zealand") adopted mixed-member proportional representation for the [1996 general elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1996_New_Zealand_general_election "1996 New Zealand general election"), it having been approved by referendums in [1990](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=1990_New_Zealand_electoral_reform_referendum&action=edit&redlink=1 "1990 New Zealand electoral reform referendum (page does not exist)") and [1993](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1993_New_Zealand_electoral_reform_referendum "1993 New Zealand electoral reform referendum").[^40] Bulgaria, Romania, Czechia, Slovakia, Poland, Federal Republic of Yugoslavia and Bosnia-Herzegovina also adopted PR in the 1990s.

After many states' electoral college caucus was determined by only a minority of state voters in the contested [2000 presidential elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2000_United_States_presidential_election "2000 United States presidential election") in the United States, several municipalities adopted [instant-runoff voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instant-runoff_voting "Instant-runoff voting"). In 2020 a referendum adopting [approval voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Approval_voting "Approval voting") in [St. Louis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Louis "St. Louis") passed with 70% support.[^41] However, a [Massachusetts referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Massachusetts_Question_2 "2020 Massachusetts Question 2") the same year on whether or not to use instant-runoff voting to elect most state representatives was defeated by a 10-point margin.

In Canada, three separate referendums on adoption of [single transferable vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_transferable_vote "Single transferable vote") or other PR electoral system were held since 2004 but producing no reform (in [2005](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2005_British_Columbia_electoral_reform_referendum "2005 British Columbia electoral reform referendum") (when a majority of voters voted for change), [2009](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2009_British_Columbia_electoral_reform_referendum "2009 British Columbia electoral reform referendum"), and [2018](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2018_British_Columbia_electoral_reform_referendum "2018 British Columbia electoral reform referendum")).

In the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom"), a [2011 referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_United_Kingdom_Alternative_Vote_referendum "2011 United Kingdom Alternative Vote referendum") on IRV saw the proposal rejected by a two-to-one margin.

#### Repeals and backlash

Some cities that adopted instant-runoff voting subsequently returned to [first-past-the-post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting "First-past-the-post voting"). Studies have found voter satisfaction with IRV falls dramatically the first time a race produces a result different from first-past-the-post.[^42] The United Kingdom used a form of [instant-runoff voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instant-runoff_voting "Instant-runoff voting") for local elections prior to 2022, before returning to [first-past-the-post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting "First-past-the-post voting") over concerns regarding the system's complexity.[^43] Ranked-choice voting has been implemented in two states and banned in 10 others [^44] (in addition to other states with constitutional prohibitions on the rule).

In November 2024, voters in the U.S. decided on 10 ballot measures related to electoral systems. Nine of the ballot measures aimed to change existing electoral systems, and voters rejected each proposal. One, in Missouri, which banned ranked-choice voting (RCV), was approved. Voters rejected ballot measures to enact ranked-choice voting and other electoral system changes in Arizona, Colorado, Idaho, Nevada, and Oregon, as well as in Montana and South Dakota. In Alaska, voters rejected a ballot initiative 50.1% to 49.9% to repeal the state's top-four primaries and ranked-choice voting general elections, a system that was adopted via ballot measure in 2020.[^45]

## Comparison

Electoral systems can be compared by different means:

1. Define criteria mathematically, such that any electoral system either passes or fails. This gives perfectly objective results, but their practical relevance is still arguable.
2. Define ideal criteria and use simulated elections to see how often or how close various methods fail to meet the selected criteria. This gives results which are practically relevant, but the method of generating the sample of simulated elections can still be arguably biased.
3. Consider criteria that can be more easily measured using real-world elections, such as [the Gallagher index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallagher_index "Gallagher index"), [political fragmentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_fragmentation "Political fragmentation"), [voter turnout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voter_turnout "Voter turnout"),[^46] [^47] [wasted votes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wasted_vote "Wasted vote"), [political apathy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_apathy "Political apathy"), complexity of [vote counting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vote_counting "Vote counting"), and [barriers to entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barriers_to_entry "Barriers to entry") for new political movements [^48] and evaluate each method based on how they perform in real-world elections or evaluate the performance of countries with these electoral systems.
4. A 2019 peer-reviewed [meta-analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meta-analysis "Meta-analysis") based on 1,037 regressions in 46 studies finds that countries with majoritary kind of electoral rules would be more "fiscally virtuous" since they would exhibit better fiscal balances in the pre-electoral period, which may be explained by less spending distortion.[^49] The meta-analysis also notes that countries with proportional kind of electoral rules would have bigger pre-electoral revenue cuts than other countries.[^50]

[Gibbard's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibbard%27s_theorem "Gibbard's theorem"), built upon the earlier [Arrow's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrow%27s_impossibility_theorem "Arrow's impossibility theorem") and the [Gibbard–Satterthwaite theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibbard%E2%80%93Satterthwaite_theorem "Gibbard–Satterthwaite theorem"), to prove that for any single-winner deterministic voting methods, at least one of the following three properties must hold:

1. The process is [dictatorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictatorship_mechanism "Dictatorship mechanism"), i.e. there is a single voter whose vote chooses the outcome.
2. The process limits the possible outcomes to two options only.
3. The process is not straightforward; the optimal ballot for a voter "requires [strategic voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_voting "Strategic voting") ", i.e. it depends on their beliefs about other voters' ballots.

According to a 2005 survey of electoral system experts, their preferred electoral systems were in order of preference:[^51]

1. Mixed member proportional
2. Single transferable vote
3. Open list proportional
4. Alternative vote
5. Closed list proportional
6. Single member plurality
7. Runoffs
8. Mixed member majoritarian
9. Single non-transferable vote
