**Politics** [^1] is the activity of settling affairs in an organized [society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society "Society").[^3] Politics is usually concerned with resolving issues within a society via a [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government").[^3] Politicians are individuals engaged in the activity of politics. The branch of [social science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science") that studies politics and [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government") is referred to as [political science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science").[^4]

A variety of methods are deployed in politics, which include [promoting one's own political views](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_campaign "Political campaign") among people, [negotiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negotiation "Negotiation") with other political subjects, making [laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law "Law"), and exercising internal and external [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_\(law\) "Force (law)"), including [warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warfare "Warfare") against adversaries.[^5] [^6] [^7] [^8] [^9] Politics is exercised on a wide range of social levels, from [clans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clans "Clans") and [tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribes "Tribes") of traditional [societies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society "Society"), through modern [local governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_government "Local government"), [companies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Company "Company") and [institutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institution "Institution") up to [sovereign states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state "Sovereign state"), to the [international level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_politics "International politics").

In modern [states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)"), people often form [political parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_parties "Political parties") to represent their ideas. Members of a party often agree to take the same position on many issues and agree to support the same changes to law and the same leaders. An [election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election "Election") is usually a competition between different parties.

A [political system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_system "Political system") is a framework which defines acceptable political methods within a society. The [history of political thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_political_thought "History of political thought") can be traced back to early antiquity, with seminal works such as [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") 's *[Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_\(Plato\) "Republic (Plato)")*, [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") 's *[Politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_\(Aristotle\) "Politics (Aristotle)")*, [Confucius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius "Confucius") 's political manuscripts and [Chanakya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chanakya "Chanakya") 's *[Arthashastra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthashastra "Arthashastra")*.[^10]

Politics may be used positively in the context of a "political solution" which is compromising and non-violent,[^11] or descriptively as "the art or science of government", but the word often also carries a negative connotation.[^12]

## Etymology

The English word *politics* has its roots in the Greek word *polis* (or *poleis*) which means "city".[^13] [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") 's classic work, *[Politiká](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_\(Aristotle\) "Politics (Aristotle)")* used the [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") term **politiká** (*Πολιτικά*, 'affairs of the cities')*.* In the mid-15th century, Aristotle's composition was rendered in [Early Modern English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Modern_English "Early Modern English") as *Polettiques* \[*[sic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sic "Sic")*\],[^2] [^14] which became *Politics* in [Modern English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_English "Modern English").

The singular *politic* first attested in English in 1430, coming from [Middle French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_French "Middle French") *politique* —itself derived from *politicus*,[^15] a [Latinization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latinisation_of_names "Latinisation of names") of the Greek *πολιτικός* (*politikos*) from πολίτης (*polites*, 'citizen') and πόλις (*[polis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polis "Polis")*, 'city').[^16]

## Approaches

### Extensive and limited

[Adrian Leftwich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrian_Leftwich "Adrian Leftwich") has differentiated views of politics based on how extensive or limited their perception of what accounts as 'political' is.[^17] The extensive view sees politics as present across the sphere of human social relations, while the limited view restricts it to certain contexts. For example, in a more restrictive way, politics may be viewed as primarily about [governance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governance "Governance"),[^18] while a [feminist perspective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_political_theory "Feminist political theory") could argue that sites which have been viewed traditionally as non-political, should indeed be viewed as political as well.[^19] This latter position is encapsulated in the slogan " *[the personal is political](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_personal_is_political "The personal is political")* ", which disputes the distinction between private and public issues. Politics may also be defined by the use of power, as has been argued by [Robert A. Dahl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_A._Dahl "Robert A. Dahl").[^20]

### Moralism and realism

Some perspectives on politics view it empirically as an exercise of power, while others see it as a social function with a [normative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normative "Normative") basis.[^21] This distinction has sometimes been called the difference between [political *moralism*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moralism "Moralism") and [political *realism*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Realism_\(international_relations\) "Realism (international relations)")*.*[^22] For moralists, politics is closely linked to [ethics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics "Ethics"), and is at its extreme in [utopian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utopia "Utopia") thinking.[^22] For example, according to [Hannah Arendt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hannah_Arendt "Hannah Arendt"), the view of [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") was that, "to be political…meant that everything was decided through words and persuasion and not through violence";[^23] while according to [Bernard Crick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Crick "Bernard Crick"), "politics is the way in which free societies are governed. Politics is politics, and other forms of rule are something else." [^24] In contrast, for realists, who some include figures such as [Niccolò Machiavelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli "Niccolò Machiavelli"), [Thomas Hobbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes "Thomas Hobbes"), and [Harold Lasswell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Lasswell "Harold Lasswell"), the true nature of politics reflects human desire for rule and the aggrandizement of self interests rather than moralism.[^25]

### Conflict and co-operation

[Agonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agonism "Agonism") argues that politics essentially comes down to conflict between conflicting interests. Political scientist Elmer Schattschneider argued that "at the root of all politics is the universal language of conflict",[^26] while for [Carl Schmitt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Schmitt "Carl Schmitt") the essence of politics is the distinction of 'friend' from 'foe'.[^27] This is in direct contrast to the more co-operative views of politics by Aristotle and Crick. However, a more mixed view between these extremes is provided by Irish political scientist Michael Laver, who noted that:

> Politics is about the characteristic blend of conflict and co-operation that can be found so often in human interactions. Pure conflict is war. Pure co-operation is true love. Politics is a mixture of both.[^28]

### Prehistoric

Early human forms of social organization—bands and tribes—lacked centralized political structures.[^30] These are sometimes referred to as [stateless societies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stateless_society "Stateless society").

### Early states

In ancient history, [civilizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_civilizations "Ancient civilizations") did not have definite boundaries as [states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation_state "Nation state") have today, and their borders could be more accurately described as [frontiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frontier "Frontier"). [Early dynastic Sumer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumer#Early_Dynastic_Period "Sumer"), and [early dynastic Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt#Early_Dynastic_Period "Ancient Egypt") were the [first civilizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_civilization "First civilization") to define their [borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Border "Border"). Moreover, up to the 12th century, many people lived in non-state societies. These range from relatively egalitarian [bands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Band_society "Band society") and [tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribe "Tribe") to complex and highly stratified [chiefdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiefdom "Chiefdom").

#### State formation

There are a number of different theories and hypotheses regarding early state formation that seek generalizations to explain why **[the state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)")** developed in some places but not others. Other scholars believe that generalizations are unhelpful and that each case of early state formation should be treated on its own.[^31]

**Voluntary theories** contend that diverse groups of people came together to form states as a result of some shared rational interest.[^32] The theories largely focus on the development of agriculture, and the population and organizational pressure that followed and resulted in state formation. One of the most prominent theories of early and primary state formation is the *hydraulic hypothesis*, which contends that the state was a result of the need to build and maintain large-scale irrigation projects.[^33]

**[Conflict theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conflict_theories "Conflict theories")** of state formation regard conflict and dominance of some population over another population as key to the formation of states.[^32] In contrast with voluntary theories, these arguments believe that people do not voluntarily agree to create a state to maximize benefits, but that states form due to some form of oppression by one group over others. Some theories in turn argue that warfare was critical for state formation.[^32]

#### Ancient history

The first states of sorts were those of [early dynastic Sumer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumer#Early_Dynastic_Period "Sumer") and [early dynastic Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt#Early_Dynastic_Period "Ancient Egypt"), which arose from the [Uruk period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uruk_period "Uruk period") and [Predynastic Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predynastic_Egypt "Predynastic Egypt") respectively around approximately 3000 BC.[^34] Early dynastic Egypt was based around the [Nile River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile_River "Nile River") in the north-east of [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa"), the kingdom's boundaries being based around the Nile and stretching to areas where [oases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oasis "Oasis") existed.[^35] Early dynastic [Sumer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumer "Sumer") was located in southern [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia"), with its borders extending from the [Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf") to parts of the [Euphrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euphrates_River "Euphrates River") and [Tigris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tigris_River "Tigris River") rivers.[^34]

Egyptians, Romans, and the Greeks were the first people known to have explicitly formulated a political philosophy of the state, and to have rationally analyzed political institutions. Prior to this, states were described and justified in terms of religious myths.[^36]

Several important political innovations of [classical antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_antiquity "Classical antiquity") came from the [Greek city-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_city_states "Greek city states") (*[polis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polis "Polis")*) and the [Roman Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome"). The Greek city-states before the 4th century granted [citizenship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athenian_democracy#Citizenship_in_Athens "Athenian democracy") rights to their free population; in [Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athens "Athens") these rights [were combined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athenian_democracy "Athenian democracy") with a [directly democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracy "Direct democracy") form of government that was to have a long afterlife in political thought and history.[^37]

### Modern states

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Women_voter_outreach_1935_English_Yiddish.jpg/250px-Women_voter_outreach_1935_English_Yiddish.jpg)

Women voter outreach (1935)

The [Peace of Westphalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia "Peace of Westphalia") (1648) is considered by [political scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science") to be the beginning of the modern international system,[^38] [^39] [^40] in which external powers should avoid interfering in another country's domestic affairs.[^41] The principle of non-interference in other countries' domestic affairs was laid out in the mid-18th century by Swiss jurist [Emer de Vattel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emer_de_Vattel "Emer de Vattel").[^42] States became the primary institutional agents in an [interstate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_system_\(world-systems_theory\) "Interstate system (world-systems theory)") of relations. The Peace of Westphalia is said to have ended attempts to impose supranational authority on European states. The "Westphalian" doctrine of states as independent agents was bolstered by the rise in 19th century thought of [nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalism "Nationalism"), under which legitimate [states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state "Sovereign state") were assumed to correspond to *[nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nations "Nations")* —groups of people united by language and culture.[^43]

In [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe"), during the 18th century, the classic non-national states were the multinational [empires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire "Empire"): the [Austrian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austrian_Empire "Austrian Empire"), [Kingdom of France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_France "Kingdom of France"), [Kingdom of Hungary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary "Kingdom of Hungary"),[^44] the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"), the [Spanish Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Empire "Spanish Empire"), the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"), and the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire"). Such empires also existed in Asia, Africa, and the Americas; in the [Muslim world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_world "Muslim world"), immediately after the [death of Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_of_Muhammad "Death of Muhammad") in 632, [Caliphates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caliphate "Caliphate") were established, which developed into multi-ethnic transnational empires.[^45] The multinational empire was an [absolute monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy "Absolute monarchy") ruled by a king, [emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor "Emperor") or [sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sultan "Sultan"). The population belonged to many ethnic groups, and they spoke many languages. The empire was dominated by one ethnic group, and their language was usually the language of public administration. The ruling [dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynasty "Dynasty") was usually, but not always, from that group. Some of the smaller European states were not so ethnically diverse, but were also [dynastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynasty "Dynasty") states, ruled by a [royal house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynasty "Dynasty"). A few of the smaller states survived, such as the independent principalities of [Liechtenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liechtenstein "Liechtenstein"), [Andorra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andorra "Andorra"), [Monaco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monaco "Monaco"), and the republic of [San Marino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Marino "San Marino").

Most theories see the nation state as a 19th-century European phenomenon, facilitated by developments such as state-mandated education, mass [literacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literacy "Literacy"), and [mass media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_media "Mass media"). However, historians also note the early emergence of a relatively unified state and identity in [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portugal "Portugal") and the [Dutch Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_Republic "Dutch Republic").[^46] Scholars such as [Steven Weber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_Weber_\(professor\) "Steven Weber (professor)"), [David Woodward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Woodward_\(cartographer\) "David Woodward (cartographer)"), [Michel Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Foucault "Michel Foucault"), and [Jeremy Black](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeremy_Black_\(historian\) "Jeremy Black (historian)") have advanced the hypothesis that the nation state did not arise out of political ingenuity or an unknown undetermined source, nor was it an accident of history or political invention.[^47] [^32] [^48] Rather, the nation state is an inadvertent byproduct of 15th-century intellectual discoveries in [political economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_economy "Political economy"), [capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalism "Capitalism"), [mercantilism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercantilism "Mercantilism"), [political geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_geography "Political geography"), and [geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography "Geography") [^49] [^50] combined with [cartography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartography "Cartography") [^51] [^52] and [advances in map-making technologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_mapping "Automated mapping").[^53]

Some nation states, such as [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany") and [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy"), came into existence at least partly as a result of political campaigns by [nationalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalism "Nationalism"), during the 19th century. In both cases, the territory was previously divided among other states, some of them very small. Liberal ideas of [free trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_trade "Free trade") played a role in German unification, which was preceded by a [customs union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customs_union "Customs union"), the [Zollverein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zollverein "Zollverein"). National [self-determination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-determination "Self-determination") was a key aspect of United States President [Woodrow Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson "Woodrow Wilson") 's [Fourteen Points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourteen_Points "Fourteen Points"), leading to the dissolution of the [Austro-Hungarian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Empire "Austro-Hungarian Empire") and the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") after the [First World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_World_War "First World War"), while the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") became the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") after the [Russian Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Civil_War "Russian Civil War"). [Decolonization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decolonization "Decolonization") lead to the creation of new nation states in place of multinational empires in the [Third World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_World "Third World").

### Globalization

Political globalization began in the 20th century through [intergovernmental organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergovernmental_organization "Intergovernmental organization") and [supranational unions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supranational_union "Supranational union"). The League of Nations was founded after [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), and after [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") it was replaced by the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"). Various [international treaties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty "Treaty") have been signed through it. [Regional integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_integration "Regional integration") has been pursued by political and fiscal unions such as [BRICS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BRICS "BRICS"), the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union"), [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN"), the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union"), and [Mercosur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercosur "Mercosur"). International political institutions on the international level include the [International Criminal Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Criminal_Court "International Criminal Court"), the [International Monetary Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund "International Monetary Fund"), and the [World Trade Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Trade_Organization "World Trade Organization").

## Political science

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Sanzio_01_Plato_Aristotle.jpg/250px-Sanzio_01_Plato_Aristotle.jpg)

Plato (left) and Aristotle (right), from a detail of The School of Athens, a fresco by Raphael. Plato's Republic and Aristotle's Politics secured the two Greek philosophers as two of the most influential political philosophers.

The study of politics is called **political science**.[^54] It comprises numerous subfields, namely three: [Comparative politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparative_politics "Comparative politics"), [international relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_relations "International relations") and [political philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_theory "Political theory").[^55] Political science is related to, and draws upon, the fields of [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_economy "Political economy"), [law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_education "Legal education"), [sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_sociology "Political sociology"), [history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_history "Political history"), [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_philosophy "Political philosophy"), [geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_geography "Political geography"), [psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_psychology "Political psychology"), [psychiatry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychiatry "Psychiatry"), [anthropology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropology "Anthropology"), and [neurosciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuropolitics "Neuropolitics").

[Comparative politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparative_politics "Comparative politics") is the science of comparison and teaching of different types of [constitutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutions "Constitutions"), political actors, legislature and associated fields.[^56] [International relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_relations "International relations") deals with the interaction between [nation-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation-state "Nation-state") as well as intergovernmental and transnational organizations. [Political philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_philosophy "Political philosophy") is more concerned with the nature of politics and seeks knowledge about topics related to justice, legitimacy, liberty, and the legal foundations of ancient and modern civilizations.[^57]

## Political system

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Map_of_European_nationals_coloured_by_percentage_of_vote_governing_party_got_in_last_election.svg/250px-Map_of_European_nationals_coloured_by_percentage_of_vote_governing_party_got_in_last_election.svg.png)

Map of European nations coloured by percentage of vote governing party got in last election as of 2022

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Systems_view_of_politics.png/250px-Systems_view_of_politics.png)

Systems view of politics

The political system defines the process for making official [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government") decisions. It is usually compared to the [legal system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_system "Legal system"), [economic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_system "Economic system"), [cultural system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_system "Cultural system"), and other [social systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_system "Social system"). According to [David Easton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Easton "David Easton"), "A political system can be designated as the interactions through which values are authoritatively allocated for a society." [^58] Each political system is embedded in a society with its own political culture, and they in turn shape their societies through [public policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policy "Public policy"). The interactions between different political systems are the basis for [global politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_politics "Global politics").

### Forms of government

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Eduskuntatalo_2_corrected_2020-03-23.jpg/250px-Eduskuntatalo_2_corrected_2020-03-23.jpg)

Legislatures are an important political institution. Pictured is the Parliament of Finland.

Forms of government can be classified by several ways. Generally, most governments are either [monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchies "Monarchies") (including [constitutional monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy")) and [republics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic "Republic") (usually [presidential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_system "Presidential system"), [semi-presidential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-presidential_system "Semi-presidential system"), or [parliamentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system")). Republics are the most common form of government, with fewer nations being monarchies.[^59]

Another model of classifying regimes is by who runs them, such as in a [democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") (rule of many),[^60] [oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy "Oligarchy") (rule by few), [^61] or [autocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy") (rule by one).[^62]

#### Source of authority

In [political philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_philosophy "Political philosophy") and [ethics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics "Ethics"), [political authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_authority "Political authority") describes any of the moral principles legitimizing differences between individuals' [rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rights "Rights") and [duties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duties "Duties") by virtue of their relationship with the [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)").[^63] Political authority grants members of a [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government") the [right to rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_to_rule "Right to rule") over citizens using [coercion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coercion "Coercion") if necessary (i.e., [political legitimacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_legitimacy "Political legitimacy")), while imposing an [obligation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obligation "Obligation") for the citizens to obey government orders (i.e., [political obligation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_obligation "Political obligation")).[^64]

A central question in political philosophy is "To what extent is political authority legitimate?" Views range from political authority and having no legitimacy ([philosophical anarchism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_anarchism "Philosophical anarchism")) to political authority being virtually unlimited in scope ([totalitarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarianism "Totalitarianism")).[^63]

#### Vertical integration

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/The_pathway_of_regional_integration_or_separation.png/250px-The_pathway_of_regional_integration_or_separation.png)

Relation between regional integration and separatism

In terms of level of vertical integration, political systems can be divided into (from least to most integrated) [confederations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confederation "Confederation"), [federations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federation "Federation"), and [unitary states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_state "Unitary state").[^65]

### State

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Map_of_unitary_and_federal_states.svg/250px-Map_of_unitary_and_federal_states.svg.png)

Federal states Unitary states No government

All the above forms of government are variations of the same basic [polity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polity "Polity"), the [sovereign state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state "Sovereign state"). The [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)") has been defined by [Max Weber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Weber "Max Weber") as a political entity that has [monopoly on violence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monopoly_on_violence "Monopoly on violence") within its territory, while the [Montevideo Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montevideo_Convention "Montevideo Convention") holds that states need to have a defined territory; a permanent population; a government; and a capacity to enter into international relations.

A stateless society is a [society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society "Society") that is not [governed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government") by a [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)").[^66] In stateless societies, there is little [concentration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centralization "Centralization") of [authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authority "Authority"); most positions of authority that do exist are very limited in [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_power "Political power") and are generally not permanently held positions; and social bodies that resolve disputes through predefined rules tend to be small.[^67] Stateless societies are highly variable in economic organization and cultural practices.[^68]

While stateless societies were the norm in human prehistory, few stateless societies exist today; almost the entire global population resides within the jurisdiction of a [sovereign state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state "Sovereign state"). In some regions, nominal state authorities may be very weak and wield [little or no actual power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Failed_state "Failed state"). Over the course of history most stateless peoples have been [integrated into the state-based societies around them](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_assimilation "Cultural assimilation").[^69]

Some political ideologies consider the state undesirable, and thus consider the formation of a stateless society a goal to be achieved. A central tenet of [anarchism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchism "Anarchism") is the advocacy of society without states.[^66] [^70] The type of society sought for varies significantly between [anarchist schools of thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchist_schools_of_thought "Anarchist schools of thought"), ranging from extreme [individualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individualism "Individualism") to complete [collectivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collectivism_and_individualism "Collectivism and individualism").[^71] In [Marxism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism "Marxism"), [Marx's theory of the state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marx%27s_theory_of_the_state "Marx's theory of the state") considers that in a [post-capitalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-capitalist "Post-capitalist") society the state, an undesirable institution, would be unnecessary and [wither away](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Withering_away_of_the_state "Withering away of the state").[^72] A related concept is that of [stateless communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stateless_communism "Stateless communism"), a phrase sometimes used to describe Marx's anticipated post-capitalist society.

### Constitutions

[Constitutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutions "Constitutions") are written documents that specify and limit the powers of the different branches of government. Although a constitution is a written document, there is also an unwritten constitution. The unwritten constitution is continually being written by the legislative and judiciary branch of government; this is just one of those cases in which the nature of the circumstances determines the form of government that is most appropriate.[^73] England did set the fashion of written constitutions during the [Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Civil_War "English Civil War") but after the [Restoration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restoration_\(England\) "Restoration (England)") abandoned them to be taken up later by the [American Colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies "Thirteen Colonies") after their [emancipation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution "American Revolution") and then [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France") after the [Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution") and the rest of Europe including the European colonies.

### Political culture

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Inglehart-Values-Map-Small.png/250px-Inglehart-Values-Map-Small.png)

Inglehart-Weltzel cultural map of countries

[Political culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_culture "Political culture") describes how [culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture "Culture") impacts politics. Every [political system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_system "Political system") is embedded in a particular political culture.[^74] [Lucian Pye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucian_Pye "Lucian Pye") 's definition is that, "Political culture is the set of attitudes, beliefs, and sentiments, which give order and meaning to a political process and which provide the underlying assumptions and rules that govern behavior in the political system." [^74]

[Trust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trust_\(social_science\) "Trust (social science)") is a major factor in political culture, as its level determines the capacity of the state to function.[^75] [Postmaterialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmaterialism "Postmaterialism") is the degree to which a political culture is concerned with issues which are not of immediate physical or material concern, such as [human rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights "Human rights") and [environmentalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmentalism "Environmentalism").[^74] [Religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion "Religion") has also an impact on political culture.[^75]

### Political dysfunction

#### Political corruption

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Map_of_Countries_by_Corruption_Perceptions_Index_%282025%29.svg/500px-Map_of_Countries_by_Corruption_Perceptions_Index_%282025%29.svg.png)

Map of the Corruption Perceptions Index, 2025, as scored by Transparency international. A higher score indicates greater transparency (i.e., less corruption). The score ranges are: Less corrupt 90–100 80–89 70–79 60–69 50–59 More corrupt 40–49 30–39 20–29 10–19 0–9 No data

[Political corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_corruption "Political corruption") is the use of powers by government officials or their network contacts for illegitimate private gain.[^76] [^77] [^78] Forms of [corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corruption "Corruption") vary but can include [bribery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bribery "Bribery"),[^79] [lobbying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lobbying "Lobbying"), [extortion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extortion "Extortion"), [cronyism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cronyism "Cronyism"), [nepotism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nepotism "Nepotism"), [parochialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parochialism "Parochialism"), [patronage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patronage "Patronage"), [influence peddling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Influence_peddling "Influence peddling"), [graft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graft_\(politics\) "Graft (politics)"), and [embezzlement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embezzlement "Embezzlement").[^80] Corruption may facilitate [criminal enterprise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criminal_enterprise "Criminal enterprise"), such as [drug trafficking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_trafficking "Drug trafficking"), [money laundering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money_laundering "Money laundering"), and [human trafficking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_trafficking "Human trafficking"), although it is not restricted to these activities.

Over time, corruption has been defined differently. For example, while performing work for a government or as a representative, it is unethical to accept a gift. Any free gift could be construed as a scheme to lure the recipient towards some biases. In most cases, the gift is seen as an intention to seek certain favors, such as work promotion, tipping in order to win a contract, job, or exemption from certain tasks in the case of junior worker handing in the gift to a senior employee who can be key in winning the favor.[^81]

Some forms of corruption, now called " [institutional corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institutional_corruption "Institutional corruption") ",[^82] are distinguished from bribery and other kinds of obvious personal gain. For example, certain state institutions may consistently act against the interests of the public, such as by misusing public funds for their own interest, or by engaging in illegal or immoral behavior with impunity. Bribery and overt criminal acts by individuals may not necessarily be evident but the institution nonetheless acts immorally as a whole. The [mafia state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mafia_state "Mafia state") phenomenon is an example of institutional corruption.

An illegal act by an officeholder constitutes political corruption only if the act is directly related to their official duties, is done under [color of law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_of_law "Color of law") or involves trading in influence. The activities that constitute illegal corruption differ depending on the country or jurisdiction. For instance, some political funding practices that are legal in one place may be illegal in another. In some cases, government officials have broad or ill-defined powers, which make it difficult to distinguish between legal and illegal actions. Worldwide, bribery alone is estimated to involve over 1 trillion US dollars annually.[^83] A state of unrestrained political corruption is known as a [kleptocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kleptocracy "Kleptocracy"), literally meaning "rule by thieves".

#### Proxy politics

The term "proxy politics" refers to specific forms of political influences and actions that are conducted through [proxy political parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxy_political_parties "Proxy political parties") (mainly in [domestic politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domestic_politics "Domestic politics")), or through [proxy wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxy_wars "Proxy wars") (mainly in [international politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_politics "International politics")).[^84]

#### Insincere politics

The words "politics" and "political" are sometimes used as pejoratives to mean political action that is deemed to be overzealous, performative, or insincere.[^85]

## Levels of politics

### Macropolitics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/RIAN_archive_828797_Mikhail_Gorbachev_addressing_UN_General_Assembly_session.jpg/250px-RIAN_archive_828797_Mikhail_Gorbachev_addressing_UN_General_Assembly_session.jpg)

Mikhail Gorbachev addressing the UN General Assembly in 1988

Macropolitics can either describe political issues that affect an entire political system (e.g. the [nation state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation_state "Nation state")), or refer to interactions between political systems (e.g. [international relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_relations "International relations")).[^86]

Global politics (or world politics) covers all aspects of politics that affect multiple political systems, in practice meaning any political phenomenon crossing national borders. This can include [cities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City "City"), nation-states, [multinational corporations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multinational_corporation "Multinational corporation"), [non-governmental organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-governmental_organization "Non-governmental organization") or [international organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_organization "International organization"). An important element is international relations: the relations between nation-states may be peaceful when they are conducted through [diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomacy "Diplomacy"), or they may be violent, which is described as [war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War "War"). States that are able to exert strong international influence are referred to as [superpowers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superpower "Superpower"), whereas less-powerful ones may be called [regional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power") or [middle powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_power "Middle power"). The international system of [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_\(international_relations\) "Power (international relations)") is called the *world order*, which is affected by the [balance of power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balance_of_power_\(international_relations\) "Balance of power (international relations)") that defines the degree of [polarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarity_\(international_relations\) "Polarity (international relations)") in the system. [Emerging powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emerging_power "Emerging power") are potentially destabilizing to it, especially if they display [revanchism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revanchism "Revanchism") or [irredentism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irredentism "Irredentism").

Politics inside the limits of political systems, which in contemporary context correspond to national [borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Border "Border"), are referred to as [domestic politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domestic_politics "Domestic politics"). This includes most forms of [public policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policy "Public policy"), such as [social policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_policy "Social policy"), [economic policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_policy "Economic policy"), or [law enforcement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_enforcement "Law enforcement"), which are executed by the state [bureaucracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureaucracy "Bureaucracy").

### Mesopolitics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Council_room_of_the_Province_of_Chieti_-_Meeting_of_the_Brothers_of_Italy.jpg/250px-Council_room_of_the_Province_of_Chieti_-_Meeting_of_the_Brothers_of_Italy.jpg)

Local political event organized by Brothers of Italy in the council room of the Province of Chieti in 2025

Mesopolitics describes the politics of intermediary structures within a political system, such as [national political parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_party "Political party") or [movements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_movement "Political movement").[^86]

A political party is a [political organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_organization "Political organization") that typically seeks to attain and maintain political power within [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government"), usually by participating in [political campaigns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_campaign "Political campaign"), educational outreach, or [protest actions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protest_action "Protest action"). Parties often espouse an expressed [ideology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideology "Ideology") or vision, bolstered by a written [platform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Party_platform "Party platform") with specific goals, forming a [coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalition "Coalition") among disparate interests.[^87]

Political parties within a particular political system together form the [party system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Party_system "Party system"), which can be either [multiparty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-party_system "Multi-party system"), [two-party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-party_system "Two-party system"), [dominant-party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominant-party_system "Dominant-party system"), or [one-party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-party_state "One-party state"), depending on the level of [pluralism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluralism_\(political_theory\) "Pluralism (political theory)"). This is affected by characteristics of the political system, including its [electoral system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_system "Electoral system"). According to [Duverger's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duverger%27s_law "Duverger's law"), [first-past-the-post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting "First-past-the-post voting") systems are likely to lead to two-party systems, while [proportional representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportional_representation "Proportional representation") systems are more likely to create a multiparty system.

### Micropolitics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/%28Pajero_Bishop_Scandal%29_Filipinos_protest_against_the_Bishops_that_allegedly_accepted_money_and_luxury_cars_from_former_president_Macapagal-Arroyo_in_exchange_for_support.jpg/250px-thumbnail.jpg)

Filipino protest for a local political controversy involving the Pajero bishop in 2011

Micropolitics describes the actions of individual actors within the political system.[^86] This is often described as [political participation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_participation "Political participation").[^88]

## Political values

### Democracy

Democracy is a system of processing conflicts in which outcomes depend on what participants do, but no single force controls what occurs and its outcomes. The uncertainty of outcomes is inherent in democracy. Democracy makes all forces struggle repeatedly to realize their interests and devolves power from groups of people to sets of rules.[^89]

Among modern political theorists, there are three contending conceptions of democracy: *aggregative*, *[deliberative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deliberative_democracy "Deliberative democracy")*, and *[radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_democracy "Radical democracy")*.[^90]

#### Aggregation

The theory of *aggregative democracy* claims that the aim of the democratic processes is to solicit the preferences of citizens, and aggregate them together to determine what social policies the society should adopt. Therefore, proponents of this view hold that democratic participation should primarily focus on [voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voting "Voting"), where the policy with the most votes gets implemented.

Different variants of aggregative democracy exist. Under *minimalism*, democracy is a system of government in which citizens have given teams of political leaders the right to rule in periodic elections. According to this minimalist conception, citizens cannot and should not "rule" because, for example, on most issues, most of the time, they have no clear views or their views are not well-founded. [Joseph Schumpeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Schumpeter "Joseph Schumpeter") articulated this view most famously in his book *[Capitalism, Socialism, and Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalism,_Socialism_and_Democracy "Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy")*.[^91] Contemporary proponents of minimalism include [William H. Riker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_H._Riker "William H. Riker"), [Adam Przeworski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Przeworski "Adam Przeworski"), and [Richard Posner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Posner "Richard Posner").

According to the theory of *[direct democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracy "Direct democracy")*, on the other hand, citizens should vote directly, not through their representatives, on legislative proposals. Proponents of direct democracy offer varied reasons to support this view. Political activity can be valuable in itself, it socialises and educates citizens, and popular participation can check powerful elites. Most importantly, citizens do not rule themselves unless they directly decide laws and policies.

Governments will tend to produce laws and policies that are close to the views of the median voter—with half to their left and the other half to their right. This is not a desirable outcome as it represents the action of self-interested and somewhat unaccountable political elites competing for votes. [Anthony Downs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthony_Downs "Anthony Downs") suggests that ideological political parties are necessary to act as a mediating broker between individual and governments. Downs laid out this view in his 1957 book *[An Economic Theory of Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/An_Economic_Theory_of_Democracy "An Economic Theory of Democracy")*.[^92]

#### Polyarchy

[Robert A. Dahl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_A._Dahl "Robert A. Dahl") argues that the fundamental democratic principle is that, when it comes to binding collective decisions, each person in a political community is entitled to have his/her interests be given equal consideration (not necessarily that all people are equally satisfied by the collective decision). He uses the term [polyarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyarchy "Polyarchy") to refer to societies in which there exists a certain set of institutions and procedures which are perceived as leading to such democracy. First and foremost among these institutions is the regular occurrence of free and open [elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elections "Elections") which are used to select representatives who then manage all or most of the public policy of the society. However, these polyarchic procedures may not create a full democracy if, for example, poverty prevents political participation.[^93] Similarly, [Ronald Dworkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Dworkin "Ronald Dworkin") argues that "democracy is a substantive, not a merely procedural, ideal".[^94]

#### Deliberation

[Deliberative democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deliberative_democracy "Deliberative democracy") is based on the notion that democracy is government by [deliberation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deliberation "Deliberation"). Unlike aggregative democracy, deliberative democracy holds that, for a democratic decision to be legitimate, it must be preceded by authentic deliberation, not merely the aggregation of preferences that occurs in voting. *Authentic deliberation* is deliberation among decision-makers that is free from distortions of unequal political power, such as power a decision-maker obtained through economic wealth or the support of interest groups.[^95] [^96] [^97] If the decision-makers cannot reach [consensus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consensus_decision-making "Consensus decision-making") after authentically deliberating on a proposal, then they vote on the proposal using a form of majority rule.

### Equality

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Political_Compass_standard_model.svg/250px-Political_Compass_standard_model.svg.png)

Two-axis political compass chart with a horizontal socio-economic axis and a vertical socio-cultural axis and ideologically representative political colours, an example for a frequently used model of the political spectrum 98 99 100 101 102 103 104 105

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/3-axis-model-of-political-ideologies-with-both-moderate-and-radical-versions-and-policies-goals.png/250px-3-axis-model-of-political-ideologies-with-both-moderate-and-radical-versions-and-policies-goals.png)

Three axis model of political ideologies with both moderate and radical versions and the goals of their policies

Equality is a state of affairs in which all people within a specific [society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society "Society") or isolated group have the same [social status](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_status "Social status"), especially [socioeconomic status](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socioeconomic_status "Socioeconomic status"), including protection of [human rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights "Human rights") and [dignity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dignity "Dignity"), as well as access to certain [social goods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_goods "Social goods") and [social services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_services "Social services"). Furthermore, it may also include [health equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_equality "Health equality"), [economic equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_\(economics\) "Equality (economics)") and other [social securities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_security "Social security"). Social equality requires the absence of legally enforced [social class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_class "Social class") or [caste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caste "Caste") boundaries and the absence of [discrimination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrimination "Discrimination") based on by an inalienable aspect of a person's identity. To this end, there must be [equal justice under law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equal_justice_under_law "Equal justice under law"), and [equal opportunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equal_opportunity "Equal opportunity") regardless of, sex, gender, ethnicity, age, sexual orientation, origin, [caste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caste "Caste") or class, income or property, language, [religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion "Religion"), convictions, opinions, health or disability.

#### Left–right spectrum

A common way of understanding politics is through the [left–right political spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left%E2%80%93right_political_spectrum "Left–right political spectrum"), which ranges from [left-wing politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-wing_politics "Left-wing politics") via [centrism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrism "Centrism") to [right-wing politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-wing_politics "Right-wing politics"). This classification is comparatively recent and dates from the [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution"), when those members of the [National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly "National Assembly") who supported the [republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic "Republic"), the common people and a [secular society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secular_society "Secular society") sat on the left and supporters of the [monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy "Monarchy"), [aristocratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristocracy_\(class\) "Aristocracy (class)") privilege and the Church sat on the right.[^106]

Today, the left is generally [progressivist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Progressivism "Progressivism"), seeking social [progress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Progress "Progress") in [society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society "Society"). The more extreme elements of the left, named the [far-left](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far-left_politics "Far-left politics"), tend to support [revolutionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolution "Revolution") means for achieving this. This includes ideologies such as [Communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communism "Communism") and [Marxism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism "Marxism"). The [center-left](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre-left_politics "Centre-left politics"), on the other hand, advocates for more [reformist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reformism "Reformism") approaches, for example that of [social democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_democracy "Social democracy").

In contrast, the right is generally motivated by [conservatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservatism "Conservatism"), which seeks to conserve what it sees as the important elements of society such as law and order, limited government and preserving individual freedoms. The [far-right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far-right_politics "Far-right politics") goes beyond this, and often represents a [reactionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactionary "Reactionary") turn against progress, seeking to undo it. Examples of such ideologies have included [Fascism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fascism "Fascism") and [Nazism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazism "Nazism"). The [center-right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre-right_politics "Centre-right politics") may be less clear-cut and more mixed in this regard, with [neoconservatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoconservatism "Neoconservatism") supporting the spread of [free markets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_market "Free market") and [capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalism "Capitalism"), and [one-nation conservatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-nation_conservatism "One-nation conservatism") more open to social welfare programs.

According to [Norberto Bobbio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norberto_Bobbio "Norberto Bobbio"), one of the major exponents of this distinction, the left believes in attempting to eradicate social inequality—believing it to be unethical or unnatural,[^107] while the right regards most social inequality as the result of ineradicable natural inequalities, and sees attempts to enforce social equality as utopian or authoritarian.[^108] Some ideologies, notably [Christian Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Democracy "Christian Democracy"), claim to combine left and right-wing politics; according to Geoffrey K. Roberts and Patricia Hogwood, "In terms of ideology, Christian Democracy has incorporated many of the views held by liberals, conservatives and socialists within a wider framework of moral and Christian principles." [^109] Movements which claim or formerly claimed to be above the left-right divide include Fascist [Terza Posizione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terza_Posizione "Terza Posizione") economic politics in Italy and [Peronism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peronism "Peronism") in Argentina.[^110] [^111]

### Freedom

Political freedom (also known as **political liberty** or **autonomy**) is a central [concept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concept "Concept") in political thought and one of the most important features of [democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") societies. [Negative liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_liberty "Negative liberty") has been described as freedom from oppression or coercion and unreasonable external constraints on action, often enacted through [civil and political rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights "Civil and political rights"), while [positive liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_liberty "Positive liberty") is the absence of disabling conditions for an individual and the fulfillment of enabling conditions, e.g. economic compulsion, in a society. This [capability approach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capability_approach "Capability approach") to freedom requires [economic, social and cultural rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic,_social_and_cultural_rights "Economic, social and cultural rights") in order to be realized.

#### Authoritarianism and libertarianism

[Authoritarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") and [libertarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_libertarianism "Civil libertarianism") disagree the amount of individual [freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_\(political\) "Freedom (political)") each person possesses in that society relative to the state. One author describes authoritarian political systems as those where "individual [rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rights "Rights") and goals are subjugated to group goals, expectations and conformities",[^112] while libertarians generally oppose the [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state "Sovereign state") and hold the [individual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individual "Individual") as sovereign. In their purest form, libertarians are [anarchists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchism "Anarchism"),[^113] who argue for the total abolition of the state, of [political parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_parties "Political parties") and of [other political entities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_organisation "Political organisation"), while the purest authoritarians are, by definition, [totalitarians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarianism "Totalitarianism") who support state control over all aspects of society.[^114]

For instance, [classical liberalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_liberalism "Classical liberalism") (also known as *[laissez-faire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laissez-faire "Laissez-faire") liberalism*) [^115] is a doctrine stressing individual freedom and [limited government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_government "Limited government"). This includes the importance of human rationality, individual [property rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_rights "Property rights"), [free markets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_market "Free market"), [natural rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_rights "Natural rights"), the protection of [civil liberties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_liberties "Civil liberties"), constitutional limitation of government, and individual freedom from restraint as exemplified in the writings of [John Locke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Locke "John Locke"), [Adam Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Smith "Adam Smith"), [David Hume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hume "David Hume"), [David Ricardo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Ricardo "David Ricardo"), [Voltaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaire "Voltaire"), [Montesquieu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montesquieu "Montesquieu") and others. According to the libertarian [Institute for Humane Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_for_Humane_Studies "Institute for Humane Studies"), "the libertarian, or 'classical liberal', perspective is that individual well-being, prosperity, and social harmony are fostered by 'as much liberty as possible' and 'as little government as necessary'." [^116] For anarchist political philosopher [L. Susan Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L._Susan_Brown "L. Susan Brown") (1993), "liberalism and [anarchism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchism "Anarchism") are two political philosophies that are fundamentally concerned with individual [freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_will "Freedom of will") yet differ from one another in very distinct ways. Anarchism shares with liberalism a radical commitment to individual freedom while rejecting liberalism's competitive property relations." [^117]
