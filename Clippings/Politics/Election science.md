**Election science** is a field that deals with the conduct and administration of [elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election "Election"). It is distinct from the study of [public opinion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_opinion "Public opinion") and election forecasting (which fall under [political science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science") and [psephology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psephology "Psephology")). Election science combines the theoretical study of [social choice theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_choice_theory "Social choice theory") (a branch of [math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Math "Math") and [welfare economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welfare_economics "Welfare economics")) with empirical research dealing with the administration of elections (a branch of [political science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science")).

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Butterfly_Ballot%2C_Florida_2000_%28large%29.jpg/330px-Butterfly_Ballot%2C_Florida_2000_%28large%29.jpg)

The "butterfly ballot" used in Palm Beach County was suspected of causing Al Gore 's supporters to accidentally vote for Pat Buchanan

The study of election science can be traced back to early scientific studies of electoral systems and particularly the development of the field of [social choice theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_choice_theory "Social choice theory"), including the [Marquis de Condorcet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marquis_de_Condorcet "Marquis de Condorcet") 's analysis of [electoral systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_system "Electoral system") in the 18th century. The field came into being following the [2000 United States presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2000_United_States_presidential_election "2000 United States presidential election"),[^1] where several administrative [^2] and technical failures [^3] may have affected the outcome of the election. Examples of subjects where election science methods are applied include [gerrymandering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerrymandering "Gerrymandering"), [electoral fraud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_fraud "Electoral fraud"), [suffrage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suffrage "Suffrage"), and [voter registration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voter_registration_in_the_United_States "Voter registration in the United States").

The Election Science, Reform, and Administration (ESRA) conference is an [academic conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_conference "Academic conference") [^4] dedicated to the study of election science. The [Southern Political Science Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Political_Science_Association "Southern Political Science Association") has a conference within a conference (CwC) dedicated to election science.[^5] In addition, multiple universities now offer a [bachelor of science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science "Bachelor of Science") in political science for an [data science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_science "Data science") track.[^6] [^7]
