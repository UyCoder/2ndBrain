A **semi-presidential system** or **dual executive system** is a system in which a [president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_\(government_title\) "President (government title)") exists alongside a [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_minister "Prime minister") and a [cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_\(government\) "Cabinet (government)"), with the prime minister and cabinet being responsible to the legislature of the [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)"). It differs from a [parliamentary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system") in that it has an [executive president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_president "Executive president") independent of the [legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature "Legislature"); and from the [presidential system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_system "Presidential system") in that the [cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_\(government\) "Cabinet (government)"), although named by the president, is responsible to the legislature, which may force the cabinet to resign through a [motion of no confidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_of_no_confidence "Motion of no confidence").[^21] [^22] [^23] [^24]

While the [Weimar Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_Republic "Weimar Republic") (1919–1933) and [Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland "Finland") (from 1919 to 2000) exemplified early semi-presidential systems, the term "semi-presidential" was first introduced in 1959, in an article by the journalist [Hubert Beuve-Méry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubert_Beuve-M%C3%A9ry "Hubert Beuve-Méry"),[^25] and popularized by a 1978 work written by the political scientist [Maurice Duverger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurice_Duverger "Maurice Duverger").[^26] Both men intended to describe the [French Fifth Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Fifth_Republic "French Fifth Republic") (established in 1958).[^21] [^22] [^23] [^24] The semi-presidential system is distinct from [semi-parliamentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-parliamentary_system "Semi-parliamentary system") systems, where instead of the executive being dual, the legislature is split into two parts, one having the authority to dismiss the cabinet through a motion of no confidence. It is also distinct from the (directly elected) [prime-ministerial system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime-ministerial_system "Prime-ministerial system") that Duverger originally proposed for France.

## Definition

[Maurice Duverger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurice_Duverger "Maurice Duverger") 's original definition of semi-presidentialism stated that the president had to be elected, possess significant power, and serve for a fixed term.[^27]

Others, such as [Robert Elgie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Elgie_\(academic\) "Robert Elgie (academic)") propose an alternate definition which simply requires that "a popularly-elected fixed-term president exists alongside a prime minister and cabinet who are responsible to parliament." [^28]

## Subtypes

There are two distinct subtypes of semi-presidentialism: premier-presidentialism and president-parliamentarism.

Under the **premier-presidential** system, the prime minister and cabinet are exclusively accountable to parliament. The president may choose the prime minister and cabinet, but only the parliament may approve them and remove them from office with a *vote of no confidence*. This system is much closer to pure parliamentarism. This subtype is used in: [Burkina Faso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burkina_Faso "Burkina Faso"), [Cape Verde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_Verde "Cape Verde"),[^29] [East Timor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Timor "East Timor"),[^29] [^30] [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France"), [Lithuania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithuania "Lithuania"), [Madagascar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madagascar "Madagascar"), [Mali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mali "Mali"), [Mongolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolia "Mongolia"), [Niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger "Niger"), [Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgia_\(country\) "Georgia (country)"),[^1] [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portugal "Portugal"), [Romania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romania "Romania"), [São Tomé and Príncipe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe "São Tomé and Príncipe"),[^29] [Sri Lanka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sri_Lanka "Sri Lanka"), [Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey"),[^2] and [Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ukraine "Ukraine").[^3]

Under the **president-parliamentary** system, the prime minister and cabinet are dually accountable to the president and the parliament. The president chooses the prime minister and the cabinet but must have the support of a parliamentary majority for his choice. To remove a prime minister, or the whole cabinet, from power, the president can either dismiss them, or the parliament can remove them through a *vote of no confidence*. This form of semi-presidentialism is much closer to pure presidentialism. It is used in: [Azerbaijan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azerbaijan "Azerbaijan"), [Guinea-Bissau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guinea-Bissau "Guinea-Bissau"),[^29] [Kazakhstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan "Kazakhstan"), [Mozambique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mozambique "Mozambique"), [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia"), and [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan"). It was also used in Ukraine,[^4] Georgia,[^5] South Korea under the [Fourth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_Republic_of_Korea "Fourth Republic of Korea") and [Fifth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fifth_Republic_of_Korea "Fifth Republic of Korea") republics, and in [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany") during the [Weimar Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_Republic "Weimar Republic").[^31] [^32]

## Cohabitation

In a semi-presidential system, the president and the prime minister may sometimes be from different political parties. This is called " [cohabitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohabitation_\(government\) "Cohabitation (government)") ", a term which originated in France after the situation first arose in the 1980s. Cohabitation can create either an effective system of [checks and balances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_powers "Separation of powers"), or a period of bitter and tense stonewalling, depending on the attitudes of the two leaders, the ideologies of themselves and their parties, and the demands of their supporters.[^33]

## Division of powers

The distribution of power between the president and the prime minister can vary greatly between countries.

In [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France"), for example, in the case of cohabitation, the president oversees [foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy "Foreign policy") and [defence policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defence_policy "Defence policy") (these are generally called *les prérogatives présidentielles*, presidential prerogatives) and the prime minister is in charge of [domestic policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domestic_policy "Domestic policy") and [economic policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_policy "Economic policy").[^34] In this case, the division of responsibilities between the [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_minister "Prime minister") and the [president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_\(government_title\) "President (government title)") is not explicitly stated in the constitution, but has evolved as a [political convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_convention_\(political_custom\) "Constitutional convention (political custom)") based on the constitutional principle that the prime minister is appointed (with the subsequent approval of a parliament majority) and dismissed by the president.[^35] On the other hand, whenever the president and the prime minister represent the same political party, which leads the cabinet, they tend to exercise *de facto* control over all fields of policy via the prime minister. However, it is up to the president to decide how much autonomy is left to the prime minister.

In most cases, cohabitation results from a system in which the two executives are not elected at the same time or for the same term. For example, in 1981, France elected both a [Socialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_Party_\(France\) "Socialist Party (France)") president and legislature, which yielded a Socialist premier. But while the president's term of office was for seven years, the [National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_of_France "National Assembly of France") only served for five. When, in the [1986 legislative election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1986_French_legislative_election "1986 French legislative election"), the French people elected a right-of-centre assembly, Socialist president [François Mitterrand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mitterrand "François Mitterrand") was forced into cohabitation with right-wing premier [Jacques Chirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Chirac "Jacques Chirac").[^33]

However, in 2000, amendments to the [French constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_constitution "French constitution") reduced the length of the French president's term to five years. This has significantly lowered the chances of cohabitation occurring, as parliamentary and presidential elections may now be conducted within a shorter span of each other.

## Advantages and disadvantages

The incorporation of elements from both presidential and parliamentary systems can bring certain advantageous elements; however, it also creates disadvantages, often related to the confusion produced by mixed authority patterns.[^36] [^37] It can be argued that a semi-presidential system is more likely to engage in [democratic backsliding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_backsliding "Democratic backsliding") and [power struggles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_struggle "Power struggle"),[^38] especially ones with a president-parliamentary system.[^39] [^40]

**Advantages**

- Parliament can remove an unpopular prime minister, therefore maintaining stability throughout the president's [fixed term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandate_\(politics\) "Mandate (politics)").
- In most semi-presidential systems, important segments of bureaucracy are taken away from the president, creating additional checks and balances where the running of the day-to-day government and its issues are separate from the head of state, and as such, its issues tend to be looked at on their own merits, with their ebbs and flows and not necessarily tied to who the head of state is.
- Having a separate head of government who needs to command the confidence of the parliament is seen as being more in tune to the political and economic development of the country. Because the head of government is elected from the parliament, there is little potential for political gridlock to occur, since the parliament has the power to remove the head of government if needed.

**Disadvantages**

- The system provides [cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_powers "Separation of powers") for the president, as unpopular policies could be blamed on the prime minister, who runs the day-to-day operations of the government.
- It creates a sense of confusion towards [accountability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accountability "Accountability"), as there is no relatively clear sense of who is responsible for policy successes and failures.
- It creates both confusion and inefficiency in the [legislative process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature "Legislature"), since the capacity of [votes of confidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vote_of_no_confidence "Vote of no confidence") makes the prime minister respond to the parliament.

The concept of a semi-presidential form of government, as used here, is defined only by the content of the constitution. A political regime is considered semi-presidential if the constitution which established it combines three elements: (1) the president of the republic is elected by universal suffrage, (2) the president possesses quite considerable powers; (3) the president has opposite him a prime minister and ministers who possess executive and governmental power and can stay in office only if the parliament does not show its opposition to this team of prime minister and ministers.
