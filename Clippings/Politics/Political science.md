**Political science** (sometimes abbreviated as **poli sci**) is a field which aims to study the scientific aspect of [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics"). It deals with systems of [governance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governance "Governance") and [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_\(social_and_political\) "Power (social and political)"), and the analysis of political activities, [political thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_philosophy "Political philosophy"), [political behavior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_behavior "Political behavior"), and associated [constitutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution "Constitution") and [laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law "Law"). Specialists in the field are **political scientists**. Unlike [political philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_philosophy "Political philosophy"), which is primarily [normative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normativity "Normativity") and evaluates politics through [value judgments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_judgments "Value judgments"), political science emphasizes descriptive and explanatory means of articulating *[what is](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_facto "De facto")* (the *de facto*, *[status quo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Status_quo "Status quo")*, *[lex non scripta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lex_non_scripta "Lex non scripta")*, etc.) and favors [empirical evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_evidence "Empirical evidence") over ethical judgements.[^1] Political science emerged as a distinct academic discipline in the late 19th century, influenced by the rise of [empiricism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empiricism "Empiricism") and [positivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positivism "Positivism"). It emerged from history, philosophy, and law amid university professionalization and the founding of bodies such as the [American Political Science Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Political_Science_Association "American Political Science Association") in 1903. The [behavioral revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Behavioralism "Behavioralism") of the 1950s played a significant role in shifting emphasis to scientifically explaining human behavior from an unbiased and neutral point of view using [quantitative research methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_research "Quantitative research") such as [surveys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survey_sampling "Survey sampling"), [historical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historiography "Historiography"), and empirical testing.

## History

### Origin

As a social science, contemporary political science started to take shape in the latter half of the 19th century and began to separate itself from political [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy") and history.[^2] Into the late 19th century, it was still uncommon for political science to be considered a distinct field from history.[^2] The term "political science" was not always distinguished from [political philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_philosophy "Political philosophy"), and the modern discipline has a clear set of antecedents including moral philosophy, political economy, [political theology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_theology "Political theology"), history, and other fields concerned with normative determinations of what ought to be and with deducing the characteristics and functions of the ideal state.[^3]

Generally, while classical [political philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_philosophy "Political philosophy") is primarily defined by a concern for [Hellenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece") and [Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment") thought,[^4] political scientists are also marked by a great concern for " [modernity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernity "Modernity") " and the contemporary [nation state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation_state "Nation state"), along with the study of classical thought, and as such share more terminology with [sociologists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociologists "Sociologists") (e.g., [structure and agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_and_agency "Structure and agency")).[^5]

The advent of political science as a university discipline was marked by the creation of university departments and chairs with the title of political science arising in the late 19th century. The designation "political scientist" is commonly used to denote someone with a doctorate or master's degree in the field.[^6] Integrating political studies of the past into a unified discipline is ongoing, and the history of political science has provided a rich field for the growth of both [normative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_\(sociology\) "Norm (sociology)") and [positive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_\(social_sciences\) "Positive (social sciences)") political science, with each part of the discipline sharing some historical predecessors. The [American Political Science Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Political_Science_Association "American Political Science Association") and the *[American Political Science Review](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Political_Science_Review "American Political Science Review")* were founded in 1903 and 1906, respectively, to distinguish the study of [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics") from economics and other social phenomena. APSA membership rose from 204 in 1904 to 1,462 in 1915.[^2] APSA members played a key role in setting up political science departments that were distinct from history, philosophy, law, sociology, and economics.[^2]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Map_of_unitary_and_federal_states.svg/330px-Map_of_unitary_and_federal_states.svg.png)

A world map distinguishing countries of the world as federations (green) from unitary states (blue), a work of political science

The journal *[Political Science Quarterly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_Science_Quarterly "Political Science Quarterly")* was established in 1886 by the Academy of Political Science. In the inaugural issue of *Political Science Quarterly*, [Munroe Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munroe_Smith "Munroe Smith") defined political science as "the science of the state. Taken in this sense, it includes the organization and functions of the state, and the relation of states one to another." [^7]

As part of a United Nations Educational, Scientific, and Cultural Organization (UNESCO) initiative to promote political science in the late 1940s, the International Political Science Association was founded in 1949, along with national associations in France in 1949, Britain in 1950, and West Germany in 1951.[^2]

Founded in 1903, the American Political Science Association (APSA) is the leading professional organization for the study of political science and serves more than 11,000 members in more than 100 countries.[^8]

### Behavioral revolution and new institutionalism

In the 1950s and 1960s, a [behavioral revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Behavioralism "Behavioralism") stressing the systematic, rigorously scientific study of individual and group behavior swept the discipline. A focus on studying political behavior, rather than institutions or interpretation of legal texts, characterized early behavioral political science, including work by [Robert Dahl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Dahl "Robert Dahl"), [Philip Converse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Converse "Philip Converse"), and in the collaboration between sociologist [Paul Lazarsfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Lazarsfeld "Paul Lazarsfeld") and public opinion scholar [Bernard Berelson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Berelson "Bernard Berelson").

The late 1960s and early 1970s witnessed a takeoff in the use of deductive, [game-theoretic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_theory "Game theory") formal modelling techniques aimed at generating a more analytical corpus of knowledge in the discipline. This period saw a surge of research that borrowed theory and methods from economics to study political institutions, such as the United States Congress, as well as political behavior, such as voting. [William H. Riker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_H._Riker "William H. Riker") and his colleagues and students at the [University of Rochester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Rochester "University of Rochester") were the main proponents of this shift.

Despite considerable research progress in the discipline based on all types of scholarship discussed above, scholars have noted that progress toward systematic theory has been modest and uneven.[^9]

### 21st century

In 2000, the [Perestroika Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perestroika_Movement "Perestroika Movement") in political science was introduced as a reaction against what its supporters called the "mathematicization" of political science. Those who identified with the movement argued for a plurality of methodologies and approaches in political science and for more relevance of the discipline to those outside of it.[^10]

Some [evolutionary psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology "Evolutionary psychology") theories argue that humans have evolved a highly developed set of psychological mechanisms for dealing with politics. However, these mechanisms evolved to deal with the small-group politics that characterized the ancestral environment, not the much larger political structures of today's world. This is argued to explain many important features and systematic [cognitive biases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_bias "Cognitive bias") in current politics.[^11]

**Main sub-disciplines of political science**, from top left to right: **1.** [Domestic politics and government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domestic_policy "Domestic policy"); **2.** [Comparative politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparative_politics "Comparative politics"); **3.** [International relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_relations "International relations"); **4.** [Political theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_theory "Political theory"); **5.** [Political economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_economy "Political economy"); **6.** [Political methodology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_methodology "Political methodology"); **7.** [Public administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_administration "Public administration"); **8.** [Public policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policy "Public policy")

Political science is a social study concerning the allocation and transfer of [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_\(social_and_political\) "Power (social and political)") in [decision making](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decision_making "Decision making"), the roles and systems of governance including [governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governments "Governments") and [international organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_organizations "International organizations"), political behaviour, and [public policies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policies "Public policies"). It measures the success of [governance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governance "Governance") and specific policies by examining many factors, including [stability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_stability "Economic stability"), [justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justice "Justice"), [material wealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material_wealth "Material wealth"), [peace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peace "Peace"), and [public health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_health "Public health"). Some political scientists seek to advance [positive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_\(social_sciences\) "Positive (social sciences)") theses (which attempt to describe how things are, as opposed to how they should be) by analysing politics; others advance [normative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_\(sociology\) "Norm (sociology)") theses, such as by making specific policy recommendations. The study of politics and policies can be closely connected—for example, through comparative analyses of which political institutions tend to produce certain policies.[^12] Political science provides analysis and predictions about political and governmental issues.[^13] Political scientists examine the processes, systems, and political dynamics of countries and regions of the world, often to raise public awareness or to influence specific governments.[^13]

Political scientists may provide the frameworks from which journalists, special interest groups, politicians, and the [electorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constituency "Constituency") analyze issues. According to Chaturvedy,

> Political scientists may serve as advisers to specific politicians, or even run for office as politicians themselves. Political scientists can be found working in governments, in political parties, or as civil servants. They may be involved with [non-governmental organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-governmental_organizations "Non-governmental organizations") (NGOs) or political movements. In a variety of capacities, people educated and trained in political science can add value and expertise to [corporations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corporation "Corporation"). Private enterprises such as [think tanks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Think_tank "Think tank"), research institutes, polling and [public relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_relations "Public relations") firms often employ political scientists.[^14]

### Country-specific studies

Political scientists may study political phenomena within one specific country. For example, they may study the [politics of the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_the_United_States "Politics of the United States") [^15] or the [politics of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_China "Politics of China").[^16]

Political scientists look at a variety of data, including constitutions, [elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elections "Elections"), [public opinion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_opinion "Public opinion"), and [public policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policy "Public policy"), [foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy "Foreign policy"), legislatures, and judiciaries. Political scientists will often focus on the politics of their own country; for example, a political scientist from Indonesia may become an expert in the politics of Indonesia.[^17]

### Anticipating crises

The theory of political transitions,[^18] and the methods of analyzing and anticipating [^19] [crises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crisis "Crisis"),[^20] form an important part of political science. Several general indicators of crises and methods for anticipating critical transitions were proposed.[^21] Among them, one statistical indicator of crisis, a simultaneous increase of [variance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variance "Variance") and [correlations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlations "Correlations") in large groups, was proposed for crisis anticipation and may be successfully used in various areas.[^22] Its applicability for early diagnosis of [political crises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_crisis "Political crisis") was demonstrated by the analysis of the prolonged stress period preceding the 2014 Ukrainian [economic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_crisis "Economic crisis") and political crisis. There was a simultaneous increase in the total correlation between the 19 major public fears in the Ukrainian society (by about 64%) and in their statistical dispersion (by 29%) during the pre-crisis years.[^23] A feature shared by certain major revolutions is that they were not predicted. The theory of the apparent inevitability of crises and revolutions was also developed.[^24]

The study of major crises, both political and external, is not limited to attempts to predict regime transitions or major changes in political institutions. Political scientists also study how governments [handle unexpected disasters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disaster_management "Disaster management"), and how voters in democracies react to their governments' preparations for and responses to crises.[^25]

## Research methods

### Research methods

Political science is [methodologically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methodology "Methodology") diverse and appropriates many methods originating in psychology, [social research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_research "Social research"), political philosophy, and many others, in addition to those that developed chiefly within the field of political science.

Political scientists approach the study of politics from a host of different ontological orientations and with a variety of different tools. Because political science is essentially a study of [human behavior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_behavior "Human behavior"), in all aspects of [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics"), observations in controlled environments are often challenging to reproduce or duplicate, though [experimental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experiment "Experiment") methods are increasingly common (see [experimental political science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimental_political_science "Experimental political science")).[^26] Citing this difficulty, former [American Political Science Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Political_Science_Association "American Political Science Association") President [Lawrence Lowell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawrence_Lowell "Lawrence Lowell") once said "We are limited by the impossibility of experiment. Politics is an observational, not an experimental science." [^19] Because of this, political scientists have historically observed political elites, institutions, and individual or group behaviour to identify patterns, draw generalizations, and build theories of politics.

Like all social sciences, political science faces the difficulty of observing human actors that can only be partially observed and who have the capacity for making conscious choices, unlike other subjects, such as non-human organisms in [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology "Biology"), minerals in [geoscience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoscience "Geoscience"), chemical elements in [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry"), stars in [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), or particles in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"). Despite its complexities, contemporary political science has advanced by adopting a variety of methods and theoretical approaches to understanding politics, and [methodological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methodology "Methodology") pluralism is a defining feature of the field.

Empirical political science methods include the use of field experiments,[^27] surveys and survey experiments,[^28] case studies,[^29] process tracing,[^30] [^31] historical and institutional analysis,[^32] ethnography,[^33] participant observation,[^34] and interview research.[^35]

Political scientists also use and develop theoretical tools such as game theory and agent-based models to study a wide range of political systems and situations.[^36] Other approaches include the study of equation-based models and opinion dynamics.[^37]

Political theorists approach theories of political phenomena with a wide range of positions and tools, including [feminist political theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_political_theory "Feminist political theory"), historical analysis associated with the [Cambridge School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge_School_\(intellectual_history\) "Cambridge School (intellectual history)"), and [Straussian approaches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leo_Strauss#Straussianism "Leo Strauss").

Political science may overlap with topics of study that are the traditional focuses of other social sciences—for example, when sociological [norms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_norm "Social norm") or psychological [biases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_bias "Cognitive bias") are connected to political phenomena. In these cases, political science may either inherit its methods of study or develop a contrasting approach.[^38] For example, [Lisa Wedeen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisa_Wedeen "Lisa Wedeen") has argued that political science's approach to the idea of culture, originating with [Gabriel Almond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabriel_Almond "Gabriel Almond") and [Sidney Verba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidney_Verba "Sidney Verba") and exemplified by authors like [Samuel P. Huntington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_P._Huntington "Samuel P. Huntington"), could benefit from aligning more closely with the study of culture in anthropology.[^38] In turn, methodologies that are developed within political science may influence how researchers in other fields, like public health, conceive of and approach political processes and policies.[^39]

The most common piece of academic writing in generalist political sciences is the research paper, which investigates an original [research question](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Research_question "Research question").[^40] [^41]

## Education

Political science, possibly like the social sciences as a whole, can be described "as a discipline which lives on the fault line between the 'two cultures' in the academy, the [sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sciences "Sciences") and the [humanities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanities "Humanities")." [^42] Thus, in most American colleges, especially [liberal arts colleges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_arts_college "Liberal arts college"), it would be located within the [school or college of arts and sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/College_of_Arts_and_Sciences "College of Arts and Sciences"). If no separate college of arts and sciences exists, or if the college or university prefers that it be in a separate constituent college or academic department, then political science may be a separate department housed as part of a division or school of humanities or [liberal arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_Arts "Liberal Arts").[^43] At some universities, especially [research universities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Research_university "Research university") and in particular those that have a strong cooperation between research, undergraduate, and graduate faculty with a stronger, more applied emphasis in public administration, political science would be taught by the university's [public policy school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policy_school "Public policy school").

Most United States [colleges and universities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher_education_in_the_United_States "Higher education in the United States") offer BA programs in political science. MA or MAT and PhD or EdD programs are common at larger universities. The term *political science* is more popular in post-1960s [North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America "North America") than elsewhere while universities predating the 1960s or those historically influenced by them would call the field of study *government*;[^44] other institutions, especially those outside the United States, see political science as part of a broader discipline of *political studies* or *politics* in general. While *political science* implies the use of the [scientific method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_method "Scientific method"), *political studies* implies a broader approach, although the naming of degree courses does not necessarily reflect their content. Separate, specialized, or, in some cases, professional degree programs in [international relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_relations "International relations"), [public policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policy "Public policy"), and [public administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_administration "Public administration") are common at both the undergraduate and postgraduate levels. However, most, but not all, undergraduate-level education in these sub-fields of political science is found in [academic concentrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_concentration "Academic concentration") within a political science [academic major](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_major "Academic major"). Master's-level programs in [public administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_administration "Public administration") are [professional degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Professional_degree "Professional degree") covering public policy and other applied subjects; they are often seen as more closely linked to politics than to other disciplines, which may be reflected in their being housed in that department.[^50]
