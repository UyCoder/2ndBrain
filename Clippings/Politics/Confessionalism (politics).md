**Confessionalism** is a [system of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government#Forms "Government") that is a *[de jure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_jure "De jure")* mix of [religion and politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_and_politics "Religion and politics"). It typically entails the distribution of political and institutional power proportionally among [confessional communities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confessional_community "Confessional community").

## Governmental structure

Some countries' political systems distribute power across the major religions in the country. This can be required by the [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution "Constitution") or through unwritten tradition.

The repartition of assembly seats on a confessional basis in the Middle East was invented by the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") (e.g. in the [Ottoman Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_the_Ottoman_Empire "Parliament of the Ottoman Empire")) and continued in several post-Ottoman countries with [reserved seats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reserved_political_positions "Reserved political positions") for non-Muslim minorities, namely Christian ([Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Jordan "Parliament of Jordan") and [Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Representatives_of_Iraq "Council of Representatives of Iraq")), or for all religious communities including Muslim subgroups and Christian churches ([Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Lebanon "Parliament of Lebanon")). A [similar system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_in_Iran#Reserved_Majlis_seats "Religion in Iran") prevails in Iran for the Armenian, Assyrian, Jewish and Zoroastrian minorities.

In the [politics of Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Iraq "Politics of Iraq"), following the [invasion of Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq "2003 invasion of Iraq") in 2003, the occupying administration introduced a system where power was shared between the three main [ethno-religious groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Iraq "Demographics of Iraq"): [Shia Muslim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shia_Islam_in_Iraq "Shia Islam in Iraq") Arabs, [Sunni Muslim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunni_Islam_in_Iraq "Sunni Islam in Iraq") Arabs and [Kurds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurds_in_Iraq "Kurds in Iraq"). The constitution of Iraq encouraged such power-sharing, due to the parliamentary system and the initial requirement for a super-majority to elect the president. Although not explicitly required in the [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Iraq "Constitution of Iraq"), political tradition has continued to date for the [president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Iraq "President of Iraq") to be a Kurd, the [speaker of Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speaker_of_the_Council_of_Representatives_\(Iraq\) "Speaker of the Council of Representatives (Iraq)") a Sunni Muslim Arab and the [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Iraq "Prime Minister of Iraq") a Shi'ite Muslim Arab.[^1]

In [Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanon "Lebanon"), the concept of confessionalism holds an important political meaning, since political power and government institutions are organized according to religious confessions (as it happened in Switzerland, Germany, the Netherlands and other countries before). For example, the [National Pact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Pact "National Pact") (an unwritten covenant) and later the [Taif Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taif_Agreement "Taif Agreement") provide for a [Maronite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Maronite_Christians "Lebanese Maronite Christians") Christian president, a [Sunni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Sunni_Muslims "Lebanese Sunni Muslims") Muslim prime minister, and a [Shia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanese_Shia_Muslims "Lebanese Shia Muslims") Muslim speaker of parliament.

Although the system was meant to be a temporary solution "until such time as the Chamber enacts new electoral laws on a non-confessional basis",[^2] more than 80 years later, it remains the system of government. All posts in government and seats in the [legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature "Legislature") are apportioned amongst different religious groups according to a political agreement, as the relative [demographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics "Demographics") weight of those groups is unknown.[^3] The constitution of 1926, amended after the [Taif Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taif_Agreement "Taif Agreement") of 1990 and the [Doha agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doha_Agreement_\(2008\) "Doha Agreement (2008)") of 2008 specified that there should be [54 Christian deputies and 54 Muslim deputies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Lebanon#Allocation_of_seats "Parliament of Lebanon"), even though in practice there are 64 deputies each.[^4] In addition, within those two groups, seats should be shared according to the demographic weight of each community.[^5]

The Lebanese constitution also guarantees segmental autonomy to 18 recognized communities in the country in domains such as education.[^6] Lebanon also presents other characteristics of confessionalism. Since 2005 Lebanese politics has been polarized around two trans-religious coalitions [^7] with the majority never able to govern alone. There is, however, another section of the constitution that addresses the development of outside parties not represented by popular support.

## Political parties

In some countries there are political parties whose main ideology is based on a religion, such as [Christian democratic parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Christian_democratic_parties "List of Christian democratic parties") and [Islamic political parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Islamic_political_parties "List of Islamic political parties").

In the [politics of the Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_the_Netherlands "Politics of the Netherlands") the term "confessionalism" refers to any political ideology based on religion. A traditional norm in society, extending to many facets of cultural life, termed [pillarisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pillarisation "Pillarisation"). Dutch parties usually labelled as confessionalist are the [Christian Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Union_\(Netherlands\) "Christian Union (Netherlands)") and the [Reformed Political Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reformed_Political_Party "Reformed Political Party"), both exclusively Protestant.

Political parties with religious ideology are sometimes banned on the grounds of promoting violence and hatred (e.g. [Vlaams Blok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vlaams_Blok "Vlaams Blok") and [Batasuna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batasuna "Batasuna")), altering the national character, or having outside support.**[^8]**

In some cases bans are written into the constitution. [Bulgaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulgaria "Bulgaria") ’s [Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Bulgaria "Constitution of Bulgaria") prohibits religious [political parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Bulgaria "List of political parties in Bulgaria").[^9] The [Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Turkey "Constitution of Turkey") of [Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey") states [the country is secular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularism_in_Turkey "Secularism in Turkey"), and the [constitutional court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Turkey "Constitutional Court of Turkey") ruled that the Islamist [Virtue Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtue_Party "Virtue Party") was unconstitutional. The [political parties in Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_parties_in_Portugal "Political parties in Portugal") are forbidden from using a name that has religious connotation, although the parties are still allowed to adopt a religious ideology, thus allowing the formation of [CDS – People's Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CDS_%E2%80%93_People%27s_Party "CDS – People's Party").[^10]

## Multiconfessionalism

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Bosanska_Krupa_Churches.JPG/250px-Bosanska_Krupa_Churches.JPG)

Catholic church, mosque and Serbian Orthodox church in Bosanska Krupa, Bosnia and Herzegovina.

**Multiconfessional** countries have a [power sharing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_sharing "Power sharing") arrangement between people of different faiths, usually three or more significant confessional groups within the same jurisdiction. Examples of modern countries deemed multiconfessional are [Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Lebanon "Politics of Lebanon") [^11] [^12] and [Bosnia and Herzegovina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Bosnia_and_Herzegovina "Politics of Bosnia and Herzegovina").[^13]

The " [National Pact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Pact "National Pact") " in [Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanon "Lebanon") is a formal agreement altering the [1926 Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Lebanon "Constitution of Lebanon"), which laid the foundation of Lebanon as a confessionalist state. Instead of a [minority wielding the most power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minoritarianism "Minoritarianism"), political power became more [representative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_democracy "Representative democracy").[^14]

The 3 dominant religions of [Bosnia and Herzegovina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina "Bosnia and Herzegovina") ([Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam"), [Eastern Orthodox Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church "Eastern Orthodox Church"), [Roman Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church "Roman Catholic Church")) are practiced by the three native ethnic groups of the country: [Bosniaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosniaks "Bosniaks"), [Bosnian Serbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serbs_of_Bosnia_and_Herzegovina "Serbs of Bosnia and Herzegovina") and [Bosnian Croats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Croats_of_Bosnia_and_Herzegovina "Croats of Bosnia and Herzegovina") respectively. Religious nationalism that emerged as part of the breakup of Yugoslavia would later cause the [Bosnian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosnian_War "Bosnian War").
