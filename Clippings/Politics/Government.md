A **government** is the system or group of people governing a [country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Country "Country") and its [administrative divisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Administrative_division "Administrative division") which is generally called as a [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)") and as such the term is a [metonym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metonymy "Metonymy") for it in governmental topics.

In the case of its broad associative definition, government normally consists of [legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature "Legislature"), [executive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_\(government\) "Executive (government)"), and [judiciary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judiciary "Judiciary"). Government is a means by which organizational [policies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Policies "Policies") are enforced, as well as a mechanism for determining policy. In many countries, the government has a kind of [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution "Constitution"), a statement of its governing principles and philosophy.

While all types of organizations have [governance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governance "Governance"), the term *government* is often used more specifically to refer to the approximately 200 [independent national governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states "List of sovereign states") and [subsidiary organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_agency "Government agency").

The main types of modern [political systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_system "Political system") recognized are [democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy"), [totalitarian regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarian_regime "Totalitarian regime"), and, sitting between these two, [authoritarian regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") with a variety of [hybrid regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybrid_regime "Hybrid regime").[^4] [^5] Modern classification systems also include [monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy "Monarchy") as a standalone entity or as a hybrid system of the main three.[^6] [^7] Historically prevalent forms of government include monarchy, [aristocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristocracy "Aristocracy"), [timocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timocracy "Timocracy"), [oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy "Oligarchy"), democracy, [theocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theocracy "Theocracy"), and [tyranny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrant "Tyrant"). These forms are not always mutually exclusive, and [mixed governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_government "Mixed government") are common. The main aspect of any philosophy of government is how political power is obtained, with the two main forms being [electoral contest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election "Election") and [hereditary succession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_succession "Order of succession").

## Definitions and etymology

A government is the [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System "System") to [govern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governance "Governance") a [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)") or community. The *[Cambridge Dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge_Advanced_Learner%27s_Dictionary "Cambridge Advanced Learner's Dictionary")* defines government as, "the system used for controlling a country, city, or group of people", or "an organization that officially manages and controls a country or region, creating laws, collecting taxes, providing public services".[^8] While all types of organizations have governance, the word *government* is often used more specifically to refer to the approximately 200 [independent national governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states "List of sovereign states") on Earth, as well as their subsidiary organizations, such as [state and provincial governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_government "State government") as well as [local governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_government "Local government").[^9]

The word *government* derives from the Greek verb κυβερνάω \[*kubernáo*\] meaning *to steer* with a [gubernaculum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gubernaculum_\(classical\) "Gubernaculum (classical)") (rudder), the metaphorical sense being attested in the literature of [classical antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_antiquity "Classical antiquity"), including [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") 's [Ship of State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ship_of_State "Ship of State").[^10] In [British English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_English "British English"), "government" sometimes refers to what is also known as a " [ministry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_\(collective_executive\) "Ministry (collective executive)") " or an " [administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Administration_\(government\) "Administration (government)") ", i.e., the policies and government officials of a particular executive or governing [coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalition_government "Coalition government"). Finally, *government* is also sometimes used in English as a [synonym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synonym "Synonym") for rule or governance.[^11]

In other languages, [cognates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognate "Cognate") may have a narrower scope, such as the [government of Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Portugal "Government of Portugal"), which is more similar to the concept of ["administration"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Administration_\(government\) "Administration (government)").

## History

### Earliest governments

The moment and place that the phenomenon of human government developed is lost in time; however, history does record the formations of early governments. About 5,000 years ago, the first small city-states appeared.[^12] By the third to second millenniums BC, some of these had developed into larger governed areas: [Sumer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumer "Sumer"), [ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt"), the [Indus Valley civilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilization "Indus Valley civilization"), and the [Yellow River civilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Neolithic_cultures_of_China "List of Neolithic cultures of China").[^13]

One reason that explains the emergence of governments includes agriculture. Since the [Neolithic Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution "Neolithic Revolution"), agriculture has been an efficient method to create food surplus. This enabled people to specialize in non-agricultural activities. Some of them included being able to rule over others as an external authority. Others included social experimentation with diverse governance models. Both these activities formed the basis of governments.[^14] These governments gradually became more complex as agriculture supported larger and denser populations, creating new [interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture "Culture") and [social pressures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_issue "Social issue") that the government needed to control. [David Christian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Christian_\(historian\) "David Christian (historian)") explains

> As farming populations gathered in larger and denser communities, interactions between different groups increased and the social pressure rose until, in a striking parallel with star formation, new structures suddenly appeared, together with a new level of complexity. Like stars, cities and states reorganize and energize the smaller objects within their gravitational field.[^12]

Another explanation includes the need to properly manage infrastructure projects such as water infrastructure. Historically, this required centralized administration and complex social organisation, as seen in regions like Mesopotamia.[^15] However, there is archaeological evidence that shows similar successes with more egalitarian and decentralized complex societies.[^16]

### Modern governments

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/The_Harmsworth_atlas_and_Gazetter_1908_Government_systems_world_map.jpg/500px-The_Harmsworth_atlas_and_Gazetter_1908_Government_systems_world_map.jpg)

Forms of government in 1908 from The Harmsworth atlas and Gazetter

Starting at the end of the 17th century, the prevalence of republican forms of government grew. The [English Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Civil_War "English Civil War") and [Glorious Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glorious_Revolution "Glorious Revolution") in England, the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution "American Revolution"), and the [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution") contributed to the growth of representative forms of government. The [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") was the first large country to have a [Communist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist "Communist") government.[^9] Since the fall of the [Berlin Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Wall "Berlin Wall"), [liberal democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_democracy "Liberal democracy") has become an even more prevalent form of government.[^17]

In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, there was a significant increase in the size and scale of government at the national level.[^18] This included the regulation of corporations and the development of the [welfare state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welfare_state "Welfare state").[^17]

## Political science

### Classification

In political science, it has long been a goal to create a typology or taxonomy of [polities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polities "Polities"), as typologies of political systems are not obvious.[^19] It is especially important in the [political science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science") fields of [comparative politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparative_politics "Comparative politics") and [international relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_relations "International relations"). Like all categories discerned within forms of government, the boundaries of government classifications are either fluid or ill-defined.

Superficially, all governments have an official *[de jure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_jure "De jure")* or ideal form. The United States is a federal constitutional republic, while the former [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") was a federal [socialist republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_state "Socialist state"). However, self-identification is not objective, and as Kopstein and Lichbach argue, defining [regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regime "Regime") can be tricky, especially *[de facto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_facto "De facto")*, when both its government and its economy deviate in practice.[^20] For example, [Voltaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaire "Voltaire") argued that "the [Holy Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire "Holy Roman Empire") is neither Holy, nor Roman, nor an Empire".[^21] In practice, the Soviet Union was a centralized autocratic one-party state under [Joseph Stalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Stalin "Joseph Stalin").

Identifying a form of government can be challenging because many [political systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_system "Political system") originate from socio-economic movements, and the parties that carry those movements into power often name themselves after those ideologies. These parties may have competing political ideologies and strong ties to particular forms of government. As a result, the movements themselves can sometimes be mistakenly considered as forms of government, rather than the ideologies that influence the governing system.[^22]

Other complications include general non-consensus or deliberate " [distortion or bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disinformation "Disinformation") " of reasonable technical definitions of political ideologies and associated forms of governing, due to the nature of politics in the modern era. For example: The meaning of "conservatism" in the United States has little in common with the way the word's definition is used elsewhere. As Ribuffo notes, "what Americans now call conservatism much of the world calls liberalism or [neoliberalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoliberalism "Neoliberalism") "; a "conservative" in Finland would be labelled a " [socialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist "Socialist") " in the United States.[^23] Since the 1950s, conservatism in the United States has been chiefly associated with [right-wing politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-wing_politics "Right-wing politics") and the [Republican Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Republican_Party_\(United_States\) "History of the Republican Party (United States)"). However, during the era of [segregation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Racial_segregation_in_the_United_States "Racial segregation in the United States") many [Southern Democrats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Democrats "Southern Democrats") were conservatives, and they played a key role in the [conservative coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_coalition "Conservative coalition") that controlled Congress from 1937 to 1963.[^24] [^1]

### Social-political ambiguity

Opinions vary by individuals concerning the types and properties of governments that exist. "Shades of gray" are commonplace in any government and its corresponding classification. Even the most liberal democracies limit rival political activity to one extent or another while the most tyrannical dictatorships must organize a broad base of support thereby creating difficulties for " [pigeonholing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pigeonholing "Pigeonholing") " governments into narrow categories. Examples include the claims of the [United States as being a plutocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plutocracy#United_States "Plutocracy") rather than a democracy since some American voters believe elections are being manipulated by wealthy [Super PACs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_PACs "Super PACs").[^25] Some consider that government is to be reconceptualised where in times of climatic change the needs and desires of the individual are reshaped to generate sufficiency for all.[^26]

## Measurement of governing

The quality of a government can be measured by [Government effectiveness index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_effectiveness_index "Government effectiveness index"), which relates to [political efficacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_efficacy "Political efficacy") and [state capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_capacity "State capacity").[^27] The [political bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_bias "Political bias") of a government can be measured relative to the [median voter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_voter_theorem "Median voter theorem").[^28] The [fiscal sustainability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiscal_sustainability "Fiscal sustainability") of a government measures the [sustainability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainability "Sustainability") of [fiscal policies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiscal_policy "Fiscal policy"), [government budget balances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_budget_balance "Government budget balance") and the [fiscal gap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiscal_gap "Fiscal gap") in the long-term.[^29] For some countries the current government budget balances and [unfunded mandates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unfunded_mandate "Unfunded mandate") were found unsustainable.[^30]

## Forms

[Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") in his book *[The Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_\(Plato\) "Republic (Plato)")* (375 BC) divided governments into five basic types (four being existing forms and one being Plato's ideal form, which exists "only in speech"):[^31]

- [Aristocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristocracy "Aristocracy") (rule by [law and order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_of_law "Rule of law"), like ideal traditional "benevolent" kingdoms that are not tyrannical)
- [Timocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timocracy "Timocracy") (rule by honor and duty, like a "benevolent" military; Sparta as an example)
- [Oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy "Oligarchy") (rule by wealth and market-based-ethics, like a [laissez-faire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laissez-faire "Laissez-faire") capitalist state)
- [Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") (rule by pure [liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberty "Liberty") and [equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_equality "Social equality"), like a [free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom "Freedom") citizen)
- [Tyranny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrant "Tyrant") ([rule by fear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_by_fear "Rule by fear"), like a [despot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Despotism "Despotism"))

These five regimes progressively degenerate starting with aristocracy at the top and tyranny at the bottom.[^32]

In his *[Politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_\(Aristotle\) "Politics (Aristotle)")*, Aristotle elaborates on Plato's five regimes discussing them in relation to the government of one, of the few, and of the many.[^33] From this follows the classification of forms of government according to which people have the authority to rule: either one person (an [autocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy"), such as monarchy), a select group of people (an aristocracy), or the people as a whole (a democracy, such as a republic).

[Thomas Hobbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes "Thomas Hobbes") stated on their classification:

> The difference of [Commonwealths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth "Commonwealth") consisteth in the difference of the [sovereign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign "Sovereign"), or the person representative of all and every one of the multitude. And because the sovereignty is either in one man, or in an assembly of more than one; and into that assembly either every man hath right to enter, or not everyone, but certain men distinguished from the rest; it is manifest there can be but three kinds of Commonwealth. For the representative must need to be one man or more; and if more, then it is the assembly of all, or but of a part. When the representative is one man, then is the Commonwealth a monarchy; when an assembly of all that will come together, then it is a democracy or popular Commonwealth; when an assembly of a part only, then it is called an aristocracy. In other kinds of Commonwealth there can be none: for either one, or more, or all, must have the sovereign power (which I have shown to be indivisible) entire.[^34]

### Modern basic political systems

According to [Yale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yale "Yale") professor [Juan José Linz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_Jos%C3%A9_Linz "Juan José Linz"), there a three main types of [political systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_system "Political system") today: [democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy"), [totalitarian regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarian_regime "Totalitarian regime") and, sitting between these two, [authoritarian regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") with [hybrid regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybrid_regime "Hybrid regime").[^5] [^35] Another modern classification system includes [monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchies "Monarchies") as a standalone entity or as a hybrid system of the main three.[^6] Scholars generally refer to a [dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictatorship "Dictatorship") as either a form of authoritarianism or totalitarianism.[^36] [^5] [^37]

### Autocracy

An autocracy is a system of government in which supreme [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_\(social_and_political\) "Power (social and political)") is concentrated in the hands of one person, whose decisions are subject to neither external legal restraints nor regularized mechanisms of popular control (except perhaps for the implicit threat of a [coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "Coup d'état") or mass [insurrection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurrection "Insurrection")).[^38] [Absolute monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy "Absolute monarchy") is a historically prevalent form of autocracy, wherein a [monarch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarch "Monarch") governs as a singular [sovereign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign "Sovereign") with no limitation on [royal prerogative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_prerogative "Royal prerogative"). Most absolute monarchies are [hereditary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hereditary_monarchy "Hereditary monarchy"), however some, notably the [Holy See](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_See "Holy See"), are [elected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elective_monarchy "Elective monarchy") by an [electoral college](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_college "Electoral college") (such as the [college of cardinals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/College_of_cardinals "College of cardinals"), or [prince-electors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince-elector "Prince-elector")). Other forms of autocracy include [tyranny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyranny "Tyranny"), [despotism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Despotism "Despotism"), and [dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictatorship "Dictatorship").

### Aristocracy

Aristocracy [^2] is a form of government that places power in the hands of a small, [elite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elite "Elite") [ruling class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruling_class "Ruling class"),[^39] such as a hereditary [nobility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobility "Nobility") or [privileged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_privilege "Social privilege") [caste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caste "Caste"). This class exercises [minority rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minority_rule "Minority rule"), often as a [landed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_tenure "Land tenure") [timocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timocracy "Timocracy"), wealthy [plutocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plutocracy "Plutocracy"), or [oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy "Oligarchy").

Many monarchies were aristocracies, although in modern constitutional monarchies, the monarch may have little effective power. The term *aristocracy* could also refer to the non- [peasant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peasant "Peasant"), non-servant, and non- [city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burgher_\(social_class\) "Burgher (social class)") classes in [feudalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism "Feudalism").[^40]

### Democracy

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Democracy_claims.svg/500px-Democracy_claims.svg.png)

National governments which self-identify as democracies National governments which do not self-identify as democracies

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Electoral_democracies.svg/500px-Electoral_democracies.svg.png)

Governments recognised as "electoral democracies" as of 2022 by the Freedom in the World survey 3

Democracy is a system of government where [citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizen "Citizen") exercise power by [voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vote "Vote") and [deliberation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deliberative_democracy "Deliberative democracy"). In a [direct democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracy "Direct democracy"), the citizenry as a whole directly forms a [participatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Participatory_democracy "Participatory democracy") governing body and vote directly on each issue. In [indirect democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indirect_democracy "Indirect democracy"), the citizenry governs indirectly through the selection of [representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_democracy "Representative democracy") or [delegates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delegated_voting "Delegated voting") from among themselves, typically by [election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election "Election") or, less commonly, by [sortition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sortition "Sortition"). These select citizens then meet to form a governing body, such as a legislature or [jury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jury "Jury").

Some governments combine both direct and indirect democratic governance, wherein the citizenry selects representatives to administer day-to-day governance, while also reserving the right to govern directly through [popular initiatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_initiative "Popular initiative"), [referendums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Referendum "Referendum") (plebiscites), and the [right of recall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recall_election "Recall election"). In a [constitutional democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_democracy "Constitutional democracy") the powers of the majority are exercised within the framework of representative democracy, but the constitution limits [majority rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majority_rule "Majority rule"), usually through the provision by all of certain [universal rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_right "Universal right"), such as [freedom of speech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_speech "Freedom of speech") or [freedom of association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_association "Freedom of association").[^41] [^42]

#### Republics

A republic is a form of government in which the country is considered a "public matter" ([Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language"): *res publica*), not the private concern or property of the rulers, and where offices of states are subsequently directly or indirectly elected or appointed rather than inherited. The people, or some significant portion of them, have supreme control over the government and where offices of state are elected or chosen by elected people.[^43] [^44]

A common simplified definition of a republic is a government where the head of state is not a monarch.[^45] [^46] [Montesquieu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montesquieu "Montesquieu") included both [democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy"), where all the people have a share in rule, and [aristocracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristocracy "Aristocracy") or [oligarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy "Oligarchy"), where only some of the people rule, as republican forms of government.[^47]

Other terms used to describe different republics include [democratic republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_republic "Democratic republic"), [parliamentary republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_republic "Parliamentary republic"), [semi-presidential republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-presidential_republic "Semi-presidential republic"), [presidential republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_republic "Presidential republic"), [federal republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_republic "Federal republic"), [people's republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_republic "People's republic"), and [Islamic republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_republic "Islamic republic").

#### Federalism

Federalism is a political concept in which a *group* of members are bound together by [covenant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covenant_\(law\) "Covenant (law)") with a governing [representative head](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_headship "Federal headship"). The term "federalism" is also used to describe a system of government in which [sovereignty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereignty "Sovereignty") is constitutionally divided between a central governing authority and constituent political units, variously called states, provinces or otherwise. Federalism is a system based upon democratic principles and institutions in which the power to govern is shared between national and provincial/state governments, creating what is often called a [federation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federation "Federation").[^48] Proponents are often called [federalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist "Federalist").

## Branches

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Separation_of_powers.png/500px-Separation_of_powers.png)

Separation of powers in the US government, demonstrating the trias politica model

Governments are typically organised into distinct institutions constituting branches of government each with particular [powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_\(social_and_political\) "Power (social and political)"), functions, duties, and responsibilities. The distribution of powers between these institutions differs between governments, as do the functions and number of branches. An independent, parallel distribution of powers between branches of government is the [separation of powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_powers "Separation of powers"). A shared, intersecting, or overlapping distribution of powers is the [fusion of powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fusion_of_powers "Fusion of powers").

Governments are often organised into three branches with separate powers: a legislature, an executive, and a judiciary; this is sometimes called the *trias politica* model. However, in [parliamentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament "Parliament") and [semi-presidential systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-presidential_system "Semi-presidential system"), branches of government often intersect, having shared membership and overlapping functions. Many governments have fewer or additional branches, such as an independent [electoral commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_commission "Electoral commission") or [auditory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_audit_institution "Supreme audit institution") branch.[^49]

## Party system

Presently, most governments are administered by members of an explicitly constituted [political party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_party "Political party") which coordinates the activities of associated government [officials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Official "Official") and [candidates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candidate "Candidate") for office. In a [multiparty system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiparty_system "Multiparty system") of government, multiple political parties have the capacity to gain control of government offices, typically by competing in [elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election "Election"), although the [effective number of parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_number_of_parties "Effective number of parties") may be limited.

A [majority government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majority_government "Majority government") is a government by one or more [governing parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governing_parties "Governing parties") together holding an absolute majority of seats in the parliament, in contrast to a [minority government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minority_government "Minority government") in which they have only a plurality of seats and often depend on a [confidence-and-supply](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidence-and-supply "Confidence-and-supply") arrangement with other parties. A [coalition government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalition_government "Coalition government") is one in which multiple parties cooperate to form a government as part of a [coalition agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalition_agreement "Coalition agreement"). In a single-party government, a single party forms a government without the support of a coalition, as is typically the case with majority governments,[^50] [^51] but even a minority government may consist of just one party unable to find a willing coalition partner at the moment.[^52]

A state that continuously maintains a single-party government within a (nominally) multiparty system possesses a [dominant-party system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominant-party_system "Dominant-party system"). In a (nondemocratic) [one-party system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-party_system "One-party system") a single [ruling party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruling_party "Ruling party") has the (more-or-less) exclusive right to form the government, and the formation of other parties may be obstructed or illegal. In some cases, a government may have a [non-partisan system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-partisan_system "Non-partisan system"), as is the case with [absolute monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy "Absolute monarchy") or [non-partisan democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-partisan_democracy "Non-partisan democracy").

## Public policy

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Government-spending-by-function.png/330px-Government-spending-by-function.png)

Government spending composition by country (2023) 57

Public policy can be considered the sum of a government's direct and indirect activities.[^58] It includes various aspects of life such as education, health care, employment, finance, economics, transportation, and all over elements of society.[^59] Public policy making can be characterized as a dynamic, complex, and interactive system through which public problems are identified and resolved through the creation of new policy or reform of existing policy.[^60] [Government spending](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_spending "Government spending") includes [social protection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_protection "Social protection"), [publicly funded health care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Publicly_funded_health_care "Publicly funded health care"), [general public services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_service "Public service"), education, economic affairs, [public security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_security "Public security"), defense, recreation and culture.[^57] The [government budget balance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_budget_balance "Government budget balance") affects the [government bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_bond "Government bond") yields.[^61]
