**Political philosophy** is the study of the [theoretical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical "Theoretical") and [conceptual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conceptual_model "Conceptual model") foundations of [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics"). It examines the nature, scope, and [legitimacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_legitimacy "Political legitimacy") of [political institutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_institutions "Political institutions"), such as [states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)"). The field investigates different [forms of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forms_of_government "Forms of government"), ranging from [democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") to [authoritarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism"), as well as the values guiding political action, such as [justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justice "Justice"), equality, and [liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberty "Liberty"). As a [normative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normative "Normative") field, political [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy") focuses on desirable norms and values, in contrast to [political science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science"), which primarily emphasizes [empirical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical "Empirical") description.

[Political ideologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_ideologies "Political ideologies") are systems of ideas and principles that outline how society should work. [Anarchism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchism "Anarchism") rejects the coercive power of centralized [governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government"). It proposes a [stateless society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stateless_society "Stateless society") to promote liberty and equality. [Conservatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservatism "Conservatism") seeks to preserve traditional institutions and practices. It is skeptical of the human ability to radically [reform society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_change "Social change"), arguing that drastic changes can destroy the [wisdom of past generations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wisdom_of_past_generations "Wisdom of past generations"). [Liberalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberalism "Liberalism") advocates for individual [rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right "Right") and [liberties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_liberties "Civil liberties"), the [rule of law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_of_law "Rule of law"), [private property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_property "Private property"), and [tolerance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toleration "Toleration"). It holds that governments should protect these values to enable individuals to pursue personal [goals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goal "Goal") without external interference. [Socialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialism "Socialism") emphasizes [collective ownership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_ownership "Collective ownership") and equal distribution of basic goods. It seeks to overcome sources of [inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_inequality "Social inequality"), including private [ownership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ownership "Ownership") of the [means of production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Means_of_production "Means of production"), [class systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_system "Class system"), and hereditary privileges. Other strands of political philosophy include [environmentalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmentalism "Environmentalism"), [realism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Realism_\(international_relations\) "Realism (international relations)"), [idealism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idealism_in_international_relations "Idealism in international relations"), [consequentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consequentialism "Consequentialism"), [perfectionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfectionism_\(philosophy\) "Perfectionism (philosophy)"), [nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalism "Nationalism"), [individualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individualism "Individualism"), and [communitarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communitarianism "Communitarianism").

Political [philosophers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophers "Philosophers") rely on various [methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_methodology "Philosophical methodology") to justify and criticize knowledge claims. Particularists use a bottom-up approach and systematize individual judgments, whereas foundationalists employ a top-down approach and construct comprehensive systems from a small number of basic principles. One foundationalist approach uses theories about [human nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_nature "Human nature") as the basis for political ideologies. [Universalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universalists "Universalists") assert that basic [moral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morality "Morality") and political principles apply equally to every culture, a view rejected by [cultural relativists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_relativists "Cultural relativists").

Political philosophy has its roots in [antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_period "Ancient period"), such as the theories of [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") and [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") in [ancient Greek philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy "Ancient Greek philosophy"), with discussions on the nature of justice and ideal states. [Confucianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucianism "Confucianism"), [Taoism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoism "Taoism"), and [legalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legalism_\(Chinese_philosophy\) "Legalism (Chinese philosophy)") emerged in [ancient Chinese philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Chinese_philosophy "Ancient Chinese philosophy"), while [Hindu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism") and [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist "Buddhist") political thought developed in [ancient India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_India "Ancient India"), each offering distinct views on the foundations of the social order and [statecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statecraft "Statecraft"). Political philosophy in the [medieval period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_period "Medieval period") was characterized by the interplay between ancient Greek thought and religion in both the [Christian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_world "Christian world") and [Islamic worlds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_world "Islamic world"). The modern period marked a shift towards [secularism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularism "Secularism") as diverse schools of thought developed, such as [social contract theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_contract_theory "Social contract theory"), liberalism, conservatism, [utilitarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utilitarianism "Utilitarianism"), [Marxism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism "Marxism"), and anarchism.

## Definition and related fields

Political philosophy is the branch of [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy") that studies the theoretical and conceptual foundations of [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics"). It considers the relation between individual and society, the best organization of collective human life, the distribution of goods and [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_\(social_and_political\) "Power (social and political)"), the limits of state authority, and the values that should guide political decisions. The field examines basic concepts such as [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)"), [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government"), [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_\(social_and_political\) "Power (social and political)"), [legitimacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legitimacy_\(political\) "Legitimacy (political)"), [political obligation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_obligation "Political obligation"), [justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justice "Justice"), equality, and [liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberty "Liberty"), analyzing their essential features and how they influence citizens, communities, and policies.[^22] Schools of political philosophy, such as [liberalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberalism "Liberalism"), [conservatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservatism "Conservatism"), [socialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialism "Socialism"), and [anarchism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchism "Anarchism"), offer diverse interpretations of these concepts. They are guided by different values and propose distinct frameworks for structuring societies.[^23] As a systematic and critical inquiry, political philosophy scrutinizes established beliefs and explores alternative views.[^24] A central motivation for this investigation is that forms of government are not [predetermined facts of nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_law "Natural law") but human creations that can be actively shaped to the benefit or detriment of some or all.[^25]

Political philosophers address various evaluative or [normative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normativity "Normativity") issues. They examine ideal forms of government, and describe the values and norms that should guide political decisions.[^26] They differ in this regard from [political scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science"), who focus on [empirical descriptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empiricism "Empiricism") of how governments and other political institutions actually work, rather than how they ideally should work.[^27] The term *political theory* is sometimes used as a synonym of *political philosophy*, but can also refer to a sister discipline. According to the latter view, political philosophy seeks to answer general and fundamental questions, whereas political theory analyzes and compares more specific aspects of political institutions and clarifies the concepts and methods used by political scientists.[^28] [^1]

Political philosophy has its roots in [ethics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics "Ethics") —the area of philosophy studying [moral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morality "Morality") phenomena—and is sometimes considered a branch of ethics.[^2] While ethics examines right conduct and the good life in the broadest sense, political philosophy has a more narrow scope, focusing on the organization and justification of political institutions rather than private moral obligations not directly related to collective life.[^31] [^3] Political philosophy is also closely related to [social philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_philosophy "Social philosophy"), and philosophical treatises often discuss the two together without clearly distinguishing between them. Despite their overlap, one difference is that social philosophy examines diverse kinds of social phenomena, while political philosophy has a more specific focus on power and [governance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governance "Governance").[^32] Because of its interest in the role of laws and economic structures, political philosophy is also connected to the [philosophy of law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_law "Philosophy of law") and [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_economics "Philosophy of economics").[^33]

The term *political philosophy* originates in the [ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") words πολιτικός (*politikos*, meaning 'belonging or pertaining to the polis') and φιλοσοφία (*philosophía*, meaning 'love of wisdom').[^34] As one of the oldest branches of philosophy, it has been practiced in many different cultures, often in response to political challenges of their time by trying to understand, justify, or critique social arrangements.[^35]

## Basic concepts

Political philosophers rely on various basic concepts, such as government, power, law, and justice, to formulate theories and conceptualize the field of [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics"). They understand politics as encompassing diverse activities associated with governance, [collective decision-making](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_decision-making "Group decision-making"), [reconciliation of conflicting interests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conflict_resolution "Conflict resolution"), and [exercise of power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_\(social_and_political\) "Power (social and political)"). It is sometimes characterized as the art of skillfully engaging in these activities.[^36]

### Government, power, and laws

The state, a fundamental concept in political philosophy, is an organized political entity. States are associations of people, called *[citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizenship "Citizenship")*. They typically exercise control over a specific [territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territory "Territory"), implement the [rule of law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_of_law "Rule of law"), and function as [juristic persons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juristic_persons "Juristic persons") subject to rights and obligations while [engaging with other states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_relations "International relations"). However, the precise definition of statehood is disputed. Some philosophical characterizations emphasize the state's [monopoly on violence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monopoly_on_violence "Monopoly on violence") and the subordination of the will of the many to the will of a dominant few. Another outlook sees the state as a [social contract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_contract "Social contract") for mutual benefit and security. States are characterized by their level of organization and the power they wield. They contrast with [stateless societies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stateless_societies "Stateless societies"), which are more loosely ordered social groups connected through a less centralized web of relationships. [Nation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation "Nation"), a related concept, refers to a group of people with a common identity such as shared culture, history, or language. [Nation-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation-states "Nation-states") are states whose citizens share a common [national identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_identity "National identity") that aligns with the state's political boundaries. Historically, the first states in [antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_period "Ancient period") were [city-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City-states "City-states").[^37]

A [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government") is an institution that exercises control and governs the people belonging to a political entity, usually a state. Some political philosophers see the government as an end in itself, while others consider it a means to other goods, such as peace and prosperity. Some governments set down fundamental principles, called a *[constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution "Constitution")*, that outline the structure, functions, and limitations of governmental authority. By institutionalizing these principles, constitutions can help to constrain and stabilize the exercise of power, prevent abuses and arbitrary rule, legitimize rule-bound governance, and safeguard rights. [Anarchists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchy "Anarchy") reject governments and advocate self-governance without a centralized authority.[^38]

Political philosophers distinguish [forms of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forms_of_government "Forms of government") based on who wields political power and how it is wielded. In [democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracies "Democracies"), the main power lies with the people. In [direct democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracies "Direct democracies"), citizens vote directly on laws and policies, whereas in [indirect democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_democracy "Representative democracy"), they elect leaders who make these decisions. Democracies contrast with [authoritarian regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarian_regimes "Authoritarian regimes"), which reject political plurality and suppress dissent through centralized, hierarchical power structures. In the case of [autocracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracies "Autocracies"), absolute power is vested in a single person, such as a [monarch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarch "Monarch") [^4] or a [dictator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictator "Dictator"). For [oligarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchies "Oligarchies"), power is concentrated in the hands of a few, typically the wealthy. An authoritarian regime is [totalitarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarian "Totalitarian") if it seeks extensive control over public and private life, such as [fascism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fascism "Fascism"), which combines totalitarianism with nationalist and [militarist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Militarist "Militarist") political ideologies.[^40]

Forms of government can also be distinguished based on the type of people making political decisions. [Aristocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristocracy "Aristocracy") implements rule by the elites, such as a privileged ruling class or [nobility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobility "Nobility").[^41] In the case of [meritocracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meritocracies "Meritocracies"), the ruling elites are chosen by skill rather than social background.[^42] For [technocracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technocracies "Technocracies"), people with technical skills, such as engineers and scientists, wield political power.[^43] [Theocracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theocracies "Theocracies") prioritize religious authority in political decision-making, implement religious laws, and claim legitimacy by following the [divine will](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divine_will "Divine will").[^44] Political philosophers further discuss [federalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalism "Federalism") and [confederalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confederalism "Confederalism"),[^5] which are systems of governance involving multiple levels: in addition to a central national government, there are several regional governments with distinct responsibilities and powers. These systems contrast with [colonialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonialism "Colonialism"),[^6] where occupied territories are exploited rather than treated as equal partners, and with [unitary states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_state "Unitary state"), where authority is centralized at the national level.[^47]

A key aspect of governments and other political institutions is the [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_\(social_and_political\) "Power (social and political)") they wield. Power is the ability to produce intended effects or control what people and institutions do. It can be based on [consent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consent "Consent"), like people following a [charismatic leader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charismatic_authority "Charismatic authority"), but can also take the form of [coercion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coercion "Coercion"), such as a tyrannical ruler enforcing compliance through fear and [repression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_repression "Political repression").[^48] The powers of government typically include the [legislative power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislative_power "Legislative power") to establish new laws or revoke existing ones, the [executive power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_power "Executive power") to enforce laws, and the [judicial power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judicial_power "Judicial power") to arbitrate legal disputes. Some governments follow the [separation of powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_powers "Separation of powers") and have distinct branches for each function to prevent overconcentration and [abuse of power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuse_of_power "Abuse of power"). Others concentrate all power in a single entity.[^49] Language is a central aspect of political power, serving as a medium of [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication "Communication") and a force shaping public opinion. This influence is reflected in the control of the [means of communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Means_of_communication "Means of communication"), such as [mass media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_media "Mass media"), and in the [freedom of speech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_speech "Freedom of speech") of each individual.[^50] Political power also includes institutional mechanisms that regulate the behavior of individuals, such as educational, disciplinary, and medical institutions.[^51]

[Legitimacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legitimacy_\(political\) "Legitimacy (political)"), another fundamental concept, is the rightful or justified use of power. Political philosophers examine whether, why, and under what conditions the powers exercised by a government are legitimate. Often-discussed requirements include that power is acquired following established rules and used for rightful ends.[^52] [^7] For instance, the rules of [representative democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_democracies "Representative democracies") assert that [elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election "Election") determine who acquires power as the legitimate ruler. [Authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authority "Authority"), a closely related concept, is the right to rule or the common belief that someone is legitimized to exercise power. In some cases, a person may have authority even if they lack the effective power to act. Some theorists also talk of *illegitimate authority* in situations where the common belief in the legitimacy of a use of power is mistaken.[^54]

Governments typically use [laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law "Law") to wield power. Laws are rules of social conduct that describe how people and institutions may or may not act. According to [natural law theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_law_theory "Natural law theory"), laws are or should be expressions of universal [moral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morality "Morality") principles inherent in human nature. This view contrasts with [legal positivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_positivism "Legal positivism"), which sees laws as human [conventions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_\(norm\) "Convention (norm)").[^55] [Political obligation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_obligation "Political obligation") is the [duty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duty "Duty") of citizens to follow the laws of their political community. Political philosophers examine in what sense citizens are subject to political obligations even if they did not explicitly consent to them. Political obligation may or may not align with moral obligation—the duty to follow moral principles. For example, if an [authoritarian state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarian_state "Authoritarian state") imposes laws that violate basic [human rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights "Human rights"), citizens may have a moral obligation to [disobey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disobedience "Disobedience").[^56] Laws also regulate the rights of individuals as legal entitlements protecting their interests and freedom.[^57]

Laws governing [property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property "Property") are foundational to many [legal systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_systems "Legal systems"). Property is the right to control a good, such as the rights to use, consume, lend, sell, and destroy it. It covers both material goods, like [natural resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_resources "Natural resources"), and immaterial goods, such as [copyrights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copyright "Copyright") associated with [intellectual property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellectual_property "Intellectual property"). [Public property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_property "Public property") pertains to the state or community, whereas [private property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_property "Private property") belongs to other entities, such as individual citizens. Many discussions in political philosophy address the advantages and disadvantages of private property.[^58] For example, [communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communism "Communism") seeks to abolish most forms of private property in favor of [collective ownership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_ownership "Collective ownership") to promote economic equality.[^59]

### Justice, equality, and liberty

![Black-and-white photo of a man with glasses wearing a collared shirt under a sweater](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/John_Rawls_%281971_photo_portrait%29.jpg/250px-John_Rawls_%281971_photo_portrait%29.jpg)

John Rawls formulated an influential theory of justice as fairness. 60

Diverse concepts in political philosophy act as values or goals of political processes.[^61] [Justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justice "Justice") is a complex concept at the core of many political concerns. It is specifically associated with the idea that people should be treated fairly and receive what they deserve. More broadly, it also refers to appropriate behavior and moral conduct, but its exact meaning varies by context: it can be an aspect of [actions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_\(philosophy\) "Action (philosophy)"), a [virtue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtue "Virtue") of actors, or a structural feature of social situations. In the context of social life, [social justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_justice "Social justice") encompasses various aspects of fairness and equality in regard to wealth, assets, and other advantages. It includes the idea of [distributive justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_justice "Distributive justice"), which promotes an impartial allocation of resources, goods, and opportunities. In legal contexts, [retributive justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retributive_justice "Retributive justice") deals with [punishment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punishment "Punishment"), with one principle being that the harm inflicted on an offender should be proportional to their [crime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crime "Crime").[^62]

Justice is closely related to equality, the ideal that individuals should have the same rights, opportunities, or resources. [Equality before the law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_before_the_law "Equality before the law") is the principle that all individuals are subject to the same legal standards, rights, and obligations. [Political equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_equality "Political equality") concerns the ability to vote for someone and to become a candidate for a political position. [Equal opportunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equal_opportunity "Equal opportunity") is the ideal that everyone should have the same chances in life, meaning that success should be based on merit rather than circumstances of birth or social class. This contrasts with [equality of outcome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_of_outcome "Equality of outcome"), the idea that all people should have similar levels of material wealth and [living standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Living_standards "Living standards"). Philosophers of politics examine and compare different conceptions of equality, discussing which of its aspects should guide political action. They also consider the influence of [discrimination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrimination "Discrimination"), which refers to unfair treatment based on characteristics such as [race](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Race_\(human_categorization\) "Race (human categorization)"), [gender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender "Gender"), [sexuality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexuality "Sexuality"), and [class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_class "Social class") that can undermine equality. The school of political thought known as [egalitarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egalitarianism "Egalitarianism") sees equality as one of the main goals of political action.[^63]

[Liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberty "Liberty") or [freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom "Freedom") [^8] is the ideal that people may act according to their will without oppressive restrictions. Political philosophers typically distinguish two complementary aspects of liberty: [positive liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_liberty "Positive liberty") —the power to act in a certain way—and [negative liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_liberty "Negative liberty") —the absence of obstacles or interference from others. Liberty is a key value of [liberalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberalism "Liberalism"), a school of political philosophy.[^65] Competing schools of thought debate whether laws necessarily limit liberty by restricting individual actions to protect the common good or enable it by creating a safe framework in which individuals can exercise their rights freely.[^66] Liberty as an ability to do something is sometimes distinguished from [license](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/License "License"), which involves explicit [permission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permission_\(philosophy\) "Permission (philosophy)") to do something.[^67] [Autonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomy "Autonomy"), another closely related concept, is the ability to make informed decisions and govern oneself by being one's own master.[^68]

Welfare, [well-being](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-being "Well-being"), and [happiness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Happiness "Happiness") express the general [quality of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quality_of_life "Quality of life") of an individual and are central standards for evaluating policies and political institutions. Subjectivists understand these phenomena as [subjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity_and_objectivity_\(philosophy\) "Subjectivity and objectivity (philosophy)") experiences, linked to the presence of [pleasant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleasure "Pleasure") feelings, the absence of unpleasant ones, and a positive self-assessment of one's life. Objectivists, by contrast, argue that the relevant factors can be objectively measured, such as economic prosperity, health, education, and security. [Welfarism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welfarism "Welfarism") is the school of political thought that states that well-being is the ultimate goal of political actions.[^69] [Welfare states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welfare_state "Welfare state") are states that prioritize the social and economic well-being of their citizens through measures such as affordable [healthcare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Healthcare "Healthcare") systems, [social security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_security "Social security"), and free access to [education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Education "Education") for all.[^70]

## Major schools of thought

### Anarchism

![Black-and-white photo of a bearded man with glasses wearing a dark coat](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Portrait_Pierre-Joseph_Proudhon_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Portrait_Pierre-Joseph_Proudhon_%28cropped%29.jpg)

Pierre-Joseph Proudhon was a founding figure of anarchism and saw state authority as an obstacle to equality and liberty. 71

Anarchism is a school of political thought [^9] that rejects hierarchical systems, arguing for self-governing social structures and a stateless society, known as *[anarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchy "Anarchy")*. Anarchists typically see liberty and equality as their guiding values. They understand authority over others as a threat to individual autonomy and criticize hierarchical structures for perpetuating power imbalances and inequalities. As a result, they challenge the legitimacy of centralized governments wielding coercive power over others.[^10] Anarchism maintains that freedom from domination is central to human flourishing. It promotes social structures based on voluntary association to advance universal egalitarianism, emphasizing free cooperation and non-coercive consensus-building.[^74]

Several schools of anarchism have been proposed.[^75] Absolute or *a priori* anarchism rejects any form of state, arguing that state power is inherently illegitimate and unjust. Contingent or *a posteriori* anarchism presents a less radical view, suggesting that states are not inherently bad but nonetheless usually fail in practice. For example, consequentialist anarchism rejects states based on the claim that they typically have negative consequences, such as inequality, discrimination, and unhappiness.[^72] [Individualist anarchists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individualist_anarchism "Individualist anarchism") emphasize the importance of individual freedom, seeking to defend it against any social structure that restricts personal autonomy, including parental authority and legal institutions. This outlook can take the form of libertarian anarchism or [anarcho-capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarcho-capitalism "Anarcho-capitalism"). [Collectivist or socialist anarchists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collectivist_anarchism "Collectivist anarchism"), by contrast, stress the importance of community and voluntary cooperation within society, advocating collective ownership of resources and the means of production. For example, [anarchist communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchist_communism "Anarchist communism") argues for decentralized social organization and communal sharing to promote well-being for all.[^76]

Diverse criticisms of anarchism have been articulated. Some see anarchism as a negative attitude that seeks to destroy established institutions without providing viable alternatives, thereby simply replacing order with chaos. Another objection holds that anarchy is inherently unstable since hierarchical structures emerge naturally, meaning that [stateless societies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stateless_societies "Stateless societies") will inevitably evolve back into some form of state. Further arguments assert that the guiding anarchist goal is based on an unreachable utopian ideal or that anarchism is incoherent since the attempt to undermine all forms of authority paradoxically is itself a new form of authority.[^77]

### Conservatism

![Black-and-white portrait of a man in a formal attire sitting next to a desk](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Edmund_Burke_EMWEA.jpg/250px-Edmund_Burke_EMWEA.jpg)

Edmund Burke was an early defender of conservatism, stressing the importance of the accumulated wisdom of past generations and the danger of radical change. 78

Conservatism is a school of political thought that seeks to preserve and promote traditional institutions and practices. It is typically driven by [skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skepticism "Skepticism") about the [human ability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanism "Humanism") to radically reconceive and reform society, arguing that such attempts, guided by a limited understanding of the consequences, often result in more harm than good. Conservatives give more weight to the wisdom of historical experience than abstract ideals of [reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reason "Reason"). They assert that since established institutions and practices have passed the test of time, they serve as foundations of stability and continuity. Despite its preference for the *[status quo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Status_quo "Status quo")*, conservatism is not opposed to political and social change in general but advocates for a cautious approach. It maintains that change should happen as a gradual and natural evolution rather than through radical reform to ensure that political arrangements deemed valuable are preserved.[^79]

While the exact institutions and practices to be preserved depend on the specific cultural and historical context of a society, conservatives generally emphasize the importance of family, religion, and national identity. They tend to support private property as a safeguard against state power and some forms of social security for the poor to maintain societal stability.[^80]

Distinct strands of conservative thought follow different but overlapping approaches. [Authoritarian conservatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarian_conservatism "Authoritarian conservatism") prioritizes centralized, established authorities over the judgment of individuals. [Traditionalist conservatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditionalist_conservatism "Traditionalist conservatism") sees general customs, conventions, and traditions as the guiding principles that inform both established institutions and individual judgments. Romantic or [reactionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactionary "Reactionary") conservatism is driven by [nostalgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nostalgia "Nostalgia") and seeks to restore an earlier state of society deemed superior. Other discussed types include [paternalistic conservatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paternalistic_conservatism "Paternalistic conservatism"), which argues that those in power should care for the less privileged, and [liberal conservatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_conservatism "Liberal conservatism"), which includes the emphasis on individual liberties and economic freedoms in the conservative agenda.[^81]

Different criticisms of conservatism have been proposed. Some focus on its resistance to change and lack of innovation, arguing that prioritizing the *status quo* perpetuates existing problems and stifles progress. In particular, this concerns situations in which rapidly evolving societal challenges require dynamic, flexible, and creative responses. Another objection targets conservative skepticism about the capacity of reason to effectively address complex social issues, arguing that this skepticism is exaggerated and hinders well-thought-out reforms and meaningful improvements. Some critics state that conservatism reinforces established social hierarchies and inequalities, often benefiting privileged social classes while disadvantaging marginalized groups.[^82]

### Liberalism

![Oil painting of a man with long, gray hair wearing a dark brown cloak over a white shirt](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/John_Locke.jpg/250px-John_Locke.jpg)

As a founder of liberalism, John Locke prioritized individual freedom over state power. 83

Liberalism is a philosophical tradition emphasizing individual [liberties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_liberties "Civil liberties") and [rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rights "Rights"), the [rule of law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_of_law "Rule of law"), [tolerance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toleration "Toleration"), and [constitutional democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_democracy "Constitutional democracy"). It encompasses a variety of ideas without a precise definition. Some liberals follow [John Locke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Locke "John Locke") 's view that all individuals are born free and equal, highlighting the government's role in protecting this natural state. Others associate liberalism more with the individual's ability to participate in democratic institutions than with equality. Liberals endorse various forms of liberty, such as [freedom of speech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_speech "Freedom of speech"), [freedom of religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_religion "Freedom of religion"), and free choice of profession. Liberalism allows for diverse life choices and advocates tolerance of lifestyles different from one's own. This outlook is grounded in [optimism about human nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanism "Humanism") [^11] and trust in the individual's responsibility to make sensible decisions. As a result, liberals assert that the government should remain neutral and uphold the rule of law to allow individuals to pursue their goals without external interference.[^85]

Most forms of liberalism support some form of [free-market economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_market "Free market") and [capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalism "Capitalism"). In a free market, the exchange of goods and services occurs with minimal state control and regulation: [privately owned](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_property "Private property") businesses compete with each other, and prices are primarily influenced by [supply and demand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supply_and_demand "Supply and demand").[^86] Capitalism is an economic system in which the [means of production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Means_of_production "Means of production") are mainly privately owned. This system is typically characterized by a contrast between capitalist owners, who aim to [maximize the profit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Profit_maximization "Profit maximization") of their investment, and workers, who sell their [labor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_\(human_activity\) "Work (human activity)") in exchange for a [salary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salary "Salary").[^87]

One broad characterization distinguishes between [classical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_liberalism "Classical liberalism") and [modern liberalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_liberalism "Social liberalism"), also called social democratic liberalism, based on the role of the state. Classical liberalism seeks to protect the liberties and rights of individuals from government interference, arguing for a limited role of the state. It promotes negative liberty and tasks the state with safeguarding individuals from obstacles or interference from others, such as aggression and theft.[^12] Modern liberalism emphasizes [positive liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_liberty "Positive liberty"), arguing that the state should foster conditions that enable individuals to achieve their personal goals. This approach advocates for a more active role of the state to promote [social justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_justice "Social justice"), [equality of opportunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_of_opportunity "Equality of opportunity"), and the right to a minimal [standard of living](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_of_living "Standard of living"). This can include state programs to ensure affordable [healthcare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Healthcare "Healthcare"), education for all, and [social security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_security "Social security").[^89]

[Libertarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libertarianism "Libertarianism") is closely related to classical liberalism. It emphasizes individual liberties and argues that people should be free to do as they want without coercion as long as they do not infringe on the liberty of others. Some libertarians consider the [non-aggression principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-aggression_principle "Non-aggression principle") —the principle forbidding aggression against a person and their property—as the foundational tenet of libertarianism. Libertarians typically support a free-market economy based on private property and voluntary cooperation. They disapprove of governmental attempts to redistribute wealth and other forms of economic regulation. This view seeks to limit the role of government to [collective defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_defense "Collective defense"), the protection of individual rights, and the enforcement of contracts.[^90]

Various criticisms of liberalism have been formulated. One objection asserts that its individualistic focus on personal liberties undermines community, arguing that the prioritization of personal freedoms leads to [social fragmentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_fragmentation "Social fragmentation"). A different criticism proposes that private property and unregulated markets threaten [economic equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_inequality "Economic inequality") and tend to create unjust hierarchies. Further objections argue that liberalism diminishes the common good by reinforcing individualistic social disputes and that its commitments to tolerance and pluralism result in [cultural relativism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_relativism "Cultural relativism").[^91]

### Socialism

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Friedrich_Engels_%281891%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Friedrich_Engels_%281891%29_%28cropped%29.jpg) ![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Karl_Marx_001_%284x5_cropped%29.jpg/250px-Karl_Marx_001_%284x5_cropped%29.jpg)

[Friedrich Engels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Engels "Friedrich Engels") and [Karl Marx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx "Karl Marx") developed [a radical form of socialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism "Marxism"), calling for a [communist revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_revolution "Communist revolution") to overcome [capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalism "Capitalism").[^92]

Socialism is a family of political views emphasizing [collective ownership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_ownership "Collective ownership") and equality.[^13] It argues that the [means of production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Means_of_production "Means of production") belong to the people in general and the workers in particular and should therefore form part of social ownership rather than private property.[^94] This outlook understands the state as a complex administrative device that manages resources and production to ensure social welfare and a fair distribution of goods.[^95]

A key motivation underlying the socialist perspective is the establishment of equality, which is seen as the natural state of humans. Socialists seek to overcome sources of inequality, such as class systems and hereditary privileges. They are critical of [capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalism "Capitalism"), arguing that private property and free markets reinforce inequalities by leading to large-scale accumulation of private wealth.[^96] Some socialists propose systems of regulation and [taxation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxation "Taxation") to mitigate the negative effects of free-market economies.[^14] Others reject free-market systems in general and promote different mechanisms to manage the production and distribution of goods, ranging from centralized state control and ownership to decentralized systems that plan and direct economic activity.[^96]

[Marxism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism "Marxism") is an influential school of socialism that focuses on the analysis of class relations and social conflicts. It rejects capitalism, arguing that it leads to inequality by dividing society into a capitalist class, which owns the means of production, and a working class, which has to sell its labor and is thereby [alienated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marx%27s_theory_of_alienation "Marx's theory of alienation") from the products of its labor. According to this view, economic forces and [class struggles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_struggle "Class struggle") are the primary drivers of [the historical development of political systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_materialism "Historical materialism"), eventually leading to the downfall of capitalism and the emergence of socialism and [communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communism "Communism").[^98] Communism is usually understood as a radical form of socialism that aims to replace private property with collective ownership and dissolve all class distinctions. In [Marxist theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxist_theory "Marxist theory"), socialism and communism are considered distinct types of [post-capitalist societies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-capitalism "Post-capitalism"). From this perspective, socialism is an intermediate stage between capitalism and communism that still carries some features of capitalism, such as material scarcity, a ruling government, and [division of labor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_of_labor "Division of labor"). Marx argued that these features would gradually dissolve, leading to a communist society characterized by material abundance, absence of occupational specialization, and self-organization without a central government.[^99] [^15]

Several objections to socialism focus on its economic theory. Some argue that central planning and the absence of competition and market-driven price signals result in lower productivity and economic stagnation. Another line of criticism asserts that the different ideals motivating socialism are in conflict with each other. For example, the establishment of a massive state required to manage economic activity and social welfare may create new class distinctions, thereby undermining equality.[^101] Liberal critics assert that egalitarian redistribution transgresses individual rights and threatens negative liberty. Feminists argue that socialists focus on class distinctions at the expense of gender inequalities, and environmentalists maintain that socialist planning marginalizes environmental concerns.[^102]

### Others

[Environmentalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmentalism "Environmentalism") is a political ideology concerned with the relationship between humans and nature. It seeks to preserve, restore, and enhance the [natural environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment "Natural environment"), including the protection of landscapes and animals. [Anthropocentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropocentric "Anthropocentric") environmentalism advocates such policies to improve human life, for example, to mitigate the global consequences of [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") or to promote local [environmental justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_justice "Environmental justice") by protecting [marginalized groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginalized_groups "Marginalized groups") from regional [environmental degradation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_degradation "Environmental degradation"). This form of environmentalism can be integrated into many other political ideologies, such as conservatism and socialism. Non-anthropocentric environmentalism, also called [ecocentrism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecocentrism "Ecocentrism") and [deep ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_ecology "Deep ecology"), differs by focusing on the [intrinsic value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_theory#Intrinsic_and_instrumental "Value theory") of nature itself. This view emphasizes that humans are only a small part of the [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem") as a whole. It seeks to protect and improve nature for its own sake, not only because it serves human interests. This outlook covers diverse and sometimes contrasting interpretations of the relation between humans and nature, including the belief that humans should act as custodians of nature and the idea that modern human civilizations are the source of the problem and threaten [natural balance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balance_of_nature "Balance of nature").[^103]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Portrait_of_Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli_by_Santi_di_Tito.jpg/250px-Portrait_of_Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli_by_Santi_di_Tito.jpg)

Realism is closely associated with Niccolò Machiavelli 's emphasis on power politics and the pragmatic pursuit of self-interest. 104

[Realism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Realism_\(international_relations\) "Realism (international relations)") and [idealism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idealism_in_international_relations "Idealism in international relations") [^16] are two opposing approaches to explaining and guiding political action. According to realism, political activity is primarily driven by [self-interest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-interest "Self-interest"). It asserts that actors pursue power to expand their [sphere of influence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence "Sphere of influence"). Realists argue that politics should not be limited by moral constraints or shy away from violent conflicts when the power aspirations of different actors collide. They highlight the importance of responding to concrete practical factors, with the primary goal of effectively shaping historical reality rather than pursuing ideals. On the international level, realism holds that the self-interest driving state actors is [not constrained by a superior authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchy_\(international_relations\) "Anarchy (international relations)") enforcing common rules. Idealism, by contrast, asserts that political action should follow [moral principles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morality "Morality"). It seeks to establish a just and fair social order based on universal ethical norms rather than narrow self-interest. Idealists reject established practices and institutions that promote unjust use of power and seek to replace them with fair governance, even if their idealized vision reflects a [utopian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utopian "Utopian") aspiration distant from current circumstances.[^106]

[Consequentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consequentialism "Consequentialism"), [perfectionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfectionism_\(philosophy\) "Perfectionism (philosophy)"), and [pluralism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluralism_\(political_philosophy\) "Pluralism (political philosophy)") are distinct but overlapping views about which things are valuable and how values should guide political activity. According to consequentialism, the value of any action depends on its concrete consequences. [Classical utilitarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_utilitarianism "Classical utilitarianism"), an influential form of consequentialism, asserts that only [happiness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Happiness "Happiness") or [pleasure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleasure "Pleasure") is ultimately valuable. This view argues that politics should strive to produce the highest overall happiness for the largest number of people.[^107] [Welfarism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welfarism "Welfarism"), a closely related view, promotes [well-being](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-being "Well-being"), which can cover other features in addition to pleasure, such as health, [personal growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_growth "Personal growth"), meaningful [relationships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpersonal_relationship "Interpersonal relationship"), and a sense of [purpose in life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning_of_life "Meaning of life").[^108] Perfectionism, a different evaluative outlook, asserts that there are certain objective goods, covering fields like morality, art, and culture, that promote the development of human nature. Although perfectionists disagree about what exactly those goods are, they all maintain that states should establish conditions that promote human excellence among their citizens.[^109] Value pluralists assert that diverse values influence political action. They often emphasize that different values can be opposed to each other and that value conflicts cannot always be resolved. For example, [Isaiah Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaiah_Berlin "Isaiah Berlin") argued that liberty and equality are conflicting values and that a gain in one value cannot make up for the loss in the other.[^110]

[Individualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individualism "Individualism") prioritizes the importance of individuals over the community, an ideal typically promoted by liberal political systems. It asserts that society is at its core made up of individuals and seeks to defend them from social attempts to interfere with their preferred lifestyles. Individualism contrasts with [collectivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collectivism "Collectivism"), which prioritizes the well-being of groups over individual interests and highlights the importance of group cohesion and unity. [Communitarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communitarianism "Communitarianism") is a similar outlook that supports a social structure in which individuals are connected through strong social relationships and shared values. It argues that the personality and [social identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_identity "Social identity") of individuals are deeply influenced by community relations and social norms.[^111] [Nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalism "Nationalism") extends the focus on social relations to the state as a whole. It is closely associated with [patriotism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriotism "Patriotism") and promotes social cohesion through [national identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_identity "National identity") based on shared customs, culture, and language.[^112]

[Republicanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republicanism "Republicanism") is a broad philosophical tradition that emphasizes [civic virtue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_virtue "Civic virtue"), [political participation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_participation "Political participation"), and the rule of law. It argues that political action should promote the common good and social equality. This tradition is opposed to oppressive and authoritarian governance. It advocates the separation of powers to prevent overconcentration of authority, encouraging citizens to participate in the political process and seeking to hold the government accountable to the people.[^113] [Populism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Populism "Populism"), another ideological tendency, encompasses a variety of political outlooks that seek to promote the interests of ordinary people, typically contrasting the will of the people with the agenda of corrupt elites wielding power. The term is often associated with the negative connotation of attempting to gain support from uninformed people by appealing to popular sentiment.[^114] Conversely, [elitism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elitism "Elitism") is the belief that elites, rather than common people, should run the government.[^115]

Various ideologies integrate religious values and principles into their political outlook. [Christian democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_democracy "Christian democracy"), an influential tradition in [Western Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Europe "Western Europe"), blends traditional [Catholic social teachings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_social_teaching "Catholic social teaching") with democratic principles, emphasizing community, family, a harmonious social order, [respect for each person](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personalism "Personalism"), and tradition. It is critical of the modern [focus on material wealth and power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_materialism "Economic materialism").[^116] [Islamism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamism "Islamism") seeks to incorporate [Islamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") principles into governance, including the implementation of [Islamic law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharia "Sharia") while maintaining a critical attitude towards Western influences.[^117] [Hindu nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_nationalism "Hindu nationalism") promotes governance and national identity rooted in [Hindu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism") values and traditions.[^118] Other religion-inspired political ideologies include [Zionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zionism "Zionism"), [Buddhist socialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist_socialism "Buddhist socialism"), and [Confucianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucianism "Confucianism").[^119] Contrasting with these approaches, [secularism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularism "Secularism") opposes the integration of religious principles into politics.[^120]

[Contractarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contractarianism "Contractarianism") and [contractualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contractualism "Contractualism") are views about the sources and legitimacy of power. They argue that political authority should be based on some form of consent among the citizens, for example, as an implicit social contract or as what people would reasonably agree to under ideal circumstances. For contractarianism, everyone's self-interest is the motivational force underlying the agreement, whereas contractualism emphasizes rationality and respect for others as the main factors.[^121]

[Postmodernism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism "Postmodernism") rejects ideological systems that claim to offer objective, universal truths and adopts a particularly critical attitude towards [Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment") ideals of reason and progress. It opposes hierarchical power structures that perpetuate and enforce these ideals, calling instead for resistance to this type of centralized power while promoting a pluralism of local practices and ideologies.[^122] [Feminism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminism "Feminism"), another critical approach, targets injustice based on [gender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender "Gender"), aiming to empower women and liberate them from unfair [patriarchal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriarchal "Patriarchal") social structures. Feminists focus on many forms of inequality, including social, economic, political, and legal inequality.[^123] [African political philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_philosophy "African philosophy"), a different tradition, is based on the concept of [Ubuntu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubuntu_philosophy "Ubuntu philosophy") or humanness, asserting that legitimate power should be guided by the communal good, compassion, and mutual respect.[^124]

## Methodology

![Oil painting of a man with gray hear wearing a formal attire](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Thomas_Hobbes_by_John_Michael_Wright1.jpg/250px-Thomas_Hobbes_by_John_Michael_Wright1.jpg)

A historically influential method seeks to justify political theories by reference to human nature, such as Thomas Hobbes 's social contract theory proceeding from the assumed brutish natural state of humans in a perpetual conflict. 125

The [methodology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_methodology "Philosophical methodology") of political philosophy [^17] examines how to arrive at, [justify](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justification_\(epistemology\) "Justification (epistemology)"), and criticize [knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge "Knowledge") claims. It helps solve theoretical [disagreements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disagreement_\(epistemology\) "Disagreement (epistemology)"), such as disputes about the ideal form of government. Methodological challenges arise from the evaluative or [normative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normativity "Normativity") nature of political philosophy as a discipline that studies desirable societal arrangements. Disagreements about normative claims often cannot be directly resolved through [observation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observation "Observation") and [experimentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimentation "Experimentation"), making them less tractable than disagreements about [empirical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical "Empirical") facts. Rational arguments can make a normative theory more plausible or compelling but are frequently not sufficient to lead to definitive or generally accepted solutions. Subjectivists conclude from this difficulty that political philosophy primarily [^18] expresses [subjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity_and_objectivity_\(philosophy\) "Subjectivity and objectivity (philosophy)") views without a universally accepted rational foundation.[^128]

Political philosophers sometimes start from [common sense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_sense "Common sense") and established beliefs, which they systematically and critically review to assess their validity. This process includes the [clarification of basic concepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conceptual_analysis "Conceptual analysis"), which can be used to formalize the underlying beliefs into precise theories while also considering arguments for and against them and exploring alternative views.[^129] The methodologies of particularism and foundationalism propose different approaches to this enterprise. Particularists use a [bottom-up approach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bottom-up_and_top-down_design "Bottom-up and top-down design") and take individual intuitions or assessments of specific circumstances as their starting point. They seek to systematize these individual judgments into a coherent theoretical framework. Foundationalists, by contrast, employ a top-down approach. They begin their inquiry from wide-reaching principles, such as the maxim of [classical utilitarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_utilitarianism "Classical utilitarianism"), which evaluates actions and policies based on the pain-pleasure balance they produce. Foundationalism aims to construct comprehensive systems of political thought from a small number of basic principles.[^130] The method of [reflective equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflective_equilibrium "Reflective equilibrium") forms a middle ground between particularism and foundationalism. It tries to reconcile general principles with individual intuitions to arrive at a balanced and coherent framework that incorporates the perspectives from both approaches.[^131]

A historically influential form of foundationalism grounds political ideologies in theories about [human nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_nature "Human nature"). It can take different forms, like reflections on human needs, abilities, and goals as well as the role of humans in the natural order or in a divine plan. Philosophers use these assumptions about human nature to infer political ideologies about the ideal form of government and other normative theories.[^132] For example, [Thomas Hobbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes "Thomas Hobbes") believed that the natural state of humans is a perpetual conflict, arguing that a strong state based on a general social contract is necessary to ensure stability and security.[^125] An influential criticism of foundationalist approaches centered on human nature argues that one cannot infer [normative claims from empirical facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Is%E2%80%93ought_problem "Is–ought problem"), meaning that empirical facts about human nature do not provide a secure foundation for normative theories about the right form of government.[^133]

Foundationalism is typically combined with [universalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universalism "Universalism"), which asserts that basic moral and political principles apply equally to every culture. Universalists suggest that the foundational values and standards of political action are the same for all societies and remain constant across historical periods. [Cultural relativism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_relativism "Cultural relativism") rejects this transcultural perspective, arguing that norms and values are inherently tied to specific cultures. This view asserts that political principles represent assumptions of specific communities and cannot serve as universal standards for evaluating other cultures.[^134]

[Methodological individualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methodological_individualism "Methodological individualism") and [holism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holism_in_science "Holism in science") are perspectives about the basic units of society. According to methodological individualism, societies are ultimately nothing but the individuals that comprise them. As a result, it analyzes political actions as the actions of the particular people who make decisions and participate within the social structure. This view sees collective entities, like states, nations, and other institutions, as mere byproducts of individual actions. Methodological holists, by contrast, argue for the irreducible existence of collective entities in addition to individuals. They contend that collective entities are more than the sum of their parts and see them as essential elements of political explanations.[^135]

Another methodological distinction is between [rationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalism "Rationalism") and [irrationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrationalism "Irrationalism"). Rationalists assume that universal [reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reason "Reason") is or should be the guiding principle underlying political action. They see reason as a common thread that unites diverse societies and can ensure peace between them. Irrationalists reject this assumption and focus on other factors influencing human behavior, including emotions, cultural traditions, and social expectations. Some irrationalists argue for [polylogism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polylogism "Polylogism"), the view that the laws of reason or logic are not universal but depend on cultural context, meaning that the same course of action may be rational from the perspective of one culture and irrational from another.[^136] [^19]

[Thought experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought_experiments "Thought experiments") are methodological devices in which political philosophers construct imagined situations to test the validity of political ideologies and explore alternative social arrangements. For example, in his thought experiment *[original position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Original_position "Original position")*, [John Rawls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Rawls "John Rawls") explores the underlying framework of a just society by imagining a situation in which individuals collectively decide the rules of their society. To ensure [impartiality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impartiality "Impartiality"), individuals do not know which position they will occupy in this society, a condition termed *veil of ignorance*.[^138]

Political philosophy has its roots in [antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_history "Ancient history"), and many foundational concepts of Western political thought emerged in [ancient Greek philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy "Ancient Greek philosophy"). Early influential contributions were made by the historian [Thucydides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thucydides "Thucydides") (c. 460 – c. 400 BCE), who inspired the school of realism by analyzing power relations and self-interest as central political factors.[^142] Inspired by the ideas of [Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates "Socrates") (470–399 BCE), [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") (428–348 BCE) discussed the role of the state, its relation to the citizens, the nature of justice, and forms of government. He was critical of democracy and favored a [utopian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utopian "Utopian") monarchy ruled by a wise and benevolent [philosopher king](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosopher_king "Philosopher king") to promote the common good.[^143] His student [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") (384–322 BCE) objected to Plato's utopianism, preferring a more practical approach to ensure [political stability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_stability "Political stability") and avoid [extremism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremism "Extremism"). He defended [perfectionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfectionism_\(philosophy\) "Perfectionism (philosophy)"), asserting that humans have an inborn goal to develop their [rational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationality "Rationality") and moral capacities and that the state should foster this tendency.[^144] In [Roman philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_philosophy "Roman philosophy"), the statesman [Cicero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cicero "Cicero") (106–43 BCE) infused earlier Greek philosophy with [Stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism "Stoicism"). He asserted that political action should be guided by [reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reason "Reason") rather than emotion and supported political participation following the [meritocratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meritocratic "Meritocratic") ideal of rule by the capable.[^145]

Diverse traditions of political thought also developed in [ancient China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_China "Ancient China"). [Confucianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucianism "Confucianism"), initiated by [Confucius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius "Confucius") (c. 551 – c. 479 BCE), saw the virtue of [humaneness or benevolence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren_\(philosophy\) "Ren (philosophy)") as the foundation of social order and norms. It sought to balance conflicting interests between [private](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_sphere "Private sphere") and [public spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_sphere "Public sphere"), seeing society as an extension of the family.[^139] [Taoism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoism "Taoism"), another tradition, focused on the relation between humans and nature, arguing that humans should act in harmony with [the natural order of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tao "Tao") while avoiding excessive desires. It is sometimes associated with anarchism because of its emphasis on natural order, spontaneity, and rejection of coercive authority.[^146] [Legalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legalism_\(Chinese_philosophy\) "Legalism (Chinese philosophy)"), a realist school of thought, proposed that effective governance of large states requires strict laws based on rewards and punishments to control the harmful effects of personal self-interest.[^147] In [ancient India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_India "Ancient India"), various social and political theories emerged in the 2nd millennium BCE, recorded in the *[Rig Veda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rig_Veda "Rig Veda")*, like the idea that the social order is naturally divided into [castes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caste_system_in_India "Caste system in India"), each fulfilling a different role in society.[^148] The *[Arthashastra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthashastra "Arthashastra")*, traditionally attributed to [Kautilya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chanakya "Chanakya") (375–283 BCE),[^149] was a political treatise on the essential components of states, such as the king, ministers, territory, and army, describing their nature and interaction.[^150] [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist "Buddhist") political thought, starting in the 6th and 5th centuries BCE, rejected the strict caste division of Hindu society, focusing instead on universal equality, [brotherhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sangha "Sangha"), and the reduction of everyone's [suffering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Du%E1%B8%A5kha "Duḥkha").[^151]

Political philosophy in the [medieval period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_period "Medieval period") was characterized by the interplay between ancient Greek thought and religion.[^152] [Augustine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustine "Augustine") (354–430 CE) saw states in the human world as fundamentally flawed compared to the divine ideal but also regarded them as vehicles for human improvement and the establishment of peace and order.[^153] Influenced by Augustine's philosophy, [Thomas Aquinas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas "Thomas Aquinas") (1225–1274 CE) developed [natural law theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_law_theory "Natural law theory") by synthesizing [Aristotelian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotelianism "Aristotelianism") and [Christian philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_philosophy "Christian philosophy"). He argued that law serves the common good, positing that God rules the world according to the [eternal law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eternal_law "Eternal law") while humans participate in this plan by following the natural law, which reflects the moral order and can be known directly.[^154]

In the Arabic–Persian tradition, philosophers sought to integrate Ancient Greek philosophy with [Islamic thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_philosophy "Islamic philosophy"). According to [Al-Farabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Farabi "Al-Farabi") (872–950), the state is a cooperative entity in which individuals voluntarily work together for common prosperity. Similar to Plato's vision, he imagines a hierarchical structure in which wise philosophers rule.[^155] [Al-Mawardi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Mawardi "Al-Mawardi") (972–1058) developed a complex theory of [caliphates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caliphates "Caliphates"), examining how this form of government combines religious and political authority in the person of the caliph.[^156] Following a descriptive approach, [Ibn Khaldun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun "Ibn Khaldun") (1332–1406) distinguished between natural states, which serve the worldly interests of the rulers, rational states, which serve the worldly interests of the people, and caliphates, which serve both worldly and [otherworldly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afterlife "Afterlife") interests of the people.[^140] Other influential contributions were made by [Avicenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avicenna "Avicenna") (980–1037), [Al-Ghazali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Ghazali "Al-Ghazali") (1058–1111), and [Averroes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Averroes "Averroes") (1126–1198).[^157] Meanwhile, in China, starting roughly 960 CE, [neo-Confucian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-Confucian "Neo-Confucian") thinkers argued for [decentralized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decentralized "Decentralized") governance. They identified two main functions of the government: to organize the social order and to [morally educate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moral_education "Moral education") citizens.[^158]

In [early modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_modern_philosophy "Early modern philosophy"), the medieval focus on religion was replaced by a [secular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularism "Secularism") outlook. The statesman [Niccolò Machiavelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli "Niccolò Machiavelli") (1469–1527), often viewed as the founder of modern political philosophy, defended a radical form of political realism, emphasizing the importance of power and pragmatic governance in which [the ends justify the means](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_ends_justify_the_means "The ends justify the means").[^104] [Thomas Hobbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes "Thomas Hobbes") (1588–1679) tried to provide a rational foundation for secular states. He argued that humans are naturally driven by [egoism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychological_egoism "Psychological egoism"), leading to a [war of all against all](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bellum_omnium_contra_omnes "Bellum omnium contra omnes") that can only be avoided through a state with [absolute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy "Absolute monarchy") centralized authority, justified by a common [social contract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_contract "Social contract").[^159] As a founder of liberalism, [John Locke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Locke "John Locke") (1632–1704) also based the state on the consent of the governed but prioritized individual freedom over state power. He suggested that humans are born free and equal, and that the primary objective of the state is to protect this natural condition.[^83] [David Hume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hume "David Hume") (1711–1776) rejected social contracts as the foundation of the state, asserting instead that governments typically evolve without a prior plan and are accepted by the people because of their utility.[^160] [Jean-Jacques Rousseau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau "Jean-Jacques Rousseau") (1712–1778) introduced the concept of the [general will](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_will "General will"), which is the will of the people to realize the common good.[^161] Influenced by Rousseau, [Immanuel Kant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant "Immanuel Kant") (1724–1804) argued that laws should reflect the general will of the people, asserting that every citizen has the fundamental right to freedom and the duty to uphold the social contract.[^162] [Edmund Burke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Burke "Edmund Burke") (1729–1797), often considered the father of conservatism, stressed the importance of the accumulated wisdom of past generations while opposing radical change, such as the [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution").[^78]

[Jeremy Bentham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeremy_Bentham "Jeremy Bentham") (1748–1832) developed [utilitarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utilitarianism "Utilitarianism"), promoting the greatest happiness for the greatest number of people. [John Stuart Mill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill "John Stuart Mill") (1806–1873) adapted this philosophy to support classical liberalism.[^163] Following ideas earlier formulated by [Montesquieu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montesquieu "Montesquieu") (1689–1755), [Alexis de Tocqueville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexis_de_Tocqueville "Alexis de Tocqueville") (1805–1859) warned of modern despotism in the form of a [tyranny of the majority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyranny_of_the_majority "Tyranny of the majority"), seeing it as a type of oppression in democracies that threatens the liberal goal of individual freedom.[^164] As a forerunner of [feminist political philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_political_theory "Feminist political theory"), [Mary Wollstonecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Wollstonecraft "Mary Wollstonecraft") (1759–1797) challenged the social subordination of women and argued for equal rights and access to education.[^165] According to [Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel "Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel") (1770–1831), the role of the state is the embodiment of ethical life and rational freedom, which he saw best realized in conservative, constitutional monarchies.[^166] Influenced by Hegel, [Karl Marx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx "Karl Marx") (1818–1883) and [Friedrich Engels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Engels "Friedrich Engels") (1820–1895) analyzed the economic forces and class conflicts in capitalist societies, calling for a [revolution to replace capitalism with socialism and communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_revolution "Communist revolution").[^92] Another radical reconceptualization of the social order was proposed by [Pierre-Joseph Proudhon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre-Joseph_Proudhon "Pierre-Joseph Proudhon") (1809–1865), often regarded as the father of [anarchism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchism "Anarchism"), who rejected state authority as an obstacle to liberty and equality.[^71] [^20]

In the 20th century, interest in political philosophy declined as a result of criticisms of its normative claims and a shifting interest towards the more descriptive discipline of [political science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science").[^167] [Max Weber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Weber "Max Weber") (1864–1920) defined states based on the justified use of force and distinguished different types of legitimate authority.[^168] A central topic in the philosophy of [Hannah Arendt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hannah_Arendt "Hannah Arendt") (1906–1975) was the nature of [totalitarian regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarian_regimes "Totalitarian regimes"), exemplified by [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") and Soviet [Stalinism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stalinism "Stalinism"). She highlighted both their ability to mobilize the population through simplistic ideologies and their use of terror as an [end in itself](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End_in_itself "End in itself").[^141] [John Rawls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Rawls "John Rawls") (1921–2002) explored the nature of [justice as fairness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justice_as_fairness "Justice as fairness") and examined the legitimate use of power in liberal democracies.[^60] Inspired by Rawls, [Robert Nozick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Nozick "Robert Nozick") (1938–2002) defended libertarianism, supporting a minimal state that protects individual rights and liberties.[^169] The [postmodern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodern_philosophy "Postmodern philosophy") thinker [Michel Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Foucault "Michel Foucault") (1926–1984) analyzed power dynamics within society, with particular interest in how societal institutions, such as [medical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical "Medical") and [correctional institutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correctional_institution "Correctional institution"), shape human behavior through [the interplay of knowledge and power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power-knowledge "Power-knowledge").[^170] In [Indian political philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_political_philosophy "Indian political philosophy"), [Mahatma Gandhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi "Mahatma Gandhi") (1869–1948) argued for [self-rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swaraj "Swaraj") and [nonviolent resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satyagraha "Satyagraha") to [colonialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonialism "Colonialism") while seeking to dismantle the caste system to achieve equality.[^171] [Sri Aurobindo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sri_Aurobindo "Sri Aurobindo") (1872–1950) advocated for a [spiritual nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_nationalism "Religious nationalism"), which formed part of his broader philosophical worldview describing the evolution of humanity and a future world-union.[^172] In China, [Marxism was reinterpreted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Marxism "Chinese Marxism") under [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") (1893–1976) and combined with Confucian thought, considering the [peasantry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peasantry "Peasantry") rather than the working class as the main force behind the communist revolution.[^173] In the [Islamic world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_world "Islamic world"), [Islamic modernism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_modernism "Islamic modernism") sought to reconcile traditional Muslim teachings with [modernity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernity "Modernity").[^174]
