The **executive** is the part of the government that executes or enforces the law and policy of a government. It can be organised as a [branch of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branch_of_government "Branch of government"), as in [liberal democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_democracy "Liberal democracy"), or as an organ of the [unified state apparatus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unified_state_apparatus "Unified state apparatus"), as is the case in [communist states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_state "Communist state").

## Function

The scope of executive power varies greatly depending on the political context in which it emerges, and it can change over time in a given country. They are usually laid out in a [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution "Constitution") In democratic countries, the executive often exercises broad influence over national politics, though limitations are often applied to the executive.[^1]

In [political systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_system "Political system") based on the [separation of powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_powers "Separation of powers"), government [authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authority "Authority") is distributed between several branches to prevent power from being concentrated in the hands of a single person or group. To achieve this, each branch is subject to checks by the other branches; in general, the executive or the [legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature "Legislature") can propose laws, the legislature can pass or block laws, laws are then enforced by the executive, and interpreted by the [judiciary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judiciary "Judiciary"). The executive can also be the source of certain types of law or law-derived rules, such as a [decree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decree "Decree"), [executive order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_order "Executive order"), or [delegated legislation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delegated_legislation "Delegated legislation"). For example, the [U.S. federal legislative branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Congress "United States Congress") frequently delegates [rulemaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rulemaking "Rulemaking") (creation of [regulations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_of_Federal_Regulations "Code of Federal Regulations")) to the [U.S. federal executive branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States#Executive_branch "Federal government of the United States").[^2]

In those that have [fusion of powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fusion_of_powers "Fusion of powers"), typically [parliamentary systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system"), such as the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom"), the executive is often referred to as the 'government', and its members generally belong to the political party that controls the legislature. Since the executive requires the support and approval of the legislature, the two bodies are "fused" together, rather than being independent. The principle of [parliamentary sovereignty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty "Parliamentary sovereignty") means powers possessed by the executive are solely dependent on those granted by the legislature, which can also subject its actions to judicial review. However, the executive often has wide-ranging powers stemming from the control of the government [bureaucracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureaucracy "Bureaucracy"), especially in the areas of overall [economic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_policy "Economic policy") or [foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy "Foreign policy").

## Political creation

Vanhanen II Cabinet in a session of the Finnish Parliament, 2007

In [parliamentary systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system"), the executive is [responsible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_of_no_confidence "Motion of no confidence") to the elected legislature, and must maintain the confidence of the legislature, or one part of it, if bicameral. In certain circumstances (varying by state), the legislature can express its lack of confidence in the executive, which causes either a change in the governing party or group of parties or a general election.

Parliamentary systems have a head of government (who leads the executive, often called [ministers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_\(government\) "Minister (government)")) normally distinct from the head of state (who continues through governmental and electoral changes). In the [Westminster type of parliamentary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westminster_system "Westminster system"), the principle of separation of powers is not as entrenched as in some others.

Members of the executive (ministers) are also members of the legislature, and hence play an important part in both the writing and enforcing of law. In [presidential systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_system "Presidential system"), the directly elected head of government appoints the ministers. Ministers can be directly elected by voters, such as in Switzerland at the cantonal (state) level.[^3]

## Internal organization and personnel

In a [presidential system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_system "Presidential system"), the leader of the executive is both the head of state and government.[^4] In some cases, such as [South Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Korea "South Korea"), there is a Prime Minister who assists the President, but who is not the head of government but is the second in the chain.

In a [parliamentary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system "Parliamentary system"), a [cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_\(government\) "Cabinet (government)") [minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_\(government\) "Minister (government)") responsible to the [legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature "Legislature") is the head of government, while the head of state is usually a largely ceremonial monarch or president.[^5]

The top leadership roles of the executive branch may include:

- [head of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_government "Head of government") – often the [prime minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_minister "Prime minister"), overseeing the [administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Administration_\(government\) "Administration (government)") of all affairs of state.
	- [defence minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defence_minister "Defence minister") – overseeing the [armed forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armed_forces "Armed forces"), determining military policy, and managing external safety.
		- [interior minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_minister "Interior minister") – overseeing the [police forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Police_forces "Police forces"), enforcing the law, and managing internal control.
		- [foreign minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_minister "Foreign minister") – overseeing the [diplomatic service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_service "Diplomatic service"), determining [foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy "Foreign policy") and managing [foreign relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations "Foreign relations").
		- [finance minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finance_minister "Finance minister") – overseeing the treasury, determining [fiscal policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiscal_policy "Fiscal policy") and managing [national budget](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_budget "National budget").
		- [justice minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justice_minister "Justice minister") – overseeing criminal prosecutions, corrections, enforcement of court orders.
