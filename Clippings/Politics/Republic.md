A **republic**, based on the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") phrase *[res publica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Res_publica "Res publica")* ('public thing' or 'people's thing'), is a [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)") in which [political power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_\(social_and_political\) "Power (social and political)") rests with the [public](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public "Public") ([people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People "People")), typically through their [representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_assembly "Representative assembly") —in contrast to a [monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy "Monarchy").[^1] [^2] Although a republic is most often a single [sovereign state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state "Sovereign state"), [subnational state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subnational_state "Subnational state") entities that have governments that are republican in nature may be referred to as republics.

As of 2017, [159](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_system_of_government "List of countries by system of government") of the world's [206 sovereign states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states "List of sovereign states") use the word "republic" as part of their official names. Not all of these are republics in the sense of having elected governments, nor is the word "republic" used in the names of all states with elected governments.

The term developed its modern meaning in reference to the constitution of the ancient [Roman Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic "Roman Republic"), from the [overthrow of the kings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overthrow_of_the_Roman_monarchy "Overthrow of the Roman monarchy") in 509 [BC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anno_Domini "Anno Domini") to the establishment of the [Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") in 27 BC. This [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Roman_Republic "Constitution of the Roman Republic") was characterized by a [Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Senate "Roman Senate") composed of wealthy [aristocrats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristocracy "Aristocracy") wielding significant influence; several popular [assemblies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_assemblies "Roman assemblies") of all free citizens, possessing the power to elect magistrates from the populace and pass laws; and a [series of magistracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cursus_honorum "Cursus honorum") with varying types of civil and political authority.

## Etymology

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Bust_of_Cicero_%281st-cent._BC%29_-_Palazzo_Nuovo_-_Musei_Capitolini_-_Rome_2016.jpg/250px-Bust_of_Cicero_%281st-cent._BC%29_-_Palazzo_Nuovo_-_Musei_Capitolini_-_Rome_2016.jpg)

Sculpture of Cicero

The term originates from the Latin translation of [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") word *[politeia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politeia "Politeia")*. [Cicero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cicero "Cicero"), among other Latin writers, translated *politeia* into Latin as *[res publica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Res_publica "Res publica")*, and it was in turn translated by Renaissance scholars as *republic* (or similar terms in various European languages).[^3] [^4] The term can literally be translated as 'public matter'.[^5] It was used by Roman writers to refer to the state and government, even during the period of the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire").[^6]

The term *politeia* can be translated as [form of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government"), [polity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polity "Polity"), or [regime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regime "Regime"), and it does not necessarily imply any specific type of regime as the modern word *republic* sometimes does. One of [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") 's major works on political philosophy, usually known in English as *[The Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Republic_\(Plato\) "The Republic (Plato)")*, was titled *Politeia*. However, apart from the title, modern translations are generally used.[^7] [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") was apparently the first classical writer to state that the term *politeia* can be used to refer more specifically to one type of *politeia*, asserting in Book III of his *[Politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_\(Aristotle\) "Politics (Aristotle)")*: "When the citizens at large govern for the public good, it is called by the name common to all governments (*to koinon onoma pasōn tōn politeiōn*), government (*politeia*)". In later Latin works the term *republic* can also be used in a general way to refer to any regime, or to refer specifically to governments which work for the public good.[^8]

In medieval [Northern Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Italy "Northern Italy"), a number of city states had [commune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_commune "Medieval commune") or [signoria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signoria "Signoria") based governments. In the late Middle Ages, writers such as [Giovanni Villani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giovanni_Villani "Giovanni Villani") described these states using terms such as *libertas populi*, a free people. The terminology changed in the 15th century as the renewed interest in the writings of [Ancient Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") caused writers to prefer classical terminology. To describe non-monarchical states, writers (most importantly, [Leonardo Bruni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonardo_Bruni "Leonardo Bruni")) adopted the Latin phrase *[res publica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Res_publica "Res publica")*.[^9]

While Bruni and [Machiavelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machiavelli "Machiavelli") used the term to describe the states of Northern Italy, which were not monarchies, the term *res publica* has a set of interrelated meanings in the original Latin. In subsequent centuries, the English word *[commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth "Commonwealth")* came to be used as a translation of *res publica*, and its use in English was comparable to how the Romans used the term *res publica*.[^10] Notably, during [The Protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Protectorate "The Protectorate") of [Oliver Cromwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell "Oliver Cromwell") the word *commonwealth* was the most common term to call the new monarchless state, but the word *republic* was also in common use.[^11]

## History

While the philosophical terminology developed in [classical Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Greece "Classical Greece") and [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic "Roman Republic"), as already noted by [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") there was already a long history of city states with a wide variety of constitutions, not only in Greece but also in the [Middle East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_East "Middle East"). After the classical period, during the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), many free cities developed again, such as [Venice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Venice "Republic of Venice").

Since the [Age of Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Revolution "Age of Revolution") the term *republic* has described mainly states with a written constitution [^12] and the legitimacy of its officials derives from the consent of the people rather than [heredity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heredity "Heredity") or [divine right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings "Divine right of kings").[^13]

### Classical republics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Republica_Romana.svg/250px-Republica_Romana.svg.png)

A map of the Roman Republic in 45 BC

Ancient and modern republics differ in many ways, like the use of representative assemblies (modern) in opposition to popular (ancient) assemblies to pass legislation.[^14] [^15] Ancient republics include ancient [Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens "Classical Athens") and the [Roman Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic "Roman Republic"). The structure and governance of these states was different from that of any modern republic, so there is debate about the extent to which classical, medieval, and modern republics form a historical continuum. [J. G. A. Pocock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._G._A._Pocock "J. G. A. Pocock") has argued that a distinct republican tradition stretches from the classical world to the present.[^5] [^16] Other scholars disagree.[^5] Paul Rahe, for instance, argues that the classical republics had a form of government with few links to those in any modern country.[^17]

The political philosophy of the classical republics has influenced republican thought throughout the subsequent centuries. Philosophers and politicians advocating republics, such as [Machiavelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli "Niccolò Machiavelli"), [Montesquieu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montesquieu "Montesquieu"), [Adams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Adams "John Adams"), and [Madison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Madison "James Madison"), relied heavily on classical Greek and Roman sources which described various types of regimes.

[Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") 's *[Politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_\(Aristotle\) "Politics (Aristotle)")* discusses various forms of government. One form Aristotle named *politeia*, which consisted of a mixture of the other forms, [oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy "Oligarchy") and [democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy"). He argued that this was one of the ideal forms of government. [Polybius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polybius "Polybius") expanded on many of these ideas, again focusing on the idea of [mixed government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_government "Mixed government") and differentiated basic forms of government between "benign" [monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy "Monarchy"), [aristocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristocracy "Aristocracy"), and democracy, and the "malignant" [tyranny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyranny "Tyranny"), oligarchy, and [ochlocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ochlocracy "Ochlocracy"). The most important Roman work in this tradition is Cicero's *[De re publica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_re_publica "De re publica")*.

Over time, the classical republics became empires or were conquered by empires. Most of the Greek republics were annexed to the [Macedonian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macedonian_Empire "Macedonian Empire") of [Alexander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great"). The Roman Republic expanded dramatically, conquering the other states of the Mediterranean that could be considered republics, such as [Carthage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carthage "Carthage"). The Roman Republic itself then became the Roman Empire.

### Other ancient republics

The term *republic* is not commonly used to refer to pre-classical city-states, especially if outside Europe and the area which was under Graeco-Roman influence.[^5] However some early states outside Europe had governments that are sometimes today considered similar to republics.

In the [ancient West Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Near_East "Ancient Near East"), a number of cities of the [Eastern Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean "Eastern Mediterranean") achieved collective rule. Republic city-states flourished in [Phoenicia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia") along the [Levantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant") coast starting from the 11th century BC. In ancient Phoenicia, the concept of [Shophet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shophet "Shophet") was very similar to a [Roman consul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consul "Consul"). Under [Persian rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persia "Persia") (539–332 BC), Phoenician city-states such as [Tyre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyre,_Lebanon "Tyre, Lebanon") abolished the king system and adopted "a system of the [suffetes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shophet "Shophet") (judges), who remained in power for short mandates of 6 years".[^18] [^19] [Arwad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arwad "Arwad") has been cited as one of the earliest known examples of a republic, in which the people, rather than a monarch, are described as sovereign.[^20] The [Israelite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israelite "Israelite") confederation of the era of the [Judges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biblical_judges "Biblical judges") [^21] before the [United Monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Monarchy "United Monarchy") has also been considered a type of republic.[^5] [^22] [^23] The system of government of the [Igbo people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igbo_people#Traditional_society "Igbo people") in what is now [Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_history "Nigerian history") has been described as "direct and participatory democracy".[^24]

### Indian subcontinent

Early republican institutions come from the independent [*gaṇasaṅgha* s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ga%E1%B9%87asa%E1%B9%85gha "Gaṇasaṅgha") — [*gaṇa*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gana "Gana") means 'tribe' and [*saṅgha*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sangha "Sangha") means 'assembly'—which may have existed as early as the 6th century BC and persisted in some areas until the 4th century AD in India. The evidence for this is scattered, however, and no pure historical source exists for that period. [Diodorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diodorus "Diodorus"), a Greek historian who wrote two centuries after the time of [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") 's invasion of India (now Pakistan and northwest India) mentions, without offering any detail, that independent and democratic states existed in India.[^25] Modern scholars note the word *democracy* at the time of the 3rd century BC and later suffered from degradation and could mean any autonomous state, no matter how aristocratic in nature.[^26] [^27]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Mahajanapadas_%28c._500_BCE%29.png/250px-Mahajanapadas_%28c._500_BCE%29.png)

The Mahajanapadas were the sixteen most powerful and vast kingdoms and republics of the era; there were also a number of smaller kingdoms stretching the length and breadth of Ancient India. Among the mahajanapadas and smaller states, the Shakyas, Koliyas Mallakas, and Licchavis followed republican government.

Key characteristics of the *gaṇa* seem to include a *gaṇa mukhya* (chief), and a deliberative assembly. The assembly met regularly. It discussed all major state decisions. At least in some states, attendance was open to all free men. This body also had full financial, administrative, and judicial authority. Other officers, who rarely receive any mention, obeyed the decisions of the assembly. Elected by the *gaṇa*, the chief apparently always belonged to a family of the noble class of *[Kshatriya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kshatriya "Kshatriya") [Varna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varna_\(Hinduism\) "Varna (Hinduism)")*. The chief coordinated his activities with the assembly; in some states, he did so with a council of other nobles.[^28] The [Licchavis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Licchavi_\(tribe\) "Licchavi (tribe)") had a primary governing body of 7,077 *gaṇa mukhyas*, the heads of the most important families. On the other hand, the [Shakyas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shakya "Shakya"), [Koliyas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koliya "Koliya"), [Mallakas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malla_\(tribe\) "Malla (tribe)"), and [Licchavis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Licchavi_\(tribe\) "Licchavi (tribe)"), during the period around [Gautama Buddha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Buddha "The Buddha"), had the assembly open to all men, rich and poor.[^29] Early republics or [*gaṇasaṅgha*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ga%E1%B9%87asa%E1%B9%85gha "Gaṇasaṅgha"),[^30] such as Mallakas, centered in the city of [Kusinagara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kusinagara "Kusinagara"), and the [Vajjika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vajjika_League "Vajjika League") (or Vṛjika) League, centered in the city of [Vaishali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaishali_\(ancient_city\) "Vaishali (ancient city)"), existed as early as the 6th century BC and persisted in some areas until the 4th century AD.[^31] The most famous clan amongst the ruling confederate clans of the Vajji [Mahajanapada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahajanapadas "Mahajanapadas") were the Licchavis.[^32] The [Empire of Magadha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magadha_\(Mahajanapada\) "Magadha (Mahajanapada)") included republican communities such as the community of Rajakumara. Villages had their own assemblies under their local chiefs called *gramakas*. Their administrations were divided into executive, judicial, and military functions.

Scholars differ over how best to describe these governments, and the vague, sporadic quality of the evidence allows for wide disagreements. Some emphasize the central role of the assemblies and thus tout them as democracies; other scholars focus on the upper-class domination of the leadership and possible control of the assembly and see an [aristocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristocracy "Aristocracy").[^33] [^34] Despite the assembly's obvious power, it has not yet been established whether the composition and participation were truly popular. This is reflected in the *[Arthashastra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthashastra "Arthashastra")*, an ancient handbook for monarchs on how to rule efficiently. It contains a chapter on how to deal with the *saṅgha* *s*, which includes injunctions on manipulating the noble leaders, yet it does not mention how to influence the mass of the citizens, indicating that the *gaṇasaṅgha* are more of an aristocratic republic, than democracy.[^35]

### Icelandic Commonwealth

The Icelandic Commonwealth was established in 930 AD by refugees from [Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway "Norway") who had fled the unification of that country under King [Harald Fairhair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harald_Fairhair "Harald Fairhair"). The Commonwealth consisted of a number of clans run by chieftains, and the [Althing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Althing "Althing") was a combination of parliament and supreme court where disputes appealed from lower courts were settled, laws were decided, and decisions of national importance were taken. One such example was the [Christianisation of Iceland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianisation_of_Iceland "Christianisation of Iceland") in 1000, where the Althing decreed that all Icelanders must be baptized into Christianity, and forbade celebration of pagan rituals. Contrary to most states, the Icelandic Commonwealth had no official leader.

In the early 13th century, the [Age of the Sturlungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_Sturlungs "Age of the Sturlungs"), the Commonwealth began to suffer from long conflicts between warring clans. This, combined with pressure from the Norwegian king [Haakon IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haakon_IV "Haakon IV") for the Icelanders to rejoin the Norwegian "family", led the Icelandic chieftains to accept Haakon IV as king by the signing of the *Gamli sáttmáli* (" [Old Covenant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Covenant_\(Iceland\) "Old Covenant (Iceland)") ") in 1262. This effectively brought the Commonwealth to an end. The Althing, however, is still Iceland's parliament, almost 800 years later.[^36]

### Mercantile republics

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Giambattista_Tiepolo_-_Venezia_riceve_l%27omaggio_di_Nettuno_-_1745-50.jpg/250px-Giambattista_Tiepolo_-_Venezia_riceve_l%27omaggio_di_Nettuno_-_1745-50.jpg)

Giovanni Battista Tiepolo, Neptune offers the wealth of the sea to Venice, 1748–1750. This painting is an allegory of the power of the Republic of Venice.

In Europe new republics appeared in the late Middle Ages when a number of small states embraced republican systems of government. These were generally small, but wealthy, trading states, like the Mediterranean [maritime republics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maritime_republics "Maritime republics") and the [Hanseatic League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanseatic_League "Hanseatic League"), in which the merchant class had risen to prominence. [Knud Haakonssen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knud_Haakonssen "Knud Haakonssen") has noted that, by the [Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Renaissance "Italian Renaissance"), Europe was divided with those states controlled by a landed elite being monarchies and those controlled by a commercial elite being republics.[^10]

Italy was the most densely populated area of Europe, and also one with the weakest central government. Many of the towns thus gained considerable independence and adopted commune forms of government. Completely free of feudal control, the Italian city-states expanded, gaining control of the rural hinterland.[^37] The two most powerful were the [Republic of Venice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Venice "Republic of Venice") and its rival the [Republic of Genoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa "Republic of Genoa"). Each were large trading ports, and further expanded by using naval power to control large parts of the Mediterranean. It was in Italy that an ideology advocating for republics first developed. Writers such as [Bartholomew of Lucca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartholomew_of_Lucca "Bartholomew of Lucca"), [Brunetto Latini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brunetto_Latini "Brunetto Latini"), [Marsilius of Padua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marsilius_of_Padua "Marsilius of Padua"), and Leonardo Bruni saw the medieval city-states as heirs to the legacy of Greece and Rome.

Across Europe a wealthy merchant class developed in the important trading cities. Despite their wealth they had little power in the [feudal system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism "Feudalism") dominated by the rural land owners, and across Europe began to advocate for their own privileges and powers. The more centralized states, such as France and England, granted limited city charters.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Commencement_r%C3%A9publique_messine_Auguste_Migette_1862.jpg/250px-Commencement_r%C3%A9publique_messine_Auguste_Migette_1862.jpg)

Beginning of the Republic of Metz. Election of the first Head-Alderman in 1289, by Auguste Migette. was then a free imperial city of the Holy Roman Emperor.

In the more loosely governed [Holy Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire "Holy Roman Empire"), 51 of the largest towns became [free imperial cities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_imperial_city "Free imperial city"). While still under the dominion of the [Holy Roman Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor "Holy Roman Emperor") most power was held locally and many adopted republican forms of government.[^37] The same rights to imperial immediacy were secured by the major trading cities of Switzerland. The towns and villages of alpine [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland") had, courtesy of geography, also been largely excluded from central control. Unlike Italy and Germany, much of the rural area was thus not controlled by feudal barons, but by independent farmers who also used communal forms of government. When the [Habsburgs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habsburgs "Habsburgs") tried to reassert control over the region both rural farmers and town merchants joined the rebellion. The [Swiss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_people "Swiss people") were victorious, and the [Swiss Confederacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_Confederacy "Swiss Confederacy") was proclaimed, and Switzerland has retained a republican form of government to the present.[^23]

Two Russian cities with a powerful merchant class— [Novgorod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Novgorod_Republic "Novgorod Republic") and [Pskov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pskov_Republic "Pskov Republic") —also adopted republican forms of government in 12th and 13th centuries, respectively, which ended when the republics were conquered by [Muscovy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Moscow "Grand Duchy of Moscow") / [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsardom_of_Russia "Tsardom of Russia") at the end of 15th – beginning of 16th century.[^38]

Following the collapse of the [Seljuk Sultanate of Rum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seljuk_Sultanate_of_Rum "Seljuk Sultanate of Rum") and establishment of the [Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey") [Anatolian Beyliks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolian_Beyliks "Anatolian Beyliks"), the [Ahiler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahiler "Ahiler") merchant fraternities established a state centered on [Ankara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ankara "Ankara") that is sometimes compared to the Italian mercantile republics.

The dominant form of government for these early republics was control by a limited council of elite [patricians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrician_\(ancient_Rome\) "Patrician (ancient Rome)"). In those areas that held elections, property qualifications or guild membership limited both who could vote and who could run. In many states no direct elections were held and council members were hereditary or appointed by the existing council. This left the great majority of the population without political power, and riots and revolts by the lower classes were common. The late Middle Ages saw more than 200 such risings in the towns of the Holy Roman Empire.[^39] Similar revolts occurred in Italy, notably the [Ciompi Revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ciompi_Revolt "Ciompi Revolt") in Florence.

### Calvinist republics

While the classical writers had been the primary ideological source for the republics of Italy, in Northern Europe, the [Protestant Reformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestant_Reformation "Protestant Reformation") would be used as justification for establishing new republics.[^40] Most important was [Calvinist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calvinist "Calvinist") theology, which developed in the Swiss Confederacy, one of the largest and most powerful of the medieval republics. [John Calvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Calvin "John Calvin") did not call for the abolition of monarchy, but he advanced the doctrine that the faithful had the duty to overthrow irreligious monarchs.[^41] Advocacy for republics appeared in the writings of the [Huguenots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huguenots "Huguenots") during the [French Wars of Religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Wars_of_Religion "French Wars of Religion").[^42]

Calvinism played an important role in the republican revolts in England and the Netherlands. Like the city-states of Italy and the Hanseatic League, both were important trading centres, with a large merchant class prospering from the trade with the New World. Large parts of the population of both areas also embraced Calvinism. During the [Dutch Revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_Revolt "Dutch Revolt") (beginning in 1566), the [Dutch Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_Republic "Dutch Republic") emerged from rejection of [Spanish Habsburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habsburg_Spain "Habsburg Spain") rule. However, the country did not adopt the republican form of government immediately: in the formal declaration of independence ([Act of Abjuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Act_of_Abjuration "Act of Abjuration"), 1581), the throne of [King Philip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain "Philip II of Spain") was only declared vacant, and the Dutch magistrates asked [Francis, Duke of Anjou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis,_Duke_of_Anjou "Francis, Duke of Anjou"), Queen [Elizabeth of England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England "Elizabeth I of England") and Prince [William of Orange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_the_Silent "William the Silent"), one after another, to replace Philip. It took until 1588 before the [Estates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Estates "The Estates") (the *Staten*, the representative assembly at the time) decided to vest the sovereignty of the country in themselves.

In 1641 the [English Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Civil_War "English Civil War") began. Spearheaded by the [Puritans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puritans "Puritans") and funded by the merchants of London, the revolt was a success, and [King Charles I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_I_of_England "Charles I of England") was executed. In England [James Harrington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Harrington_\(author\) "James Harrington (author)"), [Algernon Sidney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algernon_Sidney "Algernon Sidney"), and [John Milton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Milton "John Milton") became some of the first writers to argue for rejecting monarchy and embracing a republican form of government. The [English Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Commonwealth "English Commonwealth") was short-lived, and the monarchy was soon restored. The Dutch Republic continued in name until 1795, but by the mid-18th century the [stadtholder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stadtholder "Stadtholder") had become a *de facto* monarch. Calvinists were also some of the earliest settlers of the British and Dutch colonies of North America.
