**Policy studies** is a subdiscipline of [political science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science") that includes the analysis of the process of policymaking (the policy process) and the contents of policy ([policy analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Policy_analysis "Policy analysis")).[^1] Policy analysis includes substantive area research (such as health or education policy), [program evaluation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Program_evaluation "Program evaluation") and impact studies, and policy design.[^2] It "involves systematically studying the nature, causes, and effects of alternative [public policies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policies "Public policies"), with particular emphasis on determining the [policies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Policies "Policies") that will achieve given goals." [^3] It emerged in the United States in the 1960s and 1970s.

Policy studies also examines the conflicts and conflict resolution that arise from the making of policies in civil society, the private sector, or more commonly, in the public sector (e.g. government).

While policy studies frequently focuses on the [public sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_sector "Public sector"), it is applicable to other organizations (e.g., the not-for-profit sector). Some policy study experts graduate from [public policy schools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policy_school "Public policy school") with [public policy degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policy_degrees "Public policy degrees"). Alternatively, experts may have backgrounds in policy analysis, [program evaluation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Program_evaluation "Program evaluation"), [sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology "Sociology"), [psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychology "Psychology"), [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy"), [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics"), [anthropology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropology "Anthropology"), [geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography "Geography"), [law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law "Law"), [political science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science"), [social work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_work "Social work"), [environmental planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_planning "Environmental planning") and [public administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_administration "Public administration").

Traditionally, the field of policy studies focused on domestic policy, with the notable exceptions of [foreign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy "Foreign policy") and [defense policies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defense_policy "Defense policy"). However, the wave of [economic globalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_globalization "Economic globalization"), which ensued in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, created a need for a subset of policy studies that focuses on [global governance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_governance "Global governance"), especially as it relates to issues that transcend [national borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_borders "National borders") such as [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change"), [terrorism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrorism "Terrorism"), [nuclear proliferation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_proliferation "Nuclear proliferation"), and [economic development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_development "Economic development"). This subset of policy studies, which is often referred to as international policy studies, typically requires mastery of a [second language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_language "Second language") and attention to [cross-cultural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-cultural "Cross-cultural") issues in order to address [national](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation "Nation") and [cultural biases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_bias "Cultural bias").
