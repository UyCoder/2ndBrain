A **hybrid regime** [^1] is a type of [political system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_system "Political system") often created as a result of an incomplete [democratic transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_transition "Democratic transition") from an [authoritarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarian "Authoritarian") regime to a [democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") one (or vice versa).[^2] Hybrid regimes are categorized as having a combination of [autocratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocratic "Autocratic") features with democratic ones and can simultaneously hold [political repressions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_repression "Political repression") and regular [elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election "Election"). According to some definitions and measures, hybrid regimes are commonly found in [developing countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_countries "Developing countries") with abundant natural resources such as [petro-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petro-state "Petro-state").[^13] [^11] [^14] Although these regimes experience [civil unrest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_unrest "Civil unrest"), they may be relatively stable and tenacious for decades at a time. There has been a rise in hybrid regimes since the end of the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War").[^15] [^16]

The term *hybrid regime* arises from a polymorphic view of political regimes that oppose the dichotomy of [autocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy") or [democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy").[^17] Modern scholarly analysis of hybrid regimes focuses attention on the decorative nature of [democratic institutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_institution "Democratic institution") (elections do not lead to a change of power, different media broadcast the government point of view and the opposition in [parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament "Parliament") votes the same way as the ruling party, among others),[^18] from which it is concluded that [democratic backsliding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_backsliding "Democratic backsliding"), a transition to authoritarianism, is the most prevalent basis of hybrid regimes.[^19] Some scholars also contend that hybrid regimes may imitate a full [dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictatorship "Dictatorship").[^20] [^21]

Overall, there is no consensus among researchers about how hybrid regimes should be defined or measured. Accordingly, there is much disagreement about which countries are considered to be hybrid regimes, and any description of what typical hybrid regimes look like needs to be seen in the context of specific definitions and measures.[^22]

## Definition

Scholars vary on the definition of hybrid regimes based on their primary [academic discipline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_discipline "Academic discipline").[^23] According to Christoph Mohamad-Klotzbach, "Some scholars argue that deficient democracies and deficient autocracies can be seen as examples of hybrid regimes, whereas others argue that hybrid regimes combine characteristics of both democratic and autocratic regimes." [^6] Scholars also debate if these regimes are in transition or are inherently a stable political system.[^24] [^25] [^26] [^27] [^28] [^29] [^30] [^31] In 1995, [Terry Karl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terry_Karl "Terry Karl") introduced the notion of "hybrid" regime, which was simply defined as "combining democratic and authoritarian elements".[^32] According to professor [Matthijs Bogaards](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Matthijs_Bogaards&action=edit&redlink=1 "Matthijs Bogaards (page does not exist)") hybrid types are:[^33]

> not diminished subtypes, since they do not lack the full development of a characteristic, but rather they exhibit a mixture of characteristics of both basic types, so that they simultaneously combine autocratic and democratic dimensions or institutions

[Pippa Norris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pippa_Norris "Pippa Norris") defined hybrid regimes as:[^34]

> a system characterized by weak checks and balances on executive powers, flawed or even suspended elections, fragmented opposition forces, state restrictions on media freedoms, intellectuals, and civil society organizations, curbs on the independence of the judiciary and disregard for rule of law, the abuse of human rights by the security forces, and tolerance of authoritarian values.

[Henry E. Hale](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Henry_E._Hale&action=edit&redlink=1 "Henry E. Hale (page does not exist)") defined hybrid regimes as:[^35]

> a political regime that combines some democratic and some autocratic elements in a significant manner. It is not, however, a mere half-way category: hybrid regimes have their own distinct dynamics that do not simply amount to half of what we would see in a democracy plus half of what we would see in an autocracy.

[Leonardo Morlino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonardo_Morlino "Leonardo Morlino") defined hybrid regimes as:[^36]

> a set of institutions that have been persistent, be they stable or unstable, for about a decade, have been preceded by authoritarianism, a traditional regime (possibly with colonial characteristics), or even a minimal democracy and are characterized by the break-up of limited pluralism and forms of independent, autonomous participation, but the absence of at least one of the four aspects of a minimal democracy

Professor [Jeffrey C. Isaac](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jeffrey_C._Isaac&action=edit&redlink=1 "Jeffrey C. Isaac (page does not exist)") defined hybrid regimes as:[^37]

> Hybrid regimes have the common feature that they all have competition, although the political elite in power deliberately rearranges state regulations and the political arena as to grant itself undue advantages

### History

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Countries_democratizing_or_autocratizing_substantially_and_significantly_2010%E2%80%932020.svg/500px-Countries_democratizing_or_autocratizing_substantially_and_significantly_2010%E2%80%932020.svg.png)

Countries autocratizing (red) or democratizing (blue) substantially and significantly (2010–2020). Countries in grey are substantially unchanged. 38

The [third wave of democratization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_wave_of_democratization "Third wave of democratization") from the 1970s onward has led to the emergence of hybrid regimes that are neither fully democratic nor fully authoritarian.[^39] Neither the concept of [illiberal democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illiberal_democracy "Illiberal democracy"), nor the concept of electoral authoritarianism fully describes these hybrid regimes.[^40] [^41]

Since the end of the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), such regimes have become the most common among undemocratic countries.[^42] [^43] At the end of the process of transformation of authoritarian regimes, limited elections appear in one way or another when [liberalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberalization "Liberalization") occurs. Liberal democracy has always been assumed while in practice this process basically froze "halfway".[^44] In relation to regimes that were previously called "transitional" in the 1980s, the term *hybrid regime* began to be used and was strengthened according to [Thomas Carothers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Carothers "Thomas Carothers"):

> the majority of “transitional countries” are neither completely dictatorial nor aspiring to democracy, and by and large they cannot be called transitional. They are located in the politically stable gray zone, changes in which may not take place for decades. Thus, he stated that hybrid regimes must be considered without the assumption that they will ultimately become democracies. These hybrid regimes were called semi-authoritarianism or electoral authoritarianism.[^45]

Hybrid regimes have evolved to lean more authoritarian while keeping some democratic traits.[^46] One of the main issues with authoritarian rule is the ability to control the threats from the masses, and democratic elements in hybrid regimes can reduce social tension between the masses and the elite.[^47] After the [third wave of democratization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_wave_of_democratization "Third wave of democratization"), some regimes became stuck in the transition to democracy, causing the creation of weak democratic institutions.[^48] This results from a lack of institutional ownership during critical points in the transition period leading the regime into a gray zone between democracy and autocracy.[^49]

These developments have caused some scholars to believe that hybrid regimes are not poorly functioning democracies, but rather new forms of [authoritarian regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarian_regimes "Authoritarian regimes").[^50] Defective democratic stability is an indicator to explain and measure these new forms of autocracies.[^51] Additionally, approval ratings of political leaders play an important role in these types of regimes, and democratic elements can drive up the ratings of a [strongman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_strongman "Political strongman") leader creating a tool not utilized previously.[^52] Today, 'hybrid regime' is a term used to explain a growing field of political development where authoritarian leaders incorporate elements of democracy that stabilize their regimes.[^53]

## Indicators

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/BTI_2022_DEM.jpg/500px-BTI_2022_DEM.jpg)

Global trend report Bertelsmann Transformation Index 2022 54

According to [Guillermo O'Donnell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillermo_O%27Donnell "Guillermo O'Donnell"), [Philippe C. Schmitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippe_C._Schmitter "Philippe C. Schmitter"), [Larry Diamond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larry_Diamond "Larry Diamond") and [Thomas Carothers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Carothers "Thomas Carothers"), signs of a hybrid regime include:[^30] [^55]

1. The presence of external attributes of democracy (elections, multi-party system, legal opposition).
2. A low degree of representation of the interests of citizens in the process of political decision-making (incapacity of associations of citizens, for example [trade unions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade_union "Trade union"), or that they are in state control).
3. A low level of political participation.
4. The declarative nature of political rights and freedoms (formally there is in fact difficult implementation).
5. A low level of trust in political institutions by the citizenry.

### Transition types

#### Autocratization

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Number_of_countries_experiencing_autocratization_and_democratization%2C_1900%E2%80%932000.jpg/500px-Number_of_countries_experiencing_autocratization_and_democratization%2C_1900%E2%80%932000.jpg)

Since c. 2010, the number of countries autocratizing (blue) is higher than those democratizing (yellow).

Democratic backsliding [^3] is a form of autocratization, a process of structural government transition toward [authoritarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") in which the exercise of political power becomes less limited and more arbitrary and [repressive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_repression "Political repression").[^62] [^63] [^64] Democratic backsliding specifically assumes a starting point of a [democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") system. The process typically restricts the space for [public contest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_sphere "Public sphere") and [political participation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_participation "Political participation") in the process of government selection.[^65] [^66] Democratic backsliding involves the weakening of democratic institutions, such as the [peaceful transition of power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peaceful_transition_of_power "Peaceful transition of power") or [free and fair elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_and_fair_elections "Free and fair elections"), or the violation of individual rights that underpin democracies, especially [freedom of expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_expression "Freedom of expression").[^67] [^68] Democratic backsliding is the opposite of [democratization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratization "Democratization").

#### Democratization

[Democratization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratization "Democratization"), or democratisation, is the structural government transition from an [authoritarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarian "Authoritarian") government to a more [democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") political [regime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regime "Regime"), including substantive political changes moving in a democratic direction.[^69] [^70] The opposite process of [democratic transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_transition "Democratic transition") is known as [democratic backsliding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_backsliding "Democratic backsliding") or autocratization. Whether and to what extent democratization occurs can be influenced by various factors, including economic development, historical legacies, civil society, and international processes. Some accounts of democratization emphasize how elites drove democratization, whereas other accounts emphasize grassroots bottom-up processes.[^71] How democratization occurs has also been used to explain other political phenomena, such as whether a country goes to a war or whether its economy grows.[^72]

## Measurement

There are various democratic freedom indices produced by academic researchers, and [intergovernmental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_organization "International organization") [non-governmental organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-governmental_organization "Non-governmental organization") that publish assessments of the worlds political systems, according to their own definitions, and many of them include measures of hybrid regimes.[^73] [^22] However, because these various indices use different definitions and methodologies, they often disagree on which countries should be classified as hybrid regimes.[^22]

## Typology

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Democracy_claims.svg/500px-Democracy_claims.svg.png)

Countries in green claim to be a type of democracy while countries in red do not. Only Saudi Arabia, Oman, the UAE Qatar Brunei Afghanistan Burkina Faso, and the Vatican do not claim to be democratic.

According to [Yale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yale "Yale") professor [Juan José Linz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_Jos%C3%A9_Linz "Juan José Linz"), there are three main types of [political systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_systems "Political systems") today: [democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracies "Democracies"), [totalitarian regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarian_regimes "Totalitarian regimes") and, sitting between these two, [authoritarian regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarian_regimes "Authoritarian regimes") with many different terms that describe specific types of hybrid regimes.[^84] [^30] [^85] [^86] [^4]

Academics generally refer to a full [dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictatorship "Dictatorship") as either a form of [authoritarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") or [totalitarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarianism "Totalitarianism") over a "hybrid system".[^87] [^85] [^88] Authoritarian governments that conduct elections are in many scholars view not hybrids, but are successful well-institutionalized stable authoritarian regimes.[^89] [^90] [^91] Democratic elements can simultaneously serve authoritarian purposes and contribute to democratization.[^92]

### Electoral authoritarianism

Electoral authoritarianism means that democratic institutions are imitative and, due to numerous systematic violations of liberal democratic norms, in fact adhere to authoritarian methods.[^93] Electoral authoritarianism can be competitive and hegemonic, and the latter does not necessarily mean election irregularities.[^44] A. Schedler calls electoral authoritarianism a new form of authoritarian regime, not a hybrid regime or [illiberal democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illiberal_democracy "Illiberal democracy").[^44] Moreover, a purely authoritarian regime does not need elections as a source of [legitimacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legitimacy_\(political\) "Legitimacy (political)") [^94] while non-alternative elections, appointed at the request of the ruler, are not a sufficient condition for considering the regime conducting them to be hybrid.[^93]

### Electoral autocracy

[Electoral autocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_autocracy "Electoral autocracy") is a hybrid regime, in which democratic institutions are imitative and adhere to [authoritarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") methods. In these regimes, regular elections are held, but they are accused of failing to reach democratic standards of freedom and fairness.[^95] [^96] [^97] However, while most researchers agree on this broad definition of electoral autocracy, there is substantial variation with regard to how researchers define and measure this type of regime. As a consequence, whether or not countries are classified as electoral autocracies is highly contingent on the specific definitions and measures applied.[^97]

### Illiberal democracy

An [illiberal democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illiberal_democracy "Illiberal democracy") is a [non-democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") [governing system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government") that "hides its practices behind formally democratic institutions and procedures".[^98] While there is no universal consensus on its precise definition, the term broadly describes governments that present themselves as [liberal democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_democracies "Liberal democracies") while subtly suppressing opposing views.[^99] It has been described by researchers as a 21st-century form of [fascism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fascism "Fascism"),[^100] maintaining electoral democracy while employing state power for largely [nationalistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalistic "Nationalistic"), anti-minority, and anti-freedom purposes, often under the leadership of dominant figures and their close associates.[^100]

### Dominant-party system

A [dominant-party system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominant-party_system "Dominant-party system"), or one-party dominant system, is a political occurrence in which a single political party continuously dominates election results, overrunning opposition groups or parties.[^101] Any [ruling party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruling_party "Ruling party") staying in power for more than one consecutive term may be considered a *dominant party* (also referred to as a *predominant* or *hegemonic* party).[^102] Some dominant parties were called the *natural governing party*, given their length of time in power.[^103] [^104] [^105]

### Delegative democracy

In political science, [delegative democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delegative_democracy "Delegative democracy") is a mode of governance close to [Caesarism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesarism "Caesarism"), [Bonapartism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonapartism "Bonapartism"), [caudillismo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caudillismo "Caudillismo") or [populism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Populism "Populism") with a strong leader in a newly created otherwise democratic government.[^106] The concept arose from Argentinian political scientist [Guillermo O'Donnell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillermo_O%27Donnell "Guillermo O'Donnell"), who notes that representative democracy as it exists is usually linked solely to highly developed capitalist countries. However, newly installed democracies do not seem to be on a path of becoming fully representative democracies,[^107] and instead exhibit authoritarian tendencies.[^108] O'Donnell calls the former delegative democracies, for they are not fully consolidated democracies but may be enduring.

### Dictablanda

*[Dictablanda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictablanda "Dictablanda")* is a [dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictatorship "Dictatorship") in which [civil liberties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_liberties "Civil liberties") are allegedly preserved rather than destroyed, and authoritarian and democratic features are combined.[^109] [^110] *Dictablanda* is a [pun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pun "Pun") on *dictadura* ("dictatorship") replacing *dura*, which by itself is a Spanish word meaning "hard", with *blanda*, meaning "soft".[^111]

### Guided democracy

[Guided democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guided_democracy "Guided democracy"), also called directed democracy [^112] and managed democracy,[^113] [^114] is a formally [democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government") that functions as a *de facto* [authoritarian government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") or, in some cases, as an [autocratic government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy").[^115] Such [hybrid regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybrid_regimes "Hybrid regimes") are legitimized by elections, but these elections do not change the [state's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)") policies, motives, and goals.[^116]

### Liberal autocracy

A [liberal autocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_autocracy "Liberal autocracy") is a non-democratic government that follows the principles of [liberalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberalism "Liberalism").[^117] Until the 20th century, most countries in Western Europe were "liberal autocracies, or at best, [semi-democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anocracy "Anocracy") ".[^118] One example of a " [classic liberal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_liberalism "Classical liberalism") autocracy" was the [Austro-Hungarian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Empire "Austro-Hungarian Empire").[^119] According to [Fareed Zakaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fareed_Zakaria "Fareed Zakaria"), a more recent example was [British Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Hong_Kong "British Hong Kong"). He observed that until [1991](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1991_Hong_Kong_legislative_election "1991 Hong Kong legislative election"), the British who had ruled Hong Kong since 1841 "had never held a meaningful election, but its government epitomized [constitutional liberalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_liberalism "Constitutional liberalism"), protecting its citizens' basic rights (especially after [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II")) and administering a relatively [fair judiciary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judiciary_of_Hong_Kong "Judiciary of Hong Kong") and [bureaucracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureaucracy "Bureaucracy") ".[^120] The term is also used to describe 19th and early 20th century [constitutional monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy") that while had constitutional protection of liberties, also lacked wide [democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") participation.

### Semi-democracy

[Anocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anocracy "Anocracy"), or semi-democracy,[^121] is a [form of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Form_of_government "Form of government") that is loosely defined as part [democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") and part [dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictatorship "Dictatorship"),[^122] [^123] or as a "regime that mixes democratic with autocratic features".[^123] Another definition classifies anocracy as "a regime that permits some means of participation through opposition group behavior, but that has incomplete development of mechanisms to redress grievances." [^124] [^125] The term "semi-democratic" is reserved for stable regimes that combine democratic and [authoritarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarian "Authoritarian") elements.[^126] [^127] Scholars distinguish anocracies from [autocracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy") and democracies in their capability to maintain authority, political dynamics, and policy agendas.[^128] Anocratic regimes have democratic institutions that allow for nominal amounts of competition.[^122] Such regimes are particularly susceptible to outbreaks of armed conflict and unexpected or adverse changes in leadership.[^129]

### Defective democracy

[Defective democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defective_democracy "Defective democracy") (or flawed democracy) is a concept that was proposed by the political scientists [Wolfgang Merkel](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wolfgang_Merkel&action=edit&redlink=1 "Wolfgang Merkel (page does not exist)"), [Hans-Jürgen Puhle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans-J%C3%BCrgen_Puhle "Hans-Jürgen Puhle") and [Aurel S. Croissant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurel_S._Croissant "Aurel S. Croissant") at the beginning of the 21st century to subtilize the distinctions between [totalitarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarian "Totalitarian"), [authoritarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarian "Authoritarian"), and [democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy") [political systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_systems "Political systems").[^130] [^131] It is based on the concept of [embedded democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedded_democracy "Embedded democracy"). While there are four forms of defective democracy, how each nation reaches the point of defectiveness varies.[^132] One recurring theme is the geographical location of the nation, which includes the effects of the influence of surrounding nations in the region. Other causes for defective democracies include their path of modernization, level of modernization, [economic trends](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_indicator "Economic indicator"), [social capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_capital "Social capital"), civil society, political institutions, and education.

### Embedded democracy

[Embedded democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedded_democracy "Embedded democracy") is a form of [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government") in which democratic governance is secured by democratic partial regimes.[^133] [^134] [^135] The term "embedded democracy" was coined by political scientists Wolfgang Merkel, [Hans-Jürgen Puhle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans-J%C3%BCrgen_Puhle "Hans-Jürgen Puhle"), and [Aurel Croissant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurel_Croissant "Aurel Croissant"), who identified "five interdependent partial regimes" necessary for an embedded democracy: electoral regime, political participation, civil rights, horizontal accountability, and the power of the elected representatives to govern.[^136] The five internal regimes work together to check the power of the government, while external regimes also help to secure and stabilize embedded democracies.[^137] Together, all the regimes ensure that an embedded democracy is guided by the three fundamental principles of freedom, equality, and control.[^138] [^139]

### Competitive authoritarian regimes

Competitive authoritarianism is a subtype of [authoritarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism "Authoritarianism") and of the wider hybrid regime type. This regime type was defined to encapsulate states that contain formal democratic institutions that rulers view as the principal means of obtaining and exercising legitimate political authority against a real opposition and other semblances of democratic political society, in which officials violate election freedom and fairness in order to retain power. The officials interfere with opposition organisations, failing to respect minimum conventional standards of [democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy").[^140] [^141] [^142] [^143]

Three main instruments are used within competitive authoritarian regimes to maintain [political power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_\(social_and_political\) "Power (social and political)"): the self-serving use of state institutions (regarding abuses of electoral and judicial institutions such as voter intimidation and voter fraud); the overuse of state resources (to gain influence and/or power over proportional representation media, and use legal resources to disturb constitutional change); and the disruption of civil liberties (such as freedom of speech/press and association).[^141] In the post-Soviet era, continuing into the 2010s, the number of competitive authoritarian regimes increased.[^144] [^142]
