The **civil service** is a collective term for a sector of [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government "Government") composed mainly of career civil service personnel hired rather than [elected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elected_offical "Elected offical"), whose institutional tenure typically survives transitions of political leadership. A **civil service official**, also known as a **civil servant** or **public servant** or **public employee**, is a person employed in the [public sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_sector "Public sector") by a government department or agency for public sector undertakings. Civil servants include workers at any level of government, and in a healthy civil service answer to that government, not a [political party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_party "Political party").[^1] [^2]

The extent of civil servants of a state as part of the "civil service" varies from country to country. In the United Kingdom (UK), for instance, only [Crown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Crown "The Crown") (national government) employees are referred to as "civil servants" whereas employees of [local authorities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_authorities "Local authorities") (counties, cities and similar administrations) are generally referred to as "local government officers", who are considered public servants but not civil servants. Thus, in the UK, a civil servant is a public servant but a public servant is not necessarily a civil servant.

The study of the civil service is a part of the field of [public service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_service "Public service") (and in some countries there is no distinction between the two). Staff members in "non-departmental public bodies" (sometimes called " [QUANGOs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quango "Quango") ") may also be classed as civil servants for the purpose of statistics and possibly for their terms and conditions. Collectively a state's civil servants form its civil service or public service. The concept arose in China and modern civil service developed in Britain in the 18th century.

An international civil servant or international staff member is a civilian employee who is employed by an [intergovernmental organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergovernmental_organization "Intergovernmental organization"). These international civil servants do not resort under any national legislation (from which they have [immunity of jurisdiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergovernmental_organization#Privileges_and_immunities "Intergovernmental organization")) but are governed by internal staff regulations. All disputes related to international civil service are brought before special tribunals created by these international organizations such as, for instance, the Administrative Tribunal of the [ILO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Labour_Organization "International Labour Organization"). Specific referral can be made to the [International Civil Service Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Civil_Service_Commission "International Civil Service Commission") (ICSC) of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), an independent expert body established by the [United Nations General Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly "United Nations General Assembly"). Its mandate is to regulate and coordinate the conditions of service of staff in the United Nations common system, while promoting and maintaining high standards in the international civil service.

## History

### In China

Under the [Zhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_dynasty "Zhou dynasty"), official posts were provided to the [aristocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_aristocracy "Chinese aristocracy") of the [imperial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ji_\(surname\) "Ji (surname)") and [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Chinese_state "Ancient Chinese state") [clans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_surname "Chinese surname") and dispensed at their patronage. The [Warring States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warring_States "Warring States") and [Qin Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_Empire "Qin Empire") gradually replacing the varied ancient states with fairly standardized [counties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/County_\(China\) "County (China)") and disempowered much of the aristocracy in favor of officials appointed by the central governments, the early beginnings of the modern meritocratic civil service.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Exam_cells-large.jpg/250px-Exam_cells-large.jpg)

Imperial Civil Service Examination hall with 7500 cells in Guangdong, 1873

The [Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_dynasty "Han dynasty") initially maintained dozens of [allied kingdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdoms_under_the_Han "Kingdoms under the Han"), restored parts of the earlier aristocracy, and only directly staffed the [commanderies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commandery_\(China\) "Commandery (China)") around [Chang'an](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chang%27an "Chang'an") in the west, but this system was quickly ended after widespread revolts and even defections to the [Xiongnu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiongnu "Xiongnu"). By 134 BC during the reign of the [Wu Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wu_Emperor_of_Han "Wu Emperor of Han"), the bureaucracy was so widespread and needful of reliable manpower that the *[xiaolian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiaolian "Xiaolian")* system was instituted. Each [commandery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commandery_\(China\) "Commandery (China)") head was made responsible for nominating talented candidates, vouching—and becoming personally liable—for their unswerving [filial piety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filial_piety "Filial piety") (i.e. loyalty) and [incorruptibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corruption_in_China "Corruption in China"). Eventually public schools were provided for this purpose: the [Imperial University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taixue "Taixue") under the Wu Emperor [^3] and local academies under the [Ping Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ping_Emperor_of_Han "Ping Emperor of Han") in AD 3.[^4] Efforts were made to base appointments—especially in the [military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_of_the_Han_dynasty "Military of the Han dynasty") —solely on merit,[^5] but personal relationships continued to play a major role, particularly for the highest offices such as [chancellor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_chancellor_\(China\) "Grand chancellor (China)").[^6]

This tendency was only strengthened by the [nine-rank system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nine-rank_system "Nine-rank system") instituted by [Cao Wei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Wei "Cao Wei") during the [Three Kingdoms Period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_Period "Three Kingdoms Period") and continued under the [Jin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sima_Jin "Sima Jin"). Nominations were graded by an established rubric and overseen by professional controllers. In practice, points allocated on subjective criteria and those awarded for familial ties to earlier administrators entrenched a hereditary ruling class.

The civil service developed further with the [imperial examination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_examination "Imperial examination") founded under the [Sui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sui_dynasty "Sui dynasty").[^7] The imperial exam based on merit was designed under the [Wen Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wen_Emperor_of_Sui "Wen Emperor of Sui") to select the best administrative officials for the state's bureaucracy.[^8] Despite the resumption of recommended candidates under the [Yang Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Yang_Emperor_of_Sui&action=edit&redlink=1 "Yang Emperor of Sui (page does not exist)"), this system had a huge influence on both society and culture in Imperial China and was directly responsible for the creation of a class of [scholar-bureaucrats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scholar-bureaucrats "Scholar-bureaucrats") who notionally held high rank for their personal merit regardless of their family pedigree.[^5] The succeeding [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty") —particularly during the interregnum of the empress [Wu Zetian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wu_Zetian "Wu Zetian") 's [Zhou dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wu_Zhou_dynasty "Wu Zhou dynasty") —expanded the exams' format and their importance.[^9] The system reached its apogee during the [Song dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty "Song dynasty").[^10]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Sui_Wendi_Tang.jpg/250px-Sui_Wendi_Tang.jpg)

Emperor Wen of Sui (r. 581–604), who established the first civil service examination system in China; a painting by the chancellor and artist Yan Liben (600–673).

In theory, the Chinese civil service system provided one of the main avenues for social mobility in Chinese society, although, in practice, due to the time-consuming nature of the study, the examination was generally only taken by sons of the landed gentry.[^11] The examination tested each candidate's memorization of the [Nine Classics of Confucianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_Books_and_Five_Classics "Four Books and Five Classics"), his ability to compose poetry using fixed and traditional forms, and his [calligraphy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calligraphy "Calligraphy"). It was ideally suited to literary candidates. Thus, toward the end of the Ming Dynasty, the system attracted the candidature of Tang Xianzu (1550–1616). Tang at 14 passed the imperial examination at the county level; and at 21, he did so at the provincial level; but not until he was 34 did he pass at the national level. However, he had already become a well-known poet at age 12, and among other things he went on to such distinction as a profound literati and dramatist that it would not be far-fetched to regard him as China's answer to William Shakespeare.[^12]

### Roman Empire

The [Roman empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") (27 BC – AD 395) had several types of civil servants who fulfilled diverse functions in Roman society. They were called [apparitores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparitor "Apparitor").

#### Accensi

[Accensi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accensi#Civil_servants "Accensi") were usually professional civil servants, providing assistance to the elected [magistrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_magistrate "Roman magistrate") during their term in office. In the courts, they summoned witnesses, kept track of time, and helped keep order.[^13] Outside of the courts, they escorted the magistrate and acted as [heralds.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herald "Herald")[^14] [^15] They also helped in writing [edicts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edict "Edict") and [laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_law "Roman law").[^16] It is also possible they were messengers and [orderlies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orderlies "Orderlies").[^17] The *Accensi Velati* were non military participants of military campaigns.[^18] [^19] They probably assisted clerks, accountants, supply officials, and aides.[^20] The *y* also assisted religious affairs especially the [Feriae Latinae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feriae_Latinae "Feriae Latinae"),[^21] [^22] formed a [collegium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collegium_\(ancient_Rome\) "Collegium (ancient Rome)") dedicated to managing the streets,[^23] and had a [centuriate assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centuriate_Assembly "Centuriate Assembly") dedicated to them.[^24]

##### Carnifex

The carnifex punished slaves and foreigners, unlike lictores who punished Romans.[^25]

##### Coactor

They were the tax collectors.[^26] The name coactor is derived from its latin meaning: "to compel, to force".[^27]

#### Lictores

Similarly to accensi, [lictores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lictor "Lictor") were public officers tasked to assist magistrates since the times of the [Roman kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Kingdom "Roman Kingdom") (753 BC – 509 BC) or even earlier Etruscan times. The number of lictores a magistrate had was proportional to status. Lictores were in charge of punishing Roman citizens.[^28]

#### Praecones

They were generally employed to make announcements in public and crowds.[^29]

##### Scriba

The [scriba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scriba_\(ancient_Rome\) "Scriba (ancient Rome)") were civil servants working as public notaries as well as general bureaucracy.[^30] [^31] Greek cities had a similar figure, however the job was done by slaves.[^30]

### Modern civil service

In the 18th century, in response to economic changes and the growth of the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire"), the bureaucracy of institutions such as the [Office of Works](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Office_of_Works "Office of Works") and the [Navy Board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navy_Board "Navy Board") greatly expanded. Each had its own system, but in general, staff were appointed through patronage or outright purchase. By the 19th century, it became increasingly clear that these arrangements were falling short. "The origins of the British civil service are better known. During the eighteenth century a number of Englishmen wrote in praise of the Chinese examination system, some of them going so far as to urge the adoption for England of something similar. The first concrete step in this direction was taken by the British East India Company in 1806." [^32] In that year, the [Honourable East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honourable_East_India_Company "Honourable East India Company") established a college, the [East India Company College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_India_Company_College "East India Company College"), near London to train and examine administrators of the company's territories in India.[^32] "The proposal for establishing this college came, significantly, from members of the East India Company's trading post in Canton, China." [^32] Examinations for the Indian "civil service"—a term coined by the Company—were introduced in 1829.[^33]

British efforts at reform were influenced by the [imperial examinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_examination "Imperial examination") system and [meritocratic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meritocracy "Meritocracy") of China. Thomas Taylor Meadows, Britain's consul in [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou "Guangzhou"), China argued in his *Desultory Notes on the Government and People of China*, published in 1847, that "the long duration of the Chinese empire is solely and altogether owing to the good government which consists in the advancement of men of talent and merit only", and that the British must reform their civil service by making the institution meritocratic.[^32] On the other hand, John Browne, in the 1854 debate mentioned above, 'argued that elegant writing had become an end in itself, and the stultifying effect of this on the Chinese civil service had contributed in no small measure to China's failure to develop its early lead over Western civilisations': Coolican, p. 107.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Charles_Edward_Trevelyan.jpg/250px-Charles_Edward_Trevelyan.jpg)

Charles Trevelyan, an architect of Her Majesty's Civil Service, established in 1855 on his recommendations.

In 1853 the [Chancellor of the Exchequer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer "Chancellor of the Exchequer") [William Gladstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone "William Ewart Gladstone"), commissioned Sir [Stafford Northcote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stafford_Northcote,_1st_Earl_of_Iddesleigh "Stafford Northcote, 1st Earl of Iddesleigh") and [Charles Trevelyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sir_Charles_Trevelyan,_1st_Baronet "Sir Charles Trevelyan, 1st Baronet") to look into the operation and organisation of the Civil Service. Influenced by the Chinese imperial examinations, the [Northcote–Trevelyan Report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northcote%E2%80%93Trevelyan_Report "Northcote–Trevelyan Report") of 1854 made four principal recommendations: that recruitment should be on the basis of merit determined through competitive examination, that candidates should have a solid general education to enable inter-departmental transfers, that recruits should be graded into a hierarchy and that promotion should be through achievement, rather than "preferment, patronage or purchase". It also recommended a clear division between staff responsible for routine ("mechanical") work, and those engaged in policy formulation and implementation in an "administrative" class.[^34]

The report was well-timed, because bureaucratic chaos during the [Crimean War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crimean_War "Crimean War") was causing a clamour for the change. The report's conclusions were immediately implemented, and a permanent, unified and politically neutral civil service was introduced as [Her Majesty's Civil Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Civil_Service "Her Majesty's Civil Service"). A [Civil Service Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_Service_Commission_\(United_Kingdom\) "Civil Service Commission (United Kingdom)") was also set up in 1855 to oversee open recruitment and end patronage, and most of the other Northcote–Trevelyan recommendations were implemented over some years.[^35]

The same model, the [Imperial Civil Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Civil_Service "Imperial Civil Service"), was implemented in [British India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_India "British India") from 1858, after the demise of the [East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_India_Company "East India Company") 's rule in India through the [Indian Rebellion of 1857](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Rebellion_of_1857 "Indian Rebellion of 1857") which came close to toppling British rule in the country.[^36]

The Northcote–Trevelyan model remained essentially stable for a hundred years. This was a tribute to its success in removing corruption, delivering public services (even under the stress of two world wars), and responding effectively to political change. It also had a great international influence and was adapted by members of the [Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations"). The [Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendleton_Civil_Service_Reform_Act "Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act") established a modern civil service in the United States, and by the turn of the 20th century almost all Western governments had implemented similar reforms.

## Independence and accountability

[Autocratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy") systems of government (such as [monarchies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchies "Monarchies")) can favour appointments to administrative positions on the basis of [nepotism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nepotism "Nepotism"), [patronage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patronage "Patronage") and [favoritism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In-group_favoritism "In-group favoritism"), with [close relationships between political and administrative figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cronyism "Cronyism"). Early [Roman emperors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_emperor "Roman emperor"), for example, set their [household slaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery_in_ancient_Rome "Slavery in ancient Rome") and [freedmen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedmen "Freedmen") much of the task of administering the [Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire"),[^37] sidelining the [elected officials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cursus_honorum "Cursus honorum") who continued the traditions of the [Roman Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic "Roman Republic"). But the political appointment of bureaucrats can run the risk of tolerating [inefficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inefficiency "Inefficiency") and [corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_corruption "Political corruption"), with officials feeling secure in the protection of their political masters and possibly immune from prosecution for [bribe-taking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bribe_taking "Bribe taking"). [Song-dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty "Song dynasty") China (960–1279) standardised [competitive examinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_examination "Imperial examination") as a basis for civil-service recruitment and promotion, and in the 19th century administrations in France and Britain followed suit. Agitation against the [spoils system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spoils_system "Spoils system") in the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") resulted in increasing the independence of the civil service—seen as an important principle in modern times.[^38]

Some governmental structures include a [civil service commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_service_commission "Civil service commission") (or equivalent) whose functions include maintaining the work and rights of civil servants at arm's length from potential politicisation or political interference.[^39] Compare the governance-administrative integration of [Stalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Stalin "Joseph Stalin") 's [Orgburo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orgburo "Orgburo").

## By country

### Americas

#### Brazil

Brazil started to move away from a patronage based public service starting in the second half of the 19th century, but written tests and merit only became the norm towards the end of the 1930s, as a result from reforms introduced during [Getúlio Vargas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Get%C3%BAlio_Vargas "Getúlio Vargas") first term as the nation's President.[^40]

Civil servants in Brazil ([Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_language "Portuguese language"): *servidores públicos*) are those working in the [executive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_\(government\) "Executive (government)"), [legislative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislative "Legislative"), and [judicial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judicial "Judicial") branches of the [Federal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil "Brazil"), [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_of_Brazil "States of Brazil"), [Federal District](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_District_\(Brazil\) "Federal District (Brazil)") or [municipal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Municipalities_of_Brazil "Municipalities of Brazil") governments, including [congressmen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congressmen "Congressmen"), [senators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senate_of_Brazil "Senate of Brazil"), [mayors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mayors "Mayors"), [ministers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_\(government\) "Minister (government)"), the [president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Brazil "President of Brazil") of the [republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic "Republic"), and workers in [government-owned corporations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government-owned_corporation "Government-owned corporation").

Career civil servants (not temporary workers or politicians) are hired only externally on the basis of [entrance examinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_service_entrance_examination "Civil service entrance examination") ([Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_language "Portuguese language"): *concurso público*).[^41] It usually consists of a written test; some posts may require physical tests (such as policemen), or oral tests (such as professors, judges, prosecutors and attorneys). The rank according to the examination score is used for filling the vacancies.

Entrance examinations are conducted by several institutions with a government mandate, such as CESPE (which belongs to the [University of Brasília](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Bras%C3%ADlia "University of Brasília")) and the [Cesgranrio](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funda%C3%A7%C3%A3o_Cesgranrio "pt:Fundação Cesgranrio") Foundation (which is part of the [Federal University of Rio de Janeiro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_University_of_Rio_de_Janeiro "Federal University of Rio de Janeiro")).

The labor laws and social insurance for civil servants are different from private workers; even between government branches (like different states or cities), the law and insurance differ.

The posts usually are ranked by titles, the most common are *technician* for high school literates and *analyst* for undergraduates. There's also higher post ranks like auditor, fiscal, chief of police, prosecutor, judge, attorney, etc.

The law does not allow servants to upgrade or downgrade posts internally; they need to be selected in separate external entrance examinations.

#### Canada

Historians have explored the powerful role of civil service since the 1840s.[^42]

In Canada, the civil service at the federal level is known as the [Public Service of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_Service_of_Canada "Public Service of Canada"), with each of the ten provincial governments as well as the three territorial governments also having their own separate civil services. The federal civil service consists of all employees of the [crown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Crown "The Crown"). Ministers' exempt staff and members of the [Royal Canadian Mounted Police](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mounted_Police "Royal Canadian Mounted Police") or [Canadian Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Armed_Forces "Canadian Armed Forces") are not civil servants.[^43] There are approximately 357,000 federal civil servants (2023),[^43] and more than 350,000 employees at the provincial and territorial levels.[^44]

#### United States

In the United States, the federal civil service was established in 1871. The Civil Service is defined as "all appointive positions in the executive, judicial, and legislative branches of the Government of the United States, except positions in the uniformed services." ([5 U.S.C.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Title_5_of_the_United_States_Code "Title 5 of the United States Code") [§ 2101](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/5/2101)). In the early 19th century, government jobs were held at the pleasure of the president—a person could be fired at any time. The [spoils system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spoils_system "Spoils system") meant that jobs were used to support the political parties. This was changed in slow stages by the [Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendleton_Civil_Service_Reform_Act "Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act") of 1883 and subsequent laws. By 1909, almost two-thirds of the U.S. federal work force was appointed based on merit, that is, qualifications measured by tests. Certain senior civil service positions, including some heads of diplomatic missions and executive agencies, are filled by [political appointees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_appointments_in_the_United_States "Political appointments in the United States"). Under the [Hatch Act of 1939](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hatch_Act_of_1939 "Hatch Act of 1939"), civil servants are not allowed to engage in political activities while performing their duties.

The U.S. civil service includes the [competitive service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competitive_service "Competitive service") and the [excepted service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excepted_service "Excepted service"). The majority of civil service appointments in the U.S. are made under the competitive service, but the [Foreign Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Foreign_Service "U.S. Foreign Service"), the [FBI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation "Federal Bureau of Investigation"), and other National Security positions are made under the excepted service. (U.S. Code Title V)

As of January 2007, the federal government, excluding the Postal Service, employed about 1.8 million civilian workers. The federal government is the nation's single largest employer, although it employs only about 12% of all government employees, compared to 24% at the state level and 63% at the local level.[^45] Although most federal agencies are based in the [Washington, D.C.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington,_D.C. "Washington, D.C.") region, only about 16% (or about 284,000) of the federal government workforce is employed in this region.[^46] As of 2014, there are currently 15 federal executive branch agencies and hundreds of subagencies.[^47]

In the early 20th century, most cities in the US had a spoils system.[^48] Over the next few decades, the spoils system was replaced with a civil service system.[^48] U.S. state and local government entities often have competitive civil service systems that are modeled on the national system, in varying degrees.

### Asia

#### Brunei

The Civil Service ([Malay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malay_language "Malay language"): *Perkhidmatan Awam*) of Brunei. The role of the civil service is as the government's administrative machinery to uphold the supreme authority of His Majesty the Sultan and Yang Di-Pertuan of Brunei Darussalam, uphold the National Philosophy – MIB, Melayu Islam Beraja, ensure the development of the country and ensure the welfare of the people as well as its traditional role as the peacekeeper, law enforcer, regulator and service providers. However, the adjudication system is separate from the civil service to maintain its independence and impartiality.[^49]

#### Cambodia

The Civil Service ([Khmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_language "Khmer language"): សេវាកម្មស៊ីវិល, *Sevakamm Civil*) of Cambodia is the policy implementing arm of the [Royal Government of Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Government_of_Cambodia "Royal Government of Cambodia"). In executing this important role, each civil servant ([Khmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_language "Khmer language"): មន្រ្តីរាជការ, *Montrey Reachkar*) is obligated to act according to the law and is guided by public policy pronouncements. The *Common Statute of Civil Servants* is the primary legislative framework for the Civil Service in Cambodia.[^50]

#### China

##### Historical

One of the oldest examples of a civil service based on [meritocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meritocracy "Meritocracy") is the Imperial bureaucracy of China, which can be traced as far back as the [Qin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_dynasty "Qin dynasty") (221–207 BC). However, the civil service examinations were practiced on a much smaller scale in comparison to the stronger, centralized bureaucracy of the [Song dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty "Song dynasty") (960–1279). In response to the regional military rule of [jiedushi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiedushi "Jiedushi") and the loss of civil authority during the late Tang period and [Five Dynasties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Dynasties_and_Ten_Kingdoms_period "Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period") (907–960), the Song emperors were eager to implement a system where civil officials would owe their social prestige to the central court and gain their salaries strictly from the central government. This ideal was not fully achieved since many scholar officials were affluent landowners and were engaged in many anonymous business affairs in an age of [economic revolution in China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_the_Song_dynasty "Economy of the Song dynasty"). Nonetheless, gaining a degree through three levels of examination—prefectural exams, provincial exams, and the prestigious palace exams—was a far more desirable goal in society than becoming a merchant. This was because the mercantile class was traditionally regarded with some disdain by the [scholar-official](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scholar-official "Scholar-official") class.

This class of state bureaucrats in the Song period were far less aristocratic than their Tang predecessors. The examinations were carefully structured in order to ensure that people of lesser means than what was available to candidates born into wealthy, landowning families were given a greater chance to pass the exams and obtain an official degree. This included the employment of a bureau of [copyists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copyist "Copyist") who would rewrite all of the candidates' exams in order to mask their handwriting and thus prevent favoritism by graders of the exams who might otherwise recognize a candidate's handwriting. The advent of widespread [printing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing "Printing") in the Song period allowed many more examination candidates access to the [Confucian texts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_classics "Chinese classics") whose mastery was required for passing the exams.

#### India

In India, civil servants are selected as per the [Constitution of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_India "Constitution of India"). Civil servants serve at the pleasure of the [President of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_India "President of India"). The civil services of India can be classified into two types—the [All India Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_India_Services "All India Services") and the [Central Civil Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Civil_Services "Central Civil Services") (Group A and B). The recruits are university graduates selected through three phase exams such as the [Civil Services Examination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_Services_Examination "Civil Services Examination") (CSE) or the [Engineering Services Examination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_Services_Examination "Engineering Services Examination") (ESE) among others, conducted by the [Union Public Service Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_Public_Service_Commission "Union Public Service Commission") (UPSC). Additionally, there are also [State Civil Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_Services_of_India#State_Services "Civil Services of India"). The state civil servants are selected through an examination conducted by [state public service commissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_service_commissions_in_India "Public service commissions in India"). State civil servants serve at the pleasure of the [Governor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governor_\(India\) "Governor (India)").

#### Germany

The **Public Service in Germany** (*[Öffentlicher Dienst](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%96ffentlicher_Dienst "de:Öffentlicher Dienst")*) employed 4.6 million persons as of 2011.[^55] Public servants are organized [^56] into hired salaried employees (*Arbeitnehmer*), appointed civil servants (*[Beamte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beamter "Beamter")*), judges, and soldiers. They are employed by public bodies (*Körperschaften des öffentlichen Rechts*), such as [counties (*Kreise*)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Districts_of_Germany "Districts of Germany"), [states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_of_Germany "States of Germany"), the [federal government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Germany "Politics of Germany"), etc. In addition to employees directly employed by the state another 1.6 million persons are employed by state owned enterprises [^57]

[*Beamte*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beamter "Beamter") has been a title for government employees for several centuries in German states, but became a standardized group in 1794. Soldiers other than [conscripted soldiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conscription_in_Germany "Conscription in Germany") are not Beamte but have similar rights. Judges are not Beamte but have similar rights too.[^58] Public attorneys are all Beamte, whereas most (but not all) professors are Beamte. The group of Beamte have the most secure employment, and the amount they are paid is set by national pay regulations (*Besoldungsordnungen*). Beamte are prohibited from [striking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strike_action "Strike action").

*Arbeitnehmer* have work contracts, whereas *Beamte* are appointed, employed, and removed in accordance with the Public Sector Service and Loyalty law (*öffentlich-rechtliches Dienst- und Treueverhältnis*). Most tasks can be either done by *Arbeitnehmer* or *Beamte*, however some specific tasks of official nature are supposed to be handled by *Beamte* since they are subject to a special loyalty obligation.

*Beamte* are divided into four levels:

- *Einfacher Dienst*: ordinary civil service, corresponding to [enlisted ranks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enlisted_ranks "Enlisted ranks") in the military, now largely obsolete
- *Mittlerer Dienst*: medium-level civil service, corresponding to [non-commissioned officers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-commissioned_officer "Non-commissioned officer") in the military
- *Gehobener Dienst*: senior civil service, including civil servant positions such as *Inspektor* and above, corresponding to [commissioned officers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commissioned_officer "Commissioned officer") from [lieutenant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_lieutenant "Second lieutenant") to [captain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captain_\(armed_forces\) "Captain (armed forces)") in the military
- *Höherer Dienst*: higher civil service, including civil servant positions such as *Rat* (Councillor) and above as well as academic employees such as Professors, corresponding to [major](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_\(rank\) "Major (rank)") and above in the military

*Gehobener Dienst* and *Höherer Dienst* both require a university education or equivalent, at the very least a bachelor's or master's degree, respectively.

#### Spain

The [civil service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secretariat_of_State_for_the_Civil_Service "Secretariat of State for the Civil Service") in Spain (*función pública*) is usually considered to include all the employees at the different levels of government: [central government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Spain "Government of Spain"), [autonomous communities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomous_communities_of_Spain "Autonomous communities of Spain"), as well as [municipalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Municipalities_of_Spain "Municipalities of Spain"). There are three main categories of Spanish public positions: temporary political posts ("personal eventual"), which require a simple procedure for hiring and dismissal and is associated to top level executives and advisors, statutory permanent posts ("funcionarios de carrera"), which require a formal procedure for access that usually involves a competition among candidates and whose tenants are subject to a special statutory relationship of work with their employers, and non statutory permanent posts ("personal laboral"), which also require a formal procedure for entry similar to the procedure required for the "funcionarios de carrera", but whose tenants are subject to normal working conditions and laws. Competitions differ notably among the state, the 17 autonomous communities and the city councils, and the "funcionarios de carrera" and "personal laboral" examinations vary in difficulty from one location to another.

As of 2013,[^59] there were 2.6 million public employees in Spain, of which 571,000 were civil servants and 2 million were non-civil servants.

#### Switzerland

Civil servants in Switzerland work for the Confederation, cantons, or communes. According to the OECD, approximately 595,000 people worked for a public administration in 2022, representing 11.2% of the active population, placing Switzerland in the lower third among OECD member countries.[^62] However, some estimates place the figure at 23% when including employees of semi-public entities such as the [Post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_Post "Swiss Post"), the [Federal Railways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Railways "Swiss Federal Railways"), university hospitals, and subsidized sectors.[^62] The Swiss public service is characterized by extreme fragmentation, with the Confederation, 26 cantons, and approximately 3,000 communes each maintaining their own personnel regulations.[^63]

Historically, Swiss civil servants were appointed for fixed terms and often renewed by tacit re-election, a system rooted in republican traditions hostile to career civil service derived from royal courts. Only the cantons of Geneva and Vaud practiced lifelong civil service appointments similar to most European countries. Toward the end of the 20th century, most cantons shifted to engaging personnel under regular employment contracts with indefinite duration. By 2000, the majority of cantons and numerous municipalities had transitioned from a traditional civil service system to an administration composed of regular employees. The Confederation followed with its *Federal Personnel Act*, which entered into force in 2002, abolishing the fixed-term appointment system and introducing collective bargaining agreements in public services.[^63]

The median monthly salary in the public sector is 8,094 Swiss francs, compared to 6,510 francs in the private sector, a difference partly explained by higher qualification levels and older age profiles in public employment.[^62] Trade unions in the public sector are characterized by strong dispersion across branches, fields of activity, and ideological orientations. The [Swiss Union of Public Service Personnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_Union_of_Public_Service_Personnel "Swiss Union of Public Service Personnel") (SSP/VPOD), created in 1905, operates primarily at cantonal and communal levels, while federal-level unions were historically represented by the *Federative Association of Public Administration and Enterprise Personnel* (founded 1903), replaced in 2003 by the *Federal Staff Negotiating Community*. Strikes have been historically rare, and civil servants of the Confederation and most cantons were long forbidden to strike.[^63]

Police officers and staff are also not civil servants. Total employment in the public sector in the UK was 6.04 million in 2012 according to the UK's [Office for National Statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Office_for_National_Statistics "Office for National Statistics").[^64]
