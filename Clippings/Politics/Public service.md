A **public service** or **service of general (economic) interest** is any [service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_\(economics\) "Service (economics)") intended to address the needs of aggregate members of a community,[^1] [^2] whether provided directly by a [public sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_sector "Public sector") agency, via public financing available to private businesses or voluntary organisations, or by private businesses subject to government regulation. Some public services are provided on behalf of a government's residents or in the [interest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_interest "Public interest") of its citizens. The term is associated with a social consensus (usually expressed through democratic [elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election "Election")) that certain services should be available to all, regardless of [income](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Income "Income"), [physical ability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_ability "Physical ability") or [mental acuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence"). Examples of such services include the [fire services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire_department "Fire department"), [police](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Police "Police"), [air force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_force "Air force"), [paramedics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paramedic "Paramedic") and [public service broadcasting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_broadcasting "Public broadcasting").

Even where public services are neither publicly provided nor [publicly financed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_finance "Public finance"), they are usually subject to [regulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulation "Regulation") beyond that applying to most [economic sectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_sector "Economic sector") for social and political reasons. [Public policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policy "Public policy"),[^3] when made in the public's interest and with its motivations, is a type of public service.

In [developing countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country"), public services tend to be much less well developed. For example, water services might only be available to the [wealthy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wealth "Wealth") [middle class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_class "Middle class"). For political reasons, the service is often [subsidized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subsidy "Subsidy"), which reduces the finance potentially available for expansion to poorer communities. The United Nations [Sustainable Development Goal 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_5 "Sustainable Development Goal 5") is a global initiative that aims to influence the provision of public services and infrastructure to marginalized demographics.[^5]

## History

Governing bodies have long provided core public services. The tradition of keeping citizens secure through organized military defense dates to at least 4,000 years ago.[^6]

Maintaining order through local delegated authority originated at least as early as the [Warring States period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warring_States_period "Warring States period") (5th to 3rd centuries BCE) in ancient China with the institution of *xiàn* (prefectures) under the control of a centrally appointed prefect. Historical evidence of state provision of [dispute resolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dispute_resolution "Dispute resolution") through a legal/justice system goes back at least as far as ancient Egypt.[^7]

A primary public service in ancient history involved ensuring the general favor of the [gods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deity "Deity") through a theologically and ceremonially correct [state religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_religion "State religion").[^8]

The widespread provision of [public utilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_utility "Public utility") as public services in developed countries began in the late 19th century, often with the [municipal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Municipality "Municipality") development of [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_gas "Coal gas") and water services. Later, governments began to provide other services such as [electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity "Electricity") and [health care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_care "Health care"). In most developed countries, local or national governments continue to provide such services, the biggest exceptions being the [US](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and the [UK](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom"), where private provision is arguably proportionally more significant.[^9] Nonetheless, such privately provided public services are often strongly regulated, for example (in the US) by [Public Utility Commissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_utilities_commission "Public utilities commission").

Examples noted in a history of public services in [Oxford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford "Oxford") include street repair, cleansing, and [lighting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Streetlighting "Streetlighting"), drainage and sewage disposal, water, gas and electricity supply, police and fire services, the Post Office, transport, hospital services, and the provision of [baths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_bathing "Public bathing"), parks and cemeteries.[^10]

In the 21st century, [debt crises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt_crisis "Debt crisis") have made it difficult for developing countries to maintain and develop public services. The [US Treasury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_Treasury "US Treasury") has urged multilateral global lenders such as the [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank"), [International Monetary Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund "International Monetary Fund"), and [regional development banks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_development_bank "Regional development bank") to help protect public services in debt-distressed countries.[^11]

## Characteristics

A public service may sometimes have the characteristics of a [public good](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_good_\(economics\) "Public good (economics)") (being non-rivalrous and non-excludable), but most are services that may (according to prevailing [social norms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_norms "Social norms")) be under-provided by the [market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_\(economics\) "Market (economics)"). In most cases public services are [services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_\(economics\) "Service (economics)"), i.e., they do not involve manufacturing of goods. They may be provided by local or national monopolies, especially in sectors that are [natural monopolies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_monopoly "Natural monopoly").

They may involve outputs that are hard to attribute to specific individual effort or to measure in terms of key characteristics such as quality. They often require high levels of training and education. They may attract people with a public service ethos who wish to give something to the wider public or community through their work.

In the UK, the process of assessing the needs of the people of an area and then designing and securing an appropriate public service to meet those needs, is often called *commissioning*.[^12] Commissioned services may be delivered by organisations in the public sector, [private sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_sector "Private sector") or [third sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voluntary_sector "Voluntary sector"):[^13] when the private or third sector is involved, the process of commissioning is usually linked with a process of [procurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_procurement "Government procurement"), to determine who will provide the services, at what cost and on what terms. Commissioning is often seen as a cyclical process.[^12]

## Implications

Public services can be constructed, coordinated and operated in many ways or forms. They include government agencies, independent state-funded institutes, government-coordinated organizations, civil society, military agencies and volunteers.

### Government employees

Government agencies are not profit-oriented and their employees are often motivated differently.[^14] Studies of their work have found contrasting results, including both higher levels of effort [^14] and fewer hours of work.[^15] A survey in the UK found that private-sector hiring managers do not credit government experience as much as private-sector experience.[^16] Public workers tend to have lower wages when adjusting for education, although that difference is reduced when benefits and hours are included.[^17] Public servants have other intangible benefits such as increased job security and high wages.[^17]

### Need satisfaction and sustainability

A study concluded that public services are associated with higher human need satisfaction and lower energy requirements while contemporary forms of [economic growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_growth "Economic growth") are linked with the opposite. Authors find that the contemporary [economic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_system "Economic system") is structurally misaligned with goals of [sustainable development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_development "Sustainable development") and that to date no nation can provide decent [living standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_of_living "Standard of living") at sustainable levels of energy and resource use. They provide analysis of factors in social provisioning and assess that improving beneficial provisioning-factors and infrastructure would allow for sustainable forms of need satisfaction.[^18] [^19]

### Choice

*Open Public Services*, a white paper published by the [Cameron–Clegg coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameron%E2%80%93Clegg_coalition "Cameron–Clegg coalition") in the United Kingdom in 2011, aimed to create a comprehensive policy framework for "good public services". It set out the coalition's programme for reform of public services, described as one of "wide ambitions" expected to be implemented over a period of time, not all at once.[^20] Five principles were to underlie open public services:

- Choice, wherever possible
- Decentralisation to the lowest appropriate level
- Diversity
- Fairness
- Accountablity [^20]<sup><span title="Page / location: Chapter 2">: Chapter 2</span></sup>

The journalist [David Boyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Boyle_\(author\) "David Boyle (author)") conducted an independent review for the UK's [Treasury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HM_Treasury "HM Treasury") and the [Cabinet Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_Office "Cabinet Office") on public demand for choice in public services, which reported in 2013.[^21] The principle of choice where possible was embodied in the Choice Charter, published on 16 May 2013, where four choice principles were outlined:

- Allowing people a say in how public services are provided for them
- Allowing people the opportunity to exercise choice where it is available
- Making clear, accessible and high-quality information available to support choice
- Facilitating complaints over the degree of choice offered as well as over the quality of services [^22]

Between December 2012 and May 2013, "Choice Frameworks" were scheduled for publication covering NHS care, [social housing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_housing "Social housing"), school education, [early years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Years_Foundation_Stage "Early Years Foundation Stage") education and [adult social care](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Adult_social_care&action=edit&redlink=1 "Adult social care (page does not exist)").[^22]

### Nationalization

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/A_group_of_Damas_de_Rojo.jpg/250px-A_group_of_Damas_de_Rojo.jpg)

A group of Chilean Damas de Rojo, volunteers at their local hospital

Nationalization took off following the [world wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_war "World war") of the first half of the 20th century. In parts of [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe"), [central planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_planning "Central planning") was implemented in the belief that it would make production more efficient. Many public services, especially electricity, fossil fuels and public transport are products of this era. After [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), many countries also began to implement [universal health care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_health_care "Universal health care") and expanded [education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Education "Education") under the funding and guidance of the state.

### Privatization

There are several ways to privatize public services. A free-market corporation may be established and sold to private investors, relinquishing government control altogether. Thus it becomes a private, not public, service. Another option, used in the [Nordic countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nordic_countries "Nordic countries"), is to establish a corporation but keep ownership or voting power essentially in the hands of the government. For example, the [Finnish state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finland "Finland") owned 49% of [Kemira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kemira "Kemira") until 2007, with the rest owned by private investors. A 49% share did not make it a "government enterprise", but it meant that all other investors together would have to oppose the state's opinion to overturn the state's decisions.

A regulated corporation can also acquire permits on the agreement that they fulfill certain public service duties. When a private corporation runs a [natural monopoly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_monopoly "Natural monopoly"), the corporation is typically heavily regulated, to prevent abuse of monopoly power. Lastly, the government can buy a service on the free market. In many countries, [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication "Medication") is provided in this manner: the government reimburses part of the medication's price. Bus traffic, electricity, healthcare and waste management are also privatized in this way. One recent innovation, used in the UK increasingly as well as in Australia and Canada, is [public-private partnerships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public%E2%80%93private_partnership "Public–private partnership"), which involve giving a long lease to private consortia in return for partly or fully funding infrastructure costs.
