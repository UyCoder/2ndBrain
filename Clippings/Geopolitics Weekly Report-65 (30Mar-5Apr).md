## Pacific Region - Economics Highlights

- A year after Trump announced the highest tariffs ever imposed by the U.S., factory jobs declined and inflation rose. Trump’s habit of threatening tariffs on every issue, big or small, and his tendency to water down or forget his most aggressive threats, earned him the nickname “TACO” (Trump Always Chickens Out).
- Two major aluminum smelting plants in the Middle East were damaged as a result of Iranian attacks. Emirates Global Aluminium (EGA) and Aluminium Bahrain sustained damage following the Iranian attacks. Iran claimed that these two facilities were linked to the Pentagon and U.S. aerospace companies. These two smelting plants account for a total of 4% of global aluminum production capacity. China produced 45 million tons of aluminum last year, accounting for 60.8% of the global total. It will take at least six months for the aluminum smelters to resume operations after the shutdown. Chinese experts note that the disruption in aluminum production in the Middle East will further solidify China’s dominance in the supply chain for gallium, a critical raw material used in semiconductors, electric vehicles, and advanced radar systems. In May 2025, EGA signed a memorandum of understanding with U.S. defense firm RTX Corp and the UAE’s Tawazun Council to position itself as a new gallium producer. This initiative aims to expand extraction and refining capacities at the EGA aluminum refinery in Abu Dhabi and position the UAE as the world’s second-largest gallium producer. Without aluminum, the supply of gallium would shrink, and the direct consequence would be a further increase in China’s share of global gallium production capacity.
- South Korea has resumed naphtha imports from Russia after a four-year hiatus. LG Chem successfully imported 27,000 tons of Russian naphtha. Naphtha cracking centers in South Korea consume 4 million tons of naphtha per month. At the same time, restrictions have been imposed on South Korea’s naphtha exports.
- China imported 210.02 million tons of iron ore in the first two months of 2026. This figure represents a 10% increase compared to the previous year.
- Deep-sea mining company Glomar Minerals and Australia’s Cobalt Blue Holdings announced plans to build a refinery in the U.S. within three years to process critical minerals extracted from the bottom of the Pacific Ocean. The facility is expected to cost less than $500 million and initially have an annual processing capacity of 200,000 tons, though it is also expected to have expansion potential. Cobalt Blue will provide the technology to extract at least five minerals from so-called polymetallic nodules. While the mining of seabed nodules presents significant engineering and technical challenges, the processing of these nodules has never been done on a commercial scale.
- South Korea has approved a three-year national roadmap aimed at transitioning from being an ICT powerhouse to becoming one of the world’s top three artificial intelligence (AI) leaders by 2028. The strategy outlines four key pillars: expanding the national digital infrastructure, strengthening digital talent, advancing national smart computing, and fostering an inclusive digital environment. On the infrastructure front, the government will upgrade nationwide 5G networks to standalone mode and accelerate the development of next-generation 6G technologies with the goal of commercial deployment by 2030. Additionally, it will tighten information security management, strengthen cyber defense capabilities in both the public and private sectors, and establish a national integrated data platform to support high-quality data ecosystems. To maximize digital competitiveness, the government will prioritize support for key technologies such as artificial intelligence, semiconductors, quantum computing, and blockchain.
- Fuel-saving measures have been implemented in Japan. The Mochimune fishing port in Shizuoka City is home to one of Japan’s largest shirasu fishing grounds. The fishing season opened on March 21, but due to a diesel shortage, fishermen are not going out to sea every day. Even when vessels do set out to sea, crew members are sailing at a slower-than-normal speed to conserve fuel, and they even cancel that day’s operations when the weather is bad or a poor catch is expected.
- China has launched two retaliatory trade investigations against the U.S. In a statement, the Ministry of Commerce noted that one of the investigations will focus on U.S. trade barriers that “disrupt global production and supply chains,” while the other will cover “green products.” The investigations were launched following the U.S.’s own investigations earlier this month into China and other economies regarding excess capacity and forced labor.
- Tesla reclaimed the global pure electric vehicle (EV) crown for the first time since the fourth quarter of 2024, following a 6.5% increase in deliveries during the first three months of the year. This modest increase was enough for the U.S. automaker to surpass China’s EV king BYD, which recorded a 25.5% drop in sales during the first quarter.
- China’s heavy-lift rocket, designed to rival SpaceX, failed during launch. The Tianlong-3 rocket is being developed in the hope of overcoming a major bottleneck in the deployment of the country’s internet satellite mega-constellations, with the aim of competing with SpaceX’s Starlink.

## Africa Region

- A U.S. company has finally acquired one of the world’s largest cobalt producers not under Chinese control. Virtus Minerals has completed the $30 million acquisition of Chemaf and committed to raising approximately $720 million in investment; this gives Washington an advantage in competing with Beijing for critical minerals in the resource-rich Democratic Republic of the Congo. Virtus is contributing $200 million to the Chemaf deal through a partnership with Indian mining firm Lloyds Metals and Energy, which will serve as Virtus’s operational partner; $475 million of the financing comes from New York-based investment firm Orion Resource Partners, while $75 million is sourced from other investors.
- Rwanda is threatening to stop protecting Exxon Mobil’s natural gas project from ISIS in order to counter the new financial sanctions imposed by the U.S. Rwandan Foreign Minister Olivier Nduhungirehe stated, “If our efforts and achievements are not recognized, we are prepared to withdraw from Mozambique.” Nduhungirehe wrote, “The issue is not that ‘Rwanda might withdraw.’” “The issue is that if sustainable funding is not provided for counterterrorism operations in Cabo Delgado, Rwanda will ‘WITHDRAW’ its troops from Mozambique.”
- Kenya has resumed construction of the multi-billion-dollar Standard Gauge Railway (SGR) extension project, which had been stalled for more than six years; after renegotiating its loans with China, the country restructured its heavy public debt using an innovative financing model. According to Kenyan officials, China Communications Construction Company (CCCC) and its subsidiary China Road and Bridge Corporation will construct the remaining two sections of the railway, which has an estimated cost of $5.4 billion. Last year, Kenya renegotiated its SGR loans with China, converting its dollar-denominated debt into yuan and extending the final repayment date to 2040.
- On March 26 in Beijing, DR Congo’s Minister of Mining Louis Watum Kabamba and China’s Minister of Natural Resources Guan Zhiou signed a memorandum of understanding (MOU) on “cooperation in the fields of geology and mineral resources.” The agreement will include regular consultations between government agencies, compliance with the DRC’s laws and regulations, and the protection of investments. Chinese companies, which are estimated to control more than 70 percent of the country’s cobalt, lithium, copper, and coltan reserves, now have the opportunity to further strengthen their dominance. The DRC holds approximately 70% of the world’s cobalt and coltan reserves and 10% of its copper reserves. Other resources include zinc, tungsten, tin, iron ore, gold, and diamonds. With the agreement, attention appears to be shifting primarily toward the Mines de Fer de la Grande Orientale project. The government has announced that an investment of $28.9 billion in this project could enable the production of 50 million tons of iron within a year, and up to 300 million tons annually at peak production levels.
