**Stadium diplomacy** is the construction of stadiums and sports facilities in other nations as a form of [foreign aid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_aid "Foreign aid") with implementation also supporting the overseas expansion of donor-country construction contractors.

China is the most prominent practitioner of this strategy, using it as a form of [soft power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_power_of_China "Soft power of China") to bolster diplomatic ties.[^1] [^2] [^3] The practice dates back to at least 1958, when China funded the [National Sports Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Sports_Stadium_\(Mongolia\) "National Sports Stadium (Mongolia)") in Mongolia.[^4] The method contrasts from other methods such as [stadium subsidy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stadium_subsidy "Stadium subsidy") [^5] [^6] [^7] which uses taxpayers money and gives it to the private sector to manage the facility and profit.[^8] [^9] [^10] [^11]

## Chinese diplomacy

The following includes overseas stadiums and sports facilities that were either donated outright or built with financial assistance from the [Chinese government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_government "Chinese government"), typically in the form of grants, concessional loans, or other aid-linked financing in cooperation with the host country.[^2] Projects carried out solely by Chinese construction firms on commercial terms, without financing support from the Chinese government, are not included.

#### PRC v. Taiwan cricket war

During the 2000s, the competition between the People's Republic of China and [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") played out in the Caribbean through stadium diplomacy. Beijing funded large-scale cricket and multi-purpose stadiums in countries that switched diplomatic recognition to China, while Taiwan provided more modest support to its remaining Caribbean allies: [Saint Lucia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Lucia "Saint Lucia"), [Saint Vincent and the Grenadines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines "Saint Vincent and the Grenadines"), and [Saint Kitts and Nevis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Kitts_and_Nevis "Saint Kitts and Nevis"). In Saint Kitts and Nevis, Taiwan supported [Warner Park Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warner_Park_Stadium "Warner Park Stadium") with a US$21 million donation ahead of the 2007 Cricket World Cup. In Saint Vincent and the Grenadines, Taiwan financed warm-up facilities. In Saint Lucia, the [George Odlum Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Odlum_Stadium "George Odlum Stadium") was originally built by China in 2002, and Taiwan later contributed to refurbishment by upgrading the athletics track after the country switched diplomatic recognition back to Taiwan in 2007.[^81] [^82] [^83] [^84] [^85]

### Asia

In 2014, Prime Minister [Hun Sen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hun_Sen "Hun Sen") requested that China help construct a stadium for Cambodia in advance of the [2023 Southeast Asian Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_SEA_Games "2023 SEA Games").[^86]<sup><span title="Page: 50">: 50</span> </sup> China began building [Morodok Techo National Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morodok_Techo_National_Stadium "Morodok Techo National Stadium") in 2017 at a cost of US$169 million and completed it for Cambodia in December 2021.[^86]<sup><span title="Page: 50">: 50</span></sup>

- [Laos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laos "Laos")

[New Laos National Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Laos_National_Stadium "New Laos National Stadium") (2009) - A sports complex with a 25,000 seat main venue and a 2,000 capacity indoor aquatics complex, with an outdoor warm-up pool, a tennis centre with 2,000 seats, six other tennis courts, two indoor stadiums each with a seating for 3,000 and an indoor shooting range with 50 seats. It was built in time for the [2009 Southeast Asian Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2009_Southeast_Asian_Games "2009 Southeast Asian Games"). In August 2008 the government stated 200 hectares of land in the outskirts of Vientiane was exchanged as part of the financing agreement with the Chinese government.[^87]

- [Mongolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolia "Mongolia")

National Sports Stadium (1958) and [Buyant Ukhaa Sport Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buyant_Ukhaa_Sport_Palace "Buyant Ukhaa Sport Palace") (2011) – The National Sports Stadium in Ulaanbaatar was constructed in 1958 with Chinese aid and was among the first major sports infrastructure projects funded by China during the Cold War. It marked the start of extensive Chinese development assistance to Mongolia and remains the country’s most important sporting venue, hosting the annual [Naadam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naadam "Naadam") festival. Decades later, the National Wrestling Palace was completed in 2011 with a ¥28 million grant from the Chinese government, providing a modern indoor arena to support traditional Mongolian wrestling and enhance training and competition facilities.[^88] [^89]

- [Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar "Myanmar")

[Thuwunna Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thuwunna_Stadium "Thuwunna Stadium") and [National Theatre of Yangon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Theatre_of_Yangon "National Theatre of Yangon") were built by the Chinese government as gifts in the 1980s, those buildings were also renovated with Chinese help.[^90]

- [Nepal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nepal "Nepal")

[Dashrath Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dashrath_Stadium "Dashrath Stadium") has undergone multiple renovation phases since the 1990s. Chinese government grants contributed to refurbishment work, including infrastructure improvements reported in 1999 and 2012. The 2012 upgrades included enhancements to lighting systems, installation of a backup generator, and improvements to the scoreboard and sound systems.[^91]

- [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria")

A stadium located in [Damascus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damascus "Damascus") built in 1980 by China National Corporation for Overseas Economic Cooperation using Chinese government aid funds.[^49] [^92]

### Europe

- [Belarus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belarus "Belarus")

[Belarusian National Football Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Football_Stadium_\(Belarus\) "National Football Stadium (Belarus)") (2025) – A 33,000-seat national stadium in Minsk built with Chinese government support and constructed under a general contract by Beijing Urban Construction Group. Opened in June 2025, it is the largest and most modern football venue in Belarus and was gifted as part of China’s foreign aid program.[^93]

### South Pacific

- [Cook Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cook_Islands "Cook Islands")

Telecom Sports Arena (2009) - A 1000-seat sports complex housing netball, volleyball, handball, weightlifting, and squash. Funding for the $14 million facility came from concessional loans provided by the Chinese government.

- [Federated States of Micronesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federated_States_of_Micronesia "Federated States of Micronesia")

FSM-China Friendship Sports Center (2002) - A multipurpose gym built from 1999-2002 by the Guangzhou International Economic And Technical Cooperation Company at a cost of 5 million USD was turned over to FSM from the government of China.[^94]

- [Fiji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiji "Fiji")

National Hockey Centre - Built with financial assistance from the Chinese government for the 2003 South Pacific Games.[^95]

- [Kiribati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kiribati "Kiribati")

Betia Sports Complex (2006) - Construction of the sports complex began in 2002 with a 5.5 million USD grant from the government of China.[^96] When Kiribati severed diplomatic ties with China by switching to Taiwan, China suspended work on the partially completed project.[^97] Taiwan restarted construction and the complex opened in 2006.[^98] The facilities include indoor and outdoor basketball courts, a soccer and football ground and a gymnasium seating more than one thousand.

- [Papua New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea "Papua New Guinea")

Wewak Sports Stadium (2010) - A stadium was built at a cost of 19 million Kina, the combined contribution of 12 million Kina in funds from the Chinese government and 7 million Kina contributed by Papua New Guinea.[^99]

- [Samoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samoa "Samoa")

Apia Park Stadium (1983) - A stadium built as an aid project by China for Samoa to host the 7th South-Pacific Games.[^100] When it came time in 2007, for Samoa to once again host the games, the Chinese government provided a grant of 19 million USD to refurbish the facilities, using a team of Chinese engineers with local contractors to do the work.[^101] [^102]

- [Solomon Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon_Islands "Solomon Islands")

[National Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Stadium_\(Solomon_Islands\) "National Stadium (Solomon Islands)") (2023) - A stadium was built at a cost of 71 million US dollars, as part of an aid project by China for Solomon Islands to host the [17th Pacific Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Pacific_Games "2023 Pacific Games").[^103]

[Providence Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Providence_Stadium "Providence Stadium") (2006) – A 15,000-seat cricket venue built with Indian financing, including a US$6 million grant and US$20 million in low-interest loans from the Government of India.[^104]

- [Haiti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haiti "Haiti")

Phoenix Stadium (2010) - Funded by the United States to replace a soccer stadium destroyed by the Haiti earthquake.

Samuel Kanyon Doe (SKD) Sports Complex (2026) - Study on feasibility of renovation funded by Saudi Arabia.[^105]
