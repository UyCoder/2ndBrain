The **Belt and Road Initiative** (**BRI** or **B&R**), also known as the **One Belt One Road** ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"): 一带一路; [pinyin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin "Pinyin"): *Yīdài Yīlù*) and sometimes called the **New Silk Road**, is a global infrastructure and economic development strategy of the [government of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_China "Government of China") since 2013. Projects in over 150 countries [^5] include [ports](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port "Port"), [railways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rail_transport "Rail transport"), [highways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highways "Highways"), [power stations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_stations "Power stations"), [aviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aviation "Aviation"), and [telecommunications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunications "Telecommunications").

The initiative was launched by [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) [general secretary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") in 2013 while visiting Kazakhstan. It aims to invest in over 150 countries and international organizations through six overland economic corridors and the [21st Century Maritime Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/21st_Century_Maritime_Silk_Road "21st Century Maritime Silk Road"). The BRI is central to China's [foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy_of_China "Foreign policy of China") and [economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_China "Economy of China"). Overland transport routes include the [China–Pakistan Economic Corridor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Pakistan_Economic_Corridor "China–Pakistan Economic Corridor"), the [Eurasian Land Bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Land_Bridge "Eurasian Land Bridge"), and the [New Eurasian Land Bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Eurasian_Land_Bridge "New Eurasian Land Bridge"). Maritime projects under the 21st Century Maritime Silk Road include the ports of [Hambantota, Sri Lanka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hambantota_International_Port "Hambantota International Port"), [Gwadar, Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gwadar_Port "Gwadar Port"), and [Piraeus, Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piraeus,_Greece "Piraeus, Greece"). BRI has exported [China's high-speed rail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-speed_rail_in_China "High-speed rail in China"), such as in the [Boten–Vientiane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boten%E2%80%93Vientiane_railway "Boten–Vientiane railway") and [Jakarta-Bandung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakarta-Bandung_high-speed_rail_line "Jakarta-Bandung high-speed rail line") lines.

As of 2025, participating countries account for nearly 75% of the world's population and over half of global [GDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_domestic_product "Gross domestic product"), based on aggregated membership and international economic datasets. The initiative is widely described as having the potential to enhance global trade, connectivity, and economic growth, particularly in developing economies. At the same time, it has been associated with concerns regarding environmental impact, human rights and governance standards, debt sustainability, and the [dual-use](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual-use_technology "Dual-use technology") nature of certain infrastructure projects.[^6]

## History

### Initial foreign investments

Global Chinese investment dates all the way back to the 1950s, shortly after its socialist reform from 1949 to 1952, with projects in [North Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Korea "North Korea"), [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam"), and numerous African countries.[^7] Under the leadership of [CCP chairman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party"), [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong"), African independence movements prompted China's interest in the region, accounting for 58% of its economic aid between 1956 and 1969.[^8] China funded primarily agricultural projects through companies like the [China Merchants Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Merchants_Group "China Merchants Group") and [China Resources Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Resources "China Resources"), for which each built foreign branches. China made these investments alongside tactics of diplomacy to improve [Sino-African relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-African_relations "Sino-African relations") for the sake of future economic cooperation.[^7]

Fueled by [isolationist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolationism "Isolationism") political motivations, these investments were relatively small. China's self-reliant aims generally left it incapable of any sizable foreign investment.[^7] [^9] However, following the 1966-1976 [Chinese Cultural Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_Revolution "Cultural Revolution"), severe economic shortages incentivized a change.[^10] Border tensions with the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") furthermore worried China, as apparent in [Chinese Foreign Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(China\) "Minister of Foreign Affairs (China)"), [Huang Hua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huang_Hua "Huang Hua") 's 1977 statement, saying that China needed to win the favor of the United States to avoid "revisionist Soviet social-imperialism," which he believed made the USSR China's "archenemy." Other Chinese officials and allies held similar beliefs.[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 5">: 5</span> </sup> Estimates found that in 1979, China's GDP [per capita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Per_capita "Per capita") was US$271.20, ranked 162 globally, with foreign exchange reserves at US$840 million.[^9] Just two years later, [Chairman of the CPPCC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping "Deng Xiaoping"), Deng Xiaoping announced the adoption of the [Open Door Policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_Door_Policy "Open Door Policy") in December 1978.[^12] The policy aimed to open China's economic doors, transitioning the country into a market-based economy, which was considered critical for economic growth.[^8]

China's policy of channeling its construction companies abroad began with former CCP General Secretary [Jiang Zemin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiang_Zemin "Jiang Zemin") 's [Go Out policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Go_Out_policy "Go Out policy").[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 67">: 67</span> </sup> [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") 's BRI built on and expanded this policy [^13]<sup><span title="Page: 67">: 67</span> </sup> as well as built on Jiang's [China Western Development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Western_Development "China Western Development") policy.[^14]<sup><span title="Page: 149">: 149</span></sup>

### 2013 Silk Road Economic Belt announcement

Xi announced the BRI concept as the "Silk Road Economic Belt" on 7 September 2013 at [Nazarbayev University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazarbayev_University "Nazarbayev University") in [Astana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astana "Astana"), Kazakhstan [^13]<sup><span title="Page: 75">: 75</span> </sup> In October 2013 during a speech delivered in Indonesia, Xi stated that China planned to build a "twenty-first century Maritime Silk Road" to enhance cooperation in Southeast Asia and beyond.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 75">: 75</span></sup>

### BRICA

The Belt and Road Industrial and Commercial Alliance (BRICA) was created 16 June 2016 and had 22 members representing 20 countries.[^15] It was created by the China Federation of Industrial Economics in Beijing and its stated aim is advancing "industrial investment and trade cooperation" across Belt and Road Initiative countries.[^16] [^17]

## Objectives

The Belt and Road Initiative addresses an "infrastructure gap" and thus has the potential to accelerate economic growth across the [Asia Pacific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia_Pacific "Asia Pacific"), Africa and [Central and Eastern Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_and_Eastern_Europe "Central and Eastern Europe"), according to a report from the [World Pensions Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Pensions_%26_Investments_Forum "World Pensions & Investments Forum").[^18]

The initial focus has been infrastructure investment, education, construction materials, railway and highway, automobile, real estate, power grid, and iron and steel.[^19] Already, some estimates list the Belt and Road Initiative as one of the largest infrastructure and investment projects in history, covering more than 68 countries, including 65% of the [world population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_population "World population") and 40% of the global gross domestic product as of 2017.[^20] [^21] The project builds on the old trade routes that once connected China to the west, [Marco Polo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marco_Polo "Marco Polo") and [Ibn Battuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Battuta "Ibn Battuta") 's routes in the north and the maritime expedition routes of [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") admiral [Zheng He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zheng_He "Zheng He") in the south. The Belt and Road Initiative now refers to the entire geographical area of the historic " [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road") " [trade route](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade_route "Trade route"), which has been continuously used in antiquity.[^22]

The stated goals of the BRI were officially presented for the first time in a 2015 document, the *Vision and Actions on Jointly Building Belt and Road*.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 76">: 76</span> </sup> It outlined six economic corridors for trade and investment connectivity would be implemented.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 76">: 76</span></sup>

The BRI develops new markets for Chinese firms, channels excess industrial capacity overseas, increases China's access to resources, and strengthens its ties with partner countries.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 34">: 34</span> </sup> The initiative generates its own export demand because Chinese loans enable participating countries to develop infrastructure projects involving Chinese firms and expertise.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 43">: 43</span> </sup> The infrastructure developed also helps China to address the imbalance between its more developed [eastern regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_China "East China") and its less developed [western regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_China "Western China").[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 43">: 43</span></sup>

For developing countries, the BRI is appealing because of the opportunities it offers to alleviate their economic disadvantages relative to Western countries.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 49">: 49</span> </sup> The BRI offers them infrastructure development, financial assistance, and technical assistance from China.[^24]<sup><span title="Page: 223">: 223</span> </sup> The increase in foreign direct investment and increased trade linkages also increases employment and poverty alleviation for these countries.[^24]<sup><span title="Page: 224">: 224</span></sup>

While some countries, especially the United States, view the project critically because of Chinese government influence, others point to the creation of a new global growth engine by connecting and moving Asia, Europe and Africa closer together.[^25]

In the maritime silk road, which is already the route for more than half of all containers in the world, deep-water ports are being expanded, logistical hubs are being built, and new traffic routes are being created in the hinterland. The maritime silk road runs with its connections from the Chinese coast to the south, linking [Hanoi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanoi "Hanoi"), [Kuala Lumpur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuala_Lumpur "Kuala Lumpur"), [Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore "Singapore"), and [Jakarta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakarta "Jakarta"), then westward linking the Sri Lankan capital city of [Colombo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colombo "Colombo"), and [Malé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mal%C3%A9 "Malé"), capital of the Maldives, and onward to East Africa, and the city of [Mombasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mombasa "Mombasa"), in Kenya. From there the linkage moves northward to [Djibouti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djibouti "Djibouti"), through the Red Sea and the [Suez Canal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Canal "Suez Canal") to the Mediterranean, thereby linking [Haifa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haifa "Haifa"), [Istanbul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Istanbul "Istanbul"), and [Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athens "Athens"), to the Upper Adriatic region to the northern Italian hub of [Trieste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_Trieste "Port of Trieste"), with its international free port and its rail connections to Central Europe and the [North Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Sea "North Sea").[^26] [^27] [^28] [^29]

As a result, [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland "Poland"), the [Baltic States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_States "Baltic States"), Northern Europe, and Central Europe are also connected to the maritime silk road and logistically linked to East Africa, [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") and China via the [Adriatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adriatic "Adriatic") ports and [Piraeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piraeus "Piraeus"). All in all, the ship connections for container transports between Asia and Europe will be reorganized. In contrast to the longer East Asian traffic via north-west Europe, the southern sea route through the Suez Canal towards the junction Trieste shortens the goods transport by at least four days.[^30] [^31] [^32]

In connection with the Silk Road project, China is also trying to network worldwide research activities.[^33]

Simon Shen and Wilson Chan have compared the initiative to the post-World War II [Marshall Plan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Plan "Marshall Plan").[^34] It is the largest infrastructure investment by a great power since the Marshall Plan.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 1">: 1</span></sup>

China intentionally frames the BRI flexibly in order to adapt it to changing needs or policies, such as the addition of a "Health Silk Road" during the COVID-19 pandemic.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 147">: 147</span></sup>

### Initiative name

The official name for the initiative is the *Silk Road Economic Belt and 21st-Century Maritime Silk Road Development Strategy* (丝绸之路经济带和21世纪海上丝绸之路发展战略),[^35] which was initially abbreviated as the *One Belt One Road* (Chinese: 一带一路) or the *OBOR* strategy. The English translation has been changed to the *Belt and Road Initiative* (BRI) since 2016, when the Chinese government considered the emphasis on the words "one" and "strategy" were prone to misinterpretation and suspicion, so they opted for the more inclusive term "initiative" in its translation.[^36] [^37] However, "One Belt One Road" is still the reference term in Chinese-language media.[^38]

### International relations

The Belt and Road Initiative is believed by some analysts to be a way to extend Chinese economic and political influence.[^21] [^39] Some geopolitical analysts have couched the Belt and Road Initiative in the context of [Halford Mackinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halford_Mackinder "Halford Mackinder") 's [heartland theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Geographical_Pivot_of_History "The Geographical Pivot of History").[^40] [^41] [^42] Scholars have noted that official PRC media attempts to mask any strategic dimensions of the Belt and Road Initiative as a motivation,[^43] while others note that the BRI also serves as signposts for Chinese provinces and ministries, guiding their policies and actions.[^44] Academic [Keyu Jin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keyu_Jin "Keyu Jin") writes that while the BRI does advance strategic interests for China, it also reflects the CCP's vision of a world order based on "building a global [community of shared future](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_of_Common_Destiny "Community of Common Destiny") ".[^45]<sup><span title="Pages: 281–282">: 281–282</span></sup>

China has already invested billions of dollars in several South Asian countries like [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan"), [Nepal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nepal "Nepal"), [Sri Lanka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sri_Lanka "Sri Lanka"), [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh") and [Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afghanistan "Afghanistan") to improve their basic infrastructure, with implications for China's trade regime as well as its military influence. This project can also become a new economic corridor for different regions. For example, in the [Caucasus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caucasus "Caucasus") region, China considered cooperation with [Armenia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenia "Armenia") from May 2019. Chinese and Armenian sides had multiple meetings, signed contracts, initiated a north–south road program to solve even infrastructure-related aspects.[^46] A 2025 academic review of China's role in the Middle East interprets the BRI as not only an infrastructure program but also a diplomatic instrument for expanding Chinese influence through [soft power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_power_of_China "Soft power of China"). It notes that China utilizes digital projects, cultural exchange, and educational initiatives to project influence while maintaining strategic ambiguity in regions like the Gulf.[^47]

#### Military implications

The BRI has been viewed as part of a strategy to lessen the effect of [choke points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Choke_point "Choke point") such as the [Strait of Malacca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca "Strait of Malacca") in the event of a military conflict and to blunt the U.S. [island chain strategy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island_chain_strategy "Island chain strategy") and the [Malacca dilemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malacca_dilemma "Malacca dilemma").[^48] [^49] [^50] [^51] A 2023 study by [AidData](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AidData "AidData") of the [College of William & Mary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/College_of_William_%26_Mary "College of William & Mary") determined that overseas port locations subject to significant BRI investment raise questions of dual military and civilian use and may be favorable for future naval bases.[^52] [^53]

Writing in 2023, [David H. Shinn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_H._Shinn "David H. Shinn") and academic Joshua Eisenman state that through the BRI, China seeks to strengthen its strategic position and diminish American military influence, but that China's BRI activity is likely not a prelude to American-style military bases or American-style global military presence.[^54]<sup><span title="Page: 161">: 161</span> </sup> China's construction of ports which could have dual-uses has been characterized as an attempt to avoid the necessity of establishing strictly military bases.[^54]<sup><span title="Page: 273">: 273</span> </sup> According to academic Xue Guifang, China is not motivated to repeat the model of the [People's Liberation Army Support Base in Djibouti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army_Support_Base_in_Djibouti "People's Liberation Army Support Base in Djibouti").[^54]<sup><span title="Page: 273">: 273</span></sup>

### Western regions

Economic development of China's less developed western regions, particularly [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang"), is one of the government's stated goals in pursuing the BRI.[^55] [^56]<sup><span title="Page: 199">: 199</span> </sup> The strategic location of Xinjiang has also been recognized as central. In 2014, [state media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_media "State media") outlet [Xinhua News Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinhua_News_Agency "Xinhua News Agency") stated Xinjiang "connects Pakistan, Mongolia, Russia, India, and four other central Asian countries with a borderline extending 5,600 km, giving it easy access to the Eurasian heartland." [^57] Some analysts have suggested that the CCP considers Xinjiang's local population, the [Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghurs "Uyghurs"), and their attachment to their traditional lands potential threats to the BRI's success, or it fears that developing Xinjiang may also open it up to [radicalizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_extremism "Islamic extremism") [Islamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") influences from other states which are participating in the BRI.[^58] [^59] [^60] [^61]

## Leadership

A [leading group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Construction_Leadership_Group "Belt and Road Construction Leadership Group") was formed sometime in late 2014, and its leadership line-up publicized on 1 February 2015. This steering committee reports directly into the [State Council of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Council_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "State Council of the People's Republic of China") and is composed of several political heavyweights, evidence of the importance of the program to the government. Then Vice-Premier [Zhang Gaoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhang_Gaoli "Zhang Gaoli"), who was also a member of the 7-man [CCP Politburo Standing Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Politburo Standing Committee of the Chinese Communist Party"), was named leader of the group, and [Wang Huning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Huning "Wang Huning"), [Wang Yang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Yang_\(politician\) "Wang Yang (politician)"), [Yang Jing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_Jing "Yang Jing"), and [Yang Jiechi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_Jiechi "Yang Jiechi") named deputy leaders.[^62]

On 28 March 2015, China's State Council outlined the principles, framework, key areas of cooperation and cooperation mechanisms with regard to the initiative.[^63] The BRI is considered a central element within [China's foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy_of_China "Foreign policy of China"), and was incorporated into the [CCP's constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Constitution of the Chinese Communist Party") in 2017 during its [19th Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/19th_National_Congress_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "19th National Congress of the Chinese Communist Party").[^64] [^54]<sup><span title="Page: 58">: 58</span> </sup> The BRI represents a set of policies for Chinese engagement with the [global South](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_North_and_Global_South "Global North and Global South"), including diversifying resource and energy supplies, building loan-funded infrastructure using Chinese companies, creating new markets for Chinese companies, and engaging global South countries simultaneously at bilateral and regional levels.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 6">: 6</span></sup>

With regard to China and the African countries, the [Forum on China–Africa Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forum_on_China%E2%80%93Africa_Cooperation "Forum on China–Africa Cooperation") (FOCAC) is a significant multilateral cooperation mechanism for facilitating BRI projects.[^65] The [China–Arab States Cooperation Forum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Arab_States_Cooperation_Forum "China–Arab States Cooperation Forum") (CASCF) serves a similar coordinating role with regard to BRI projects in the Arab states.[^65]

## Membership

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Countries_of_the_Belt_and_Road_Initiative.svg/250px-Countries_of_the_Belt_and_Road_Initiative.svg.png)

Countries in the Belt and Road Initiative are in Dark Green, countries that have officially withdrawn are in Light Green, and countries that have de facto withdrawn but still have active MoU's or other agreements are in yellow.

Countries join the Belt and Road Initiative by signing a Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) with China regarding their participation in it. The Government of China maintains a listing of all involved countries on its Belt and Road Portal,[^66] and state media outlet Xinhua News Agency puts out a press release whenever a memorandum of understanding related to the Belt and Road Initiative is signed with a new country.[^67] As of May 2025, according to observers at [Fudan University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fudan_University "Fudan University") 's Green Finance and Development Center, between 146 and 150 states were members of the Belt and Road Initiative.[^68]

## Financing

China's investment in the BRI began at a moderate level in 2013 and increased significantly over 2014 and 2015.[^83]<sup><span title="Page: 214">: 214</span> </sup> Investment volume peaked in 2016 and 2017.[^83]<sup><span title="Page: 214">: 214</span> </sup> Afterwards, investments decreased gradually, and then significantly during the COVID-19 pandemic.[^83]<sup><span title="Page: 214">: 214</span> </sup> The BRI's lowest investment volume was in 2023.[^83]<sup><span title="Page: 214">: 214</span></sup>

China's investment in the Maritime Silk Road portion of the BRI has grown at a steady pace.[^83]<sup><span title="Page: 214">: 214</span> </sup> As of 2023, Maritime Silk Road investments were 60% of the BRI's total investment volume.[^83]<sup><span title="Page: 214">: 214</span></sup>

### Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB)

The [Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_Infrastructure_Investment_Bank "Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank"), first proposed in October 2013, is a development bank dedicated to lending for infrastructure projects. As of 2015, China announced that over one trillion yuan (US$160 billion) of infrastructure-related projects were in planning or construction.[^84]

The primary goals of AIIB are to address the expanding infrastructure needs across Asia, enhance regional integration, promote economic development and improve public access to social services.[^85] At inception, the AIIB was explicitly linked to the BRI.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 166">: 166</span> </sup> The AIIB was subsequently broadened to include investments with states that are not involved with the BRI.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 166">: 166</span></sup>

Loans through AIIB are accessible on AIIB's website, unlike many other forms of Chinese investment through the BRI.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 85">: 85</span></sup>

### Silk Road Fund

On 29 December 2014, China established the Silk Road Fund with total capital of US$40 billion and ¥100 billion.[^24]<sup><span title="Page: 221">: 221</span> </sup> The Silk Road Fund invests in BRI infrastructure, resource development, energy development, industrial cooperation, and financial cooperation.[^24]<sup><span title="Page: 221">: 221</span> </sup> The [Karot Hydropower Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karot_Hydropower_Project "Karot Hydropower Project") in Pakistan was its first project.[^24]<sup><span title="Pages: 221–222">: 221–222</span></sup>

### China Investment Corporation

[China Investment Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Investment_Corporation "China Investment Corporation") supports the BRI by investing in its infrastructure projects, participating in other BRI-related development funds, and assisting Chinese corporations with foreign mergers and acquisitions.[^86]<sup><span title="Page: 124">: 124</span> </sup> China Investment Corporation also invested in the Silk Road Fund.[^24]<sup><span title="Page: 221">: 221</span></sup>

CIC's domestic subsidiary [Central Huijin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Huijin_Investment "Central Huijin Investment") indirectly supports the BRI through its support of domestic financial institutions, such as [policy banks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Policy_bank "Policy bank") or [state-owned](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State-owned_enterprises_of_China "State-owned enterprises of China") commercial banks, which in turn fund BRI projects.[^86]<sup><span title="Page: 124">: 124</span></sup>

### Policy banks

Policy banks, including the [Chinese Development Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Development_Bank "China Development Bank") and the [Export-Import Bank of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Export%E2%80%93Import_Bank_of_China "Export–Import Bank of China"), have important roles in funding BRI projects.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 167">: 167</span></sup>

[Chinese state‑owned enterprises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State-owned_enterprises_of_China "State-owned enterprises of China") (SOEs) also play a major role in financing and executing BRI projects. In 2024, SOEs regained a dominant position in BRI investments, led by Sinopec, PowerChina, and China National Chemical Engineering.[^87]

### Other financing

Between 2015 and 2020, the [Bank of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_of_China "Bank of China") lent over US$185.1 billion for BRI projects.[^88]<sup><span title="Page: 143">: 143</span></sup>

As of April 2019, the [Industrial and Commercial Bank of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_and_Commercial_Bank_of_China "Industrial and Commercial Bank of China") had lent over US$100 billion for BRI projects.[^88]<sup><span title="Page: 143">: 143</span></sup>

### Debt sustainability

In 2017, China joined the G20 Operational Guidelines for Sustainable Financing and in 2019 to the G20 Principles for Quality Infrastructure Investment. The [Center for Global Development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_for_Global_Development "Center for Global Development") described China's New Debt Sustainability Framework as "virtually identical" to the World Bank's and IMF's own debt sustainability framework.[^89] [^90] According to academic Jeremy Garlick, for many impoverished countries, China is the best available option for development finance and practical assistance.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 6">: 6</span> </sup> Western investors and the World Bank have been reluctant to invest in troubled countries like Pakistan, Cambodia, Tajikistan, and Montenegro, which China is willing to invest in through the BRI.[^13]<sup><span title="Pages: 146–147">: 146–147</span> </sup> Generally, the United States and EU have not offered global South countries with investment comparable to what China offers through the BRI.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 147">: 147</span></sup>

China is the largest bilateral lender in the world.[^91] Loans are backed by [collateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collateral_\(finance\) "Collateral (finance)") such as rights to a mine, a port or money.[^92]

This policy has been alleged by the US Government to be a form of [debt-trap diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt-trap_diplomacy "Debt-trap diplomacy"); however, according to the [Alibaba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alibaba_Group "Alibaba Group") -owned *[South China Morning Post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Morning_Post "South China Morning Post")*, the term itself has come under scrutiny as analysts and researchers have pointed out that there is no evidence to prove that China is deliberately aiming to do debt-trap diplomacy.[^93] Research from [Deborah Brautigam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deborah_Br%C3%A4utigam "Deborah Bräutigam"), an international political economy professor at [Johns Hopkins University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University "Johns Hopkins University"), and Meg Rithmire, an associate professor at [Harvard Business School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Business_School "Harvard Business School"), have disputed the allegations of debt-trap diplomacy by China and pointed out that "Chinese banks are willing to restructure the terms of existing loans and have never actually seized an asset from any country, much less the [port of Hambantota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_Hambantota "Port of Hambantota") ". In an editorial letter, they argued that it was 'long overdue' for people to know the truth and not to have it be "wilfully misunderstood".[^94] Writing in 2023, academic and former UK diplomat [Kerry Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerry_Brown_\(historian\) "Kerry Brown (historian)") states that China's relationship to the Hambantota port has become the opposite of the theorized debt-trap modus operandi.[^95]<sup><span title="Page: 56">: 56</span> </sup> Brown observes that China has had to commit more money to the project, expose itself to further risk, and has had to become entangled in complex local politics.[^95]<sup><span title="Page: 56">: 56</span> </sup> As of 2024, the port has not been a significant economic success, although shipping through the port is on the increase.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 69">: 69</span></sup>

In his comparison of BRI loans to IMF loans and [Paris Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Club "Paris Club") loans, which have not been very successful in reducing the debt of developing countries, academic Jeremy Garlick concludes that there is no reason to believe the BRI framework is worse for developing countries' debt than Western lending frameworks.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 69">: 69</span></sup>

For China itself, a report from [Fitch Ratings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fitch_Ratings "Fitch Ratings") doubts Chinese banks' ability to control risks, as they do not have a good record of [allocating resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resource_allocation "Resource allocation") efficiently at home. This may lead to new [asset quality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asset_quality "Asset quality") problems for Chinese banks where most funding is likely to originate.[^96]

In August 2022, China announced that it would forgive 23 of its interest-free loans to 17 African nations.[^97] The loans had matured at the end of 2021.[^97]

A 2025 study by the [Lowy Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lowy_Institute "Lowy Institute") found that for 54 developing countries debt repayments to China exceed those owed to [Paris Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Club "Paris Club") countries.[^98] [^99] [^100] [^101] The study stated that "Chinese lending has been a driver of debt sustainability problems in many countries around the world." [^101]

### Effects

An analysis of BRI loans from 2007 to 2022 found that they make other forms of financing on the [bond market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_market "Bond market") more expensive for the debtor country.[^102]

## Infrastructure networks

The BRI is composed of six urban development land corridors linked by road, rail, energy, and digital infrastructure and the Maritime Silk Road linked by the development of ports.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 1">: 1</span></sup>

The Silk Road has proven to be a productive but at the same time elusive concept, increasingly used as an evocative metaphor.[^103] With China's 'Belt and Road Initiative', it has found fresh invocations and audiences.[^104] These are the belts in the name, and there is also a maritime silk road.[^105] Infrastructure corridors spanning some 60 countries, primarily in Asia and Europe but also including Oceania and East Africa, will cost an estimated US$4–8 trillion.[^106] [^107] The land corridors include:[^105]

- The [China–Pakistan Economic Corridor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Pakistan_Economic_Corridor "China–Pakistan Economic Corridor") (CPEC) (Chinese:中国-巴基斯坦经济走廊; [Urdu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urdu_language "Urdu language"): پاكستان-چین اقتصادی راہداری) is the most developed land corridor of the BRI, as of at least 2024.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 42">: 42</span> </sup> It is a US$62 billion collection of infrastructure projects throughout Pakistan [^108] [^109] [^110] which aims to rapidly modernize Pakistan's transportation networks, energy infrastructure, and economy.[^109] [^110] [^111] [^112] On 13 November 2016, CPEC became partly operational when Chinese cargo was transported overland to [Gwadar Port](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gwadar_Port "Gwadar Port") for onward maritime shipment to Africa and West Asia.[^113] CPEC and Gwadar port infrastructure is particularly significant because it opens routes independent of the [Malacca strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malacca_strait "Malacca strait").[^114]<sup><span title="Page: 99">: 99</span></sup>
- The [New Eurasian Land Bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Eurasian_Land_Bridge "New Eurasian Land Bridge"), which runs from Western China to [Western Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Russia "European Russia") through Kazakhstan, and includes the [Silk Road Railway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chongqing-Xinjiang-Europe_Railway "Chongqing-Xinjiang-Europe Railway") through China's [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang") Autonomous Region, Kazakhstan, Russia, [Belarus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belarus "Belarus"), Poland and Germany. [Astana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astana "Astana"), Kazakhstan is a major hub for the BRI, including related financial services and legal services.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 45">: 45</span> </sup> [Khorgos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khorgos "Khorgos") which straddles the Kazakhstan-China border, is the major [dry port](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry_port "Dry port") for this corridor and is the place where rail cargo switches from the standard gauge used in China to the wider gauge used in the former Soviet Union.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 57">: 57</span></sup>
- The [China–Mongolia–Russia Economic Corridor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Mongolia%E2%80%93Russia_Economic_Corridor "China–Mongolia–Russia Economic Corridor"),[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 39">: 39</span> </sup> running from Northern China through Mongolia to the [Russian Far East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Far_East "Russian Far East"). The Russian government-established [Russian Direct Investment Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Direct_Investment_Fund "Russian Direct Investment Fund") and China's China Investment Corporation, a Chinese sovereign wealth fund, partnered in 2012 to create the [Russia-China Investment Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia-China_Investment_Fund "Russia-China Investment Fund"), which concentrates on opportunities in bilateral integration.[^115] [^116]
- The [China–Central Asia–West Asia Corridor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Central_Asia%E2%80%93West_Asia_Corridor "China–Central Asia–West Asia Corridor"), which will run from Western China to Turkey.
- The [China-Indochina Peninsula economic corridor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China-Indochina_Peninsula_economic_corridor "China-Indochina Peninsula economic corridor"), which will run from Southern China to Singapore.
- The [Trans-Himalayan Multi-dimensional Connectivity Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Himalayan_Multi-dimensional_Connectivity_Network "Trans-Himalayan Multi-dimensional Connectivity Network"), which will turn Nepal from a landlocked to a land-linked country.

This kind of connectivity is the focus of BRI efforts because China's significant economic growth has been supported by exports and the overland import of major quantities of raw materials and intermediate components.[^114]<sup><span title="Page: 98">: 98</span> </sup> By 2022, China had built cross-border highways and expressway networks to almost every nearby region.[^114]<sup><span title="Page: 99">: 99</span></sup>

Railway connectivity is a major focus of the BRI.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 62">: 62</span> </sup> Use of BRI-related rail surged after the COVID-19 pandemic, which had congested air freight and sea shipping, and hampered port access.[^114]<sup><span title="Page: 99">: 99</span> </sup> As of 2024, multiple BRI railway projects were branded as the China Railways Express, which linked approximately 60 Chinese cities to approximately 50 European cities.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 62">: 62</span></sup>

### Silk Road Economic Belt

Xi Jinping visited [Astana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astana "Astana"), Kazakhstan, and Southeast Asia in September and October 2013, and proposed jointly building a new economic area, the Silk Road Economic Belt (SREB) ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters "Simplified Chinese characters"): 丝绸之路经济带).[^117] The "belt" includes countries on the original Silk Road through Central Asia, West Asia, the Middle East, and Europe. The initiative would create a cohesive economic area by building both hard infrastructure such as rail and road links and soft infrastructure such as trade agreements and a common commercial legal structure with a court system to police the agreements.[^118]

Besides a zone largely analogous to the historical Silk Road, an expansion includes South Asia and Southeast Asia. The BRI is important from the Southeast Asian perspective because, with the exception of Singapore, Southeast Asian countries require significant infrastructure investment to advance their development.[^119]<sup><span title="Page: 210">: 210</span></sup>

Three belts are proposed. The North belt would go through Central Asia and Russia to Europe. The Central belt passes through Central Asia and West Asia to the [Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf") and the [Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean "Mediterranean"). The South belt runs from China through Southeast Asia and South Asia and on to the Indian Ocean through Pakistan. The strategy will integrate China with Central Asia through Kazakhstan's [Nurly Zhol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurly_Zhol "Nurly Zhol") infrastructure program.[^120]

### 21st Century Maritime Silk Road

The "21st Century Maritime Silk Road" (Chinese:21世纪海上丝绸之路), or just the Maritime Silk Road, is the sea route 'corridor.' [^118] It is a complementary initiative aimed at investing and fostering collaboration in Southeast Asia, Oceania and Africa through several contiguous bodies of water: the [South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Sea "South China Sea"), the [South Pacific Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Pacific_Ocean "South Pacific Ocean"), and the wider Indian Ocean area.[^121] [^122] [^123] It was first proposed in October 2013 by Xi Jinping in a speech to the [Indonesian Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Representative_Council "People's Representative Council").[^124]

The maritime Silk Road runs with its links from the Chinese coast to the south via Hanoi to Jakarta, Singapore and Kuala Lumpur through the [Strait of Malacca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca "Strait of Malacca") via the Sri Lankan Colombo opposite the southern tip of India via Malé, the capital of the Maldives, to the East African Mombasa, from there to Djibouti, then through the Red Sea over the Suez Canal into the Mediterranean, there via Haifa, Istanbul and Athens to the Upper Adriatic to the northern Italian junction of [Trieste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trieste "Trieste") with its international free port and its rail connections to Central Europe and the North Sea.

Container terminals in Kwai Chung, Hong Kong

According to estimates in 2019, the land route of the Silk Road remains a niche project and the bulk of the Silk Road trade continues to be carried out by sea. The reasons are primarily due to the cost of container transport. The maritime Silk Road is also considered to be particularly attractive for trade because, in contrast to the land-based Silk Road leading through the sparsely populated Central Asia, there are on the one hand, far more states on the way to Europe and, on the other hand, their markets, development opportunities, and population numbers are far larger. In particular, there are many land-based links, such as the Bangladesh-China-India-Myanmar Corridor (BCIM). Due to the attractiveness of this now subsidized sea route and the related investments, there have been major shifts in the logistics chains of the shipping sector in recent years.[^125] Due to its unique geographical location, Myanmar is viewed to be playing a pivotal role in China's BRI projects.[^126]

From the Chinese point of view, Africa is important as a market, raw material supplier and platform for the expansion of the new Silk Road, so investment in the coasts of Africa (particularly East Africa) is part of its economic strategy. In Kenya's port of [Mombasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mombasa "Mombasa"), China has built a rail and road connection to the inland and to the capital [Nairobi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nairobi "Nairobi"). To the northeast of Mombasa, a large port with 32 berths including an adjacent industrial area including infrastructure with new traffic corridors to [South Sudan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Sudan "South Sudan") and [Ethiopia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopia "Ethiopia") is being built. A modern deep-water port, a satellite city, an airfield and an industrial area are being built in [Bagamoyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bagamoyo "Bagamoyo"), Tanzania. Further towards the Mediterranean, the [Teda Egypt special economic zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Economic_and_Trade_Cooperation_Zone "Suez Economic and Trade Cooperation Zone") is being built near the Egyptian coastal town of [Ain Sochna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ain_Sokhna "Ain Sokhna") as a joint Chinese-Egyptian project.[^127] [^128]

As part of its Silk Road strategy, China is participating in large areas of Africa in the construction and operation of train routes, roads, airports and industry. In several countries, such as [Zambia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zambia "Zambia"), Ethiopia and [Ghana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghana "Ghana"), dams have been built with Chinese help. In Nairobi, China is funding the construction of the tallest building in Africa, the Pinnacle Towers. With the Chinese investments of 60 billion dollars for Africa announced in September 2018, on the one hand, sales markets are created, and the local economy is promoted, and, on the other hand, African raw materials are made available for China.[^129]

One of the Chinese bridgeheads in Europe is the port of Piraeus. Overall, Chinese companies are to invest a total of 350 million euros directly in the port facilities there by 2026 and a further 200 million euros in associated projects such as hotels.[^130] In Europe, China wants to continue investing in [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portugal "Portugal") with its deep-water port in [Sines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sines "Sines"), but especially in Italy and there at the Adriatic logistics hub around Trieste. [Venice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venice "Venice"), the historically important European endpoint of the maritime Silk Road, has less and less commercial importance today due to the shallow depth or silting of its port.[^131]

The international free zone of Trieste provides in particular special areas for storage, handling and processing as well as transit zones for goods.[^132] [^133] At the same time, logistics and shipping companies invest in their technology and locations in order to benefit from ongoing developments.[^134] [^135] This also applies to the logistics connections between Turkey and the free [port of Trieste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_Trieste "Port of Trieste"), which are important for the Silk Road, and from there by train to Rotterdam and Zeebrugge. There is also direct cooperation, for example between Trieste, [Bettembourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bettembourg "Bettembourg"), and the Chinese province of [Sichuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sichuan "Sichuan"). While direct train connections from China to Europe, such as from Chengdu to Vienna overland, are partially stagnating or discontinued, there are (as of 2019) new weekly rail connections between [Wolfurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfurt "Wolfurt") or [Nuremberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuremberg "Nuremberg") and Trieste or between Trieste, [Vienna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vienna "Vienna") and [Linz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linz "Linz") on the maritime Silk Road.[^136] [^137]

Port of Trieste

There are also extensive intra-European infrastructure projects to adapt trade flows to current needs. Concrete projects (as well as their financing), which are to ensure the connection of the Mediterranean ports with the European hinterland, are decided among others at the annual China-Central-East-Europe summit, which was launched in 2012. This applies, for example, to the expansion of the Belgrade-Budapest railway line, the construction of the high-speed train between Milan, Venice and Trieste [^138] and connections on the Adriatic-Baltic and Adriatic-North Sea axis. Poland, the Baltic States, Northern Europe and Central Europe are also connected to the maritime Silk Road through many links and are thus logistically networked via the Adriatic ports and Piraeus to East Africa, India and China. Overall the pan is that the ship connections for container transport between Asia and Europe will be reorganized. In contrast to the longer East Asia traffic via northwest Europe, the south-facing sea route through the Suez Canal towards the Trieste bridgehead shortens the transport of goods by at least four days.[^139]

According to a study by the [University of Antwerp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Antwerp "University of Antwerp"), the maritime route via Trieste dramatically reduces transport costs. The example of [Munich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munich "Munich") shows that the transport there from Shanghai via Trieste takes 33 days, while the northern route takes 43 days. From Hong Kong, the southern route reduces transport to Munich from 37 to 28 days. The shorter route leads to better use of the ships and lower CO <sub>2</sub> emissions. Therefore, in the Mediterranean area, where the economic zone of the [Liverpool–Milan Axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liverpool%E2%80%93Milan_Axis "Liverpool–Milan Axis") meets functioning railroad connections and deep-water ports, there are significant growth zones. Henning Vöpel, Director of the Hamburg World Economic Institute, says that the North Range (i.e. transport via the North Sea ports to Europe) will not necessarily remain dominant in the medium term.[^140]

From 2025, the [Brenner Base Tunnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brenner_Base_Tunnel "Brenner Base Tunnel") will also link the upper Adriatic with southern Germany. The port of Trieste, next to Gioia Tauro the only deep water port in the central Mediterranean for container ships of the seventh generation, is therefore a special target for Chinese investments. In March 2019, the [China Communications Construction Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Communications_Construction_Company "China Communications Construction Company") (CCCC) signed agreements to promote the ports of Trieste and Genoa. Accordingly, the port's annual handling capacity will be increased from 10,000 to 25,000 trains in Trieste (Trihub project) and a reciprocal platform to promote and handle trade between Europe and China will be created. It is also about logistics promotion between the North Adriatic port and Shanghai or [Guangdong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangdong "Guangdong"). This also includes a state Hungarian investment of 100 million euros for a 32 hectare logistics center and funding from the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") of 45 million euros in 2020 for the development of the railway system in the port city.[^141] Furthermore, the Hamburg port logistics group [HHLA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HHLA "HHLA") invested in the logistics platform of the port of Trieste (PLT) in September 2020.[^142] In 2020, Duisburger Hafen AG (Duisport), the world's largest intermodal terminal operator, took a 15% stake in the Trieste freight terminal.[^143] There are also further contacts between Hamburg, Bremen and Trieste with regard to cooperation.[^144] There are also numerous collaborations in the Upper Adriatic, for example with the logistics platform in Cervignano.[^145] In particular, the area of the upper Adriatic is developing into an extended intersection of the economic areas known as the [Blue Banana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_Banana "Blue Banana") and the [Golden Banana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Banana "Golden Banana"). The importance of the free port of Trieste is expected to continue to increase in the coming years due to the planned port expansion and the expansion of the Baltic-Adriatic railway axis ([Semmering Base Tunnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semmering_Base_Tunnel "Semmering Base Tunnel"), [Koralm Tunnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koralm_Tunnel "Koralm Tunnel") and in the wider area Brenner Base Tunnel).[^146] [^147] [^148]

### Ice Silk Road

In addition to the Maritime Silk Road, Russia and China are reported to have agreed to jointly build an 'Ice Silk Road' along the [Northern Sea Route](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Sea_Route "Northern Sea Route") in the Arctic, along a maritime route within Russian territorial waters.[^149] [^150]

China [COSCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COSCO "COSCO") Shipping Corp. has completed several trial trips on [Arctic shipping routes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_shipping_routes "Arctic shipping routes"), and Chinese and Russian companies are cooperating on [oil and gas exploration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum_exploration_in_the_Arctic "Petroleum exploration in the Arctic") in the area and to advance comprehensive collaboration on infrastructure construction, tourism and scientific expeditions.[^150]

### Digital Silk Road

In 2015, Xi announced the Digital Silk Road.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 71">: 71</span> </sup> The Digital Silk Road is a component of the BRI which includes digital technological development, the development of digital standards, and the expansion of digital infrastructure.[^151]<sup><span title="Pages: 177–188">: 177–188</span> </sup> Its stated aim is to improve digital connectivity among participating countries, with China as the main driver of the improved digital infrastructure, with the benefit to China of reducing its reliance on American digital technology.[^152]<sup><span title="Page: 205">: 205</span> </sup> The Digital Silk Road has also been a way to export China's system of [mass surveillance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_surveillance_in_China "Mass surveillance in China") and [censorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Censorship_in_China "Censorship in China") technology of the [Great Firewall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Firewall "Great Firewall") to multiple countries.[^153] [^154] [^155] In a 2024 literature review, academics Christopher Foster et al. write that the focus on concerns about the potential negative impacts of Chinese digital expansion in the global south are often speculative, not evidence-based, worries and that there is "virtually no specific evidence as yet about the positive local economic impact of Digital Silk Road." [^156]

Like the BRI more broadly, the Digital Silk Road is not monolithic and involves many actors across both China's public and private sectors.[^152]<sup><span title="Page: 205">: 205</span> </sup> [Alibaba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alibaba_Group "Alibaba Group") supplies a significant amount of technology for the Digital Silk Road.[^157]<sup><span title="Page: 272">: 272</span></sup>

In an age of increasing separation between the West and China regarding advanced technologies, both sides are working to establish or strengthen their own spheres of influence in technology markets. One notable example of this is China's Digital Silk Road.[^158] China frames the Digital Silk Road as part of an effort to create a [community of common destiny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_of_Common_Destiny "Community of Common Destiny") in cyberspace.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 72">: 72</span> </sup> A key component of the strategy is to build digital infrastructure in areas of the global south where private providers have not been willing to develop infrastructure and where local governments do not have the capacity to do so.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 76">: 76</span> </sup> China's willingness to develop digital infrastructure in such locations is in part due to the expectation that future population growth will be especially high in global south regions.[^23]<sup><span title="Pages: 76–77">: 76–77</span></sup>

As part of the Digital Silk Road, China built 34 terrestrial cables and dozens of underwater cables within 12 BRI countries in Asia, Africa, and Europe over the period 2017–2022.[^151]<sup><span title="Page: 180">: 180</span> </sup> Digital Silk Road-related investments in projects outside China reached an estimated US$79 billion as of 2018.[^152]<sup><span title="Page: 205">: 205</span></sup>

At the opening ceremony of the first Belt and Road Forum, Xi Jinping emphasized the importance of developing a Digital Silk Road through innovation in intelligent cities concepts, the digital economy, artificial intelligence, nanotechnology, and quantum computing.[^151]<sup><span title="Page: 77">: 77</span></sup>

The [Eurasian Economic Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Economic_Union "Eurasian Economic Union") cooperates with China on the development of the Digital Silk Road.[^159]<sup><span title="Page: 187">: 187</span> </sup> Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, and Belarus are actively involved in the Digital Silk Road while Russia incorporated Chinese technologies into its digital infrastructure.[^160]

In February 2024, leaked documents from a Chinese government contractor based in Shanghai called [I-Soon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I-Soon_leak "I-Soon leak") provided details into a hacking campaign involving the critical infrastructure of Digital Silk Road partner countries.[^161]

### Super grid

The [super grid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_Super_Grid "Asian Super Grid") project aims to develop six [ultra high voltage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-voltage_electricity_transmission_in_China "Ultra-high-voltage electricity transmission in China") [electrical grids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_grid "Electrical grid") across China, Northeast Asia, Southeast Asia, South Asia, Central Asia and West Asia. The [wind power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wind_power "Wind power") resources of Central Asia would form one component of this grid.[^162] [^163] Because renewable resources are far from the centres of population, a long-distance transmission system is needed, which needs to be ultra-high voltage to operate efficiently.

### Academic and cultural

People-to-people bonds are one of the five major goals of the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), with its educational policy emphasizing mutual recognition of qualifications, academic mobility, student exchanges, and joint study programmes.[^164] To support this, President Xi Jinping announced plans to allocate 30,000 scholarships for SCO citizens and 10,000 scholarships for students and teachers from countries along the Belt and Road.[^165] The Silk Road Scholarship is part of China's broader agenda of increasing academic and cultural cooperation with BRI-participating countries.[^166]<sup><span title="Page: 158">: 158</span></sup>

### Additionally proposed

[The Bangladesh–China–India–Myanmar Economic Corridor (BCIM)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BCIM_Economic_Corridor "BCIM Economic Corridor") was proposed to run from southern China to Myanmar and was initially officially classified as "closely related to the Belt and Road Initiative".[^167] Since the second [Belt and Road Forum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Forum_for_International_Cooperation "Belt and Road Forum for International Cooperation") in 2019, BCIM has been dropped from the list of projects due to India's refusal to participate in the Belt and Road Initiative.[^168]

## Projects

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Nairobi_SGR_Train_Station.jpg/250px-Nairobi_SGR_Train_Station.jpg)

Train station in Nairobi, Kenya built with BRI funding in 2017

China has engaged 149 countries and 30 international organizations in the BRI.[^169] Infrastructure projects span ports, railways, highways, power stations, aviation, and telecommunications.[^170] Flagship projects include the China–Pakistan Economic Corridor, the [Boten–Vientiane railway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boten%E2%80%93Vientiane_railway "Boten–Vientiane railway") in Laos, and the Khorgos land port.[^171] [^172] The China-Europe Freight Train preceded the BRI but was later incorporated into it.[^173]

Interpretations of which projects constitute the BRI vary because the Chinese government does not publish a comprehensive project list.[^173] The [Chinese Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Academy_of_Sciences "Chinese Academy of Sciences") takes a narrow definition, including only projects derived from or included in cooperation dialogues between China and other BRI countries.[^174]

### Environmental impact

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Solar_Panel_Array_in_Laos_Laowotaiyangban.jpg/250px-Solar_Panel_Array_in_Laos_Laowotaiyangban.jpg)

A solar panel array in Laos built with BRI funding

The BRI has attracted attention from environmental organizations. A joint report by the [World Wide Fund for Nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature "World Wide Fund for Nature") and [HSBC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HSBC "HSBC") identified significant risks alongside opportunities for sustainable development, including overuse of natural resources, ecosystem disruption, and pollutant emissions.[^175] [Coal-fired power stations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal-fired_power_station "Coal-fired power station") built under the BRI, such as [Emba Hunutlu power station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emba_Hunutlu_power_station "Emba Hunutlu power station") in Turkey, have increased [greenhouse gas emissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions "Greenhouse gas emissions").[^176] BRI coal projects accounted for 42% of China's overseas investment in 2018, and 93% of energy investments from the BRI-linked Silk Road Fund went to fossil fuels.[^177] [^178] Central Asian nations face particular concerns including [glacier melting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glacier_melting "Glacier melting"), [endangered species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered_species "Endangered species") preservation, [desertification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desertification "Desertification"), [soil erosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_erosion "Soil erosion") from overgrazing and over-farming, mining practices, water resource management, and air and water pollution from poorly planned infrastructure.[^179] Maritime infrastructure development could impact sensitive species and habitats including [coral reefs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coral_reef "Coral reef"), [mangroves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mangrove "Mangrove"), [seagrass meadows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seagrass_meadow "Seagrass meadow"), and [saltmarshes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saltmarsh "Saltmarsh").[^180] Critics have also raised concerns about pollution outsourcing to poorer nations whose governments may disregard environmental consequences; in Serbia, where pollution-related deaths already top Europe, Chinese-owned coal plants have increased coal dependency and contributed to air and soil pollution.[^181]

However, approximately 54% of BRI energy projects are in clean or alternative energy sectors.[^182] Many partner countries prioritize sustainable development, and China has responded by emphasizing a "Green Silk Road" and framing [ecological civilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_civilization "Ecological civilization") as integral to the initiative.[^183] The Belt and Road Initiative International Green Development Coalition (BRIGC), launched at the 2nd [Belt and Road Forum for International Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Forum_for_International_Cooperation "Belt and Road Forum for International Cooperation") in April 2019, aims to integrate environmental sustainability and international standards across BRI priorities.[^184] [^185] Scholars remain uncertain whether these practices will be implemented, as all BRI-specific environmental goals appear in informal guidelines rather than legally binding regulations, and member nations may prioritize economic development over environmental protection.[^186] [^187]

According to the Green Finance and Development Center, 2023 was China's "greenest" year for BRI investment since inception.[^188] China devoted US$7.9 billion to solar and wind development—its largest contribution to green energy—with projects in Brazil and Indonesia, plus US$1.9 billion for [hydropower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydropower "Hydropower") including dam projects in Cambodia, Pakistan, Uganda, Tajikistan, Georgia, Myanmar, and Indonesia.[^188] [^189] As of 2025, renewable energy is a focus in the Middle East, including China's cooperation [with Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Saudi_Arabia_relations "China–Saudi Arabia relations") on renewable energy and [Dubai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dubai "Dubai") 's Rashid Al Maktoum Solar Park as a regional flagship.[^190] Reports from the [United Nations Development Programme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme "United Nations Development Programme") and [China Center for International Economic Exchanges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Center_for_International_Economic_Exchanges "China Center for International Economic Exchanges") frame the BRI as an opportunity for environmental protection if used to provide green trade, [finance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_finance_and_the_Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Green finance and the Belt and Road Initiative"), and investment aligned with each country's [Sustainable Development Goals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals "Sustainable Development Goals").[^191]

In September 2021, Xi Jinping announced China would "step up support" for developing countries to adopt green energy and cease financing overseas coal plants.[^192] This pledge has faced scrutiny: in January 2023, Pakistan approved a Chinese-funded 300 MW coal plant in Gwadar,[^193] and in January 2024, a 380 MW coal plant began operation in Sulawesi, Indonesia.[^188]

### Governance issues

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Novi_Sad_railway_station_canopy_collapse.jpg/250px-Novi_Sad_railway_station_canopy_collapse.jpg)

Canopy of Novi Sad railway station after its November 2024 collapse

A 2021 analysis by [AidData](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AidData "AidData") at the [College of William & Mary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/College_of_William_%26_Mary "College of William & Mary") found that 35% of BRI infrastructure projects encountered major implementation problems including labor violations, corruption, environmental hazards, and public protests.[^194] Limited data on corruption and organized crime appears in Chinese government sources,[^195] but scholars have documented increased [Chinese organized crime syndicate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triad_\(organized_crime\) "Triad (organized crime)") activity in some BRI countries.[^196] [^197] BRI projects often support connectivity within and between countries; rapid development and integration of border areas has brought security and transnational crime risks in some regions.[^198] Criminal groups have in some cases exploited BRI branding to promote projects not endorsed by the Chinese government, such as the [Yatai New City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yatai_New_City "Yatai New City") project in Myanmar, linked to illegal gambling and online fraud.[^199]

The AidData analysis identified Pakistan, Indonesia, Malaysia, Vietnam, Kenya, Kyrgyzstan, Papua New Guinea, Cambodia, Mozambique, and Belarus as the top countries for reported BRI-related corruption scandals.[^194] In response to public scandals including the bribery and money laundering conviction of BRI advocate [Patrick Ho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrick_Ho "Patrick Ho"), the CCP's [Central Commission for Discipline Inspection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Commission_for_Discipline_Inspection "Central Commission for Discipline Inspection") announced in 2019 it would embed officers in participating countries.[^200]

A prominent controversy occurred in Serbia. On 1 November 2024, the concrete [canopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canopy_\(architecture\) "Canopy (architecture)") of [Novi Sad railway station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Novi_Sad_railway_station "Novi Sad railway station") [collapsed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Novi_Sad_railway_station_canopy_collapse "Novi Sad railway station canopy collapse") onto a busy pavement, killing 15 people. The 1964 station building had been renovated from 2021 to mid-2024 with BRI support as part of the [Budapest–Belgrade railway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budapest%E2%80%93Belgrade_railway "Budapest–Belgrade railway") project, with work completed by a consortium of China Railway International Co., Ltd and [China Communications Construction Company, Ltd.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Communications_Construction_Company,_Ltd. "China Communications Construction Company, Ltd.")[^201] The collapse's official cause remains under investigation, with government corruption and opaque dealings with Chinese contractors cited as factors.[^202] The incident sparked [mass protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024%E2%80%932025_Serbian_anti-corruption_protests "2024–2025 Serbian anti-corruption protests") throughout Serbia, fueled by broader dissatisfaction with government corruption and media censorship.[^203]

Labor practices have also drawn criticism. According to [China Labor Watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Labor_Watch "China Labor Watch"), widespread human rights violations affect Chinese migrant workers on BRI projects abroad. The report alleges companies confiscate workers' passports upon arrival, compel them to apply for illegal business visas, threaten to report their illegal status if they refuse compliance, deny adequate medical care and rest, restrict personal freedom and speech, force excessive overtime, cancel vacations, delay wages, issue deceptive recruitment advertisements, threaten high damages if workers attempt to leave, provide substandard conditions, and punish those who lead protests.[^204] [^205]

## Reactions

Generally, it is more important for China to persuade its domestic audiences of the benefits of BRI than it is to persuade foreign audiences at-large.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 148">: 148</span> </sup> Academic Jeremy Garlick writes that this is a reason why the Chinese government presents the BRI in a way more intuitively understandable to domestic than global audiences.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 148">: 148</span></sup>

Former EU diplomat [Bruno Maçães](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruno_Ma%C3%A7%C3%A3es "Bruno Maçães") describes the BRI as the world's first transnational [industrial policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_policy "Industrial policy") because it goes beyond national policy to influence the industrial policy of other states.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 165">: 165</span></sup>

### Support

As of 2020, more than 130 countries had issued endorsements.[^92] UN Secretary General [António Guterres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Guterres "António Guterres") has described the BRI as capable of accelerating the UN Sustainable Development Goals.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 164">: 164</span> </sup> Institutional connections between the BRI and multiple UN bodies have also been established.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 164">: 164</span></sup>

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Wizyta_prezydenta_Chi%C5%84skiej_Republiki_Ludowej_%2827699551272%29.jpg/250px-Wizyta_prezydenta_Chi%C5%84skiej_Republiki_Ludowej_%2827699551272%29.jpg)

Poland's Prime Minister Beata Szydło and China's paramount leader Xi Jinping during meeting, 2016

In June 2016, Xi Jinping visited Poland and met with Poland's President [Andrzej Duda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrzej_Duda "Andrzej Duda") and Prime Minister [Beata Szydło](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beata_Szyd%C5%82o "Beata Szydło").[^206] [^207] Duda and Xi signed a declaration on strategic partnership in which they reiterated that Poland and China viewed each other as long-term strategic partners.[^208]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Vladimir_Putin_met_with_Xi_Jinping_in_advance_of_2022_Beijing_Winter_Olympics_%281%29.jpg/250px-Vladimir_Putin_met_with_Xi_Jinping_in_advance_of_2022_Beijing_Winter_Olympics_%281%29.jpg)

Russian President Vladimir Putin and the Chinese leader Xi Jinping on 4 February 2022

As a wealthy country, Singapore does not need massive external financing or technical assistance for domestic infrastructure building, but has repeatedly endorsed the BRI and cooperated in related projects in a quest for global relevance and to strengthen economic ties with BRI recipients. It is also one of the largest investors in the project. Furthermore, there is a strategic defensive factor: making sure a single country is not the single dominant factor in Asian economics.[^209]

While the Philippines historically has been closely tied to the United States, China sought its support for the BRI in terms of the quest for dominance in the South China Sea. The Philippines adjusted its policy in favor of Chinese claims in the South China Sea under President [Rodrigo Duterte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodrigo_Duterte "Rodrigo Duterte").[^210]

In 2017, [Yanis Varoufakis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yanis_Varoufakis "Yanis Varoufakis"), the former [Greek Minister of Finance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_Minister_of_Finance "Greek Minister of Finance"), wrote in [Project Syndicate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Syndicate "Project Syndicate") that his experience with the Belt and Road Initiative has been highly encouraging.[^211] He remarked that the Chinese authorities managed to combine their sense of self-interest with a patient investment attitude and a genuine commitment to negotiate over and over again, in order to achieve a mutually advantageous agreement.[^211]

In April 2019 and during the second [Arab Forum for Environment and Development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Forum_for_Environment_and_Development "Arab Forum for Environment and Development"), China engaged in an array of partnerships called "Build the Belt and Road, Share Development and Prosperity" with 18 Arab countries. The general stand of African countries sees BRI as a tremendous opportunity for independence from foreign aid and influence.[^212]

[Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece"), [Croatia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Croatia "Croatia"), and 14 other Eastern European countries are already dealing with China within the framework of the BRI. In March 2019, Italy was the first member of the [Group of Seven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_of_Seven "Group of Seven") nations to join the BRI. The new partners signed a Memorandum of Understanding worth €2.5 billion across an array of sectors such as transport, logistics, and port infrastructure.[^213]

Despite initially criticizing BRI, the former Malaysian Prime Minister [Mahathir Mohamad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahathir_Mohamad "Mahathir Mohamad") pledged support for the BRI project in 2019.[^214]<sup><span title="Page: 54">: 54</span> </sup> He stated that he was fully in support of the Belt and Road Initiative and that his country would benefit from BRI. "Yes, the Belt and Road idea is great. It can bring the land-locked countries of [Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia") closer to the sea. They can grow in wealth and their poverty reduced," Mahathir said.[^215]

Russian political scientist [Sergey Karaganov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergey_Karaganov "Sergey Karaganov") argues that the Eurasian Economic Union (EEU) and China's Belt and Road Initiative will work together to promote economic integration throughout the region.[^216]

### Opposition

Some observers and skeptics, mainly from non-participant countries, including the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), interpret it as a plan for a [Sinocentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinocentric "Sinocentric") [international trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_trade "International trade") [network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport_network "Transport network").[^217] [^106] In response the United States, [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan"), and [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia") had formed a counter initiative, the [Blue Dot Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_Dot_Network "Blue Dot Network") in 2019, followed by the G7's [Build Back Better World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Build_Back_Better_World "Build Back Better World") initiative in 2021.[^218] [^219]

The United States proposes a counter-initiative called the " [Free and Open Indo-Pacific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_and_Open_Indo-Pacific "Free and Open Indo-Pacific") strategy" (FOIP). US officials have articulated the strategy as having three pillars: security, economics, and governance.[^220] At the beginning of June 2019, there has been a redefinition of the general definitions of "free" and "open" into four stated principles: respect for sovereignty and independence; peaceful resolution of disputes; free, fair, and reciprocal trade; and adherence to international rules and norms.[^221]

Government officials in India have repeatedly objected to China's Belt and Road Initiative.[^222] In particular, they believe the "China–Pakistan Economic Corridor" (CPEC) project ignores New Delhi's essential concerns about its sovereignty and territorial integrity.[^223]

Former Malaysian prime minister Mahathir Mohamad had initially found the terms of BRI to be too harsh for most countries and recommended countries avoid joining the BRI, but has changed his stance since.[^224] [^225]

According to a report by [Reuters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuters "Reuters"), in 2019 the United States [Central Intelligence Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency "Central Intelligence Agency") began a clandestine campaign on [Chinese social media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_in_China "Internet in China") to spread negative narratives about the [Xi's general secretaryship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_secretaryship_of_Xi_Jinping "General secretaryship of Xi Jinping") in an effort to influence Chinese public opinion against the government. The CIA promoted narratives that CCP leaders were hiding money overseas and that the BRI was corrupt and wasteful. As part of the campaign, the CIA also targeted foreign countries where the United States and China compete for influence.[^226] [^227]

British author [Matthew Lynn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matthew_Lynn "Matthew Lynn"), in a commentary in *[The Washington Post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Washington_Post "The Washington Post")*, concluded that the BRI has failed to increase Chinese influence overseas through financial investment. He suggested that soft power without the backing of hard military power is fragile, and he argued that the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") 's regulatory power (the [Brussels effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brussels_effect "Brussels effect")) and China's commercial activities cannot serve as effective deterrents or sources of leverage if a country does not have strong military power.[^228]

### Mixed

Vietnam historically has been at odds with China, so it has been indecisive about supporting or opposing BRI.[^229] In December 2023, Vietnam and China agreed a number of cooperation documents, including one related to the BRI.[^230]

South Korea has tried to develop the "Eurasia Initiative" (EAI) as its own vision for an east–west connection. In calling for a revival of the ancient Silk Road, the main goal of former President [Park Geun-hye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Park_Geun-hye "Park Geun-hye") was to encourage a flow of economic, political, and social interaction from Europe through the [Korean Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_Peninsula "Korean Peninsula"). Her successor, President [Moon Jae-in](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon_Jae-in "Moon Jae-in") announced his own foreign policy initiative, the "New Southern Policy" (NSP), which seeks to strengthen relations with Southeast Asia.[^231]

In 2017, former secretary of state [Henry Kissinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Kissinger "Henry Kissinger") stressed that cooperation between the US and Chinese governments was preferable to a race towards a new cold war. In his view the United States should embed itself into the Belt and Road Initiative by way of joining the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank, which would allow the United States to legitimately object to Chinese diplomatic over-reach.[^232] [^233]

While Italy and Greece have joined the Belt and Road Initiative, other European countries have voiced ambivalent opinions. German chancellor [Angela Merkel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angela_Merkel "Angela Merkel") declared that the BRI "must lead to a certain reciprocity, and we are still wrangling over that bit". In January 2019, French president [Emmanuel Macron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron "Emmanuel Macron") said, "the ancient Silk Roads were never just Chinese … New roads cannot go just one way." [^213] [European Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Commission "European Commission") Chief [Jean-Claude Juncker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Claude_Juncker "Jean-Claude Juncker") and Japanese Prime Minister [Shinzo Abe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shinzo_Abe "Shinzo Abe") signed an infrastructure agreement in [Brussels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brussels "Brussels") in September 2019 to counter China's Belt and Road Initiative and link Europe and Asia to coordinate infrastructure, transport, and digital projects.[^234] [^235]

In 2018, the premier of the south-eastern Australian [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/States_of_Australia "States of Australia") of [Victoria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victoria_\(Australia\) "Victoria (Australia)"), [Daniel Andrews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Andrews "Daniel Andrews"), signed a memorandum of understanding on the Belt and Road Initiative to establish infrastructure ties and further relations with China.[^236] [^237] Home Affairs Minister [Peter Dutton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Dutton "Peter Dutton") described the BRI as "a propaganda initiative from China" that brings an "enormous amount of foreign interference", while arguing that "Victoria needs to explain why it is the only state in the country that has entered into this agreement". Prime Minister [Scott Morrison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_Morrison "Scott Morrison") said Victoria was stepping into federal government policy territory, stating "We didn't support that decision at the time \[the Victoria State Government and the National Development and Reform Commission of the People's Republic of China\] made \[the agreement\]. National interest issues on foreign affairs are determined by the federal government, and I respect their jurisdiction when it comes to the issues they're responsible for, and it's always been the usual practice for states to respect and recognise the role of the federal government in setting [foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy "Foreign policy") ". In April 2021, Foreign Minister [Marise Payne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marise_Payne "Marise Payne") declared Australia would pull out of the Belt and Road Initiative, cancelling Victoria's agreements signed with China, and pulling out of the "Belt and Road" initiative completely.[^238] [^237] [^239] [^92] The federal government's decision to veto the deal reflected the increasingly soured relationship between the Australian Government and the Chinese Government, after China's alleged attempts to employ economic coercion [^240] in response to the Australian Government's support for an inquest into the origins of COVID-19,[^241] as well as the Australian Government's decision to support the US in several regional disputes opposing China, such as the issue of Taiwan [^242] or the South China Sea.[^243]

In October 2024, Brazil opted against joining the BRI,[^244] but offered to "establish synergies" with the Initiative.[^245]

### Accusations of neo-colonialism and debt-trap diplomacy

#### Accusations

Some, including Western governments,[^246] have accused the Belt and Road Initiative of being a form of [neocolonialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neocolonialism "Neocolonialism"), due to what they allege is China's practice of debt-trap diplomacy to fund the initiative's infrastructure projects.[^247] The concept of debt-trap diplomacy was first coined by an Indian [think tank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Think_tank "Think tank"), the New Delhi-based [Centre for Policy Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre_for_Policy_Research "Centre for Policy Research"), before being picked up and expanded on by papers by two Harvard students, which subsequently gained media attention in the mainstream press.[^248]

China contends that the initiative has provided markets for commodities, improved prices of resources, and thereby reduced inequalities in exchange, improved infrastructure, created employment, stimulated industrialization, and expanded [technology transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technology_transfer "Technology transfer"), thereby benefiting host countries.[^249]

Tanzanian President [John Magufuli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Magufuli "John Magufuli") said the loan agreements of BRI projects in his country were "exploitative and awkward".[^250] He said Chinese financiers set "tough conditions that can only be accepted by mad people," because his government was asked to give them a guarantee of 33 years and an extensive lease of 99 years on a port construction. Magufuli said Chinese contractors wanted to take the land as their own, but his government had to compensate them for drilling the project construction.[^251]

In May 2021, DRC's President [Félix Tshisekedi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Tshisekedi "Félix Tshisekedi") called for a review of mining contracts signed with China by his predecessor [Joseph Kabila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Kabila "Joseph Kabila"),[^252] in particular, the Sicomines multibillion-dollar ['minerals-for-infrastructure' agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo_relations#Sicomines "China–Democratic Republic of the Congo relations").[^253] [^254] [^255] China's plans to link its western Xinjiang province to Gwadar in the Balochistan province of Pakistan with its US$500m investment in the Gwadar Port has met resistance from local fishermen protesting against Chinese trawlers and [illegal fishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illegal,_unreported_and_unregulated_fishing "Illegal, unreported and unregulated fishing").[^256] [^257]

#### Rebuttals

[Deborah Bräutigam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deborah_Br%C3%A4utigam "Deborah Bräutigam"), a professor at the School of Advanced International Studies (SAIS) at Johns Hopkins University, described the debt-trap diplomacy theory as a "meme" that became popular due to "human negativity bias" based on anxiety about the rise of China.[^258] A 2019 research paper by Bräutigam revealed that most of the debtor countries voluntarily signed on to the loans and had positive experiences working with China, and "the evidence so far, including the Sri Lankan case, shows that the drumbeat of alarm about Chinese banks' funding of infrastructure across the BRI and beyond is overblown" and "a large number of people have favorable opinions of China as an economic model and consider China an attractive partner for their development." [^258] She said that the theory lacked evidence and criticized the media for promoting a narrative that "wrongfully misrepresents the relationship between China and the developing countries that it deals with," highlighting the fact that Sri Lanka owed more to Japan, the [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank"), and the [Asian Development Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_Development_Bank "Asian Development Bank") than to China.[^259] A 2018 [China Africa Research Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Africa_Research_Initiative "China Africa Research Initiative") report, co-authored by Bräutigam, remarked that "Chinese loans are not currently a major contributor to debt distress in Africa." [^260]

According to [New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City "New York City") -based economist Anastasia Papadimitriou, partnering countries are equally responsible when making deals with China. China's alleged "neocolonialist intentions" can be disproved by Sri Lanka's [Hambantota Port](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hambantota_International_Port "Hambantota International Port") project on the southeast coast of Sri Lanka, one of the most cited examples of debt-trap diplomacy.[^261] After an analysis of the Belt and Road Initiative, Papadimitriou concludes that it is "not so much neocolonialism, rather it is [economic regionalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_Regionalisation "Economic Regionalisation") ".[^261] Additionally, [The Royal Institute of International Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chatham_House "Chatham House"), a [London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London "London") -based international affairs think tank, asserted that the debt crisis in Sri Lanka was unrelated to Chinese lending, but was instead caused mainly from "the misconduct of local elites and Western-dominated financial markets".[^262] It was confirmed by [Lowy Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lowy_Institute "Lowy Institute"). A 2019 report by the Lowy Institute said that China had not engaged in deliberate actions in the Pacific which justified accusations of debt-trap diplomacy (based on contemporaneous evidence), and China had not been the primary driver behind rising debt risks in the Pacific; the report expressed concern about the scale of the country's lending, however, and the institutional weakness of Pacific states which posed the risk of small states being overwhelmed by debt.[^89] A 2020 Lowy Institute article called Sri Lanka's Hambantota International Port the "case par excellence" for China's debt-trap diplomacy, but called the narrative a "myth" because the project was proposed by former Sri Lankan president [Mahinda Rajapaksa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahinda_Rajapaksa "Mahinda Rajapaksa"), not Beijing.[^263] The article added that Sri Lanka's debt distress was not caused by Chinese lending, but by "excessive borrowing on Western-dominated capital markets." [^263]

The Rhodium Group, an American research company, analyzed Chinese [debt renegotiations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt_restructuring "Debt restructuring") and concluded that China's [leverage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leveraged_buyout "Leveraged buyout") in them are often exaggerated and realistically limited in power. The findings of their study frequently showed an outcome in favor of the borrower rather than the supposedly predatory Chinese lender. The firm found that " [asset seizures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asset_forfeiture "Asset forfeiture") are a very rare occurrence" and that instead debt write-off was the most common outcome.[^264]

Darren Lim, senior lecturer at the [Australian National University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_National_University "Australian National University"), said that the debt-trap diplomacy claim was never credible, despite the [first Trump administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_presidency_of_Donald_Trump "First presidency of Donald Trump") pushing it.[^265] Dawn C. Murphy, Professor of International Security Studies at the [USAF Air War College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USAF_Air_War_College "USAF Air War College"), concludes that describing China's behavior as "neocolonialist" is an "exaggeration and misrepresentation".[^65]<sup><span title="Page: 158">: 158</span></sup>

According to political scientist and researcher Zhexin Zhang, the overwhelming enthusiasm of [developing countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country") in the Belt and Road Initiative, as seen first in the "Belt and Road Forum for International Cooperation" held in May 2017, is sufficient to invalidate the neo-colonialism argument.[^266]

A March 2018 study released by the [Center for Global Development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_for_Global_Development "Center for Global Development"), a [Washington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington,_D.C. "Washington, D.C.") -based think tank, remarks that between 2001 and 2017, China restructured or waived loan payments for 51 debtor nations, the majority of BRI participants, without seizing state assets.[^267] The study concluded that in most cases, it was unlikely that there would be severe problems with debt.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 87">: 87</span> </sup> In September 2018, [Gyude Moore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyude_Moore "Gyude Moore"), a former Liberian public works minister and senior policy fellow at the Center for Global Development, stated that "\[t\]he language of "debt-trap diplomacy" resonates more in Western countries, especially the United States, and is rooted in anxiety about China's rise as a global power rather than in the reality of Africa." [^268] He also stated that "China has been a net positive partner with most African countries." [^269] According to Pradumna Bickram Rana and Jason Ji Xianbai of Singapore's [Nanyang Technological University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanyang_Technological_University "Nanyang Technological University"), although there is a number of implementation issues confronting the Belt and Road Initiative mostly due to the COVID-19 Pandemic, China's alleged debt-trap diplomacy "more myth than reality".[^270] While they acknowledge that various countries are facing difficulties in paying their debts to China, they highlight China's willingness to help these countries restructure their debt through forgiving policies, such as partial [debt relief](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt_relief "Debt relief").[^270] In eleven cases, China postponed loans for indebted countries, one being Tonga.[^265]

Writing in 2023, academic Süha Atatüre stated that United States opposition to the Belt and Road Initiative stems from the fact that it lacks the capacity to implement a rival project.[^271]<sup><span title="Page: 40">: 40</span></sup>

Publishing in 2023, academic Austin Strange concludes that scholars have challenged the narrative of a Chinese debt-trap and that analysis of BRI projects does not substantiate the debt-trap narrative.[^272]<sup><span title="Pages: 15–16">: 15–16</span> </sup> On the issue of criticism of the BRI more broadly, Strange writes that unintended negative consequences can result from global infrastructure projects generally (citing examples of issues arising from overseas projects by Japan and South Korea) and are not a BRI-specific or China-specific challenge.[^272]<sup><span title="Pages: 58–59">: 58–59</span></sup>

Academics Yan Hairong and [Barry Sautman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barry_Sautman "Barry Sautman") stated in a 2024 study that the debt-trap narrative is incorrect, as China does not foreclose on borrower assets.[^273]<sup><span title="Page: 223">: 223</span></sup>

[London School of Economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_School_of_Economics "London School of Economics") Professor Keyu Jin writes that the claim that China leads borrowers into a [debt trap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt-trap_diplomacy "Debt-trap diplomacy") is misleading.[^45]<sup><span title="Page: 280">: 280</span> </sup> Jin observes that the majority of BRI countries' debt is owed to international organizations or private institutions like hedge funds, rather than to China.[^45]<sup><span title="Pages: 280–281">: 280–281</span> </sup> Jin also writes that China has written off many of its loans and also provided debt relief to borrowers.[^45]<sup><span title="Page: 281">: 281</span></sup>

### Country-specific

Through the BRI, China has a major role in infrastructure development in Cambodia.[^214]<sup><span title="Page: 29">: 29</span> </sup> In 2017, China financed approximately 70% of Cambodia's road and bridge development.[^214]<sup><span title="Page: 29">: 29</span> </sup> China built a major expressway between [Sihanoukville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sihanoukville_\(city\) "Sihanoukville (city)") and [Phnom Penh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phnom_Penh "Phnom Penh"), which began operating in 2023.[^214]<sup><span title="Page: 29">: 29</span> </sup> As of at least 2024, the expressway is the largest BRI project in Cambodia.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 132">: 132</span></sup>

China aligns its BRI economic [cooperation in Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Egypt_relations "China–Egypt relations") with the points of emphasis in Egypt's Vision 2030 national development plan.[^274]<sup><span title="Page: 66">: 66</span> </sup> China's BRI projects with Egypt therefore focus on developing labor-intensive manufacturing and traditional infrastructure in Egypt.[^274]<sup><span title="Page: 66">: 66</span></sup>

Chinese leadership describes Ethiopia as a bridge between the Belt and Road Initiative and Africa's development, stating that the relationship between the two countries is a model of [South-South cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South%E2%80%93South_cooperation "South–South cooperation") and "a pilot program for [China-Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa%E2%80%93China_economic_relations "Africa–China economic relations") production capacity cooperation." [^273]<sup><span title="Pages: 222–223">: 222–223</span></sup>

Greece hosts the most successful BRI port project as of at least 2024, the port at Piraeus.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 67">: 67</span> </sup> The port's incorporation as part of the BRI has been one of the mechanisms through which China has strengthened [its relationship with Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Greece_relations "China–Greece relations"), following the increased strain in the European Union-Greece relationship after [Greece's bailout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Economic_Adjustment_Programme_for_Greece "First Economic Adjustment Programme for Greece").[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 67">: 67</span></sup>

As of at least 2024, Hungary and Serbia are also two of the major European supporters of the BRI.[^275]

Italy was the only G7 country which had been a partner in the development of the BRI, having been involved since March 2019, but in July 2023 declared its intention to quit the BRI.[^276] Prime Minister [Giorgia Meloni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giorgia_Meloni "Giorgia Meloni") stated that the project was not of any real benefit to Italy's economy.[^277] The Ministry of Foreign Affairs of China described Italy's actions as "disrupting cooperation and creating divisions".[^274]<sup><span title="Page: 71">: 71</span> </sup> Publishing in 2025, academic Chuchu Zhang writes that bilateral economic cooperation between Italy and China was not disrupted by Italy's intent to withdraw, and trade between the two countries increased through February 2024.[^274]<sup><span title="Page: 70">: 70</span></sup>

The China–Laos Railway is one of the most sectorally integrated of the major BRI projects.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span> </sup> It has brought together expertise in large-scale engineering, finance, construction, and economic design.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span> </sup> The Boten-Vientiane Railway is the Laos section of the broader railway, which is in turn part of the [China–Laos Economic Corridor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Laos_Economic_Corridor "China–Laos Economic Corridor").[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span> </sup> The railway has reduced travel time from [Ventiane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vientiane "Vientiane") to [Luang Prabang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luang_Prabang "Luang Prabang") from a day to two hours.[^214]<sup><span title="Page: 39">: 39</span></sup>

Due to its opposition to the Gwadar Port City in Pakistan, in 2019 the [Baloch Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balochistan_Liberation_Army "Balochistan Liberation Army") targeted Chinese nationals in an attack at the [Pearl-Continental Hotel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearl-Continental_Hotels_%26_Resorts "Pearl-Continental Hotels & Resorts").[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 60">: 60</span></sup>

During a November 2024 [visit by Xi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_international_trips_made_by_Xi_Jinping "List of international trips made by Xi Jinping") to Peru, [Boluarte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dina_Boluarte "Dina Boluarte") and Xi celebrated the opening of the [Chancay port](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_Chancay "Port of Chancay"), which is part of the Belt and Road Initiative.[^278] Xi described the port as the beginning of a new maritime-land corridor between China and Latin America.[^278] The port was built by [COSCO Shipping Ports](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COSCO_Shipping_Ports "COSCO Shipping Ports").[^278]
