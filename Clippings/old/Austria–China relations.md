[Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austria "Austria") established diplomatic relations with the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") in May 1971. Austria holds an embassy in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing"), and China holds an embassy in [Vienna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vienna "Vienna"). There are also two [Confucius Institutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius_Institutes "Confucius Institutes") in Austria in Vienna and Graz.

Austria's foreign policy opposite China is strongly oriented on the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") 's relationship with China. The EU sees China as "a partner for cooperation, an economic competitor and a systemic rival." [^1] Austria's Foreign Ministry has on multiple occasions referred to this position as its guiding definition for its own China policy.[^2]

In 1869, [Austria-Hungary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austria-Hungary "Austria-Hungary") established diplomatic relations with the [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty").[^3]

### Austro-Hungarian concession of Tianjin

The [Austro-Hungarian concession of Tianjin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_concession_of_Tianjin "Austro-Hungarian concession of Tianjin") ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters "Simplified Chinese characters"): 天津奥租界; [pinyin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin "Pinyin"): *Tiānjīn ào zūjiè*, [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language"): *österreichisch-ungarische Konzession*, [Hungarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_language "Hungarian language"): *Osztrák–magyar tiencsini koncesszió*) was a territory ([concession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concessions_and_leases_in_international_relations "Concessions and leases in international relations")) in the [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") city of [Tientsin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianjin "Tianjin") occupied by [Austria-Hungary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austria-Hungary "Austria-Hungary") between 1902 and 1920. It had been obtained by Austria-Hungary after the signing of the [Boxer Protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxer_Protocol "Boxer Protocol") at the conclusion of the conflict between China and the [Alliance of Eight Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eight-Nation_Alliance "Eight-Nation Alliance"), which had sent an international expeditionary force to quell the [Boxer Rebellion of 1901](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxer_Rebellion "Boxer Rebellion"). Although the Austro-Hungarian occupation corps had been present from the previous year, the concession formally began on 27 December 1902. It is the shortest lived concession, having existed for only 14 years.

[Austria-Hungary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austria-Hungary "Austria-Hungary") was a part of the [Eight-Nation Alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eight-Nation_Alliance "Eight-Nation Alliance") which put down the [Boxer Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxer_Rebellion "Boxer Rebellion"), and as a signatory of the [Boxer Protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxer_Protocol "Boxer Protocol") gained a [concession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concessions_in_Tianjin "Concessions in Tianjin") in [Tianjin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianjin "Tianjin") alongside the other members of the alliance.[^4] The concession would be regained by China with the dissolution of Austria-Hungary in [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I").

### Cold War

Austria officially recognized the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") on 28 May 1971.[^5]

## Human rights

Austrian officials have repeatedly criticized China for its violation of [human rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights_in_China "Human rights in China"), particularly regarding minority groups. On the occasion of a 2011 visit of China's President Hu Jintao, the later Austrian President Alexander Van der Bellen said: "The considerable human rights violations in China must be brought to the table and must not be swept under the carpet out of supposed fear for Austria's economic interests. As a member of the UN Human Rights Council, the Austrian government has taken up the cause of protecting religious freedom. It must not suddenly forget this in its relations with China, but must stand up for religious minorities, freedom of expression and human rights." [^6]

In 2021, a parliamentary resolution supported by all parties, condemned [human rights violations against the Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution_of_Uyghurs_in_China "Persecution of Uyghurs in China") and other minorities in the Xinjiang province and demanded European Union sanctions against the Chinese government.[^7]

When the European Union implemented sanctions in March 2021, Austria's Foreign Minister Alexander Schallenberg said he approved of the step and called it an "important signal." [^8]

## Economic relations

China is among the top 5 of Austria's most important economic partners regarding direct trade relations and also crucial for Austria's economy through its trade with the European Union and Austria's most important economic partner Germany.[^9]
