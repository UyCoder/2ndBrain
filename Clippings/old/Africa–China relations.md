**Africa–China relations**, also referred to as **Sino-African relations**, **Afro–Chinese relations** and **China–Africa relations**, are the historical, political, economic, military, social, and cultural connections between [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") and [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa").

Little is known about ancient relations between China and Africa, though there is some evidence of early trade connections. Highlights of medieval contacts include the 14th-century journey of [Moroccan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco "Morocco") scholar [Ibn Battuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Battuta "Ibn Battuta"),[^1] the 14th-century visit of [Somali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_people "Somali people") scholar and explorer [Sa'id of Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sa%27id_of_Mogadishu "Sa'id of Mogadishu") and the 15th-century [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") voyages of [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_people "Chinese people") [admiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Admiral "Admiral") [Zheng He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zheng_He "Zheng He").

Modern political and economic relations between mainland China and the African continent commenced in the era of [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong"), following the victory of the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) in the [Chinese Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War "Chinese Civil War"). At the turn of the 21st century, the modern state of the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC) built increasingly strong economic ties with [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_African_countries "List of African countries"). In 2013, it was estimated that one million Chinese citizens were residing in Africa.[^2] Additionally, [Howard French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Howard_W._French "Howard W. French") estimated that two million Africans were working in China in 2017.[^3]

In 1971, China received the support from 26 African nations in the UN to take over the seat from [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan").[^4] [Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party") [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") was grateful for the support and said, "It is our African brothers who have carried us into the UN".[^5] Today almost all African nations officially recognise the economically larger China (People's Republic of China) over Taiwan in search of economic advantage (aid, trade and FDI).[^6] As of 2026, [Eswatini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eswatini "Eswatini") and the self-declared [Republic of Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Somaliland "Republic of Somaliland") are the only two African states to have official relations with Taiwan, although Eswatini is the only African UN member that officially recognizes the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") rather than the People's Republic.[^7]

There have increasingly been closer political, security and economic ties between China and African nations.[^5] Trade between China and Africa increased by 700% during the 1990s,[^8] and China is currently Africa's largest trading partner.[^9] In 2000, the [Forum on China–Africa Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forum_on_China%E2%80%93Africa_Cooperation "Forum on China–Africa Cooperation") (FOCAC) was established as a forum between African countries and China.

The [China Africa Research Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Africa_Research_Initiative "China Africa Research Initiative") estimated that there were over 88,371 Chinese workers in Africa in 2022, down from a high of 263,696 in 2015.[^10]

## Historical relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Chinese_celadon_vase_Branly_71.1886.89.1.jpg/250px-Chinese_celadon_vase_Branly_71.1886.89.1.jpg)

Yuan dynasty era Celadon vase from Mogadishu.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/ShenDuGiraffePainting.jpg/250px-ShenDuGiraffePainting.jpg)

A giraffe brought from Somalia in the twelfth year of Yongle (AD 1415). 11

China and Africa have a [history of trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_trade "History of trade") relations, sometimes through third parties, dating back as far as 202 BC and 220 AD.[^12] [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy"), writing in [Roman Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Egypt "Roman Egypt") in the second century, knew of China by two separate routes: the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road") and the [Indian Ocean trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean_trade "Indian Ocean trade"). He identified two Chinese peoples: the *Seres* or silk people and the *Sinai* of the southern trade, whose name probably derives from the [Qin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_dynasty "Qin dynasty").[^13]

The first mention of Africa in Chinese sources was in the *[Yu-yang-tsa-tsu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscellaneous_Morsels_from_Youyang "Miscellaneous Morsels from Youyang")* by [Tuan Ch'eng-shih](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duan_Chengshi "Duan Chengshi") (died 863), a compendium of general knowledge where he wrote about the *land of Po-pa-li* ([Berbera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berbera "Berbera")).

In 1071, an embassy arrived in China from an unidentified East African kingdom. Since it was a formal tribute mission (in the eyes of the Chinese), it is described in the official *[History of the Song Dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Song_Dynasty "History of the Song Dynasty")*. The name of the kingdom was *Ts'eng t'an* and it was said to lie inland and mint its coin. This name is probably derived from the Persian *[Zangistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zanj "Zanj")*, and the title of its ruler, *a-mei-lo a-mei-lan* is probably derived from the Persian *[amir-i-amiran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amir_al-umara "Amir al-umara")* (emir of emirs).[^14]

Archaeological excavations at [Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mogadishu "Mogadishu") in the [Ajuran Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajuran_Empire "Ajuran Empire") and [Kilwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilwa_Kisiwani "Kilwa Kisiwani"), [Tanzania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanzania "Tanzania") have recovered many coins from China. The majority of the [Chinese coins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Chinese_coinage "Ancient Chinese coinage") date back to the [Song dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty "Song dynasty"), although the [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") and [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") are also represented, according to [Richard Pankhurst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Pankhurst_\(academic\) "Richard Pankhurst (academic)").[^15] In 1226, [Chao Jukua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chao_Jukua "Chao Jukua"), the commissioner of foreign trade at [Quanzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quanzhou "Quanzhou") in the [Fujian province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fujian_Province,_Republic_of_China "Fujian Province, Republic of China") of China, completed his *Chu-fan-chih* (*Description of Barbarous Peoples*) which discusses [Zanzibar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zanzibar "Zanzibar") (*Ts'ong-pa*) and Somalia (*Pi-P'a-Lo*).[^16]

Giraffes, zebras, and incense were exported to the Ming dynasty of China, making [Somalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalis "Somalis") merchants leaders in the commerce between Asia and Africa [^17] while influencing the [Chinese language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese "Mandarin Chinese") in Somalia in the process.

In the 14th century, [Moroccan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco "Morocco") traveler and scholar, [Ibn Battuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Battuta "Ibn Battuta"), made a long journey to Africa and Asia. He reached China in April 1345 after a stay in [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") before serving as an envoy of [Sultan Muhammad Tughlaq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_bin_Tughluq "Muhammad bin Tughluq") of the [Indian Tughlaq dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tughlaq_dynasty "Tughlaq dynasty") to China.[^1] [^18] He wrote:[^19]

> China is the safest, best regulated of countries for a traveler. A man may go by himself on a nine-month journey, carrying with him a large sum of money, without any fear. Silk is used for clothing even by poor monks and beggars. Its porcelains are the finest of all makes of pottery and its hens are bigger than geese in our country.

The 14th-century visit of [Sa'id of Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sa%27id_of_Mogadishu "Sa'id of Mogadishu"), the [Somali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_people "Somali people") scholar and explorer was another point of Medieval contact between Africa and China.[^20]

The Ming dynasty admiral, Zheng He, and his fleet rounded the coast of [Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalia "Somalia") and followed the coast down to the [Mozambique Channel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mozambique_Channel "Mozambique Channel"). The goal of those expeditions was to spread [Chinese culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_culture "Chinese culture") and display Chinese strength. Zheng brought gifts and granted titles from the Ming emperor to local rulers. In October 1415, Zheng He reached the eastern coast of Africa and sent the first of two giraffes as gifts to the Chinese [Yongle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yongle_Emperor "Yongle Emperor") Emperor.[^21]

Other accounts mention Chinese ships sinking near [Lamu Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamu_Island "Lamu Island") in [Kenya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenya "Kenya") in 1415. Survivors are said to have settled on the island and married local women.[^22] [^23]

Archaeologists have found Chinese porcelains made during the [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty") (618–907) in Kenyan villages; however, these were believed to have been brought over by [Zheng He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zheng_He "Zheng He") during his fifteenth-century ocean voyages.[^24] On Lamu Island off the Kenyan coast, local [oral tradition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oral_tradition "Oral tradition") maintains that twenty shipwrecked Chinese sailors, possibly part of Zheng's fleet, washed up on shore there hundreds of years ago. Given permission to settle by local tribes after having killed a dangerous python, they [converted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_conversion#Islam "Religious conversion") to [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") and married local women. Now, they are believed to have just six descendants remaining there. In 2002, DNA tests conducted on one of the women confirmed that she was of Chinese descent. Her daughter, Mwamaka Sharifu, later received a PRC government scholarship to study [traditional Chinese medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_medicine "Traditional Chinese medicine") (TCM) in China.[^25] [^26]

[National Geographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Geographic_Society "National Geographic Society") published an article by [Frank Viviano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Viviano "Frank Viviano") in July 2005 about his visit to [Pate Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pate_Island "Pate Island"). During his time on Lamu, ceramic fragments had been found which the administrative officer of the local Swahili history museum claimed were of Chinese origin, specifically from Zheng He's voyage to East Africa. The eyes of the Pate people resembled Chinese, and Famao and Wei were among the names, which were speculated to be of Chinese origin. Their ancestors were said to have been indigenous women who intermarried with Chinese Ming sailors when they were shipwrecked. Two places on Pate were called "Old Shanga", and "New Shanga", which the Chinese sailors had named. A local guide, who claimed to be of Chinese descent, showed Viviano a graveyard made out of coral on the island, indicating that they were graves of Chinese sailors, which the author described as "virtually identical", to Chinese Ming dynasty tombs, complete with "half-moon domes" and "terraced entries".[^27]

Archaeologists have discovered glass beads and [porcelain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porcelain "Porcelain") from China inside [Great Zimbabwe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Zimbabwe "Great Zimbabwe"), a medieval stone city located in present-day [Zimbabwe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zimbabwe "Zimbabwe").[^28]

According to Melanie Yap and Daniel Leong Man in their book "Colour, Confusions, and Concessions: the History of Chinese in South Africa", Chu Ssu-pen, a Chinese mapmaker in 1320, had southern Africa drawn on one of his maps. Ceramics found in Zimbabwe and South Africa dated back to the [Song dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty "Song dynasty"). Some tribes to Cape Town's north claimed descent from Chinese sailors during the thirteenth century. Their physical appearance is similar to Chinese with paler skin and a Mandarin-sounding tonal language. Their name for themselves is "abandoned people", *Awatwa* in their language.[^29]

## Contemporary relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/1964-04_1964%E5%B9%B41%E6%9C%8830%E6%97%A5_%E4%B8%AD%E5%9B%BD%E8%AE%BF%E9%97%AE%E5%9F%83%E5%A1%9E%E4%BF%84%E6%AF%94%E4%BA%9A_%E5%91%A8%E6%81%A9%E6%9D%A5%E4%B8%8E%E6%B5%B7%E5%B0%94%C2%B7%E5%A1%9E%E6%8B%89%E8%A5%BF%E4%B8%80%E4%B8%96.jpg/250px-1964-04_1964%E5%B9%B41%E6%9C%8830%E6%97%A5_%E4%B8%AD%E5%9B%BD%E8%AE%BF%E9%97%AE%E5%9F%83%E5%A1%9E%E4%BF%84%E6%AF%94%E4%BA%9A_%E5%91%A8%E6%81%A9%E6%9D%A5%E4%B8%8E%E6%B5%B7%E5%B0%94%C2%B7%E5%A1%9E%E6%8B%89%E8%A5%BF%E4%B8%80%E4%B8%96.jpg)

In the 1960s, the People's Republic of China established diplomatic relationships with a host of African countries in quick succession. Pictured is Premier Zhou Enlai meeting with Emperor Haile Selassie on a visit to Ethiopia in 1964.

The establishment of modern China-African relations began in the late 1950s, when China signed bilateral trade agreements with [Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria "Algeria"), [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt"), [Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guinea "Guinea"), [South Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Africa "South Africa"), and [Sudan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudan "Sudan"). Chinese Premier Zhou Enlai made a ten-country tour of Africa between December 1963 and January 1964.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 27">: 27</span> </sup> Zhou Enlai visited [Ghana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghana "Ghana") and established close relations with [Kwame Nkrumah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kwame_Nkrumah "Kwame Nkrumah"), who desired a united Africa.[^31] Relations at that time often reflected [China's foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Foreign relations of the People's Republic of China") in general: China "began to cultivate ties and offer...economic, technical and military support to African countries and liberation movements in an effort to encourage [wars of national liberation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wars_of_national_liberation "Wars of national liberation") and revolution as part of an international united front against both superpowers".[^32]

[Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") viewed Africa (along with Latin America) as the "First Intermediate Zone," in which China's status as a non-white power might enable it to compete with and supersede both United States and Soviet Union influence.[^33]<sup><span title="Page: 48">: 48</span></sup>

China works hard to cultivate favorable votes from African countries at the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_and_the_United_Nations "China and the United Nations").[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 29">: 29</span> </sup> It has often been successful, and the voting pattern of the Africa countries which rotate on the [Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council") and African members of the [Human Rights Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council "United Nations Human Rights Council") generally align more frequently with China than with the United States, France, and the United Kingdom.[^30]<sup><span title="Pages: 29–30">: 29–30</span> </sup> The General Assembly votes of African countries and China have also generally aligned.[^30]<sup><span title="Pages: 30–31">: 30–31</span></sup>

From the Chinese perspective, collaboration with the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union") and African subregional bodies (such as [ECOWAS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECOWAS "ECOWAS"), [SADC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_African_Development_Community "Southern African Development Community"), and [COMESA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Market_for_Eastern_and_Southern_Africa "Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa")) is attractive because these bodies function largely independent of Western influence.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 63, 330">: 63, 330</span> </sup> China and the [Southern African Development Community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_African_Development_Community "Southern African Development Community") (SADC) have a long-standing relationship and China provides the SADC with an annual $100,000 grant to help fund its Secretariat.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 70">: 70</span></sup>

### Diplomacy

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Member_of_FOCAC.png/250px-Member_of_FOCAC.png)

Members of FOCAC

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/China%27s_permanent_seat_in_the_UN_in_Africa_and_the_diplomatic_game_of_%22two_Chinas%22_in_Africa.jpg/250px-China%27s_permanent_seat_in_the_UN_in_Africa_and_the_diplomatic_game_of_%22two_Chinas%22_in_Africa.jpg)

China's permanent seat in the UN in Africa and the diplomatic maneuvers of "two Chinas" in Africa

Early modern bilateral relations were mainly affected by the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") and [communist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist "Communist") ideology. Following the Mao era and continuing as of at least 2023, China seeks relationships with African governments and political parties on the basis of political and security reliability, rather than with regard to ideological ties.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 333">: 333</span></sup>

China originally had close ties with the [anti-apartheid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apartheid "Apartheid") and [liberation movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberation_movement "Liberation movement"), [African National Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_National_Congress "African National Congress") (ANC), in [South Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Africa "South Africa"), but as China's relations with the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") deteriorated and the ANC moved closer to the Soviet Union, China shifted away from the ANC towards the [Pan-Africanist Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan_Africanist_Congress_of_Azania "Pan Africanist Congress of Azania").[^34] The Soviets supported [Joshua Nkomo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joshua_Nkomo "Joshua Nkomo") 's [Zimbabwe African People's Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zimbabwe_African_People%27s_Union "Zimbabwe African People's Union"), and supplied them with arms; [Robert Mugabe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Mugabe "Robert Mugabe") 's attempts to gain Soviet support for his [Zimbabwe African National Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zimbabwe_African_National_Union "Zimbabwe African National Union") were rebuffed, leading him to enter into relations with China.[^35] [^36] [^37] China adopted several principles, among them was the support of the independence of African countries while investing in infrastructure projects.[^38] [^39]

In the 1970s, the expulsion of Soviet military advisers from Egypt and Sudan was welcomed with arms supplied by China.[^40] [^41] [^42] China, [Zaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zaire "Zaire"), and [Safari Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safari_Club "Safari Club") (an alliance of intelligence services) shared a common goal in Africa: to curb Soviet influence in the area. Accordingly, both Zaire and China covertly funneled aid to the [National Front for the Liberation of Angola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola "National Liberation Front of Angola") ([FNLA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FNLA "FNLA")) (and later, [UNITA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNITA "UNITA")) to prevent the [People's Movement for the Liberation of Angola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MPLA "MPLA") (MPLA), which was supported and augmented by [Cuba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuba "Cuba"), from coming to power.[^43] China and Safari Club sent assistance to support the [Mobutu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobutu_Sese_Seko "Mobutu Sese Seko") regime during the [Shaba I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shaba_I "Shaba I") conflict in 1977.[^44]

The [Somali Democratic Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Democratic_Republic "Somali Democratic Republic") established good relations with the Soviet Union throughout the Cold War era. When Somalia sought to create a [Greater Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Somalia "Greater Somalia"), it [declared war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ogaden_War "Ogaden War") on [Ethiopia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopia "Ethiopia") and took the [Ogaden region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ogaden_region "Ogaden region") in three months with Soviet aid. When the Soviet Union shifted its support from Somalia to Ethiopia, the latter retook the Ogaden. This angered Somali President, [Siad Barre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siad_Barre "Siad Barre"), who expelled all Soviet advisors and citizens from Somalia. China and Safari Club supported Somalia diplomatically and with token military aid.[^45] [^46]

The [Forum on China-Africa Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forum_on_China%E2%80%93Africa_Cooperation "Forum on China–Africa Cooperation") (FOCAC), first officially held in Beijing in 2006, following two major ministerial conferences in Beijing and [Addis Ababa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addis_Ababa "Addis Ababa") in 2000 and 2003, is a [triennial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triennial "Triennial") high-level forum between China and the African nations.[^47] FOCAC is the primary multi-lateral coordination mechanism between African countries and China.[^48] At the 2018 FOCAC summit, [General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") emphasized the "Five Nos" which guide [its foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy_of_China "Foreign policy of China") in dealing with African countries and other developing countries: (1) non-interference in other countries' pursuit of development paths suitable to their national conditions, (2) non-interference in domestic affairs, (3) not imposing China's will on others, (4) not attaching political conditions to foreign aid, and (5) not seeking political self-interest in investment and financing.[^49]<sup><span title="Pages: 108–109">: 108–109</span></sup>

In May 2016, ten African countries signed a declaration during a meeting of the [China-Arab States Cooperation Forum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Arab_States_Cooperation_Forum "China–Arab States Cooperation Forum") (CASCF) which expressed appreciation for China's efforts to resolve [its maritime and territorial disputes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_disputes_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Territorial disputes of the People's Republic of China") through dialogue and negotiation.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 40">: 40</span> </sup> The declaration also stated that the [United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea "United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea") signatories should have the right to choose their own approach to resolving maritime issues.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 40">: 40</span></sup>

Various subregional multilateral groups in Africa, including the [Economic Community of West African States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECOWAS "ECOWAS") (ECOWAS) and the [East African Community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_African_Community "East African Community") (EAC) also engage with China.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 11">: 11</span></sup>

Wafula Okumu of *[Pambazuka News](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pambazuka_News "Pambazuka News")* wrote in 2005 that for a permanent UN seat for Africa, [Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigeria "Nigeria"), the largest African country, relied on Chinese support while Egypt looked to the U.S. for backing.[^50]

In 2022, the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union") announced its move to establish a delegation with a resident Ambassador to [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing"), China.[^51]

From 1991 until at least 2023, each Chinese foreign minister's first overseas visit of the year has been to an African country.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 329">: 329</span> </sup> In its relations with African countries, China has shown a willingness to grant diplomatic audiences at the highest levels to even delegations from the smaller African countries.[^52]<sup><span title="Page: 107">: 107</span></sup>

As of 2024, China spends approximately US$1 billion on [public diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_diplomacy "Public diplomacy") to Africa in the areas of media, education, and culture.[^53]<sup><span title="Page: 414">: 414</span></sup>

#### Foreign policy

##### China policy towards Africa

China's foreign policy towards Africa upon the founding of the People's Republic of China was based on Beijing's "Five Principles of Peaceful Co-existence": a) national sovereignty, b) win-win co-operation, c) mutual non-aggression and non-alignment, d) neutrality and non-interference in internal affairs, and e) equality.[^54] Throughout the 1960, China deployed formal and informal foreign policy instruments. Formal instruments included friendship treaties, cultural pacts, financial and technical assistance. When Deng Xiaoping replaced Mao Zedong as China's paramount leader in the late 1970s, his foreign policy focused on establishing co-operative relations with the West. Consequently, Africa lost much of its geopolitical significance to China. Shortly after 1989 however, Beijing relaunched a diplomatic offensive in Africa marked by high-level official visits and increased aid to Africa. The relationship has since grown exponentially with China's foreign policy towards Africa currently shaped by its broader Belt and Road Initiative.[^54]

##### Africa's foreign policy towards China

Africa, particularly sub-Saharan Africa's relationship towards China has been marked by both friendship and friction.[^54] African governments are mostly pro-China, whereas civil society tend to be alarmed by the impacts of environmental degradation, wildlife loss and exploitation of local workers, etc. associated with Chinese migrants in African countries.[^54]

#### Taiwan

The [political status of Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_status_of_Taiwan "Political status of Taiwan") has been a key political issue for the People's Republic of China (PRC). In 1971, the support of African nations was crucial in the PRC's joining the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") (UN), taking over the seat of the ROC on Taiwan.[^55] Many African countries, such as Algeria, Egypt, Ethiopia, and [Zambia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zambia "Zambia") have stressed their support for a " [one-China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China "One China") policy". Only one African country, [Eswatini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swaziland "Swaziland"), still maintains relations with Taipei.[^56] All African countries except Eswatini recognize the People's Republic of China as the sole government of China and [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") as an integral part of China's territory, and support all efforts by the PRC to "achieve [national reunification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_unification "Chinese unification") ".[^57]

#### Xinjiang

African countries have generally defended China's treatment of its Muslim minorities.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 38">: 38</span> </sup> All African countries except Eswatini consider Xinjiang to be China's internal affairs.[^57] In July 2019, UN ambassadors of 37 countries, including Algeria, Angola, Cameroon, Congo, [DRC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Democratic Republic of the Congo"), Egypt, Eritrea, Nigeria, Somalia, South Sudan, Sudan, Zimbabwe, and other African states, signed a joint letter to the [United Nations Human Rights Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council "United Nations Human Rights Council") (UNHRC) defending China's treatment of [Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghurs "Uyghurs") and other Muslim minority groups in the [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang").[^58] [^59] Algeria, Burkina Faso, Republic of Congo, Djibouti, Nigeria, Somalia, and Zambia were among the 16 countries that defended China's policies in Xinjiang in 2019 but did not do so in 2020.[^60]

In 2021, ambassadors from Burkina Faso, Republic of Congo, and Sudan made statements in support of China's Xinjiang policies.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 39">: 39</span> </sup> African countries which are members of the UNHRC had a significant impact in narrowly defeating a proposal in October 2022 by that body to debate human rights in Xinjiang.[^61] Somalia was the only African UNHRC member voting in favor of debate.[^61]

#### Hong Kong

All African countries except Eswatini consider Hong Kong to be China's internal affairs.[^57] Several African countries expressed support for the Chinese government during the [2019–2020 Hong Kong protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019%E2%80%932020_Hong_Kong_protests "2019–2020 Hong Kong protests").[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 41">: 41</span> </sup> In October 2019, Uganda's Ministry of Foreign Affairs issued a statement that Uganda "firmly supports the one country, two systems policy of the People's Republic of China on the matter of Hong Kong and other areas" and that "Hong Kong's affairs are China's domestic affairs." [^30]<sup><span title="Page: 41">: 41</span> </sup> Also in October 2019, Tanzania's chief government spokesperson stated that the country supports China's one country, two systems policy, that the Hong Kong government was taking the best approach to the situation, and that other countries should support China.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 41">: 41</span> </sup> In January 2020, Namibia's land reform minister stated that Namibia fully supports Chinese territorial integrity and sovereignty, including with respect to Hong Kong.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 41">: 41</span> </sup> In June 2020, 53 countries, mostly in Africa, declared their support for the [Hong Kong national security law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Hong_Kong_national_security_law "2020 Hong Kong national security law") at the UNHRC.[^62]

#### Tibet

All African countries except Eswatini have declared [issues of Tibetan sovereignty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_sovereignty_debate "Tibetan sovereignty debate") to be a Chinese internal matter.[^57]

#### Party-to-party relations

The [International Department of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Department_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "International Department of the Chinese Communist Party") has served as the principal body coordinating party-to-party relations between African countries and China.[^63]

In February 2022, the Mwalimu Julius Nyerere Leadership School opened in Tanzania, with US$40 million in funding by the [Central Party School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Party_School "Central Party School") of the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party").[^64] [^65] The school was jointly established with the support of the six ruling parties of Tanzania, South Africa, Mozambique, Angola, Namibia, and Zimbabwe.[^66]

### Economic relations

China's economic interests in Africa have dramatically increased since the 1990s.[^67] In 1980, the total China-African trade volume amounted to US$1 billion.[^9] In 1999, it was US$6.5 billion [^68] and US$10 billion in 2000.[^9] By 2005, the total Africa–China trade volume had reached US$39.7 billion before jumping to US$55 billion in 2006, making China the second-largest trading partner of Africa after the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), which had US$91 billion in trade with African nations. The PRC also passed its traditional African economic partner and [former colonial power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_colonial_empires#Africa "French colonial empires"), [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France"), which had trade worth US$47 billion.[^69] In 2010, trade between Africa and China was valued at US$114 billion [^9] and US$166.3 billion in 2011.[^70] By 2022, total trade grew to US$282 billion.[^71] China has been Africa's largest trading partner since 2009 when it surpassed the United States,[^72] and continues to be by far its largest trading partner as of 2022.[^71] As of 2024, Africa makes up less than 5% of China's global trade.[^73]

During the year 2011, trade between Africa and China increased 33% from the previous year to US$166 billion. This included Chinese imports from Africa equaling US$93 billion, consisting largely of mineral ores, petroleum, and agricultural products, and Chinese exports to Africa totaling US$93 billion, consisting largely of manufactured goods.[^74] Trade between the regions also increased further by over 22% year-over-year to US$80.5 billion in the first five months of the year 2012.[^74] Imports from Africa were up 25.5% to $49.6 billion during these first five months of 2012 and exports of Chinese-made products, such as machinery, electrical and consumer goods and clothing/footwear increased 17.5% to reach $30.9 billion.[^74]

As China began a [new town](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planned_community "Planned community") construction boom around 2010, the Chinese government and state-owned enterprises began developing new towns with African governments, including Angola, South Africa, Congo, Ethiopia, and Rwanda.[^75]<sup><span title="Page: 316">: 316</span> </sup> Angola's [Quilamba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quilamba "Quilamba") city (developed by [CITIC Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CITIC_Group "CITIC Group")) is the largest development of this kind.[^75]<sup><span title="Page: 316">: 316</span></sup>

Findings from 2017 estimate there are in excess of 10,000 Chinese corporations doing business in Africa, the value of which amounts to more than $2 trillion.[^76] The majority of these companies are private [multinational corporations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multinational_corporation "Multinational corporation") investing in China's infrastructure, energy, and banking sectors.[^77] Investments from Chinese entrepreneurial migration have culminated in positive (indirect jobs) and negative (displacing local traders) effects in local African societies.[^78]

In 2008, one-third of China's [oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum") supplies came from the African continent, mainly from [Angola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angola "Angola").[^79] Investments of Chinese companies in the energy sector reached US$78.1 billion in 2019.[^80] In some cases, as in Nigeria and Angola, oil and gas exploration and production deals crossed $2 billion.[^81] [^82] [^83]

In agriculture, [Benin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benin "Benin") and the [Sahel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahel "Sahel") countries of [Burkina Faso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burkina_Faso "Burkina Faso") and [Mali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mali "Mali") supply up to 20% of China's [cotton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton "Cotton") needs. While [Côte d'Ivoire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Ivoire "Côte d'Ivoire") supplies China with [cocoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cocoa_bean "Cocoa bean"), from US$39.7 million in 2001 to $113.5 million in 2005,[^84] the most significant African provider of [coffee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee "Coffee") is Ethiopia.[^85]

The need to protect China's increased investments in Africa has driven a shift away from China's traditional non-interference in the internal matters of other countries to new diplomatic and military initiatives to try to resolve unrest in [South Sudan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Sudan "South Sudan") and Mali.[^86]

During the December 2015 FOCAC meeting in Johannesburg, South Africa, China's [paramount leader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paramount_leader "Paramount leader") [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") pledged $60 billion over three years in loans and assistance to the African continent.[^87] The stated aim of China's effort was to support factories manufacturing goods for export. Along with roads and ports, Nigerian President [Muhammadu Buhari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammadu_Buhari "Muhammadu Buhari") showed his desire to finish stalled railway projects along the coastline, specifically a 1,400 km railway from [Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos "Lagos") to [Calabar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabar "Calabar") representing approximately 200,000 jobs.[^88]

A 2020 report synthesizing close to a hundred studies on [Africa–China economic relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa%E2%80%93China_economic_relations "Africa–China economic relations") finds that economic engagement with China supported Africa's [economic transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_transformation "Economic transformation").[^89] At the same time, criticism against China has been growing from labour unions and civil society groups about the "poor labor conditions, unsustainable environmental practices, and job displacement" caused by Chinese enterprises.[^90] According to [RAND](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RAND "RAND"), China is also thought to be taking advantage of African governments' weaknesses, thereby encouraging corruption and wasteful decision-making.[^90]

Effective 1 December 2024, China eliminated [tariffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tariff "Tariff") for goods imported from all of the countries that the United Nations categorizes as [least developed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_developed_countries "Least developed countries") and with which China has [diplomatic relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_China "Foreign relations of China"), including thirty-three African countries.[^91] In May 2026, China expanded its zero-tariff policy to cover 53 African countries, excluding only [Eswatini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eswatini "Eswatini") due to its diplomatic relations with Taiwan. The expanded policy will remain in effect until 30 April 2028. China is the first major economy to offer unilateral duty-free access to Africa.[^92]

#### Communications infrastructure

Beginning in the late 1990s, Chinese [national champions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_champions "National champions") telecommunications company [Huawei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huawei "Huawei") built significant amounts of telecommunications infrastructure in [sub-Saharan Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa "Sub-Saharan Africa").[^93]

To improve commercial relationships and telecommunication services as part of the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative") (BRI), significant investments in fiber networks have been undertaken. The PEACE Cable (Pakistan & East Africa Connecting Europe) is a 9,300 mile (12,000 km) submarine fiber optic cable owned by a subsidiary of the China-based Hengtong Group and supplied by Huawei Marine. It is expected to reach initial completion in 2021–2022. The Cable's landfall in Pakistan provide for low-latency overland connection to China. The Cable's route is around the [Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula"), first dividing north into the [Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea"), crossing land in Egypt and then proceeding through the Mediterranean to the Interxion MRS2 Data Center in Marseille, France. The southern fork extends along the east coast of Africa, which in Phase 2 will reach South Africa. Additional landfalls are in Cyprus, Djibouti, Somalia, Kenya, and Seychelles.[^94]

In August 2021, China announced more digital projects on the Continent in areas such as the digital economy, smart cities and 5G.[^95] These projects are thought to be part of the Digital Africa initiative that was proposed during a trip of Foreign Minister Wang Yi in Africa in 2020.[^95]

#### Aid and loans

China began its foreign aid program in with assistance to sub-Saharan and Middle Eastern countries.[^96] The first sub-Saharan African country to receive Chinese foreign aid was Guinea; the aid assisted Guinea in building its infrastructure following independence from France.[^96]

In the 1960s and 1970s, the Chinese government supported [African Independence Movements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Independence_Movements "African Independence Movements") and gave aid to newly independent African nations. China provided significant amounts of aid at a time when it had relatively little in the way of financial resources itself.[^97]<sup><span title="Page: 126">: 126</span> </sup> Among the most notable early projects were the 1,860 km [TAZARA Railway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TAZARA_Railway "TAZARA Railway"), linking Zambia and Tanzania, which China helped to finance and build from 1970 to 1975.[^98] Some 50,000 Chinese engineers and workers were sent to the continent to complete the project. By 1978, China was giving aid to more African countries than the United States.[^99]

China provides aid in the forms of debt forgiveness, aid grants, concessional loans, and interest-free loans, including through the [Forum on China-Africa Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forum_on_China%E2%80%93Africa_Cooperation "Forum on China–Africa Cooperation") (FOCAC).[^47] According to [Marxist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism "Marxism") journalist [Martin Jacques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Jacques "Martin Jacques") in his book *[When China Rules the World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/When_China_Rules_the_World "When China Rules the World")*, Chinese aid is "far less restrictive and doctrinaire" and comes with fewer strings attached than Western aid.[^100] Unconditional and low-rate credit lines (rates at 1.5% over fifteen years to twenty years) [^101] have largely taken the place of more restrictive and conditional Western loans.[^102] The sole political condition China requires from aid recipients is recognition of the [One China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-China_Principle "One-China Principle").[^48]

Following her interviews of African scholars and diplomats, U.S. Professor of International Securities Studies Dawn C. Murphy concludes that many African countries genuinely appreciate this moral stance by China against political conditions for foreign aid.[^48]

Estimates regarding the amount of African debt cancelled by China varies. Since 2000, over $10bn in debt owed by African nations to the PRC has been cancelled, according to *[Le Monde diplomatique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Monde_diplomatique "Le Monde diplomatique")*.[^101] According to a 2020 report by the China Africa Research Initiative at [Johns Hopkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University "Johns Hopkins University"), "China has only offered debt write-offs for zero-interest loans", which account for at least $3.4 billion of cancelled debt in Africa between 2000 and 2019.[^103]

Scott N. Romaniuk, a researcher at the [University of Alberta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Alberta "University of Alberta") 's [China Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Institute "China Institute"), cautioned that Africa should "beware of 'no strings attached'" regarding development financing from China. He said that China's low-interest loans have been used to trade for extraction rights of proven deposits of natural resources, constraining African countries' future use of these resources.[^104] [Patrick Bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrick_Bond "Patrick Bond") said, "the conditions on Chinese loans and investments become very clear when the recipient countries have a debt crisis".[^105]

In 2015, the [China Africa Research Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Africa_Research_Initiative "China Africa Research Initiative") identified 17 African countries with loans from China facing potential default.[^106] Kenyan economist Anzetse Were has argued that some African nations' narratives of Chinese [debt-trap diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt-trap_diplomacy "Debt-trap diplomacy") stem from a lack of [fiscal transparency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiscal_transparency "Fiscal transparency") and a weaker bargaining position vis-à-vis China.[^107]

Although several countries express concern that China has been engaging in [debt-trap diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt-trap_diplomacy "Debt-trap diplomacy") to neo-colonise the continent; however, academics including [Deborah Bräutigam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deborah_Br%C3%A4utigam "Deborah Bräutigam") have disputed those accusations.[^108] [^109] [^110] Following her review of available data, U.S. Air War College Professor Dawn C. Murphy concluded that calling China's behavior in Africa “neocolonial” is “an exaggeration and misrepresentation.” [^111] [London School of Economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_School_of_Economics "London School of Economics") Professor [Keyu Jin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keyu_Jin "Keyu Jin") writes that the claim that China leads borrowers into a debt trap is misleading.[^112]<sup><span title="Page: 280">: 280</span> </sup> Jin observes that the majority of BRI countries' debt is owed to international organizations or private Western institutions like hedge funds, rather than to China.[^112]<sup><span title="Pages: 280–281">: 280–281</span> </sup> Jin also writes that China has written off many of its loans and also provided debt relief to borrowers.[^112]<sup><span title="Page: 281">: 281</span></sup>

The China Africa Research Initiative reported that Chinese financiers loaned $153 billion to African public-sector borrowers between 2000 and 2019; at least or over 80 percent of those loans were used for economic and social infrastructure projects in the transport, power, telecom, and water sectors of underdeveloped and developing countries.[^113] [^114] A 2007 report published by [International Rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Rivers "International Rivers") said that several infrastructure projects funded by Chinese loans, such as the [Merowe Dam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merowe_Dam "Merowe Dam"), had a positive impact on the economies of African countries.[^115]

As of 2021, China is estimated to hold at least 21% of all African debt.[^116] [^117] In August 2022, the Chinese foreign ministry announced that it would forgive 23 interest-free loans that matured at the end of 2021 to 17 unspecified African countries.[^118]

#### Health care

China has been engaged in a kind of " [health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health "Health") diplomacy" towards Africa since the 1960s. [Health care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_care "Health care") development and medical assistance have been among the chief areas of support. Between the early 1960s and 2005, more than 15,000 [Chinese doctors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_care_in_China "Health care in China") travelled to Africa to help treat patients in more than 47 countries.[^119]

In 2001, the member nations of G8 formed the United Nations-backed [Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Fund_to_Fight_AIDS,_Tuberculosis_and_Malaria "Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria") with an initial budget of $10 billion. In 2007, another additional $1.1 billion was approved in [Kunming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kunming "Kunming"), China, of which 66% was dedicated to Africa.[^120] In September of the same year, China promised the Democratic Republic of the Congo to build 31 hospital units and 145 smaller health care centres, a project due to be completed in March 2010.[^121] [^122]

During the [2014 Ebola epidemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_African_Ebola_virus_epidemic "Western African Ebola virus epidemic"), China provided financial donations, medical supplies and personnel to Sierra Leone, Ghana, Liberia, and Guinea.[^123]

China provided vaccines to African countries during the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic"). As of November 2021, it had supplied 200 million vaccine doses to Africa, pledged to donate 600 million more doses, and pledged a further 400 million doses via other means such as joint Chinese-African production.[^124] China also sent medical teams to Algeria, Zimbabwe, and Nigeria.[^125]

#### African Centre for Disease Control

Under the Belt and Road Initiative, in 2023 the African Union along with the People's Republic of China is slated to open Phase-1 of the African continent's [Centres for Disease Control and Prevention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa_Centres_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention "Africa Centres for Disease Control and Prevention") (CDCP) complex.[^126]

#### Agriculture

Since the mid-1990s, China has encouraged its agricultural enterprises to seek economic opportunities abroad as part of its [go out policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Go_Out_policy "Go Out policy").[^127]

Agricultural Technology Demonstration Centers are a major component of China's agricultural cooperation with African countries.[^128] The function of these centers is to transmit agricultural expertise and technology from China to developing countries in Africa while also creating market opportunities for Chinese companies in the agricultural sector.[^128] The Chinese government is motivated to establish these centers out of both an ideological commitment to fostering [South-South cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South%E2%80%93South_cooperation "South–South cooperation") with less developed countries and by a desire to increase food security.[^129]

China first announced its Agricultural Technology Demonstrations Centers at the 2006 meeting of the [Forum on China-Africa Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forum_on_China%E2%80%93Africa_Cooperation "Forum on China–Africa Cooperation").[^96] It launched 19 of these centers between 2006 and 2018, all in sub-Saharan Africa.[^128] As of 2023, Agricultural Technology Demonstration Centers exist in 24 African countries.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 173">: 173</span></sup>

### Security

Military cooperation goes back to the Cold War period when China was keen to help [African liberation movements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decolonization_of_Africa#Timeline "Decolonization of Africa"). [Eritrea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eritrea "Eritrea") 's first president [Isaias Afwerki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaias_Afwerki "Isaias Afwerki"), a leader in the fight for independence, received military training in China. Apart from some traditional allies such as [Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalia "Somalia") and Tanzania, China also had military ties with [non-aligned countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement") like Egypt. Military equipment worth $142 million was sold to African countries between 1955 and 1977.[^101] In July 2017, China set up [its first overseas military base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_People%27s_Liberation_Army_Support_Base_in_Djibouti "Chinese People's Liberation Army Support Base in Djibouti") in [Djibouti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djibouti "Djibouti"), which is a small town located in the Horn of Africa between the Gulf of Aden and the Red Sea,[^130] as a logistics facility for peacekeeping missions on the continent.[^131] [^132] [Bertil Lintner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertil_Lintner "Bertil Lintner"), as well as various Indian analysts, have described the base in Djibouti as part of China's " [String of Pearls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_of_Pearls_\(Indian_Ocean\) "String of Pearls (Indian Ocean)") " geopolitical and military strategy in the [Indian Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean").[^133] [^134] [^131]

Among the channels that China has used for security diplomacy with the African countries are the China-Africa Defense and Security Forum (established in 2018) and the China-Africa Peace and Security Forum (which first met in 2019).[^30]<sup><span title="Pages: 213–214">: 213–214</span></sup>

The African countries and China also build military-to-military relations through military exchange visits.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 213">: 213</span> </sup> China generally prioritizes military-to-military exchanges in the security field, because it perceives military personnel as more effective interlocutors in this area than civilians.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 217">: 217</span></sup>

According to academic Obert Hodzi, African countries often prefer China's approach to security norms and principles such as [non-interventionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-interventionism "Non-interventionism") and respect for state sovereignty, which effectively limit Western influence in many African countries.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 162">: 162</span> </sup> [David H. Shinn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_H._Shinn "David H. Shinn") and academic Joshua Eisenman state that the Chinese principle of non-interventionism has long been well received by African countries both because of the historical experience of [European colonialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_Africa "Colonial Africa"), as well as the fact that many smaller countries often have concerns about outside interference with their sovereignty.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 336">: 336</span></sup>

#### Peacekeeping missions

In 2004, China deployed around 1,500 military personnel between [Liberia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberia "Liberia") and the Democratic Republic of the Congo.[^101] Since 2011, it has sent infantry troops describable (arguably) as 'combat' forces.[^135]

In July 2007, China supported the passage of [UN Security Council Resolution 1769](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_1769 "United Nations Security Council Resolution 1769") and contributed troops to African Union-United Nations Hybrid Operation in Darfur ([UNAMID](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNAMID "UNAMID")).[^136] China also has fourteen [attachés](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_attach%C3%A9s "Military attachés") in fourteen different African countries as of 2007, while eighteen African countries maintain attachés in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing").[^137]

#### Arms sales

Since the 1960s, when China provided small amounts of arms often free of charge to African rebels, liberation movements, and left-wing governments, China's transfer of arms has been an important component of its relations with African countries.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 222">: 222</span> </sup> Although measures of arms supplies vary, as of 2023 China is often regarded as the third most important source of arms to Africa.[^30]<sup><span title="Pages: 222–223">: 222–223</span></sup>

An increasing number of African countries have shifted their source of munitions from traditional providers such as [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia") to China due to the competitive prices offered by Chinese suppliers.[^138] It is estimated that between 2013 and 2017, Chinese arms imports to Africa totaled 17%, representing a 55% increase compared to the period covering 2009 to 2013.[^139] It also sold more arms than any other supplier, with sales to 23 African countries.[^139]

Arms sales by China to some African states have troubled critics who point out that some buyers like Sudan are accused of [war crimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_crime "War crime").[^140] Chinese-made drones, especially those made by the [Chengdu Aircraft Industry Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chengdu_Aircraft_Industry_Group "Chengdu Aircraft Industry Group"), and [China Aerospace Science and Technology Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Aerospace_Science_and_Technology_Corporation "China Aerospace Science and Technology Corporation"), have been sold across Africa, and have been utilized in hundreds of attacks in Egypt, Libya, Yemen, and Nigeria.[^141]

Former U.S. military contractor [Erik Prince](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erik_Prince "Erik Prince") 's [Frontier Services Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frontier_Services_Group "Frontier Services Group") has close ties to the Chinese state-owned [CITIC Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CITIC_Group "CITIC Group") and provides security training services to Chinese firms operating in Africa.[^142] [^143]

#### Military training

When China trains African military personnel, it typically does so in China.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 239">: 239</span> </sup> In conjunction with the [Confucius Institutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius_Institute "Confucius Institute") in Sudan and Democratic Republic of Congo, China has also developed Chinese language training programs for African military personnel.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 239">: 239</span></sup>

#### Allegations of espionage

The [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union") [headquarters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AU_Conference_Center_and_Office_Complex "AU Conference Center and Office Complex") in Addis Ababa was built and fully funded by the [Chinese government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Government of the People's Republic of China").[^144] *[Le Monde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Monde "Le Monde")* wrote that the Chinese government was alleged to have spied on the computer servers at the headquarters from 2012 to 2017.[^144] [^145] Chinese officials and African Union denied the accusation.[^146] African Union Chairman [Moussa Faki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moussa_Faki "Moussa Faki") described the allegations as "all lies" and stated that "no maneuvers could distract and divert us from our mission" of strengthening ties between the AU and China.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 323">: 323</span></sup>

In 2018, the African Union replaced its servers.[^144]

In June 2019, the African Union Commission and Huawei signed a memorandum of understanding to further increase IT cooperation.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 323">: 323</span> </sup> Wang stated that the agreement should end rumors of data leakage and that "AU has totally audited their IT system for the whole organization and nothing corroborates what was said in media reports one year ago." [^30]<sup><span title="Pages: 323–324">: 323–324</span></sup>

In 2020, Japan's [Computer Emergency Response Team](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_emergency_response_team "Computer emergency response team") (CERT) reported that a suspected Chinese hacking organization dubbed "Bronze President" had hacked and extracted footage from the AU Headquarters' security cameras.[^147]

In 2024, academics from the [University of Manchester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Manchester "University of Manchester") noted that the allegations generated more interest in the [global north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_North_and_Global_South "Global North and Global South") countries than in Africa.[^148]

### Space cooperation

Africa-China space cooperation occurs through a variety of channels, including through [BRICS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BRICS "BRICS"), the [China-Brazil Earth Resource Satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Brazil_Earth_Resources_Satellite "China Brazil Earth Resources Satellite") for Africa Program, the [Arab Satellite Communications Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Satellite_Communications_Organization "Arab Satellite Communications Organization"), the China-Arab States [BeiDou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BeiDou "BeiDou") Global Satellite Navigation System, and the [Disaster Monitoring Constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disaster_Monitoring_Constellation "Disaster Monitoring Constellation"), and the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative").[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 301">: 301</span></sup>

African countries are increasingly cooperating with China on satellite launches and specialized training.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 301">: 301</span> </sup> As of 2022, China has launched two satellites for Ethiopia, two for Nigeria, one for Algeria, one for Sudan, and one for Egypt.[^30]<sup><span title="Pages: 301–302">: 301–302</span></sup>

The 2022-2024 action plan for FOCAC commits China to using space technology to enhance cooperation with African countries and to create centers for Africa-China cooperation on satellite remote sensing application.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 300">: 300</span></sup>

## Culture

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/%E8%8F%AF%E4%B8%AD%E8%BE%B2%E6%A5%AD%E5%A4%A7%E5%AD%B8%E7%9A%84%E7%95%99%E5%AD%B8%E7%94%9F.jpg/250px-%E8%8F%AF%E4%B8%AD%E8%BE%B2%E6%A5%AD%E5%A4%A7%E5%AD%B8%E7%9A%84%E7%95%99%E5%AD%B8%E7%94%9F.jpg)

Foreign students at Huazhong Agricultural University, Wuhan

The first overseas Chinese cultural centre in Africa was opened in [Mauritius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauritius "Mauritius") in 1988. Two others followed in Egypt and Benin.[^149] The Confucius Institute has at least 54 locations across Africa, in addition to another 27 Confucius Classrooms in various countries (as of 2018).[^150] [^151]

Historically, little is known about early African immigration to China.[^152] As economic and political ties have strengthened, many Africans have relocated to China to seek better economic opportunities. Places dubbed 'Little Africa' and 'Chocolate City' are increasingly receiving new immigrants, mostly Nigerians. Most African immigrants, an estimated 20,000 individuals, are concentrated in the area of [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africans_in_Guangzhou "Africans in Guangzhou").[^153] [^154] An estimated 10,000 illegal African immigrants were in China as of 2009, with police crackdowns intensifying in that period.[^155]

In contrast, early Chinese immigration to the African continent is slightly better documented. In 1724, a few Chinese convicts were brought as laborers to South Africa from the [Dutch East Indies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies "Dutch East Indies") (modern-day [Indonesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesia "Indonesia")) by the colonial [Dutch Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_Empire "Dutch Empire"). In the early nineteenth century, another wave of immigrants were brought to South Africa by the [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_people "British people") to work in agriculture, infrastructure building, and mining.[^156] In recent years, there has been an increasing presence of [Chinese in Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinatowns_in_Africa "Chinatowns in Africa") with one estimate numbering Chinese nationals at one million.[^157]

China has also been increasingly involved in [sport in Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_in_Africa "Sport in Africa"). Since 1970, Chinese-owned companies have been building sports stadiums throughout most African countries. Each project costs dozens of millions of dollars, a fee that China gives as a [soft loan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_loan "Soft loan"). The stadiums strengthen China's diplomatic and commercial ties with African countries. African governments accept China's loans because they enable them to promote development projects. On the other hand, concerns have been raised as to the working conditions at these stadiums. Also, some of the stadiums turned out to be [white elephants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_elephants "White elephants") given their meager usage.[^158]

### Film and television

#### Chinese broadcasting and film screenings in Africa

The Chinese government has funded various projects and companies with a focus on localizing and distributing Chinese film throughout Africa. The first China-African film festival, the Egyptian Film Week, took place in 1957. In 1957 in Cairo the first Chinese Film Week was also organized.[^159] In 2019 the first Pan-African Online Film Festival was launched.[^160]

In 2002 [StarTimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/StarTimes "StarTimes") was authorized by the [Chinese Ministry of Commerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Commerce_\(China\) "Ministry of Commerce (China)") to start development out of China.[^161] Its first operator license was obtained in Rwanda in 2007, and it became known for providing cheaper packages to viewers.[^161] StarTimes now has chapters in over 30 countries.[^162] In 2018 StarTimes began producing its own content. These were first launched in Nigeria before becoming available to other African countries.[^161]

Since 2006 the importance of Chinese investment in African media has been "in all the action plans of the [Forum on China-Africa Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forum_on_China%E2%80%93Africa_Cooperation "Forum on China–Africa Cooperation")." [^163] On January 11, 2012, [China Central Television](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Central_Television "China Central Television") opened CCTV-Africa (now [CGTN Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CGTN_Africa "CGTN Africa")) in Nairobi, Kenya.[^164] As of the late 2010s, CGTN Africa continues to struggle against competitors like the [BBC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC "BBC") and [Al Jazeera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Jazeera_Media_Network "Al Jazeera Media Network") for ratings.[^164] The China-Africa Film and Television Cooperation project, launched in 2012, was founded with the goal of translating and distributing Chinese film and television.[^165] Annually the group translates ten series and 52 films into seven African languages.[^166]

Some academics have criticized these efforts as an attempt to bolster the Chinese government's influence through employment of soft power.[^166] [^167] Academic Haina Jin describes these projects as "a reflection of the eagerness on the part of Chinese institutions for increasing the international presence of Chinese films and television dramas and exporting Chinese culture to the world." [^166] Other academics, such as Wanning Sun, argues that increasing soft power through film is impossible due to the Chinese government's "top down" style of influence.[^168] Academics, such as Julia Strauss, have taken issue with the discussion of soft power as overly simplistic.[^169] [^170]

#### Chinese Film and Television Set in Africa

In 2016 Beijing Huanying Shidai Media released the film *[Ultimate Hero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultimate_Hero "Ultimate Hero").*[^171] The film takes place in a fictional African country named Tukaga. The film grossed CN¥4.5 million at the box office.[^172] Yong Zhang and Yiwen Xia describe the film as "making the African setting just a superficial gimmick," which then "failed \[...\] as an African-themed film." [^165]

[Wolf Warrior 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf_Warrior_2 "Wolf Warrior 2"), released in China on July 27, 2017;[^173] is set in an unnamed Africa country. Filmed in 2016, a majority of the shooting on location was done in [South Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Africa "South Africa"), in cities such as [Soweto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soweto "Soweto") and [Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandria "Alexandria").[^174] [^175] The film grossed CN¥5.68 billion at the box office, the equivalent $874 million.[^176] While the film has been praised for presenting "a lively imagine of Chinese people in Africa rarely seen in Hollywood-dominated portrayals," it has also been criticized as depicting "nearly all negative stereotypes of Africa," such as child abuse, abject poverty, widespread disease, and government corruption.[^165]

In 2011 the show *[A Beautiful Daughter-in-Law Era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Beautiful_Daughter-in-law_Era "A Beautiful Daughter-in-law Era")* premiered in Tanzania. The first Chinese series dubbed into Swahili, the show became a hit.[^166] It was referenced by Xi Jinping in a speech in Tanzania on March 25, 2013.[^166]

### Migration

Many Chinese men who engaged in gold mining in Ghana married local Black African Ghanaian women and had children with them and then the Ghana government deported illegal miners, leaving the mixed race Chinese fathered children stranded in Ghana while their fathers were sent back to China.[^177] [^178]

Many Ugandan women have been marrying Chinese businessmen who moved to Uganda.[^179]

New interest in Kenya's natural resources has attracted over $1 billion of investment from Chinese firms. This has propelled new development in Kenya's infrastructure with Chinese firms bringing in their own male workers to build roads.[^180]

In Kenya, there is a trend of the following influx of Chinese male workers in Kenya with a growing number of abandoned babies of Chinese men who fathered children with local women, causing concern.[^181] [^182]

### Racism

Human rights and advocacy groups for Africans in China have criticized the use of [blackface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackface "Blackface") performances on Chinese television, particularly on the [CCTV New Year's Gala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CCTV_New_Year%27s_Gala "CCTV New Year's Gala").[^183] [^184]

The expansion of Chinese companies and their investments in Africa has raised issues of Chinese racism against the local population.[^185] [^186] [^187] For example, after a video shot by a Kenyan worker whose Chinese boss referred to Kenyans as "monkeys" went viral in 2018, more examples of discrimination by Chinese nationals in the country, such as separated bathrooms, have emerged.[^186]

Around April 2020, African nationals in [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou "Guangzhou") were being evicted from their homes by local police and told to leave, with no place to sleep, amidst some recent Chinese news articles negatively reporting on Nigerians in the city.[^188] [^189] The reports of discrimination created controversy in Africa damaging Africa–China relations,[^190] [^191] [^192] and sparked a diplomatic crisis, with African governments and diplomats speaking out against the incidents in Guangzhou.[^193] The Nigerian legislator [Oloye Akin Alabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otunba_Akin_Alabi "Otunba Akin Alabi") posted a video of his confronting the PRC's ambassador [Zhou Pingjian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Pingjian "Zhou Pingjian") over the alleged mistreatment of Nigerians in the city. The governments of Ghana, Kenya, and Uganda have also asked for explanations from the PRC government, and the [African Union Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union_Commission "African Union Commission") invited the PRC ambassador to the African Union to discuss the mistreatment allegations.[^194] The African ambassadors summarized the complaints in an official protest letter demanding the cessation of reported ejection from hotels or apartments, forced testing and quarantine, the seizure of passports, and threats of visa revocation, arrest or deportation of Africans particularly in the Guangdong province.[^195]

In response, authorities in [Guangdong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangdong "Guangdong") encouraged foreigners to report instances of discrimination to a 24-hour support hotline and told businesses and rental houses to treat Chinese and foreigners equally. The COVID-19 targeting of Africans had eased by June 2020.[^196] [^197]

Reports as of 2023 have highlighted the generalised view in China of Africans as "backward or primitive and blackness as unattractive", with racist attitudes specifically on [Chinese social media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_in_China "Internet in China") going untouched by [censors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_censorship_in_China "Internet censorship in China").[^198] [^199] Interracial relationships between Chinese women and African men are especially vilified online.[^200] [^201]

## Criticism

There are a variety of critical perspectives scrutinizing the balance of power relationship between China and Africa, and China's role concerning human rights in Africa.[^202] [^203] Increasingly, concerns have been raised by Africans and Western observers that China's relationship with Africa is [neocolonialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neocolonialism "Neocolonialism") in nature.[^204] [^205] As a response to such criticism, China issued the *Nine Principles to Encourage and Standardise Enterprises' Overseas Investment*, a charter and conduct guide for Chinese companies operating abroad.[^206]

### Environmental degradation

Chinese mining and infrastructure projects have been criticized for contributing to destructive levels of pollution and wildlife loss in Africa.[^54] [^207] [^208] [^209] Chinese demand for [rosewood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosewood "Rosewood") has incentivized illegal logging [^210] and demand for donkey hides for [traditional Chinese medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_medicine "Traditional Chinese medicine") has been blamed for fueling illegal slaughter of donkeys across the continent, adversely affecting women in rural African communities and contributing to poverty.[^211]

### Illegal fishing in Africa

In Africa, the Chinese commercial fishing fleet is responsible for more [illegal, unreported and unregulated fishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illegal,_unreported_and_unregulated_fishing "Illegal, unreported and unregulated fishing") (IUU) fishing than that of any other nation.[^212] [^213] [^214] [^215]

### Kenya

Kenya's relations with China figured prominently in the [2022 Kenyan general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Kenyan_general_election "2022 Kenyan general election"), in which both candidates criticized the country's existing relations with China.[^216] [^217]

### Zimbabwe

The China-Zimbabwe relationship drew the attention of critics. China was accused of supplying Zimbabwe with jet fighters, vehicles, and other military equipment.[^218] China declared in 2007 that it was limiting assistance to [humanitarian aid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanitarian_aid "Humanitarian aid").[^219] In July 2008, Chinese diplomatic channels asked Mugabe "to behave", though critics see that as a way for China to protect its interests in this country should a regime change.[^220]

### War in Darfur

Another high-profile event of concern for critics of China in Africa was in the run-up to the [2008 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics "2008 Summer Olympics"). Human rights groups criticized China for its supportive relationship with the government of Sudan, which had been accused of mass killings in [Darfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darfur "Darfur").[^221] [^222] China is Sudan's largest economic partner, with a 40% share in its oil,[^223] and also sells Sudan small arms.[^224] China has threatened to veto [UN Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UN_Security_Council "UN Security Council") actions to combat the war in Darfur.[^225] In response, a 2008 editorial in the CCP-owned daily tabloid *[Global Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Times "Global Times")* stated that "As the Darfur issue is not an internal affair of China, nor was it caused by China, to link the two together is utterly unreasonable, irresponsible and unfair." [^226]

Following pressure and criticism from the international community, China appointed a Special Envoy for Africa Issues, [Liu Guijin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liu_Guijin "Liu Guijin").[^48] To facilitate resolution of the Darfur issue, Liu coordinated with the AU, EU, United States, and UN.[^48]

## African students in China

China has received African students since 1958 as part of its [Mao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") era foreign policy of Afro-Asian solidarity.[^227] By the early 1960s, African students reported of difficulties living in China, some of which were reportedly caused by [racial discrimination by the Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Racism_in_China "Racism in China").[^228] Incidents of racism against African students in China have later resurfaced, notably in the 1980s in the form of [campus protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanjing_anti-African_protests "Nanjing anti-African protests").[^229]

As of 2016, most African students in China were from the English-speaking African countries.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 136">: 136</span></sup>

In 2018, the Chinese government announced at the triennial Forum on China-Africa Cooperation that China would increase its scholarship offerings to African students from 30,000 in 2015 to 50,000. According to the Ministry of Education of the People's Republic of China, 81,562 African students studied in China in 2018, a 770% increase compared to 1996. China is now the second largest African student-hosting country behind [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France").[^230] As of 2024, China hosts approximately 80,000 African students per year.[^53]<sup><span title="Page: 414">: 414</span></sup>

In 2020, according to UNESCO's Global Annual Education Report, China offered 12,000 university scholarships to African students for the next academic year, to support their studies at Chinese universities.[^231]

## Public perception

In 2013, BB World Public Opinion polls (PIPA) showed positive views towards China in Nigeria (78%), Ghana (68%), however noted a decline in positive views in Kenya to 58%. But 2014 survey later showed an increase in positive public opinion in Kenya (65%), as well as 67% positive opinions in Ghana and an increase to 85% in Nigeria.[^232]

A 2015 Pew Global Attitudes survey found majority positive views of China, ranging from 52% to 80% favorable, in Ghana, Ethiopia, Burkina Faso, Tanzania, Senegal, Nigeria, Kenya, Uganda, and South Africa.[^97]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span></sup>

Younger Africans report particularly positive views of China.[^97]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span> </sup> In 2022, [Bloomberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloomberg_News "Bloomberg News") reported that the past two decades of China's investments into African infrastructure and its supplying of the continent with affordable consumer goods, has made young Africans feel more positive towards China. A survey conducted by the Ichikowitz Family Foundation in June 2022 and which involves lengthy face-to-face interviews, shown that 76% of 4,507 young Africans across 15 African countries had named China as a foreign power with having the biggest positive influence on their lives, surpassing the US and Europe. The positive views toward China was strongest in Rwanda, Malawi and Nigeria. However, 56 percent of those surveyed also believed in an unsubstantiated theory that COVID-19 was created and intentionally spread by China.[^233] [^234]

According to academic Rhys Jenkins, public opinion polls in Africa show that a majority of respondents in Africa view China favorably due to the positive impacts of Chinese investment and infrastructure-building.[^97]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span></sup>

According to a 2022 survey funded by the [European Regional Development Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Regional_Development_Fund "European Regional Development Fund") and run by [Palacky University Olomouc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palacky_University,_Olomouc "Palacky University, Olomouc") in collaboration with the Central European Institute of Asian Studies, a slightly larger share of Chinese respondents viewed Africans positively rather than negatively and most respondents favoured friendly rather than tough government policies towards Africa.[^235]
