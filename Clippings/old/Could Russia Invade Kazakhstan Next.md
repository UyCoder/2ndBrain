In early 2026, Alexander Dugin and Vladimir Solovyov — two of the Kremlin's most prominent voices — publicly questioned Kazakhstan's right to exist as a sovereign state. Solovyov even suggested "special military operations" in Central Asia — the same phrase used before the invasion of Ukraine.  
  
Kazakhstan shares a 7,600-kilometer border with Russia — nearly four times longer than Ukraine's pre-2014 border. Its northern regions are home to large ethnic Russian populations. And Russian politicians have been making claims on Kazakh territory for over a century.  
  
We trace the full arc: from tsarist-era colonization and Soviet demographic engineering to post-independence separatist movements, leaked GRU destabilization plans, and President Tokayev's multi-vector balancing act between Moscow and Washington. The video includes exclusive interviews with Kazakhstani analysts Dosym Satpayev and Gaziz Abishev.  
  
The question isn't whether Kazakhstan faces a threat. The question is how long the balance holds.
