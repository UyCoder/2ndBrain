**China–Somalia relations** are the [bilateral relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilateralism "Bilateralism") between [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") and [Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalia "Somalia"). Somalia maintains an embassy in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing"). China has an embassy in [Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mogadishu "Mogadishu"). Somalia and China are upgrading their relationship to a [Strategic Partnership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_partnerships_of_China "Diplomatic partnerships of China").[^1]

## History

[Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalia "Somalia") and [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") have a long relationship in terms of trade, military, culture and language.[^2]

### Middle Ages

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/ShenDuGiraffePainting.jpg/120px-ShenDuGiraffePainting.jpg)

Exotic animals such as the giraffe caught and sold by Somali merchants were very popular in medieval China.

Relations between Somalia and China long predate the Middle Ages. Through trade, the peoples of both areas established good relations. [Giraffes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giraffes "Giraffes"), [zebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zebras "Zebras") and [incense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incense "Incense") were exported to the [Ming Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_Empire "Ming Empire") of China, which established Somali merchants as leaders in the commerce between [Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia "Asia") and [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa"),[^3] and in the process influenced the Chinese language with the Somali language and vice versa. The Chinese exported [celadon wares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celadon "Celadon"), [spices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spice "Spice") and [muskets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musket "Musket") in return for [horses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horse "Horse"), exotic animals and [ivory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivory "Ivory"). The prominent [Hui-Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hui_people "Hui people") [explorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explorer "Explorer"), [mariner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sailor "Sailor"), [diplomat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomat "Diplomat") and [fleet admiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fleet_admiral "Fleet admiral"), [Zheng He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zheng_He "Zheng He"), arrived in [his fourth and fifth voyage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treasure_voyages "Treasure voyages") to the Somali cities of [Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mogadishu "Mogadishu"), [Zeila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeila "Zeila"), [Merca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merca "Merca") and [Berbera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berbera "Berbera").[^4] [Sa'id of Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sa%27id_of_Mogadishu "Sa'id of Mogadishu"), a Somali [explorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explorer "Explorer"), travelled to China in the 14th century, when China was ruled by the [Yuan Dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty "Yuan Dynasty"), and noted the trading communities of the Chinese ports and cities.

### Cold War era

#### Somali Democratic Government (1960–1969)

On 30 June 1960, Foreign Minister [Chen Yi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chen_Yi_\(marshal\) "Chen Yi (marshal)") announced the recognition of the [Somali Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Republic "Somali Republic") by the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China").[^5] Official relations between the Somali and Chinese governments were established on 14 December 1960.[^6]<sup><span title="Page: 346">: 346</span> </sup> Somalia and China later signed their first official trade agreement in June 1963.[^7]

During the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") period, the Somali government maintained active relations with its Chinese counterpart. The Somali government campaigned for an end to China's diplomatic isolation and supported instead its entry into the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations").[^8] The [Sino-Soviet split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split "Sino-Soviet split") had a large influence on China's relations with countries in Africa. As early as 1964, Somalia was described as the first major center of Sino-Soviet rivalry on the continent.[^9]

#### Somali Democratic Republic 1969–1991

By 1971, Chinese economic aid to Somalia far exceeded the Soviet Union.[^10]

When the Somali government expelled [Soviet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") representatives in late 1977, China agreed to take over many of the development projects started by them.[^11] The Chinese government declared Somalia's decision to expel the Soviets as marking a, "another important victory in the struggle against [hegemonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hegemony "Hegemony") ".[^10] 1970 \]\]In the late 1970s the Chinese carried out and completed what was considered their second most ambitious project in [Sub-Saharan Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa "Sub-Saharan Africa") known as "The Somali North-South Road". The PRC also became involved in the construction of hospitals, factories and further extended large loans to Somalia.[^12]

China supported Somalia during the [Ogaden War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ogaden_War "Ogaden War"), which saw the Somali army and insurgents fighting for independence from [Ethiopia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopia "Ethiopia") face off against [Soviet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"), [Cuban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuba "Cuba") and Ethiopian forces. However, [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") offered only nominal military support to [Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mogadishu "Mogadishu"), instead focusing on taking over the efforts of abandoned Soviet projects. Following the war, China maintained a 3,000 man presence in Somalia.[^13]

### Present-day

#### Somali Civil War

In January 1991, the Chinese embassy in Mogadishu closed down operations due to the start of the [civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Civil_War "Somali Civil War") in Somalia.[^14] Despite the departure of most Chinese officials, the two countries maintained a small trading relationship in the ensuing years. Total trade volume in 2002 was [US$](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_dollar "United States dollar") 3.39 million, with Somalia exporting US$1.56 million of goods to China and importing $1.83 million.[^15]

From 2000 to 2011, approximately seven Chinese development projects were launched in Somalia.[^16] These initiatives included $6 million in economic assistance,[^17] donation of anti-malaria drugs,[^18] and $3 million in debt relief.[^19]

#### 2010s

Following the establishment of the [Federal Government of Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Government_of_Somalia "Federal Government of Somalia") in mid-2012, the Chinese authorities reaffirmed their support for the Somali government and called on the international community to strengthen its commitment to the Somali peace process. China's Permanent Representative to the UN, Li Baodong, also emphasized his administration's support for the Somali federal government's stabilization plan, including the latter's efforts at "implementing an [interim Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Somalia "Constitution of Somalia"), carrying out its six-point plan, strengthening institutional capacity, exercising government functions and extending effective authority over all its national territory." [^20]

In August 2013, follow a meeting with Chinese Vice Premier [Wang Yang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Yang_\(politician\) "Wang Yang (politician)"), Somalia's Foreign Minister [Fowziya Yusuf Haji Adan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fowziya_Yusuf_Haji_Adan "Fowziya Yusuf Haji Adan") announced that the Somali authorities looked forward to cooperation with the Chinese government in the energy, infrastructure, national security and agriculture sectors, among others. Wang also praised the traditional friendship between both nations and re-affirmed China's commitment to the Somali peace process.[^21]

In June 2014, during the Arab-China Summit in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing"), Somali Foreign Minister [Abdirahman Duale Beyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdirahman_Duale_Beyle "Abdirahman Duale Beyle") met with his Chinese counterpart [Wang Yi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Yi_\(politician\) "Wang Yi (politician)") to discuss bilateral cooperation between Somalia and China. The meeting was held at the Chinese foreign ministry center and focused on trade, security and reconstruction. Among the issues discussed were the various Chinese development projects that were in the process of being implemented in Somalia. Beyle also indicated that the Chinese authorities were slated to broaden their support for Somalia, which would serve to create new employment opportunities. Additionally, Wang commended the Somali federal government on its peace-building efforts. He likewise reaffirmed the historically close diplomatic ties between both territories, recalling China's recognition of the nascent [Somali Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somali_Republic "Somali Republic") in 1960 and Somalia's subsequent campaigning which helped China obtain a permanent seat in the [United Nations Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council").[^22] On 30 June 2014, Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesman Hong Lei announced that China would dispatch a diplomatic team on 1 July to reopen the Chinese embassy in Mogadishu. He described the move as both recognition that the Somali authorities were making progress in their national reconstruction efforts and a symbol of the importance that the Chinese government attaches to its bilateral relations with Somalia.[^23] On 3 July 2014, Chargé d'Affaires of the Embassy of the People's Republic of China [Wei Hongtian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wei_Hongtian "Wei Hongtian") presented his credentials to Foreign Minister of Somalia Abdirahman Duale Beyle at an event in the Somali capital. Beyle similarly hailed the appointment as a sign of the nation's strengthening security and foreign diplomatic relations.[^24] On 12 October 2014, the new Chinese embassy officially opened in Mogadishu.[^25]

#### 2020s

In July 2020, [Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somaliland "Somaliland") established relations with [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") which also included the opening of representative offices in their respective countries.[^26] [^27] The Chinese foreign ministry claimed that Taiwan aimed to ""undermine Somalia's sovereignty and territorial integrity" and it opposes the two entities establishing ties.[^26] [^27]

As a result, in April 2025, Somalia implemented a ban on the entry and transit of all [Taiwanese passport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_passport "Taiwan passport") holders, a move widely interpreted as aligning with the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") 's (PRC) foreign policy objectives. The decision, which took effect on 30 April, was communicated to [Taiwan's Ministry of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(Taiwan\) "Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Taiwan)") by Somalia's civil aviation authority and immediately drew protest from [Taipei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taipei "Taipei"), which accused [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") of pressuring [Mogadishu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mogadishu "Mogadishu"). Somalia justified the policy by invoking [United Nations General Assembly Resolution 2758](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_Resolution_2758_\(XXVI\) "United Nations General Assembly Resolution 2758 (XXVI)") (1971), affirming the PRC as the sole legitimate representative of China, and reiterated its adherence to the " [One China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China "One China") " principle. China welcomed the decision, with its [Ministry of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(China\) "Ministry of Foreign Affairs (China)") stating it "highly appreciates" Somalia's stance and reaffirming support for Mogadishu's claim over [Somaliland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somaliland "Somaliland").[^28]

## Sovereignty issues

Somalia follows the [one China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_principle "One China principle"). It recognizes the People's Republic of China as the sole government of China and [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") as an integral part of China's territory, and supports all efforts by the PRC to "achieve [national reunification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_unification "Chinese unification") ". It also considers [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong"), [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang") and [Tibet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region "Tibet Autonomous Region") to be China's internal affairs.[^29]

In July 2019, UN ambassadors of 37 countries, including Somalia, signed a joint letter to the [United Nations Human Rights Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council "United Nations Human Rights Council") defending [China's persecution of Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution_of_Uyghurs_in_China "Persecution of Uyghurs in China").[^30] [^31] Somalia was one of 16 countries that defended China in 2019 but did not do so in 2020.[^32] In June 2020, Somalia was one of 53 countries that backed the [Hong Kong national security law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Hong_Kong_national_security_law "2020 Hong Kong national security law") at the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations").[^33]

## Agreements

In July 2007, the Chinese state-owned oil company [CNOOC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNOOC "CNOOC") also signed an [oil exploration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_exploration_in_Puntland "Oil exploration in Puntland") agreement with the Somali government over the north-central [Mudug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mudug "Mudug") province, situated in the autonomous [Puntland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puntland "Puntland") region.[^34]

In September 2013, both governments signed an official cooperation agreement in Mogadishu as part of a five-year national recovery plan in Somalia. The pact will see the Chinese authorities reconstruct several major infrastructural landmarks in the Somali capital and elsewhere, including the National Theatre, a hospital, and the Mogadishu Stadium, as well as the road between [Galkayo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galkayo "Galkayo") and [Burao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burao "Burao") in northern Somalia. Additionally, Chinese ambassador Liu Guangyoun indicated that China would re-open its embassy in Mogadishu on land that had been donated for the purpose by the Somali government.[^35]

In April 2015, Foreign Minister of Somalia [Abdisalam Omer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdisalam_Omer "Abdisalam Omer") met in Mogadishu with Ambassador of China to Somalia Wei Hongtian to discuss ties between both nations. The meeting concluded with a signed bilateral agreement, which strengthens diplomatic relations and collaboration. As part of the treaty's stipulations, $13 million in Chinese funds will be earmarked for reconstruction and development initiatives in the economy, health, education and infrastructure sectors in Somalia. According to Ambassador Wei, the Chinese authorities are slated to continue their support for the Somali government's stabilizations efforts. Omer in turn commended the Chinese government for its sustained cooperation.[^36]
