Relations between [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam") and [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"): 中越关系, [pinyin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin "Pinyin"): *Zhōng Yuè Guān Xì*; [Vietnamese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_language "Vietnamese language"): *Quan hệ Việt–Trung*) have been extensive for millennia, with [Northern Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Vietnam "Northern Vietnam") especially under heavy Sinosphere influence during historical times. Despite their [Sinospheric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinosphere "Sinosphere") and [socialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialism "Socialism") background, centuries of [conquest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_domination_of_Vietnam "Chinese domination of Vietnam") by modern China's [imperial predecessors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_China#Imperial_China "History of China") as well as modern-day tensions have made relations occasionally wary.[^1] [^2] The modern relationship has been marked with extensive economic, political and cultural ties, as well as [territorial disputes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_disputes_in_the_South_China_Sea "Territorial disputes in the South China Sea") in the [South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Sea "South China Sea").

China and Vietnam have lengthy historical connections, including nearly a thousand years during which Vietnam was a dependency of China. Vietnam emerged from the disintegration of China's [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty") in the 900s. Subsequently, China supported the [Trần dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tr%E1%BA%A7n_dynasty "Trần dynasty") while the Ming dynasty established a tributary relationship with Vietnam. In 1884, after Chinese defeat in the [Sino-French War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-French_War "Sino-French War"), Vietnam under the [Nguyễn dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_dynasty "Nguyễn dynasty") became a protectorate of France, marking the end of formal Chinese influence on Vietnam. During the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), the People's Republic of China (PRC) ruled by the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) assisted [North Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Vietnam "North Vietnam") and the [Communist Party of Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Vietnam "Communist Party of Vietnam") (CPV) during the [Vietnam War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_War "Vietnam War") whilst the Taiwan-based Republic of China (ROC) [was allied with South Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_in_the_Vietnam_War "Republic of China in the Vietnam War").

Following the [fall of Saigon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_Saigon "Fall of Saigon") in 1975 and the subsequent Vietnamese reunification as the Socialist Republic in 1976, relations between the two countries started to deteriorate. Vietnam [ousted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodian%E2%80%93Vietnamese_War "Cambodian–Vietnamese War") the [Khmer Rouge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Rouge "Khmer Rouge"), a party that China propped up which [had become genocidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodian_genocide "Cambodian genocide"), from power in Cambodia. China invaded Vietnam in February 1979, beginning the [Sino-Vietnamese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Vietnamese_War "Sino-Vietnamese War"). China perceived Vietnam's domination over Indochina from Vietnam's historical legacy (emperor [Minh Mạng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minh_M%E1%BA%A1ng "Minh Mạng")) whilst Vietnam desired Vietnamese-friendly neighbors (Laos and Cambodia) on its immediate western borders. Cross-border raids and skirmishes ensued, in which China and Vietnam had a series of [border and naval clashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Vietnamese_conflicts_\(1979%E2%80%931991\) "Sino-Vietnamese conflicts (1979–1991)") from 1979 to 1990. The two countries officially normalized diplomatic ties in 1991.

China and Vietnam share [a 1,281 kilometres (796 miles) border](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Vietnam_border "China–Vietnam border"). The two countries remain in dispute over political and territorial issues in the South China Sea.[^3] Since the normalization of diplomatic ties, both sides have since worked to improve their diplomatic and economic relations. The two countries have been striving for restraint as well as present and future stability.[^4] [^5] The two countries signed a [comprehensive strategic partnership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_partnerships_of_China "Diplomatic partnerships of China") in 2008. China and Vietnam maintain extensive economic ties, with the Vietnamese economy becoming increasingly connected with China's. As two ruling [communist parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_party "Communist party") of [socialist states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_state_\(communism\) "Socialist state (communism)"), the CCP and CPV maintain close political and ideological ties.[^6]

## History

China and Vietnam have lengthy historical connections, including nearly a thousand years during which Vietnam was a dependency of China.[^7]<sup><span title="Page: 127">: 127</span> </sup> As a result, Vietnam was influenced by Chinese literature, scholarship, modes of family organization, civil administration, and bureaucratic practices.[^7]<sup><span title="Page: 127">: 127</span></sup>

### Early history

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Han_Expansion.png/250px-Han_Expansion.png)

Southward expansion of the Han dynasty, including its annexation of Northern Vietnam in 111 BC

The earliest reference of the proto-Vietnamese in Chinese annals was the *Lạc* (Chinese: Luo), *[Lạc Việt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%E1%BA%A1c_Vi%E1%BB%87t "Lạc Việt")*, or the [Dongsonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dongsonian "Dongsonian"),[^8] an ancient tribal confederacy of perhaps polyglot [Austroasiatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austroasiatic_language "Austroasiatic language") and [Kra-Dai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kra-Dai_language "Kra-Dai language") speakers who occupied the [Red River Delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_River_Delta "Red River Delta") in northern Vietnam.[^9] [^10] China and Vietnam had contact since the Chinese [Warring States period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warring_States_period "Warring States period") and the Vietnamese [Thục dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Th%E1%BB%A5c_dynasty "Thục dynasty") in the 3rd century BC (disputed), as noted in the 15th-century Vietnamese historical record *[Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%90%E1%BA%A1i_Vi%E1%BB%87t_s%E1%BB%AD_k%C3%BD_to%C3%A0n_th%C6%B0 "Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư")*. Between 111 BC and 939, Vietnam was subject to [three separate periods of imperial Chinese domination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_domination_of_Vietnam "Chinese domination of Vietnam") although it successfully asserted a degree of autonomy with the [Khúc clan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kh%C3%BAc_clan "Khúc clan") in 905 and later independence with the establishment of the [Ngô dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_dynasty "Ngô dynasty") in 939. In 968, [Đinh Bộ Lĩnh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%90inh_B%E1%BB%99_L%C4%A9nh "Đinh Bộ Lĩnh") declared himself emperor of the [Đinh dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%90inh_dynasty "Đinh dynasty"), however Vietnam nominally remained a vassal of China until the 19th century. Vietnam was again ruled by China under the [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") from 1407 until the establishment of the [Lê dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%AA_dynasty "Lê dynasty") in 1428.

According to the old Vietnamese historical records [Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%90%E1%BA%A1i_Vi%E1%BB%87t_s%E1%BB%AD_k%C3%BD_to%C3%A0n_th%C6%B0 "Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư") and [Khâm Định Việt Sử Thông Giám Cương Mục](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kh%C3%A2m_%C4%90%E1%BB%8Bnh_Vi%E1%BB%87t_S%E1%BB%AD_Th%C3%B4ng_Gi%C3%A1m_C%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_M%E1%BB%A5c "Khâm Định Việt Sử Thông Giám Cương Mục"), [An Dương Vương](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/An_D%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_V%C6%B0%C6%A1ng "An Dương Vương") (Thục Phán) was a prince of the Chinese state of [Shu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shu_\(state\) "Shu (state)") ([蜀](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%9C%80 "wikt:蜀"), which shares the same Chinese character as his surname Thục),[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 19">: 19</span> </sup> sent by his father first to explore what are now the southern Chinese provinces of [Guangxi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangxi "Guangxi") and [Yunnan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yunnan "Yunnan") and then to move their people to what is now northern Vietnam during the invasion of the [Qin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_dynasty "Qin dynasty").

Some modern Vietnamese scholars believe that [Thục Phán](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Th%E1%BB%A5c_Ph%C3%A1n "Thục Phán") came upon [Âu Việt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%82u_Vi%E1%BB%87t "Âu Việt"), which is now northernmost Vietnam, western [Guangdong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangdong "Guangdong"), and southern [Guangxi Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangxi "Guangxi"), with its capital in what is today [Cao Bằng Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_B%E1%BA%B1ng_Province "Cao Bằng Province")).[^12] After assembling an army, he defeated [Hùng Vương](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%B9ng_V%C6%B0%C6%A1ng "Hùng Vương") XVIII, the last ruler of the [Hồng Bàng dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%E1%BB%93ng_B%C3%A0ng_dynasty "Hồng Bàng dynasty"), in 258 BC. He proclaimed himself An Dương Vương ("King An Dương"), renamed his newly acquired state from [Văn Lang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V%C4%83n_Lang "Văn Lang") to [Âu Lạc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%82u_L%E1%BA%A1c "Âu Lạc") and established the new capital at [Phong Khê](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phong_Kh%C3%AA&action=edit&redlink=1 "Phong Khê (page does not exist)") (now [Phú Thọ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ph%C3%BA_Th%E1%BB%8D_\(town\) "Phú Thọ (town)"), a town in northern Vietnam), where he tried to build [Cổ Loa Citadel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%E1%BB%95_Loa_Citadel "Cổ Loa Citadel"), the spiral fortress approximately ten miles north of his new capital.

[Han Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_Chinese "Han Chinese") migration into Vietnam has been dated back to the era of the 2nd century BC, when [Qin Shi Huang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_Shi_Huang "Qin Shi Huang") first placed [northern Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Vietnam "Northern Vietnam") under Chinese rule (disputed), Chinese soldiers and fugitives from [Central China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_China "Central China") have migrated en masse into northern Vietnam since then and introduced Chinese influences into [Vietnamese culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Vietnam "Culture of Vietnam").[^13] [^14] The Chinese military leader [Zhao Tuo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhao_Tuo "Zhao Tuo") founded the [Triệu dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tri%E1%BB%87u_dynasty "Triệu dynasty"), which ruled [Nanyue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanyue "Nanyue") in southern China and northern Vietnam. The Qin governor of Canton advised Zhao to found his own independent kingdom since the area was remote, and there were many Chinese settlers in the area.[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 23">: 23</span> </sup> Under the [Eastern Han dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Han_dynasty "Eastern Han dynasty"), the Chinese prefect of [Jiaozhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiaozhi_Province "Jiaozhi Province"), [Shi Xie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shi_Xie "Shi Xie"), ruled Vietnam as an autonomous warlord and was posthumously deified by later Vietnamese emperors. Shi Xie was the leader of the elite ruling class of [Han Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_Chinese "Han Chinese") families that immigrated to Vietnam and played a major role in developing [Vietnam's culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Vietnam "Culture of Vietnam").[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 70">: 70</span></sup>

### Imperial period

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Chinese_invasion_of_vietnam_1406-07.jpg/250px-Chinese_invasion_of_vietnam_1406-07.jpg)

Ming conquest of Vietnam in 1406–1407

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/%E6%B8%85%E4%BA%BA%E7%94%BB%E5%B9%B3%E5%AE%9A%E5%AE%89%E5%8D%97%E6%88%98%E5%9B%BE%E5%86%8C.png/250px-%E6%B8%85%E4%BA%BA%E7%94%BB%E5%B9%B3%E5%AE%9A%E5%AE%89%E5%8D%97%E6%88%98%E5%9B%BE%E5%86%8C.png)

Qing invasion of northern Vietnam in 1788–1789

Vietnam emerged from the disintegration of China's [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty") in the early 900s.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 49">: 49</span> </sup> The border between China and Vietnam was generally stable for the next 800 years, with China challenging the border once.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 49">: 49</span></sup>

China invested the [Trần dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tr%E1%BA%A7n_dynasty "Trần dynasty") (1225–1400) as Vietnam's rulers.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 50">: 50</span> </sup> The Trần's control of Vietnam weakened in the 1390s and in 1400, [Hồ Quý Ly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%E1%BB%93_Qu%C3%BD_Ly "Hồ Quý Ly") deposed the Trần ruler and declared himself the founder of a new dynasty.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 50">: 50</span> </sup> The Trần requested that China's [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") intervene on its behalf.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 50">: 50</span> </sup> The Ming sent troops and envoy to re-establish the Trần and quickly defeated Hồ.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 50">: 50</span> </sup> The Ming subsequently deemed the Trần to be in disarray and instead reclaimed Vietnam as its own territory.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 50">: 50</span> </sup> Subsequent Ming emperors returned the relationship to a tributary with a Vietnamese ruler in Vietnam.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 50">: 50</span></sup>

According to a 2018 study in the *[Journal of Conflict Resolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journal_of_Conflict_Resolution "Journal of Conflict Resolution")* on Vietnam-China relations from 1365 to 1841, they could be characterized as a "hierarchic [tributary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tributary_system_of_China "Tributary system of China") " from 1365 to 1841.[^16] During Vietnam's [Đại Việt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%90%E1%BA%A1i_Vi%E1%BB%87t "Đại Việt") period (968–1804), the Vietnamese court explicitly recognized its unequal status compared to China through explicit institutional mechanisms.[^16] [^15]<sup><span title="Page: 55">: 55</span> </sup> Vietnam conducted its relations with China through the tributary system.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 55">: 55</span> </sup> During this period, Vietnam was primarily [focused](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nam_ti%E1%BA%BFn "Nam tiến") on addressing its domestic instability and its external relations generally focused to the south and west,[^16] including with the [Champa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Champa "Champa") kingdom, among others.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 55">: 55</span></sup>

In 1884, during Vietnam's [Nguyễn dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_dynasty "Nguyễn dynasty"), the [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") and [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Third_Republic "French Third Republic") fought the [Sino-French War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-French_War "Sino-French War"), which ended in a Chinese defeat. The [Treaty of Tientsin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Tientsin_\(1885\) "Treaty of Tientsin (1885)") recognized French dominance in Vietnam and the [protectorate treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Hu%E1%BA%BF_\(1884\) "Treaty of Huế (1884)") France signed with Vietnam, spelling the end of formal Chinese influence on Vietnam and the beginning of Vietnam's [French colonial period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Indochina "French Indochina").

Both China and Vietnam faced invasion and occupation by [Imperial Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Japan "Imperial Japan") during [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), and Vietnam languished under the rule of [Vichy France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vichy_France "Vichy France"). In the Chinese provinces of [Guangxi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangxi "Guangxi") and [Guangdong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangdong "Guangdong"), Vietnamese revolutionaries, led by [Phan Bội Châu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phan_B%E1%BB%99i_Ch%C3%A2u "Phan Bội Châu"), had arranged alliances with the Chinese Nationalists, the [Kuomintang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuomintang "Kuomintang"), before the war by marrying [Vietnamese women](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_Vietnam "Women in Vietnam") to Chinese [National Revolutionary Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Revolutionary_Army "National Revolutionary Army") officers. Their children were at an advantage since they could speak both languages and so they worked as agents for the revolutionaries, spreading their ideologies across borders. The [intermarriage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interracial_marriage "Interracial marriage") between Chinese and Vietnamese was viewed with alarm by the French. Chinese merchants also married Vietnamese women and provided funds and help for revolutionary agents.[^17]

Late in the war, with Japan and [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") nearing defeat, US President [Franklin Roosevelt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franklin_Roosevelt "Franklin Roosevelt") privately decided that the French should not return in Indochina after the war was over. Roosevelt offered the Kuomintang leader, [Chiang Kai-shek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek "Chiang Kai-shek"), all of Indochina to be under Chinese rule, but Chiang Kai-shek reportedly replied, "Under no circumstances!" [^18] In August 1943, China broke diplomatic relations with Vichy France, with the *[Central Daily News](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Daily_News "Central Daily News")* announcing that diplomatic relations were to be solely between the Chinese and the Vietnamese, with no French intermediary. China had planned to spread massive propaganda on the [Atlantic Charter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Charter "Atlantic Charter") and Roosevelt's statement on Vietnamese [self-determination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-determination "Self-determination") to undermine the French authority in Indochina.[^19]

### World War II and aftermath

The Chinese [Kuomintang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuomintang "Kuomintang") provided support to both the [Việt Nam Cách mệnh Đồng minh Hội](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vi%E1%BB%87t_Nam_C%C3%A1ch_m%E1%BB%87nh_%C4%90%E1%BB%93ng_minh_H%E1%BB%99i "Việt Nam Cách mệnh Đồng minh Hội") (Viet Cach) and the [Việt Nam Độc lập Đồng minh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vi%E1%BB%87t_Nam_%C4%90%E1%BB%99c_l%E1%BA%ADp_%C4%90%E1%BB%93ng_minh "Việt Nam Độc lập Đồng minh") (Viet Minh) in their struggle against Japanese forces.

After the Second World War ended, a [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") [mandate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandate_\(politics\) "Mandate (politics)"), had 200,000 Chinese troops, led by General [Lu Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lu_Han_\(general\) "Lu Han (general)"), sent by [Chiang Kai-shek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek "Chiang Kai-shek") to [Indochina north of](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_occupation_of_northern_Vietnam,_1945%E2%80%931946 "Chinese occupation of northern Vietnam, 1945–1946") the [16th parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16th_parallel_north "16th parallel north") to accept the surrender of Japanese occupying forces. The troops remained in Indochina until June 1946.[^20] The Chinese supported the [Việt Nam Quốc Dân Đảng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vi%E1%BB%87t_Nam_Qu%E1%BB%91c_D%C3%A2n_%C4%90%E1%BA%A3ng "Việt Nam Quốc Dân Đảng") and Viet Cach to put pressure on the communist-led [Viet Minh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viet_Minh "Viet Minh").[^21] Nevertheless, Chinese occupational forces allowed [Ho Chi Minh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh "Ho Chi Minh") 's [Democratic Republic of Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Vietnam "North Vietnam") (established in 1945) more influence than their British counterparts in the south.[^22] Chiang Kai-shek threatened the French with war to force them to negotiate with Ho Chi Minh. In February 1946, Chiang forced the French colonists to surrender all of their [concessions in China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concessions_in_China "Concessions in China") and to renounce their [extraterritorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterritoriality "Extraterritoriality") privileges in exchange for withdrawing from northern Indochina and for allowing French troops to reoccupy the region.[^23] [^24] [^25] [^26]

### Cold War

#### First Indochina War and Vietnam War

Along with the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"), [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") was an important strategic ally of Vietnamese communists ([Democratic Republic of Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Vietnam "Democratic Republic of Vietnam")) during the [First Indochina War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Indochina_War "First Indochina War") and [Vietnam War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_War "Vietnam War"). The [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") provided arms, military training and essential supplies to help the Vietnamese communists against the [French Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Union "French Union"), the anticommunist [Republic of Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Vietnam "Republic of Vietnam"), their ally the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), and other anti-communists between 1950 and 1975.[^27] As for the Chinese communists, in the face of the fact that this force was waging a civil war against the Kuomintang's government, the Vietnamese communists supported them with shelter and some necessities. In mid-1949, the Vietnamese communists even sent troops to coordinate with the Chinese communists to expand the control area in the Sino-Vietnamese border area. The efforts of the [Vietnamese communists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Vietnam "Communist Party of Vietnam"), operating under the cover of the [Việt Minh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viet_Minh "Viet Minh") front, were noticed and secretly supported by the Soviet Union and the Chinese communists before 1950. In 1947, the Chinese communists helped the Vietnamese communists buy weapons in Thailand. After Mao Zedong founded the People's Republic of China on October 1, 1949, Ho Chi Minh immediately sent two groups to China simultaneously to ask for help on October 6. In November 1949, at the World Trade Union Congress held in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing"), Communist China publicly announced its intention when it announced that it would actively support the revolutionary movement in Indochina. During Mao's December 1949 visit to the Soviet Union, Stalin sought Mao's assistance in supporting the Vietnamese Communists against France in the First Indochina War.[^28] [^29]<sup><span title="Page: 65">: 65</span> </sup> [^30] Mao accepted Stalin's view of a "worldwide communist revolution" and agreed to share "the international responsibility" and support the Vietnamese communists.[^29]<sup><span title="Page: 65">: 65</span> </sup> After Mao's return to China, the country began sending military advisors and military aid to the Vietnamese.[^29]<sup><span title="Page: 66">: 66</span> </sup> In January 1950, China and the Soviet Union recognized the government of the Vietnamese communists and formed the basis of relations changed from party-to-party to state-to-state.[^29]<sup><span title="Page: 101">: 101</span> </sup> In April 1951, communist China raised relations with the Vietnamese communists to the ambassadorial level. Meanwhile, the French-associated [State of Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_of_Vietnam "State of Vietnam") was established in June 1949 and later established diplomatic relations with the Republic of China, commonly known as [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan").[^28] [^31]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Georgi-Malenkov-Ho-Chi-Minh-Mao.jpg/250px-Georgi-Malenkov-Ho-Chi-Minh-Mao.jpg)

Postage stamp of Ho Chi Minh with Mao Zedong and Georgy Malenkov in 1954.

China provided enormous and important support to the Viet Minh both civilly and militarily during the war against France. However, during the [1954 Geneva Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1954_Geneva_Conference "1954 Geneva Conference") ending the [First Indochina War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Indochina_War "First Indochina War"), Chinese premier [Zhou Enlai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Enlai "Zhou Enlai") urged the Viet Minh delegation to accept partition at the [17th parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_Demilitarized_Zone "Vietnamese Demilitarized Zone") with the communists ruling the North and pro-French government keeping the South, which was regarded as a betrayal.[^32]

During 1964 to 1969, China reportedly sent over 300,000 troops, mostly in anti-aircraft divisions to combat in Vietnam.[^33] However, the Vietnamese communists remained suspicious of China's perceived attempts to increase its influence over Vietnam.[^34] From 1960 to 1978, China sent aid $20 billion worth of aid to Vietnam. The aid was not a loan and no repayment was asked.[^35]<sup><span title="Page: 277">: 277</span></sup>

In 1960, China became the first country to recognize the National Liberation Front (the [Viet Cong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viet_Cong "Viet Cong")) in Vietnam.[^36] Ho Chi Minh and Mao Zedong frequently characterized the bilateral relationship as "comrade plus brother".[^37]<sup><span title="Pages: 96–97">: 96–97</span> </sup> In 1963, [Liu Shaoqi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liu_Shaoqi "Liu Shaoqi") praised the strength of the relationship, stating, "Our friendship has a long history. It is a militant friendship, forged in the storm of revolution, a great class friendship that is [proletarian internationalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proletarian_internationalism "Proletarian internationalism") in character, a friendship that is indestructible." [^37]<sup><span title="Page: 97">: 97</span></sup>

Vietnam was an ideological battleground during the 1960s [Sino-Soviet split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split "Sino-Soviet split"). After the 1964 [Gulf of Tonkin incident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Tonkin_incident "Gulf of Tonkin incident"), Chinese premier [Deng Xiaoping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping "Deng Xiaoping") secretly promised the North Vietnamese 1 billion [yuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_yuan "Chinese yuan") in military and economic aid if they refused all Soviet aid.

In response to U.S. bombing of North Vietnam, China launched the Resist America, Aid Vietnam campaign.[^38]<sup><span title="Page: 29">: 29</span> </sup> The campaign themes denounced U.S. imperialism and promoted Vietnamese resistance.[^38]<sup><span title="Page: 29">: 29</span> </sup> Local [communist party cadre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cadre_system_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Cadre system of the Chinese Communist Party") organized mass meetings and street demonstrations, and millions of people across the country marched in China to support the campaign between February 9 and February 11, 1965.[^38]<sup><span title="Page: 29">: 29</span> </sup> The communist party also expanded the campaign into cultural media such as [film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cinema_of_China "Cinema of China") and photography exhibitions, singing contests, and street performances.[^38]<sup><span title="Page: 29">: 29</span></sup>

During the Vietnam War, the North Vietnamese and the Chinese had agreed to defer tackling their territorial issues until South Vietnam was defeated. Those issues included the lack of delineation of Vietnam's [territorial waters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_waters "Territorial waters") in the [Gulf of Tonkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Tonkin "Gulf of Tonkin") and the question of sovereignty over the [Paracel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracel_Islands "Paracel Islands") and [Spratly Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spratly_Islands "Spratly Islands") in the [South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Sea "South China Sea").[^34] During the 1950s, half of the Paracels were controlled by China and the rest by South Vietnam. In 1958, North Vietnam accepted China's claim to the Paracels and relinquished its own claim;[^39] one year earlier, China had ceded [White Dragon Tail Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Dragon_Tail_Island "White Dragon Tail Island") to North Vietnam.[^40] The potential of offshore oil deposits in the [Gulf of Tonkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Tonkin "Gulf of Tonkin") heightened tensions between China and South Vietnam.

Vietnam disapproved of China's efforts to improve [relations with the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93United_States_relations "China–United States relations").[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 93">: 93</span> </sup> Prime Minister [Phạm Văn Đồng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ph%E1%BA%A1m_V%C4%83n_%C4%90%E1%BB%93ng "Phạm Văn Đồng") asked [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") to cancel the [1972 visit of United States President Richard Nixon to China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1972_visit_by_Richard_Nixon_to_China "1972 visit by Richard Nixon to China"), but Mao declined.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 93">: 93</span></sup>

In 1973, with the Vietnam War drawing to a close, North Vietnam announced its intention to allow foreign companies to explore oil deposits in disputed waters. In January 1974, a [clash between Chinese and South Vietnamese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Paracel_Islands "Battle of the Paracel Islands") forces resulted in China taking complete control of the Paracels, leading to South Vietnam having to strengthen its defense in the Spratly Islands.[^34] When South Vietnam nearly [fell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_Saigon "Fall of Saigon") in 1975, North Vietnam [took over](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Sea_Campaign "East Sea Campaign") the South Vietnamese-controlled portions of the Spratly Islands.[^34] The unified Vietnam then canceled its earlier renunciation of its claim to the Paracels, and both China and Vietnam claim control over all the Spratlys and actually control some of the islands.[^39]

1970s

By the mid-1970s, the relationship between China and Vietnam was strained.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 93">: 93</span> </sup> The tensions between the two countries developed in relation to a number of issues, including Vietnam's support of the Soviet side during the Sino-Soviet split, Vietnam's invasion of Cambodia, Vietnam's mistreatment of ethnic Chinese in Vietnam, and border conflicts.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 43">: 43</span></sup>

Tensions were heightened in the 1970s by the Vietnamese government's oppression of the [Hoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hoa_people "Hoa people") minority (Vietnamese of Chinese ethnicity [^34] [^41]). In February 1976, Vietnam implemented registration programs in the south.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 94">: 94</span> </sup> Ethnic Chinese in Vietnam were required to adopt Vietnamese citizenship or leave the country.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 94">: 94</span> </sup> In early 1977, Vietnam implemented what it described as a purification policy in its border areas to keep Chinese border residents to the Chinese side of the border.[^37]<sup><span title="Pages: 94–95">: 94–95</span> </sup> Following another discriminatory policy introduced in March 1978, a large number of Chinese fled from Vietnam to southern China.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 95">: 95</span> </sup> China and Vietnam attempted to negotiate issues related to Vietnam's treatment of ethnic Chinese, but these negotiations failed to resolve the issues.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 95">: 95</span></sup>

China's support of [Angkar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Kampuchea "Communist Party of Kampuchea") and the [Khmer Rouge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Rouge "Khmer Rouge") in [Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1978_in_Cambodia "1978 in Cambodia") concerned Vietnamese leadership, which feared encirclement by China.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 94">: 94</span> </sup> The 1977 [Cambodian–Vietnamese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodian%E2%80%93Vietnamese_War "Cambodian–Vietnamese War") caused tensions with China, which had allied itself with [Democratic Kampuchea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Kampuchea "Democratic Kampuchea") that was founded by the [Maoist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maoism "Maoism") leader [Pol Pot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pol_Pot "Pol Pot") (*Saloth Sâr*).[^34] [^41]

In June 1978, China rescinded the appointment of its [consul general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consul_\(representative\) "Consul (representative)") to [Ho Chi Minh City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_City "Ho Chi Minh City") and informed Vietnam that it must close three of its consulates in China.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 96">: 96</span> </sup> By the end of July 1978, China ended all of its aid programs to Vietnam and recalled all of its experts from Vietnam.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 96">: 96</span></sup>

#### Third Indochina War

Vietnam had signed a treaty of friendship with the Soviet Union and established extensive commercial and military ties.[^34] [^41]

On February 17, 1979, the Chinese [People's Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") crossed the Vietnamese border but withdrew on March 5, after a two-week campaign had devastated northern Vietnam and briefly threatened the Vietnamese capital, [Hanoi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanoi "Hanoi").[^34] Both sides suffered relatively heavy losses, with thousands of casualties. Subsequent peace talks broke down in December 1979, and China and Vietnam began a major buildup of forces along the border. Vietnam fortified its border towns and districts and stationed as many as 600,000 troops. China stationed 400,000 troops on its side of the border. Sporadic fighting on the border occurred throughout the 1980s, and China threatened to launch another attack to force Vietnam's exit from Cambodia.[^34]

### 1990–present

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Xi_jinping_and_Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Ph%C3%BA_Tr%E1%BB%8Dng.jpg/250px-Xi_jinping_and_Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Ph%C3%BA_Tr%E1%BB%8Dng.jpg)

Vietnamese general secretary Nguyễn Phú Trọng with Chinese general secretary Xi Jinping in November 2022

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Xi_Jinping_and_Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Ph%C3%BA_Tr%E1%BB%8Dng_2023.jpg/250px-Xi_Jinping_and_Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Ph%C3%BA_Tr%E1%BB%8Dng_2023.jpg)

General secretary Nguyễn Phú Trọng and General secretary Xi Jinping in December 2023

With the 1991 [dissolution of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union "Dissolution of the Soviet Union") and Vietnam's 1990 exit from Cambodia, China–Vietnam ties began to improve. Both nations planned the normalization of their relations in a secret summit in [Chengdu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chengdu "Chengdu") in September 1990, and officially normalized ties on 5 November 1991.[^41] [^42] Since 1991, the leaders and high-ranking officials of both nations have exchanged visits. China and Vietnam both recognized and supported the post-1991 government of [Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia "Cambodia"), and supported each other's bid to join the [World Trade Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Trade_Organization "World Trade Organization") (WTO).[^41]

In their contemporary diplomatic framework, China and Vietnam regularly reference the Sixteen Word Guideline and the Four Good Guiding Spirits.[^7]<sup><span title="Page: 125">: 125</span> </sup> The Sixteen Word Guideline includes principles such as "Friendly neighbourliness, comprehensive cooperation, long-term stability, and future-oriented thinking".[^7]<sup><span title="Page: 138">: 138</span> </sup> The Four Good Guiding Spirits are "Good neighbours, good friends, good comrades, and good partners".[^7]<sup><span title="Page: 138">: 138</span></sup>

As part of efforts to develop border trade between the two countries, [People's Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") (PLA) soldiers began large scale [mine-clearing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demining "Demining") operations along the border in 1993.[^29]<sup><span title="Page: 193">: 193</span></sup>

In 1999, the [General Secretary of the Communist Party of Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam "General Secretary of the Communist Party of Vietnam"), [Lê Khả Phiêu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%AA_Kh%E1%BA%A3_Phi%C3%AAu "Lê Khả Phiêu"), visited [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing"), where he met [General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") [Jiang Zemin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiang_Zemin "Jiang Zemin") and announced a joint [16 Word Guideline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16_Word_Guideline "16 Word Guideline") for improved bilateral relations; a Joint Statement for Comprehensive Cooperation was issued in 2000.[^41] In 2000, Vietnam and China successfully resolved longstanding disputes over their land border and maritime rights in the [Gulf of Tonkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Tonkin "Gulf of Tonkin"), including the cession of land surrounding the [Friendship Pass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friendship_Pass "Friendship Pass") to China.[^27] [^41] [^43] Phiêu delayed the signing of Vietnam's first bilateral trade treaty with the United States because he was concerned about the potential Chinese response.[^7]<sup><span title="Page: 138">: 138</span></sup>

#### 2000s

A joint agreement between China and [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN") in 2002 marked out a process of peaceful resolution and guarantees against armed conflict.[^41] In 2002, Jiang Zemin made an official visit to Vietnam in which numerous agreements were signed to expand trade and co-operation and to resolve outstanding disputes.[^27]

China and Vietnam signed a [comprehensive strategic partnership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comprehensive_strategic_partnerships_of_Vietnam "Comprehensive strategic partnerships of Vietnam") in 2008.[^44]<sup><span title="Page: 14">: 14</span> </sup> In 2009, they signed a protocol on science and technology cooperation.[^45]<sup><span title="Page: 158">: 158</span> </sup> In November 2009, the two countries signed a land boundary demarcation agreement on their land borders.[^45]<sup><span title="Page: 158">: 158</span></sup>

#### 2010s

In October 2011, CPV general secretary [Nguyễn Phú Trọng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Ph%C3%BA_Tr%E1%BB%8Dng "Nguyễn Phú Trọng") made an official visit to China at the invitation of CCP general secretary [Hu Jintao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hu_Jintao "Hu Jintao") with the aim of improving relations in the wake of the border disputes.[^46]

On 1 December 2015, Vietnam and China signed an agreement to resolve border marker problems and to enhance cooperation in protecting and managing border markers.[^45]<sup><span title="Page: 167">: 167</span></sup>

#### 2020s

In 2020, for the celebration of Vietnam's 75th [National Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Day_\(Vietnam\) "National Day (Vietnam)"), CCP general secretary [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") and CPV general secretary Nguyễn Phú Trọng reaffirmed their bilateral ties while looking back saying: "In the past 70 years, although there have been some ups and downs in bilateral relations, friendship and cooperation had always been the main flow." [^47] Nguyễn Phú Trọng visited China in 2022 where he met Xi, becoming the first foreign leader to meet Xi after he secured a third term in the [20th CCP National Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/20th_National_Congress_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "20th National Congress of the Chinese Communist Party").[^48] Both leaders released a joint statement, calling for cooperation in economic, political, defense and security areas and working together in "the fight against terrorism, 'peaceful evolution', 'colour revolution' and the politicisation of human rights issues".[^48]

Vietnamese prime minister [Phạm Minh Chính](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ph%E1%BA%A1m_Minh_Ch%C3%ADnh "Phạm Minh Chính") visited China in June 2023 to attend a summit of the [World Economic Forum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Economic_Forum "World Economic Forum"). While in China, he met with Xi, Chinese premier [Li Qiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_Qiang "Li Qiang"),[^49] chairman of the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress [Zhao Leji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhao_Leji "Zhao Leji"),[^50] and chairman of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference [Wang Huning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Huning "Wang Huning").[^51] Phạm additionally visited [Xiong'an New Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiong%27an "Xiong'an"), a key project of Xi. In December 2023, Xi visited Vietnam, where both countries upgraded their relationship to a " [community with *a* shared future of humanity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_of_Common_Destiny "Community of Common Destiny") ", also translated as "community of common destiny for mankind" by Vietnam.[^52]

In August 2024, CPV general secretary [Tô Lâm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C3%B4_L%C3%A2m "Tô Lâm") visited China, meeting [Wang Huning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Huning "Wang Huning"), a member of the [CCP Politburo Standing Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Politburo Standing Committee of the Chinese Communist Party"), on August 19, 2024. They discussed the strategic plans established by their leaders to enhance the China–Vietnam relationship and promote their shared future. Both parties reaffirmed their commitment to advancing cooperation between their countries. The meeting also included Hu Chunhua and Wang Dongfeng.[^53] While in Beijing, Tô Lâm visited the [Chairman Mao Memorial Hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_Mao_Memorial_Hall "Chairman Mao Memorial Hall"), and visited several former headquarters, bases, and memorials of the Vietnamese communists in [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou "Guangzhou").[^52] On 13 October 2024, Vietnam and China signed 10 agreements to boost cross-border rail links, payment systems, and economic cooperation, while also enhancing defense and trade relations.[^54]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Xi_Jinping_and_To_Lam_meeting_each_other_in_Moscow_%282025%29.jpg/250px-Xi_Jinping_and_To_Lam_meeting_each_other_in_Moscow_%282025%29.jpg)

Chinese general secretary Xi Jinping meets Vietnamese general secretary Tô Lâm in Moscow (2025)

In April 2025, CCP General Secretary Xi Jinping [visited Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_visits_by_Xi_Jinping_to_Vietnam,_Malaysia_and_Cambodia "State visits by Xi Jinping to Vietnam, Malaysia and Cambodia") to reaffirm bilateral relations and to urge Vietnam to oppose "unilateral bullying" in response to US President [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump") 's [Liberation Day tariffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberation_Day_tariffs "Liberation Day tariffs"). Xi also met with General Secretary Tô Lâm and Vietnamese Prime Minister Phạm Minh Chính.[^55] During the visit, the two countries signed 45 agreements aimed at strengthening bilateral relations. These agreements covered a range of sectors, including [supply chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supply_chain "Supply chain") integration, [artificial intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_intelligence "Artificial intelligence"), joint maritime patrols, and railway infrastructure development.[^56] On 30 April 2025, the 50th anniversary of the end of the Vietnam War, the PLA for the first time joined armies of Laos and Cambodia as part of Vietnam's military parade for the occasion. Vietnamese state media also disclosed for the first time that over 300,000 Chinese military personnel in North Vietnam provided assistance during the Vietnam War. On 2 September 2025, at the 80th [National Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Day_\(Vietnam\) "National Day (Vietnam)") celebrations of Vietnam, the PLA joined the armies of Russia, Laos, and Cambodia in Vietnam's military parade to commemorate the day.[^52]

In early 2026, Vietnam protested China's land reclamation and construction at Antelope Reef in the disputed [Paracel Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracel_Islands "Paracel Islands"), where dredging and infrastructure, including plans for an airstrip and maritime facilities, could make it China's largest outpost in the South China Sea. The expansion raised regional security concerns, while China maintained its claim over the islands, reflecting ongoing tensions in the South China Sea with overlapping territorial and maritime claims.[^57]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/T%C3%B4_L%C3%A2m_and_Ng%C3%B4_Ph%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_Ly_arrived_at_Nanning_East_railway_station-20260416.png/250px-T%C3%B4_L%C3%A2m_and_Ng%C3%B4_Ph%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_Ly_arrived_at_Nanning_East_railway_station-20260416.png)

Tô Lâm and Ngô Phương Ly arrived at Nanning, Guangxi in April 2026

In April 2026, Tô Lâm visited China, which is the first overseas trip after he appointed as the Vietnamese president. The analysis said Lâm's visit will strengthen the China–Vietnam relationship.[^58] [^59] While meeting Lâm, Xi Jinping said that "Safeguarding the socialist system and the ruling status of the Communist Party constitutes the largest shared strategic interest of the two parties of China and Vietnam".[^60]

## Economic relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Belt_and_Road_Initiative_participant_map.svg/250px-Belt_and_Road_Initiative_participant_map.svg.png)

Countries that have signed co-operation agreements related to the Belt and Road Initiative

As of at least 2024, the Vietnamese economy is becoming increasingly connected with China's.[^61]<sup><span title="Page: 102">: 102</span> </sup> Vietnam is China's eighth largest trading partner, and largest among southeast Asian countries.[^61]<sup><span title="Page: 102">: 102</span> </sup> After both sides resumed trade links in 1991, growth in annual bilateral trade increased from only US$32 million in 1991 to almost US$7.2 billion in 2004.[^62] By 2011, the trade volume had reached US$25 billion.[^46] In 2019, the total value of trade between the two countries amounted to US$224 billion.[^63] As of 2024, China accounted for 18.2% of the total exports values of Vietnam and 37.8% of imports of Vietnam.[^64]

Vietnam's exports to China include [crude oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crude_oil "Crude oil"), [coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal "Coal"), [coffee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee_production_in_Vietnam "Coffee production in Vietnam"), and food, and China exports [pharmaceuticals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry_in_China "Pharmaceutical industry in China"), [machinery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_industry "Machine industry"), [petroleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum_industry_in_China "Petroleum industry in China"), [fertilizers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertilizers "Fertilizers"), and [automobile parts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_auto_parts "List of auto parts") to Vietnam. Both nations are working to establish an "economic corridor" from China's [Yunnan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yunnan "Yunnan") Province to Vietnam's northern provinces and cities and similar economic zones linking China's [Guangxi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangxi "Guangxi") Province with Vietnam's [Lạng Sơn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%E1%BA%A1ng_S%C6%A1n_Province "Lạng Sơn Province") and [Quang Ninh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qu%E1%BA%A3ng_Ninh_Province "Quảng Ninh Province") Provinces, and the cities of [Hanoi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanoi "Hanoi") and [Haiphong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haiphong "Haiphong").[^62] Air and sea links and a railway line have been opened between the countries, along with national-level seaports in the frontier provinces and regions of the two countries.[^27] Joint ventures have furthermore been launched, such as the Thai Nguyen Steel Complex,[^62] but the deal eventually fell through, resulting in the bankruptcy of state-owned Thai Nguyen Iron and Steel VSC and the withdrawal of the [China Metallurgical Group Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Metallurgical_Group_Corporation "China Metallurgical Group Corporation") from the project.[^65] Vietnam became able to export [durian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durian "Durian") to China in 2022.[^66]

Chinese investments in Vietnam have been rising since 2015, reaching US$2.17 billion in 2017.[^67] In 2018, protesters went on the streets in Vietnam against government plans to open new [special economic zones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_economic_zones "Special economic zones"), including one in [Quang Ninh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quang_Ninh "Quang Ninh"), near the Chinese border, which would allow 99-year land leases, citing concerns about Chinese dominance.[^68] [^69] In July 2025, China criticized a trade agreement that Vietnam reached with the United States, which imposed a tariff on the [transshipment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transshipment "Transshipment") of non-Vietnamese good via Vietnam.[^70] [^71]

## Military relations

In 2014, China and Vietnam reached an agreement on holding annual border defense friendly exchange activities. In March of the same year, China's Guangxi Zhuang Autonomous Region and Vietnam's Quang Ninh Province held the first Sino-Vietnamese border defense friendly exchange activity, which was co-chaired by the defense ministers of the two countries. Since then, the two sides have taken turns to hold this activity every year. As of July 2025, it has been held nine times. In July 2025, the Ministry of National Defense of China announced that it would hold the "Walking Hand in Hand-2025" joint army training with Vietnam in Guangxi in mid-to-late July. This activity will be the first joint army training between China and Vietnam. China stated that this joint training will be based on the theme of "joint duty training in border areas" to strengthen mutual learning and mutual reference of border duty experience and further deepen practical cooperation between the two militaries.[^72]

## Territory issues

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Qu%C3%A2n_%C4%91%E1%BB%99i_duy%E1%BB%87t_binh_%E1%BB%9F_Tr%C6%B0%E1%BB%9Dng_Sa.JPG/250px-Qu%C3%A2n_%C4%91%E1%BB%99i_duy%E1%BB%87t_binh_%E1%BB%9F_Tr%C6%B0%E1%BB%9Dng_Sa.JPG)

Vietnamese troops on Spratly Island in 2009.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/South_China_Sea_claims_map.svg/250px-South_China_Sea_claims_map.svg.png)

Territorial claims in the South China Sea

The land border of China and Vietnam is 1,347 kilometers.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 95">: 95</span> </sup> Two Chinese provinces adjoin the border, and seven Vietnamese provinces do.[^37]<sup><span title="Pages: 94–95">: 94–95</span></sup>

Border disputes between the two countries were significant in the 1970s.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 95">: 95</span> </sup> One hundred sixty-four locations on the land border totaling 227 square kilometers were disputed.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 95">: 95</span> </sup> Because there was not yet clear border demarcation, the countries engaged in a pattern of retaliatory land grabs and violence.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 95">: 95</span> </sup> The number of border skirmishes increased yearly from 125 in 1974 to 2,175 in 1978.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 95">: 95</span></sup>

The two countries attempted a first round of negotiations to resolve land border issues, but were not successful.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 95">: 95</span> </sup> A second round of negotiations in August 1978 was also unsuccessful because of the [Youyi Pass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friendship_Pass "Friendship Pass") Incident in which the Vietnamese army and police expelled 2,500 refugees across the border into China.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 95">: 95</span> </sup> Vietnamese authorities beat and stabbed refugees during the incident, as well as 9 Chinese civilian border workers.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 95">: 95</span> </sup> Following this event, Vietnam occupied the Pu Nian Ling area, which China also claimed.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 95">: 95</span></sup>

In June 2011, Vietnam announced that its military would conduct new exercises in the [South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Sea "South China Sea"). China had previously voiced its disagreement over Vietnamese oil exploration in the area, stating that the [Spratly Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spratly_Islands "Spratly Islands") and the surrounding waters were its sovereign territory.[^73] Defense of the South China Sea was cited as one of the possible missions of the first Chinese [PLA Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army_Navy "People's Liberation Army Navy") aircraft carrier, the *[Liaoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_aircraft_carrier_Liaoning "Chinese aircraft carrier Liaoning")*, which entered service in September 2012.[^74]

On 21 June 2012, Vietnam passed a law entitled the "Law on the Sea", which placed both the [Spratly Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spratly_Islands "Spratly Islands") and the [Paracel Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracel_Islands "Paracel Islands") under Vietnamese jurisdiction, prompting China to label the move as "illegal and invalid." [^75] [^37]<sup><span title="Page: 228">: 228</span> </sup> A month afterwards, China enacted a previously delayed plan established [prefecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prefecture "Prefecture") of [Sansha City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sansha_City "Sansha City"),[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 228">: 228</span> </sup> which encompassed the [Xisha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracel_Islands "Paracel Islands") (Paracel), [Zhongsha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macclesfield_Bank "Macclesfield Bank"), and [Nansha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spratly_Islands "Spratly Islands") (Spratly) Islands and the surrounding waters.[^76] Vietnam proceeded to a strong opposition to the measure and the reaffirmation of its sovereignty over the islands. Other countries surrounding the South China Sea have claims to the two island chains, including [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan"), [Brunei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brunei "Brunei"), [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia"), and the [Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines "Philippines").[^75] [^77]

### 2013–2015 fishing and oil standoffs

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Vietnamese_Embassy_in_Beijing.JPG/250px-Vietnamese_Embassy_in_Beijing.JPG)

Embassy of Vietnam in China

In May 2013 Vietnam accused China of hitting one of its fishing boats.[^78]

On 7 June 2013, the two countries established a naval hotline.[^45]<sup><span title="Page: 162">: 162</span> </sup> On 21 June 2013, Vietnam and China established a hot line to deal with fisheries incidents.[^45]<sup><span title="Page: 162">: 162</span></sup>

In May 2014, Vietnam accused China of ramming and sinking a fishing boat.[^79] In recent years, Beijing oversaw the replacement of traditional Chinese wooden fishing vessels with steel-hulled trawlers, fitted with modern communication and high-tech navigation systems. The better-equipped boats sailed into the disputed waters as a state-subsidized operation to extend Chinese sovereignty, while in Vietnam, private citizens, not the government, would donate to Vietnamese fishermen to maintain their position in the South China Sea and to defend national sovereignty.[^80] That dynamic continues to be a major source of tension between the two countries.

In May 2014, both countries sparred over [an oil rig in disputed territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hai_Yang_Shi_You_981_standoff "Hai Yang Shi You 981 standoff") in the South China Sea, which triggered [deadly anti-Chinese protests in Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Vietnam_anti-China_protests "2014 Vietnam anti-China protests").[^81] Rioters attacked hundreds of foreign-owned factories in an industrial park in southern Vietnam, targeting Chinese ones.[^82] Following the damage, the Vietnamese government pursued a more moderate foreign policy approach with China and sought to improve the bilateral relations.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 3">: 3</span></sup>

In June China declared there would be no military conflict with Vietnam.[^81] China then had 71 ships in the disputed area, and Vietnam had 61.[^83]

However, on 2 June 2014, it was reported by VGP News, the online newspaper of the Vietnamese Government that the previous day, Chinese ships had in three waves attacked two [Vietnam Coast Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_Coast_Guard "Vietnam Coast Guard") ships, a Vietnamese fisheries surveillance ship and a number of other ships by physically ramming the ships and with water cannons.[^84]

In 2017, Beijing warned Hanoi that it would attack Vietnamese bases in the Spratly Islands if gas drilling continued in the area. Hanoi then ordered Spain's [Repsol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Repsol "Repsol"), whose subsidiary was conducting the drilling, to stop drilling.[^85] [^86]

### 2019–present

Through 2019 and 2020, Chinese ships have continued attacking and sinking of Vietnamese fishing and other vessels in different incidents.[^87] Vietnam only reacted to these incidents by official statements and diplomatic protests.[^88] In late 2020, Chinese Defense Minister [Wei Fenghe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wei_Fenghe "Wei Fenghe") met with [Vietnamese ambassador to China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ambassadors_of_Vietnam_to_China "List of ambassadors of Vietnam to China") [Phạm Sao Mai](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ph%E1%BA%A1m_Sao_Mai&action=edit&redlink=1 "Phạm Sao Mai (page does not exist)") in an attempt to cool down tensions after an increased number of incidents.[^89] The Vietnamese strategy on the South Chinese Sea disputes has been described as a long term consistent act of "balancing, international integration and 'cooperation and struggle'." [^90]

In 2020, [Bloomberg News](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloomberg_News "Bloomberg News") reported that a [hacker group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advanced_persistent_threat "Advanced persistent threat") known as *APT32* or *OceanLotus*, allegedly affiliated with the Vietnamese government, targeted China's [Ministry of Emergency Management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Emergency_Management "Ministry of Emergency Management") and the Wuhan municipal government in order to obtain information about the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic"). The [Vietnamese Ministry of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(Vietnam\) "Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Vietnam)") called the accusations unfounded.[^91] [^92] [^93] In May 2020, an Israeli cybersecurity company reported to have discovered ransomware attacks targeting government systems in Vietnam and several other countries by China-linked groups.[^94]

[Illegal border crossings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illegal_entry "Illegal entry") by Chinese nationals was linked by the Vietnamese public as the perceived cause of new COVID-19 infections in Vietnam, although there had been no evidence for this.[^95] [^96]

In August 2021 shortly before an expected visit by US Vice President [Kamala Harris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kamala_Harris "Kamala Harris"), Vietnamese Prime Minister Pham Minh Chinh reassured Xiong Bo, the Chinese envoy to Hanoi that Vietnam will not enter an alliance to counter China.[^97] Pham suggested the two nations should join with [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN") to expedite "the slow-moving negotiations" and achieve a code of conduct in the disputed South China Sea region and that his country wanted to build political trust, cooperation and promote exchange with China. Xiong stated in the meeting that the two communist nations shared the same political system and beliefs and that China was willing to work with Vietnam and stick to the two countries' high-level strategic directive to further develop ties. Xiong also requested for Vietnam's support in opposing what China claims as "politicisation" of [COVID-19 origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investigations_into_the_origin_of_COVID-19 "Investigations into the origin of COVID-19") investigations.[^98]

In March 2023, Chinese and Vietnamese vessels had chased each other in South China Sea in an incident where Chinese ship intruded into Vietnam's Special Economic Zone.[^99]

On December 12, 2023, the two countries announced 36 cooperation agreements during a [visit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_state_visit_by_Xi_Jinping_to_Vietnam "2023 state visit by Xi Jinping to Vietnam") by CCP General Secretary [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") to Vietnam.[^100] The agreements addressed a variety of issues, including cross-border rail development, digital infrastructure, and establishing joint patrols in the Gulf of Tonkin and a hotline to handle [South China Sea fishing incidents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_disputes_in_the_South_China_Sea "Territorial disputes in the South China Sea").[^100] China and Vietnam also issued a joint statement to support building a [community of shared future for humankind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_of_shared_future "Community of shared future").[^100] In September 2024, Vietnamese media reported that Chinese personnel boarded a Vietnamese fishing vessel near the Parcel Islands and beat its crew with iron bars.[^101] Vietnam and the Philippines condemned the attack while China disputed Vietnam's version of the incident.[^102]

In 2024, Chinese maritime forces reportedly clashed with Vietnamese fishing vessels near the Paracel and [Spratly Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spratly_Islands "Spratly Islands"), causing injuries and vessel damage. The incidents highlighted ongoing territorial disputes, the repression of local fishers, and limited protection from authorities, reflecting broader regional tensions.[^103]

## Cyberwarfare

In 2012, Chinese state-sponsored hackers attacked [PetroVietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrovietnam "Petrovietnam"), [Vietnam News Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_News_Agency "Vietnam News Agency"), and [Vietnam Post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_Post "Vietnam Post"). During the [Hai Yang Shi You 981 standoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hai_Yang_Shi_You_981_standoff "Hai Yang Shi You 981 standoff") in 2014, multiple Vietnamese government websites were attacked by Chinese hackers.[^104] In 2016, following the [South China Sea Arbitration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Sea_Arbitration "South China Sea Arbitration") ruling, multiple [Vietnamese airports](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_airports_hackings "Vietnamese airports hackings") were hacked.[^104] [^105] In 2020, Vietnamese government officials were targeted in [phishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phishing "Phishing") campaigns by the China-based state-sponsored hacking group Pirate Panda.[^106] In 2023, Vietnam publicly named several Chinese advanced persistent threat groups targeting it.[^105]

## Public opinions

In 2014, a survey conducted by the [Pew Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pew_Research_Center "Pew Research Center") showed 84% of Vietnamese were concerned that disputes relating to the South China Sea could lead to military conflict.[^107] During the 2010s, due to factors including territorial disputes, Vietnamese public was largely negative to China, coinciding with [pro-American sentiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pro-Americanism "Pro-Americanism").[^52] Since the 2020s, Vietnamese public opinion was noted to have moved to become more favorable towards China.[^52] [^108] According to a 2026 [feeling thermometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeling_thermometer "Feeling thermometer") poll by the [Carter Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carter_Center "Carter Center") and [Emory University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emory_University "Emory University"), Chinese opinion of Vietnam was on average 30 out of 100.[^109]
