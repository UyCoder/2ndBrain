**China–Cuba relations** are the [bilateral relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilateral_relations "Bilateral relations") between the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") and the [Republic of Cuba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Cuba "Republic of Cuba"). Historically, these relations began when the [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") established a consulate in [Havana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Havana "Havana") while Cuba was still a [Captaincy General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captaincy_General_of_Cuba "Captaincy General of Cuba") of [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain") in 1879. In 1902, the [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") recognized the independence of the [Republic of Cuba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Cuba_\(1902%E2%80%9359\) "Republic of Cuba (1902–59)") from the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), which had taken it from Spain in 1898. Cuba recognized the PRC in September 1960.

The relations are based on trade, credits, and investments, which have increased significantly since the 1990s. China is Cuba's second-largest trading partner after [Venezuela](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuba%E2%80%93Venezuela_relations "Cuba–Venezuela relations"). At a ceremonial trade gathering in Havana in early 2006, China's ambassador to Cuba said, "Our government has a firm position to develop trade co-operation between our countries. The policy, the orientation, has been determined. What's left is the work to complete our plans." [^1] Although both Cuba and China are ruled by a communist party, they were on different sides during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), with Cuba being an ally of the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"), which China usually opposed following the [Sino-Soviet Split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_Split "Sino-Soviet Split").

China and Cuba maintain good relations, including through being members of the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative"). China has [partnered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_partnerships_of_China "Diplomatic partnerships of China") with Cuba to upgrade the rail network, host military facilities, and undertake other initiatives.

## History

On September 16, 1902, the Cuban government established relations with the [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") in [Peking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing"). This continued with the [Beiyang government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beiyang_government "Beiyang government") of the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)") and later, the [Nationalist government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalist_government "Nationalist government") in [Nanking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanjing "Nanjing") and [Taipei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taipei "Taipei") after losing most of its territory. Both countries were allies in [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"). In 1960, post-revolutionary Cuba shifted its recognition to the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China").[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span> </sup> It was the first Latin American country to recognize the PRC.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span></sup>

The relationship between Cuba and China deteriorated during the [Sino-Soviet split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split "Sino-Soviet split"), in part because Cuba valued its need for Soviet oil more than its need for Chinese rice.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span> </sup> In 1979 and the following years, Cuba supported Vietnam in the [Sino-Vietnamese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Vietnamese_War "Sino-Vietnamese War").[^3]

Tensions between Cuba and China remained until the late 1980s.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span> </sup> In the late 1980s, Cuba's relationship with the Soviet Union became strained.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span> </sup> After the [1989 Tiananmen Square protests and massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1989_Tiananmen_Square_protests_and_massacre "1989 Tiananmen Square protests and massacre"), international relations with China were hampered.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span> </sup> A series of Cuban-Chinese state visits followed and relations improved.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span></sup>

In the late 1990s, China provided the Cuban government with equipment to block signals from [Radio Martí](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_y_Televisi%C3%B3n_Mart%C3%AD "Radio y Televisión Martí").[^4]

[CCP general secretary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") [Hu Jintao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hu_Jintao "Hu Jintao") visited Cuba in November 2004,[^5] followed by a visit by his successor, [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping"), in July 2014.[^6] In 2023, China and Cuba signed a bilateral cybersecurity agreement designed to prevent political subversion.[^7] China subsequently invested US$100 million in Cuban cybersecurity.[^7]

During the [2026 Cuban crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Cuban_crisis "2026 Cuban crisis"), CCP general secretary [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") approved an aid package for Cuba, which includes financial assistance valued at 80 million dollars and a donation of 60,000 tons of rice.[^8]

## Economic relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/1965-4_1965%E5%B9%B4_%E5%8F%A4%E5%B7%B4%E5%88%87%C2%B7%E6%A0%BC%E7%93%A6%E6%8B%89%E8%AE%BF%E9%97%AE%E4%B8%AD%E5%9B%BD%EF%BC%8C%E9%82%93%E5%B0%8F%E5%B9%B3%E6%8E%A5%E8%A7%81.jpg/250px-1965-4_1965%E5%B9%B4_%E5%8F%A4%E5%B7%B4%E5%88%87%C2%B7%E6%A0%BC%E7%93%A6%E6%8B%89%E8%AE%BF%E9%97%AE%E4%B8%AD%E5%9B%BD%EF%BC%8C%E9%82%93%E5%B0%8F%E5%B9%B3%E6%8E%A5%E8%A7%81.jpg)

Che Guevara visiting China in 1965. To his left (on the right in the photo) is Deng Xiaoping.

After Cuba's 1960 recognition of the People's Republic of China, China became a major rice exporter to Cuba.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span> </sup> In 1966, during the Sino-Soviet Split, China reduced its rice export quota to Cuba.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span> </sup> The move increased diplomatic tensions, with [Fidel Castro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fidel_Castro "Fidel Castro") describing [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") as a senile idiot.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span> </sup> As relations improved, China provided Cuba with interest-free credit for it to import home appliances in the early 2000s.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span> </sup> The two countries then established a joint venture for the assembly of household electronics in Havana.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span></sup>

Bilateral trade between China and Cuba in 2005 totaled US$777 million, of which US$560 million were Chinese exports to Cuba.[^9] Bilateral trade between China and Cuba in 2014 totaled US$1.6 billion. China is sending a growing amount of durable goods to Cuba. Chinese goods have become the primary tools both in the planned revitalization of [Cuban transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_Cuba "Transportation in Cuba") infrastructure and in the [Energy Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_Revolution "Energy Revolution") of 2006 to provide electricity to the Cuban population. In 2010, China became Cuba's second-largest trading partner.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span> </sup> Economic ties frayed in the 2010s due to troubles in the Cuban economy. Cuban imports from China fell from $1.7 billion in 2017 to $1.1 billion in 2022.[^10]

According to the *[Financial Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_Times "Financial Times")*, Chinese officials have encouraged Cuba to implement economic reforms to shift from a planned economy to one similar to [China's economic model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_market_economy "Socialist market economy"), and "have been perplexed and frustrated at the Cuban leadership’s unwillingness to decisively implement a market-oriented reform programme despite the glaring dysfunction of the status quo".[^10]

### Transportation

As of mid-2006, Cuba had purchased 100 locomotives from China for US$130 million.[^11] [^12] As of early 2006, Cuba had signed a contract for 1,000 Chinese buses for urban and inter-provincial transportation.[^13]

### Consumer goods

The Cuban government is replacing older appliances with newer, more energy-efficient models, including (as of early 2006) 30,000 Chinese refrigerators.[^14]

### Renewable energy

China has been a key partner in the development of Cuba's domestic solar panel production.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 118">: 118</span> </sup> It has provided Cuba with training, technology, and extended credit to Cuba to assist in this process.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 118">: 118</span></sup>

## Investments

### Nickel

As of 2004, China had agreed to planning to invest US$500 million in the completion and operation of Las Camariocas, an unfinished processing facility from the [Soviet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") era. Under the agreement, [Cubaníquel](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cuban%C3%ADquel&action=edit&redlink=1 "Cubaníquel (page does not exist)"), the state-run nickel producer, owns 51 percent, and the Chinese government-owned [Minmetals Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Minmetals "China Minmetals") owns 49 percent. Financing for the project is from the China Development Bank, with [Sinosure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinosure "Sinosure"), the Chinese Export and Credit Insurance Corporation, providing guarantees.[^16]

### Oil

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Chinese_oil_rig_in_Cuba_near_Havana.jpg/250px-Chinese_oil_rig_in_Cuba_near_Havana.jpg)

Chinese oil drilling rig in Cuba near Havana (2014)

[SINOPEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SINOPEC "SINOPEC"), the Chinese state [oil company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_company "Oil company"), has an agreement with state-owned [CUPET](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cupet "Cupet") (Cuba Petroleum) to develop oil resources. As of mid-2008, SINOPEC had conducted some [seismic testing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploration_geophysics "Exploration geophysics") for oil resources on the island of Cuba, but no drilling.[^17] The company also has a contract for joint production in one of Cuba's offshore areas of high yield potential, off the coast of [Pinar del Río](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinar_del_R%C3%ADo "Pinar del Río"),[^18] but had conducted no offshore drilling as of mid-2008.[^17]

In November 2005, PetroChina Great Wall Drilling Co., Ltd. and CUPET held a ceremony for the signing of two drilling service contracts, to provide di;[^19] Great Wall Drilling has provided [drilling rigs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drilling_rig "Drilling rig") for [oil exploration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_exploration "Oil exploration") on Cuba's north coast.

### Biotechnology

In December 2005, the two countries signed an agreement to develop biotech joint ventures within the next three to five years.[^20] Two manufacturing plants using Cuban technology and processes were operating in China as of early 2006.[^21] As of 2020, Cuba's [Center for Genetic Engineering and Biotechnology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_for_Genetic_Engineering_and_Biotechnology "Center for Genetic Engineering and Biotechnology") (CIGB) had two joint ventures with China and its [Center of Molecular Immunology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_Molecular_Immunology "Center of Molecular Immunology") (CIM) has three.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 143">: 143</span> </sup> When China made its first [monoclonal antibodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoclonal_antibody "Monoclonal antibody"), it did so with Cuban technology.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 143">: 143</span></sup>

## Military and intelligence

Chinese personnel have been operating two [signals intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signals_intelligence "Signals intelligence") (SIGINT) [listening stations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Listening_station "Listening station") in Cuba since early 1999.[^22] [Bejucal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bejucal "Bejucal") hosts a station operated by the [People's Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") [Third Department of the Joint Staff Department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_Department_of_the_Joint_Staff_Department "Third Department of the Joint Staff Department").[^23] [^24] [^25] [^26] Other listening stations have been reported in [Wajay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wajay "Wajay") and El Salao, [Santiago de Cuba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santiago_de_Cuba "Santiago de Cuba").[^27] In May 2026, *[The Wall Street Journal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal "The Wall Street Journal")* reported that China operates three SIGINT stations in Cuba, capable of intercepting communications across parts of the southeastern United States.[^28]

## Sovereignty issues

Cuba follows the [One China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_principle "One China principle"). It recognizes the People's Republic of China as the sole government of China and [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") as an integral part of China's territory, and supports all efforts by the PRC to "achieve [national reunification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_unification "Chinese unification") ".[^29]

Cuba was one of 53 countries that, in June 2020, backed the [Hong Kong national security law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Hong_Kong_national_security_law "2020 Hong Kong national security law") at the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations").[^30]

In May 2026, China's foreign minister spokesperson, [Guo Jiakun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guo_Jiakun "Guo Jiakun"), issued a statement in support of Cuba's government following the [U.S. indictment of Raul Castro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indictment_of_Raul_Castro "Indictment of Raul Castro").[^31]
