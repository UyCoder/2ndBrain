**Qinling Station** ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"): 秦岭站; [pinyin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin "Pinyin"): *Qínlǐng zhàn*) is an Antarctic research station operated by the [Polar Research Institute of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_Research_Institute_of_China "Polar Research Institute of China"). It is situated on the southern edge of [Inexpressible Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inexpressible_Island "Inexpressible Island") in [Terra Nova Bay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terra_Nova_Bay "Terra Nova Bay"), [Scott Coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_Coast "Scott Coast"), [Victoria Land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victoria_Land "Victoria Land"), on the [Ross Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ross_Sea "Ross Sea") coast of [East Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Antarctica "East Antarctica").

## Design

The station has an area of 5,244 m <sup>2</sup> (56,450 ft <sup>2</sup>), and is in the shape of the [Southern Cross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crux "Crux"), honouring [Zheng He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zheng_He "Zheng He"), an admiral and diplomat during the [Ming Dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_Dynasty "Ming Dynasty").[^1] It is designed to be run year-long, with enough space for up to 80 people during the antipodal summer.[^2] The station's power system became operational in early 2025 and includes a generation capacity of 100 kW of wind power, 130 kW of solar power, 30 kW of hydrogen energy, and a 300 kWh low-temperature storage system, alongside a diesel power generator and a control system.[^3]

## History

The station first broke ground in 2018, but construction was delayed because of the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic").[^4] In November 2023, China sent a fleet with over 460 personnel to help complete construction of the station.[^5] On 7 February 2024, Qinling Station was officially opened by the People's Republic of China.[^6] [^7] [^8] The station was China's fifth research station on the continent and the third year-long station, after [Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Wall_Station_\(Antarctica\) "Great Wall Station (Antarctica)") and [Zhongshan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhongshan_Station_\(Antarctica\) "Zhongshan Station (Antarctica)").[^2]

The [Center for Strategic and International Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_for_Strategic_and_International_Studies "Center for Strategic and International Studies"), an American [think tank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Think_tank "Think tank"), reported that the station was expected to include an observatory with a satellite ground station, and that the equipment could be used to collect [signals intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signals_intelligence "Signals intelligence") and for tracking rockets launched from the [Arnhem Space Centre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnhem_Space_Centre "Arnhem Space Centre") in Australia. China rejected claims that the station would be used for espionage.[^9] [^10] A satellite ground station was expected to be part of the research station, but early photos of the site did not detect the presence of any such structure.[^2]

In March 2025 China announced plans of its intentions to build its sixth Antarctic station, for summer research, at Cox Point (74°57'S, 136°41'W) [^11] in [Marie Byrd Land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marie_Byrd_Land "Marie Byrd Land"), West Antarctica.[^12]
