The [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") and the [Federal Republic of Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigeria "Nigeria") established diplomatic relations on February 10, 1971 - a decade after Nigeria gained its independence from the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire"). Relations between Nigeria and China have expanded on growing [bilateral trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilateral_trade "Bilateral trade") and strategic cooperation. China is also one of Nigeria's important trading and export partners.

Although Nigeria maintains trade relations with [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan"), and has a representative office in [Taipei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taipei "Taipei"), it issued a joint communiqué with China in 2005, reaffirming that [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") was "the only legitimate government representing the whole of China and Taiwan is an unalienable part of its territory".[^1]

Nigeria has an embassy in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing"), a consulate in [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou "Guangzhou"), and consulates-general in [Shanghai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai "Shanghai") and [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong").[^2] China has an embassy in [Abuja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuja "Abuja") and a consulate-general in [Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos "Lagos").[^3]

## History

Nigeria and the People's Republic of China established formal [diplomatic relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_relations "Diplomatic relations") on February 10, 1971.[^4] [^5] [^6] Relations between the two nations grew closer as a result of the international isolation and Western condemnation of Nigeria's military dictatorships (1970s-1998). Nigeria has since become an important source of oil and petroleum for China's rapidly growing economy and Nigeria is looking to China for help in achieving high economic growth; China has provided extensive economic, military, and political support.[^7] [^8]

In 1996, as the [Clinton administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidency_of_Bill_Clinton "Presidency of Bill Clinton") lobbied in favor of sanctions against Nigeria. China, along with West-European countries, were unfavorable to a global freeze of Nigerian assets.[^9]

In 2004 and again in 2006, [Chinese President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_China "President of China") [Hu Jintao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hu_Jintao "Hu Jintao") made [state visits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_visit "State visit") to Nigeria and addressed a [joint session](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_session "Joint session") of the [National Assembly of Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Nigeria "National Assembly of Nigeria"). Both nations signed a [memorandum of understanding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memorandum_of_understanding "Memorandum of understanding") on establishing a [strategic partnership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_partnerships_of_China "Diplomatic partnerships of China").[^10] China has supported Nigeria's bid for a seat in the [U.N. Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.N._Security_Council "U.N. Security Council").[^4] As of February 2013, the Chinese Ambassador in Nigeria was Deng Boqing.[^11]

In response to the hesitation of the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and other Western countries to aid Nigeria in their efforts to combat insurgents in the oil-rich [Niger Delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger_Delta "Niger Delta") region, the Nigerian government has developed close military cooperation with China, which has supplied arms, equipment, training, and technology to the Nigerian armed forces.[^12] Both nations also signed a US$311 million agreement to develop cooperation in [communications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communications "Communications") and [space programs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_program "Space program");[^13] China helped develop and launch the Nigerian [communications satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communications_satellite "Communications satellite") ([NigComSat-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NigComSat-1 "NigComSat-1")) by 2007 to expand [cellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_phone "Mobile phone") and internet networks in [Central Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Africa "Central Africa").[^13] [^14] [^15]

In January 2017, the Nigerian government ordered Taiwan to move its unofficial embassy out of Abuja and into Lagos (Nigeria's economic center), stating that the African country was a defender of the [One-China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-China_policy "One-China policy") vision.[^16] [^17] This order came after Nigeria obtained a $40 billion investment pledge from China.[^16] Taiwan was required to rename the unofficial embassy (the "Business Delegation of the ROC in the Federal Republic of Nigeria") to the "Trade Representative Office of Taipei in Lagos").[^17]

In 2021, the two countries celebrated their 50 years of official relations.[^18]

## Treatment of Nigerians in China

In April 2020, the foreign minister, [Geoffrey Onyeama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Onyeama "Geoffrey Onyeama"), condemned China's discriminatory attitude towards Nigerians, after a video emerged on the web showing Nigerian residents in China being discriminated against by locals. While Chinese officials said they took the issue very seriously,[^19] they also blamed Western media for emphasizing isolated events to feed a PR smear campaign over its Nigerian interests.[^20]

In November 2020, the Chinese government blocked entry to China for all foreigners, including Nigerians, over [COVID-19](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronavirus_disease_2019 "Coronavirus disease 2019") concerns, except for "essential services" including diplomatic service.[^21]

It was reported that Nigerians in China were placed on [lockdown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_lockdowns "COVID-19 lockdowns") for more than the average 2-week quarantine a [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_people "Chinese people") citizen would experience.[^22]

## Economic relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Belt_and_Road_Initiative_participant_map.svg/250px-Belt_and_Road_Initiative_participant_map.svg.png)

Countries which signed cooperation documents related to the Belt and Road Initiative

Bilateral trade reached US$3 billion in 2006 – up from $384 million in 1998.[^5] [^10] During Chinese President Hu Jintao's visit in 2006, China secured four oil drilling licenses and agreed to invest $4 billion in oil and infrastructure development projects in Nigeria,[^8] and both nations agreed to a four-point plan to improve bilateral relations – a key component of which was to expand trade and investments in agriculture, telecommunications, energy, and infrastructure development.[^5] Furthermore, China agreed to buy a controlling stake in the Kaduna oil refinery that would produce 110,000 barrels per day (17,000 m <sup>3</sup> /d).[^8] Nigeria also promised to give preference to Chinese oil firms for contracts for oil exploration in the [Niger Delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger_Delta "Niger Delta") and [Chad Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chad_Basin "Chad Basin").[^8] In 2006, China also agreed to grant a loan of $1 billion to Nigeria to help it upgrade and modernize its railway networks.[^7] In 2005, Nigeria agreed to supply [PetroChina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PetroChina "PetroChina") with 30,000 barrels per day (4,800 m <sup>3</sup> /d) of oil for $800 million.[^12] In 2006, the [CNOOC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNOOC "CNOOC") purchased a share for $2.3 billion in an oil exploration block owned by a former defence minister. China has also pledged to invest $267 million to build the Lekki [free trade zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_trade_zone "Free trade zone") near [Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos "Lagos"). However, the "flooding" of Nigerian markets with cheap Chinese goods has become a sensitive political issue, as – combined with the importation of second-hand European products – it has adversely affected domestic industries, especially in [textiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textiles "Textiles"), and led to the closure of 65 textile mills and the laying-off of 150,000 textile workers over the course of a decade.[^10] Nigerian militants have also threatened to attack Chinese workers and projects in the Niger Delta.[^10] In 2010, trade between the two countries was worth US$7.8 billion.[^23] In 2011, Nigeria was the 4th largest trading partner of China in Africa and in the first 8 months of 2012, it was the 3rd.[^24]

In April 2018, Nigeria signed a $2.4-billion currency swap deal valid for 3 years.[^25] In 2019, bilateral trade between China and Nigeria reached $19.27 billion.[^18]

### Chinese development finance to Nigeria

From 2000 to 2011, there were approximately 40 Chinese official development finance projects identified in Nigeria through various media reports.[^26] These projects ranged from a $2.5 billion loan for Nigerian rail, power, or telecommunications projects in 2008, to an [MoU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memorandum_of_understanding "Memorandum of understanding") for $1 billion for the construction of houses and water supply in Abuja in 2009, and several rail networks.[^27]

Since 2000, trade relations have risen exponentially. There has been an increase in total trade of over 10,384 million dollars between the two nations from 2000 to 2016.[^28] However, the structure of the China-Nigerian trade relationship has become a major political issue because Chinese exports accounted for around 80 percent of total bilateral trade volumes. This has resulted in a serious [trade imbalance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade_imbalance "Trade imbalance") with Nigeria importing ten times more than it exports to China. Nigeria's economy is becoming over-reliant on cheap foreign imports to sustain itself, resulting in a clear decline in Nigerian Industry under such arrangements.[^29] In September 2018, Nigeria signed a $328 million loan with China to heavily boost the development of telecommunication infrastructures in Nigeria.[^30]

China provided the financing for the following projects in Nigeria:[^18]

- [Abuja-Kaduna Railway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuja-Kaduna_Railway "Abuja-Kaduna Railway"), [Abuja Metro Light Rail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuja_Light_Rail "Abuja Light Rail"), [Abuja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nnamdi_Azikiwe_International_Airport "Nnamdi Azikiwe International Airport") and [Port Harcourt Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_Harcourt_International_Airport "Port Harcourt International Airport") terminals
- [Lekki Free Trade Zones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lekki_Free_Trade_Zone "Lekki Free Trade Zone"), Ogun – Guangdong
- [Zungeru Hydro Power Dam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zungeru_Hydro_Power_Dam "Zungeru Hydro Power Dam")
- University of Transportation, Daura

In exchange, Nigeria often/systematically hires a Chinese firm to oversee its development projects, such as the 3,050 MW [Mambilla hydroelectric Power Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mambilla_Hydroelectric_Power_Station "Mambilla Hydroelectric Power Station").[^31]

### Chinese military equipment sales

In 2015, a China-made drone crashed in the countryside of Nigeria.[^32] It is believed the drone was involved in Nigeria's struggle against the Islamic militant group, [Boko Haram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boko_Haram "Boko Haram").[^32] China supplied the [CH-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CASC_Rainbow#CH-3 "CASC Rainbow") to Nigeria's government prior to 2014, along with YC-200 guided bombs and AR-1 air-to-ground missiles.[^33]

In 2020, the Nigerian Air Force (NAF) Chief, Air Marshal [Sadique Abubakar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sadique_Abubakar "Sadique Abubakar"), disclosed that the NAF has concluded the acquisition of eight [Wing Loong II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wing_Loong_II "Wing Loong II"), [CH-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CASC_Rainbow#CH-4 "CASC Rainbow"), and CH-3 drones.[^34]

## Space cooperation

In 2006, Nigeria obtained $200 million in preferential buyer's credit from the [Export-Import Bank of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Export%E2%80%93Import_Bank_of_China "Export–Import Bank of China") to help fund its first communication satellite.[^35]<sup><span title="Page: 303">: 303</span> </sup> Nigerian scientists received training in China regarding satellite construction and design.[^35]<sup><span title="Page: 303">: 303</span> </sup> The satellite was manufactured by [China Great Wall Industry Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Great_Wall_Industry_Corporation "China Great Wall Industry Corporation").[^35]<sup><span title="Page: 303">: 303</span> </sup> The satellite successfully launched in 2007, but failed the next year due to problems with its [solar arrays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_arrays "Solar arrays").[^35]<sup><span title="Page: 303">: 303</span> </sup> Funded by insurance proceeds, a replacement was launched from China in 2011.[^35]<sup><span title="Pages: 302–303">: 302–303</span></sup>

In 2018, Nigeria signed an agreement with China to purchase two communications satellites with funds provided the Export-Import Bank of China.[^35]<sup><span title="Pages: 303–304">: 303–304</span> </sup> In exchange, China will receive part ownership of [Nigerian Communications Satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Communications_Satellite "Nigerian Communications Satellite"), a Nigerian government-owned company that manages satellite communications.[^35]<sup><span title="Page: 304">: 304</span></sup>

As of mid-2019, China had provided 500 Nigerian students with scholarships for training as space engineers.[^35]<sup><span title="Page: 303">: 303</span></sup>

## Political relations

Nigeria follows the [one China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_principle "One China principle"). It recognizes the People's Republic of China as the sole government of China and [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") as an integral part of China's territory, and supports all efforts by the PRC to "achieve [national reunification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_unification "Chinese unification") ". It also considers [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong"), [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang") and [Tibet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region "Tibet Autonomous Region") to be China's internal affairs.[^36]

In July 2019, UN ambassadors of 37 countries, including Nigeria, signed a joint letter to the [United Nations Human Rights Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council "United Nations Human Rights Council") defending [China's persecution of Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution_of_Uyghurs_in_China "Persecution of Uyghurs in China").[^37] [^38] Nigeria was one of 16 countries that defended China in 2019 but did not do so in 2020.[^39]

## Public opinion

A survey published in 2025 by the [Pew Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pew_Research_Center "Pew Research Center") found that 81% of Nigerian people had a favorable view of China, while 13% had an unfavorable view.[^40]
