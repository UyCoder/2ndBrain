Diplomatic relations between the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") and [Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgia_\(country\) "Georgia (country)") were established on 9 June 1992. Since then, bilateral ties have advanced gradually and mostly focused on economic cooperation. China has an embassy in [Tbilisi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tbilisi "Tbilisi"), and Georgia has an embassy in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing"). By 2017, China had become Georgia's fourth largest trading partner and the second largest exporting market for Georgian wine.[^1] China has been appreciative of Georgia's commitment to [One-China policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-China_policy "One-China policy"). To maintain balanced relations with both Georgia and Russia, China does not recognize the independence of Abkhazia and South Ossetia. Currently, China relies on Georgia and its infrastructure for the [China–Central Asia–West Asia Economic Corridor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Central_Asia%E2%80%93West_Asia_Economic_Corridor "China–Central Asia–West Asia Economic Corridor"), which traverses the South Caucasus, linking China with Europe. As a result, China is cautious in its approach to South Caucasian geopolitics, striving to develop connections with all regional players and effectively implement its geostrategy.[^2] Georgia, for its part, has been increasingly attractive for China as a transportation hub and one of the shortest routes for Chinese goods on their way to the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union").[^3] In 2023, China and Georgia formed a [strategic partnership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_partnerships_of_China "Diplomatic partnerships of China").[^4]

## History

The *[Yuan Shi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Yuan "History of Yuan")*, the official history of the [Yuan dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_dynasty "Yuan dynasty") of China, records the fate of Georgia in 1252. In that year, the Mongol khagan [Möngke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6ngke_Khan "Möngke Khan"), who [was expanding into China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_conquest_of_China "Mongol conquest of China"), granted the [Kingdom of Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Georgia "Kingdom of Georgia"), which was then under Mongol control, to [Berke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berke "Berke"). *Chu'ü-erh-chih* ([\[tɕʰỳ ǎɚʈʂɻ̩ɨ̀\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Mandarin "Help:IPA/Mandarin"); [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"): 曲兒只), the Chinese name used for Georgia in the *Yuan Shi*, is etymologically the same as "Georgia".[^5] [^6]

## Political relations

The China–Georgia relations were officially established on 9 June 1992, when China extended its diplomatic recognition to the Republic of Georgia after the [dissolution of the Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union "Dissolution of the Soviet Union").[^7] [Eduard Shevardnadze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduard_Shevardnadze "Eduard Shevardnadze"), then Georgia's head of state, paid a state visit to China in June 1993 [^8] and signed several agreements, principally on economic and trade cooperation. [Mikheil Saakashvili](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikheil_Saakashvili "Mikheil Saakashvili"), the then-President of Georgia, was in China on an official visit in April 2006.[^9]

The two countries maintained communication and coordination at the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") and other international organizations. Georgia follows the [one China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_principle "One China principle"), and recognizes [government of the People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_China "Government of China") as the sole legal government representing the whole of China and Taiwan as "an inalienable part" of China.[^10] [^11] In its turn, China, member of the [Shanghai Cooperation Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Cooperation_Organisation "Shanghai Cooperation Organisation") (SCO), refused to follow the suit of [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia"), a fellow SCO member, in recognition of the independence of Georgia's breakaway Abkhazia and South Ossetia in the aftermath of the August 2008 [Russo–Georgian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russo%E2%80%93Georgian_War "Russo–Georgian War") despite the appeal by the Russian Foreign Ministry. Instead, the SCO issued the Dushanbe Declaration, calling on all parties to solve the "existing problems" through diplomacy.[^7] Similarly, a Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesman expressed the agency's concern due to the "latest development in South Ossetia and Abkhazia", responding to a journalist's question regarding China's position on Russia's recognition of the disputed territories.[^7] In the view of the political analyst Joseph Larsen, "while China does not present an alternative to [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") and [EU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") integration... relations with China have the potential to complement Georgia's existing foreign policy." [^12]

## Economic relations

Bilateral economic ties have gradually expanded since 1992 and witnessed further growth beginning in 2010 as Georgia's economy recovered from the 2008 war. China views Georgia as part of the [One Belt One Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_Belt_One_Road_Initiative "One Belt One Road Initiative"), a project it launched in 2013 to "shorten the distance between China and Europe" through improved infrastructure connections.[^12] [^13] By 2014, China had accounted for $217.94 million in [foreign direct investment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_direct_investment "Foreign direct investment") in Georgia, putting it in fourth place after [Azerbaijan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azerbaijan "Azerbaijan"), the [Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands"), and the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States").[^7]

A number of Chinese companies have launched major operations in Georgia. China's [Hualing Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hualing_Group&action=edit&redlink=1 "Hualing Group (page does not exist)"), mainly focused on construction and management of hotels and trade centers, was Georgia's single largest foreign investor as of 2017. Some of the company's projects in Georgia include the Hualinge Hualing [Free Industrial Zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free-trade_zone "Free-trade zone") [^14]<sup><span title="Page: 111">: 111</span> </sup> in [Kutaisi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kutaisi "Kutaisi"), Georgia's second largest city. The Hualinge Hualing Free Industrial zone is an important hub for Chinese manufacturers to access the European market.[^14]<sup><span title="Page: 111">: 111</span> </sup> Hualing Group has also built a large residential and commercial complex in a suburb of the capital city of [Tbilisi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tbilisi "Tbilisi").

In January 2017, the [CEFC China Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CEFC_China_Energy "CEFC China Energy") agreed to purchase 75% of shares in the Free Industrial Zone at [Poti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poti "Poti") on Georgia's [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea") coastline. Georgia's main export product to China is wine, which amounted to 5,299,820 bottles in 2016, nearly double the amount exported in the previous year.[^15] In addition to wine, other significant exports from Georgia to China include copper ore and copper products.[^14]<sup><span title="Page: 111">: 111</span> </sup> Significant Chinese exports to Georgia include mechanical products, electrical products, and steel.[^14]<sup><span title="Page: 111">: 111</span></sup>

On 13 May 2017, China and Georgia signed a [free trade agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_trade_agreement "Free trade agreement").[^16]

In June 2023, [Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgia_\(country\) "Georgia (country)") passed a bill to allow [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_people "Chinese people") citizens to travel to [Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgia_\(country\) "Georgia (country)") without a [visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Travel_visa "Travel visa"), further improving relations and diplomatic ties between two nations. [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping"), the [General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") met with the Georgian [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister "Prime Minister"), [Irakli Garibashvili](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irakli_Garibashvili "Irakli Garibashvili"), discussing improvements in [economic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic "Economic") ties and marking "new stage" in relations between the two countries.[^17]
