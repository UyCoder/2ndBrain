**China–New Zealand relations** or **Sino–New Zealand relations** are relations between the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC) and [New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand "New Zealand"). New Zealand recognised the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China") after it lost the [Chinese Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War "Chinese Civil War") and retreated to [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_\(island\) "Taiwan (island)") in 1949, but switched recognition to the People's Republic of China on 22 December 1972.[^3] [^4] Since then, economic, cultural, and political relations between the two countries have grown. China is New Zealand's largest trading partner in goods, and second largest trading partner in services. In 2008, New Zealand became the first developed country to enter into a [free trade agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand%E2%80%93China_Free_Trade_Agreement "New Zealand–China Free Trade Agreement") with China.[^5] In recent years, New Zealand's extensive economic relations with China have been complicated by its [security ties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand%E2%80%93United_States_relations "New Zealand–United States relations") to the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States").

In addition to formal diplomatic and economic relations, there has been significant people-to-people contact between China and New Zealand. Chinese immigration to New Zealand dates back to the [gold rushes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otago_gold_rush "Otago gold rush") of the 1860s and has substantially increased since the 1980s.[^6]

## History

### Qing dynasty China

New Zealand's contact with China started in the mid 19th century. The first records of ethnic Chinese in New Zealand were migrant workers from [Guangdong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangdong "Guangdong") province, who arrived during the 1860s [Otago gold rush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otago_gold_rush "Otago gold rush"). Most of the migrant workers were male, with few women migrants. Emigration from China was driven by overpopulation, land shortages, famine, drought, banditry, and peasant revolts, which triggered a wave of Chinese migration to [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia"), [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia"), New Zealand, the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), and [Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada "Canada").[^7]

Early Chinese migrants encountered considerable racial discrimination and prejudice. In 1871, the New Zealand Government imposed a [poll tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_tax_\(New_Zealand\) "Head tax (New Zealand)") on Chinese migrants that was not repealed until 1944. Other discriminatory policies included an English literacy test, restrictive immigration measures, denial of old age pensions, and being barred from permanent residency and citizenship (from 1908 to 1952). After the Gold Rush ended in the 1880s, many of the former Chinese miners found work as market gardeners, shopkeepers, and laundry operators. There was some limited intermarriage with [White](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_New_Zealanders "European New Zealanders") and indigenous [Māori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people "Māori people") women.[^8] [^9]

### Republic of China, 1912–1949

In 1903, the [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") had established a consulate in Wellington to deal with trade, immigration, and local Chinese welfare. Following the [Xinhai Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinhai_Revolution "Xinhai Revolution") in 1912, the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)") took over the consulate. The lack of a reciprocal New Zealand mission in China made the Republic of China's mission in Wellington serve as the primary point of contact between both governments until 1972.[^10] During the Republican era, New Zealand interests in China were largely represented by British diplomatic and consular missions. However, there were some attempts to establish New Zealand trade commissions in [Tianjin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianjin "Tianjin") and [Shanghai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai "Shanghai").[^11]

Between 1912 and 1949, there were over 350 New Zealand expatriates living and working in China, including missionaries for various Christian denominations, medical workers, [United Nations Relief and Rehabilitation Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Relief_and_Rehabilitation_Administration "United Nations Relief and Rehabilitation Administration") (UNRRA) workers, teachers, and telegraph workers. Some notable expatriates included the missionaries Annie James and James Huston Edgar, and the communist writer, teacher, and activist [Rewi Alley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rewi_Alley "Rewi Alley").[^12]

During the [Second World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_World_War "Second World War"), New Zealand society developed a more favourable view of China because of its status as a wartime ally against Japan. Chinese market gardeners were viewed as an important contribution to the wartime economy. New Zealand also eased its immigration policy to admit Chinese refugees and grant them permanent residency. In the postwar years, many Chinese migrants, including women and children, settled in New Zealand since the Communist victory in 1949 made it difficult for many to return home.[^13]

### Cold War, 1949–1972

Following the establishment of the People's Republic of China (PRC) in 1949, New Zealand did not initially recognise the new government. Instead, it joined Australia and the United States in continuing to recognise the Republic of China (ROC) government, which had relocated to [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_\(island\) "Taiwan (island)"), as the legitimate government of China. Between 1951 and 1960, New Zealand and Australia consistently supported a US moratorium proposal to block Soviet efforts to seat the PRC as the lawful representatives of China in the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") and to expel the ROC representatives. By contrast, the United Kingdom had established diplomatic relations with the PRC in 1949. While the conservative [National Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party "New Zealand National Party") favoured the ROC, the social democratic [Labour Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party "New Zealand Labour Party") favoured extending diplomatic relations to the PRC. New Zealand and the PRC also fought on opposite sides during the [Korean War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_War "Korean War"), with the former supporting the [United Nations forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Command "United Nations Command") and the latter backing [North Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Korea "North Korea").[^14] [^15]

The PRC government also expelled many missionaries and foreigners, including most New Zealand expatriates by 1951. One missionary, Annie James of the [New Zealand Presbyterian Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presbyterian_Church_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand "Presbyterian Church of Aotearoa New Zealand") 's Canton Villages Mission, was imprisoned and interrogated. However, some pro-communist Westerners, including [Rewi Alley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rewi_Alley "Rewi Alley"), were allowed to remain in China. Alley pioneered a working model for secular "cooperative education" in vocational subjects and rural development. Despite the lack of official relations between the two countries, unofficial relations were conducted through the auspices of the [Chinese People's Association for Friendship with Foreign Countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_People%27s_Association_for_Friendship_with_Foreign_Countries "Chinese People's Association for Friendship with Foreign Countries") and the [New Zealand China Friendship Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_China_Friendship_Society "New Zealand China Friendship Society") (NZCFS). In addition, the [Communist Party of New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_New_Zealand "Communist Party of New Zealand") and some trade unions were sympathetic to the PRC rather than the Soviet Union.[^16] [^17]

In 1955 [Warren Freer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warren_Freer "Warren Freer") (then an opposition Labour MP) was the first Western politician to visit China, against the wishes of Labour leader [Walter Nash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Nash "Walter Nash") but with the encouragement of Prime Minister [Sidney Holland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidney_Holland "Sidney Holland").[^18]

New Zealand photographer [Brian Brake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_Brake "Brian Brake") was given irregular access to China in 1957 and 1959, photographing [Nikita Khrushchev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev "Nikita Khrushchev") 's visit to the country, members of the PRC government like [Chairman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party") [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong"), and scenes of life around the country at the time. Brake was the only Western photojournalist who documented the [10th anniversary of the People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10th_anniversary_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "10th anniversary of the People's Republic of China").[^19]

[Prime Minister of New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand "Prime Minister of New Zealand") [Keith Holyoake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keith_Holyoake "Keith Holyoake") visited [ROC President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_the_Republic_of_China "President of the Republic of China") [Chiang Kai-shek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek "Chiang Kai-shek") in 1960. Holyoake had a favourable view of the ROC and permitted the upgrading of the ROC consulate to full embassy status in 1962. However, New Zealand declined to establish any diplomatic or trading mission in Taiwan but opted to conduct its relations with the ROC through trade commissioners based in [Tokyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tokyo "Tokyo") and [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong"). As pressure for PRC representation at the United Nations grew, the New Zealand Government came to favour dual representation of both Chinese governments, but that was rejected by both the ROC and the PRC. In 1971, New Zealand and other US allies unsuccessfully opposed [United Nations General Assembly Resolution 2758](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_Resolution_2758 "United Nations General Assembly Resolution 2758") to recognise the PRC as the "only legitimate representative of China to the UN." [^20] [^21]

### People's Republic of China, 1971–present

In 1971, 78 countries invited Chinese table tennis teams to tour. New Zealand was the sixth nation whose invitation was accepted, for a tour in July 1972. The Chinese delegation arrived in Auckland, then flew to Wellington on 17 July, where they were met by protesters advising them to defect. They played in the Lower Hutt Town Hall. The following day an official afternoon tea reception was attended by the Prime Minister [Jack Marshall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Marshall "Jack Marshall"), half the cabinet, Labour leader [Norman Kirk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norman_Kirk "Norman Kirk"), Wellington Mayor [Frank Kitts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Kitts "Frank Kitts"), and [Bryce Harland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bryce_Harland "Bryce Harland") who was soon to be the first Ambassador to China. A tour followed, to the farm of former All Black [Ken Gray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ken_Gray_\(rugby_union\) "Ken Gray (rugby union)") at [Pauatahanui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauatahanui "Pauatahanui"), where they watched sheep shearing and sheep dogs.[^22]

On 22 December 1972 the newly elected [Third Labour Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand "Third Labour Government of New Zealand") formally recognised the People's Republic of China, with the two governments signing a joint communique to govern bilateral relations. According to former New Zealand diplomat [Gerald Hensley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerald_Hensley "Gerald Hensley"), Prime Minister Norman Kirk initially hesitated recognising the PRC until his second term but changed his mind because of the [Ministry of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade_\(New_Zealand\) "Ministry of Foreign Affairs and Trade (New Zealand)"). Kirk was influenced by his Australian counterpart [Gough Whitlam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gough_Whitlam "Gough Whitlam") 's decision to recognise the PRC.[^23] [^24] [^25] In recognising the PRC, New Zealand adopted a [One China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China "One China") policy, by which it acknowledges the PRC's [One China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_principle "One China principle"), which is that there is one China, it is governed by the PRC, and Taiwan is part of its territory. New Zealand did not say it agreed with the principle, just that it acknowledges that the PRC government holds that position. Thus New Zealand adopted a [policy of deliberate ambiguity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Policy_of_deliberate_ambiguity "Policy of deliberate ambiguity") over Taiwan.[^26]

Despite ending diplomatic relations with the ROC, the New Zealand [Permanent Representative to the UN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Permanent_Representatives_of_New_Zealand_to_the_United_Nations_in_New_York "List of Permanent Representatives of New Zealand to the United Nations in New York") negotiated an agreement with his ROC counterpart Huang Hua for both countries to continue maintaining trade and other non-official contacts with Taiwan. The last ROC Ambassador to New Zealand was Konsin Shah, the dean of the diplomatic corps in Wellington.[^27] [^28]

In April 1973 [Joe Walding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Walding "Joe Walding") became the first New Zealand government minister to visit China and met Premier [Zhou Enlai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Enlai "Zhou Enlai"). In return, Chinese Foreign Trade Minister [Bai Xiangguo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bai_Xiangguo "Bai Xiangguo") visited Wellington, seeking to sign a trade agreement in New Zealand. The same year, the PRC established an embassy in Wellington, and Pei Tsien-chang was appointed as the first Chinese ambassador to New Zealand. In September 1973, the New Zealand Embassy was established in Beijing with Bryce Harland serving as the first [New Zealand Ambassador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ambassadors_of_New_Zealand_to_China "List of ambassadors of New Zealand to China") to China.[^28] [^29]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Mao_Zedong_and_Robert_Muldoon%2C_April_1976.jpg/250px-Mao_Zedong_and_Robert_Muldoon%2C_April_1976.jpg)

Robert Muldoon meeting Mao Zedong on a state visit to China in April 1976

Following the [1975 general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1975_New_Zealand_general_election "1975 New Zealand general election"), the [Third National Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_National_Government_of_New_Zealand "Third National Government of New Zealand") abandoned National's support for the "Two Chinas policy" and expanded upon its Labour predecessors' diplomatic and trade relations with the PRC.[^28] [^30] In April–May 1976, [Robert Muldoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Muldoon "Robert Muldoon") became the first New Zealand Prime Minister to visit China. He visited Beijing and met with [Premier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_China "Premier of China") [Hua Guofeng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hua_Guofeng "Hua Guofeng") and Mao, being one of the last foreign leaders to meet the chairman before he passed in July. Muldoon's visit served to strengthen diplomatic and trading ties between the two countries, and to reassure the New Zealand public that China did not pose a threat to New Zealand.[^31]

Since the end of the Cold War, bilateral relations between New Zealand and China have grown, particularly in the areas of trade, education, tourism, climate change, and public sector co-operation. Bilateral relations have been characterised by trade and economic co-operation. In August 1997, New Zealand became the first Western country to support [China's accession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_and_the_World_Trade_Organization "China and the World Trade Organization") to the [World Trade Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Trade_Organization "World Trade Organization") by concluding a bilateral agreement. In April 2004, New Zealand became the first country to recognise China as a market economy during a second round of trade negotiations. In November 2004, New Zealand and China launched negotiations towards a [free trade agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_trade_agreement "Free trade agreement") in November 2004, with [the agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand%E2%80%93China_Free_Trade_Agreement "New Zealand–China Free Trade Agreement") being signed in April 2008. In November 2016, the two countries entered into negotiations to upgrade their free trade agreement.[^5] [^32]

In the mid-2020s, New Zealand began to increase defence spending and capabilities, which was recognised by observers to be driven by China's rapid military build-up and growing military exercises in the region.[^33] In November 2025 [HMNZS Aotearoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HMNZS_Aotearoa "HMNZS Aotearoa") transited the [Taiwan Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_Strait "Taiwan Strait"), with China objecting.[^34]

## Cultural and migration relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Osmanthus_Garden_Cornwall_Park_Hastings.jpg/250px-Osmanthus_Garden_Cornwall_Park_Hastings.jpg)

A Chinese garden constructed in Hastings to celebrate the city's friendship with its sister city Guilin

China and New Zealand have a long history of people-to-people contacts. During the 19th century, Chinese migrated to New Zealand to work as miners. Despite racial prejudice and anti-immigrant legislation, a small number settled to work as market gardeners, businessmen, and shopkeepers. Following World War II, official and public attitudes and policies towards Chinese migrants were relaxed and more Chinese women and children were allowed to settle. During the post-war years, the Chinese population in New Zealand increased, with many becoming middle-class professionals and businessmen.[^6]

In 1987, the New Zealand Government abandoned its long-standing preference for British and Irish immigrants in favour for a skills-based immigration policy. By 2013, the Chinese New Zealander population had increased to 171,411, comprising 4% of the country's population. Within this group, three-quarters were foreign-born and only one-quarter were locally-born. Of the foreign-born population, 51% came from China, 5% from Taiwan, and 4% from Hong Kong.[^35]

In addition, several New Zealand missionaries, businessmen, aid workers, and telegraph workers have lived and worked in China as long-term residents.[^12] One notable New Zealand expatriate in China was Rewi Alley, a New Zealand-born writer, educator, social reformer, potter, and member of the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP). He lived and worked in China for 60 years until his death in 1987. He came to symbolise the important role of people to people contacts in building good relations and accentuating common ground between countries as different as New Zealand and China. In 1997, the 100th anniversary of Alley's birth was marked by celebrations in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") and New Zealand.[^36] [^37] [^38] [^39]

In an effort to build cultural relations between Maori and Chinese, New Zealand has increasingly utilised a "taniwha and dragon" framework. In 2013, the Taniwha and Dragon Festival, organised in part by the minister of Māori affairs, [Pita Sharples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pita_Sharples "Pita Sharples"), was held at Orakei Marae in Auckland to commemorate historical interactions between Māori and Chinese migrants in New Zealand.[^40] Later, it was used to connect iwi businesses with Chinese counterparts, such as the 'Taniwha Dragon' economic summit that was held in the city of Hastings in 2017. More recently, it has been used by New Zealand's foreign minister, Nanaia Mahuta, to conceptutalise Sino-New Zealand relations more broadly.[^41]

In mid-June 2024, China agreed to extend visa-free travel to New Zealanders during a state visit by [Chinese Premier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_China "Premier of China") [Li Qiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_Qiang "Li Qiang"). In return, New Zealand agreed to support Chinese language training and cultural exchange programmes provided by local [Confucius Institutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius_Institute "Confucius Institute").[^42] [^43]

On 15 June 2025, Prime Minister [Christopher Luxon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Luxon "Christopher Luxon") and [Immigration Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_Immigration_\(New_Zealand\) "Minister of Immigration (New Zealand)") [Erica Stanford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erica_Stanford "Erica Stanford") announced that the New Zealand Government would be launching a three-month visa waiver trial for [Chinese citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_nationality_law "Chinese nationality law") with valid Australian visitor, work, family or student visas from November 2025.[^44] On 18 June, Stanford followed up with an announcement that Chinese nationals would no longer need to apply for [transit visas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_visa "Transit visa") from November 2025 and would be eligible to apply for the [New Zealand Electronic Travel Authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visa_policy_of_New_Zealand#New_Zealand_Electronic_Travel_Authority "Visa policy of New Zealand") (NZETA).[^45] On 27 October, Chinese visitors transiting through New Zealand became eligible for the NZETA visa scheme. From 3 November, Chinese nationals travelling from Australia to New Zealand also became eligible for the NZETA visa.[^46]

## Economic relations

### Trade

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Belt_and_Road_Initiative_participant_map.svg/250px-Belt_and_Road_Initiative_participant_map.svg.png)

Countries that signed cooperation documents related to the Belt and Road Initiative

In 1972, New Zealand's trade relations with mainland China were paltry with NZ exports to China estimated to being less than NZ$2 million per annum. Early New Zealand exports to China included timber, pulp and paper while early Chinese exports to NZ were high-quality printing paper and chemicals.[^47] [^48] Over the successive decades, trade between the two countries grew. In terms of the Chinese share of New Zealand trade, New Zealand's exports to China rose from about 2% in 1981 to about 4.9% in 1988. In 1990, it dropped to 1% due to the fallout from the [1989 Tiananmen Square protests and massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1989_Tiananmen_Square_protests_and_massacre "1989 Tiananmen Square protests and massacre"). By 2001, NZ exports to China accounted for 7% of China's New Zealand's overseas trade. Meanwhile, New Zealand imports to China rose from below 1% of New Zealand's trade volume in 1981 to 7% by 2001.[^49]

Mainland China (i.e. excluding Hong Kong and Macau) is New Zealand's largest trading partner, with bilateral trade between the two countries in 2023 valued at [NZ$](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NZ$ "NZ$") 37.9 billion. Hong Kong SAR is New Zealand's 13th-largest trading partner, with bilateral trade of NZ$2.1 billion.[^50]

New Zealand's main exports to China are dairy products, travel and tourism, wood and wood products, meat, fish and seafood, and fruit. China's main exports to New Zealand are electronics, machinery, textiles, furniture, and plastics.[^51] [^50]

### Free trade agreement

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/HMZMS_Te_Kaha.jpg/330px-HMZMS_Te_Kaha.jpg)

HMNZS Te Kaha docked in Victoria Harbour, Hong Kong, China, in 2004

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/The_Peoples_Liberation_Army_Navy_FUQING_class_Tanker%2C_HONGZEHU_%28AOR881%29.jpg/330px-The_Peoples_Liberation_Army_Navy_FUQING_class_Tanker%2C_HONGZEHU_%28AOR881%29.jpg)

Chinese Navy ship in Auckland, New Zealand

A free trade agreement (FTA) between China and New Zealand was signed on 7 April 2008 by Premier of China [Wen Jiabao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wen_Jiabao "Wen Jiabao") and Prime Minister of New Zealand [Helen Clark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helen_Clark "Helen Clark") in Beijing.[^52] Under the agreement, about one third of New Zealand exports to China will be free of tariffs from 1 October 2008, with another third becoming tariff free by 2013, and all but 4% by 2019.[^52] In return, 60% of China's exports to New Zealand will become tariff free by 2016 or earlier; more than a third are already duty-free.[^53] Investment, migration, and trade in services will also be facilitated.[^54]

The free trade agreement with China is New Zealand's most significant since the [Closer Economic Relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closer_Economic_Relations "Closer Economic Relations") agreement with Australia was signed in 1983. It was also the first time China has entered into a comprehensive free trade agreement with a [developed country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country").[^55]

The agreement took more than three years to negotiate. On 19 November 2004 Helen Clark and [President of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_China "President of China"), [Hu Jintao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hu_Jintao "Hu Jintao") announced the commencement of negotiations towards an FTA at the [APEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/APEC "APEC") Leaders meeting in [Santiago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santiago,_Chile "Santiago, Chile"), [Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile "Chile"). The first round of negotiations was held in December 2004. Fifteen rounds took place before the FTA was signed in April 2008.[^56]

While the FTA enjoys the support of New Zealand's two largest political parties, Labour and National, other parties such as the [Green Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand "Green Party of Aotearoa New Zealand") and the [Māori Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C4%81ori_Party "Māori Party") opposed the agreement at the time.[^57] [Winston Peters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winston_Peters "Winston Peters") was also a vocal opponent of the agreement, but agreed not to criticise it while acting as [Minister of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs "Minister of Foreign Affairs") overseas (a position he held from 2005 to 2008).[^58]

In early November 2019, New Zealand and China agreed to upgrade their free trade agreement. China has eased restrictions on New Zealand exports and given New Zealand preferential access to the wood and paper trade with China. In return, New Zealand agree to lessen visa restrictions for Chinese tour guides and [Chinese language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language") teachers.[^59] [^60]

On 26 January 2021, New Zealand and China signed a deal to upgrade their free trade agreement to give New Zealand exports greater access to the Chinese market, eliminating or reducing tariffs on New Zealand exports such as dairy, timber, and seafood as well as compliance costs.[^61]

On 1 January 2024, China lifted all tariffs on New Zealand dairy imports including milk powder as part of the NZ-China free trade agreement. This development was welcomed by Minister of Trade and Agriculture [Todd McClay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Todd_McClay "Todd McClay"), who said that it would bring NZ$330 million worth of revenue to the New Zealand economy.[^62]

### Film cooperation

In May 2015, *[The Hollywood Reporter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter "The Hollywood Reporter")* reported that several Chinese, New Zealand, and Canadian film companies including the [China Film Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Film_Group "China Film Group"), the [Qi Tai Culture Development Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Qi_Tai_Culture_Development_Group&action=edit&redlink=1 "Qi Tai Culture Development Group (page does not exist)"), New Zealand's [Huhu Studios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huhu_Studios "Huhu Studios"), and the Canadian Stratagem Entertainment had entered into a US$800 million agreement to produce 17 live-action and animated films over the next six to eight years. As part of the agreement, the China Film Group's animation division [China Film Animation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Film_Animation "China Film Animation") would be working with Huhu Studios to produce an animated film called *Beast of Burden* with a US$20 million budget.[^63] This partnership between Huhu Studios and China Film Animation was the first official New Zealand–Chinese film co-production agreement. The film was subsequently released as *[Mosley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mosley_\(film\) "Mosley (film)")* on 10 October 2019.[^64] [^65]

## Education relations and scientific cooperation

China and New Zealand have a history of education links and exchanges, including bilateral scholarship programmes and academic cooperation. There was a dramatic expansion in student flows and other engagement in the late 1990s. During the 1990s, the number of Chinese nationals studying at public tertiary institutions in New Zealand rose from 49 in 1994, 89 in 1998, 457 in 1999, 1,696 in 2000, 5,236 in 2001, and 11,700 in 2002. The percentage of full fee paying Asian students from China at public tertiary institutions also rose from 1.5% in 1994 to 56.3% by 2002. The increase in Chinese international students in New Zealand accompanied the increase in the percentage of international students at New Zealand universities and polytechnics.[^66]

Between 2003 and 2011, the number of Chinese students studying in New Zealand dropped from 56,000 to about 30,000 by 2011. In 2003, Chinese students accounted for 46% of all international students in New Zealand. By 2011, this figure had dropped to 25%.[^67] As of 2017, China was the largest source of international students in New Zealand. In 2017, there were over 40,000 Chinese student enrolments in New Zealand.[^5]

In 2019 Chinese Vice Consul General [Xiao Yewen](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Xiao_Yewen&action=edit&redlink=1 "Xiao Yewen (page does not exist)") intervened at [Auckland University of Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auckland_University_of_Technology "Auckland University of Technology") in relation to an event marking the 30th Anniversary of the [Tiananmen Square massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiananmen_Square_massacre "Tiananmen Square massacre"). AUT cancelled the booking for the event and it went ahead at a council-owned facility.[^68]

In mid-February 2025, the *[Otago Daily Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otago_Daily_Times "Otago Daily Times")* reported that the [National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_of_Water_and_Atmospheric_Research "National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research") (NIWA) and the Chinese Institute of Deep Sea Science and Engineering were collaborating in deep sea marine exploration in the [Puysegur Trench](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puysegur_Trench "Puysegur Trench"), which lies off the south-west coast of [Fiordland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiordland "Fiordland") in the south [Tasman Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tasman_Sea "Tasman Sea").[^69]

## Diplomatic relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/WellingtonEmbassy-China.jpg/250px-WellingtonEmbassy-China.jpg)

The Chinese Embassy in Wellington

### People's Republic of China

New Zealand is represented in China through the New Zealand Embassy in Beijing, with consulates in Shanghai, [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou "Guangzhou"), [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong") and [Chengdu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chengdu "Chengdu").[^70] The Chengdu Consulate-General was opened by New Zealand Prime Minister [John Key](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Key "John Key") in November 2014.[^71] [^72] China is represented in New Zealand through the Embassy of China in [Wellington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wellington "Wellington"), with consulates in [Auckland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Consulate-General,_Auckland "Chinese Consulate-General, Auckland") and [Christchurch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christchurch "Christchurch").[^73] [^74] [^75]

### Hong Kong

In addition to its diplomatic relations with mainland of China, New Zealand also maintains diplomatic and economic relations with the Hong Kong [Special Administrative Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_administrative_regions_of_China "Special administrative regions of China"). In March 2010, New Zealand and Hong Kong entered into a [bilateral economic partnership agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong%E2%80%93New_Zealand_Closer_Economic_Partnership_Agreement "Hong Kong–New Zealand Closer Economic Partnership Agreement"). New Zealand maintains a [Consulate-General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consular_missions_in_Hong_Kong "Consular missions in Hong Kong") in Hong Kong, which is also accredited to the [Macau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macau "Macau") SAR. Hong Kong's interests in New Zealand are represented by the Chinese Embassy in Wellington and the Hong Kong Economic and Trade Office in [Sydney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sydney "Sydney").[^76] [^77]

### Republic of China (Taiwan)

Though New Zealand no longer has diplomatic relations with [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan"), New Zealand still maintains trade, economic, and cultural relations with Taiwan. Taiwan has two [Economic and Cultural offices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taipei_Economic_and_Cultural_Office_in_New_Zealand "Taipei Economic and Cultural Office in New Zealand") in Auckland and Wellington. New Zealand also has a [Commerce and Industry Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_Commerce_and_Industry_Office "New Zealand Commerce and Industry Office") in Taipei.[^78] [^79]

## Controversies and disputes

China–New Zealand relations have not been without discord. The crackdown by the Chinese Government on the [Tiananmen Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiananmen_Square "Tiananmen Square") demonstrations of June 1989 was strongly condemned in New Zealand and official ministerial contact was suspended for more than a year.[^92] Other disagreements between Wellington and Beijing have included the suppression of political liberties, Chinese militarisation, the sale of weapons and nuclear technology to Middle Eastern countries, Chinese nuclear testing, and Chinese policies towards Taiwan, Tibet,[^93] [^94] and the [Uyghur Muslim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghurs "Uyghurs") minority in China's [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang") province.[^95] [^96]

### Influence operations and espionage

In September 2017, the [University of Canterbury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Canterbury "University of Canterbury") [political scientist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_scientist "Political scientist") and China expert Dr [Anne-Marie Brady](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anne-Marie_Brady "Anne-Marie Brady") presented a conference paper entitled "Magic Weapons: China's political influence activities under Xi Jinping" alleging that the Chinese Government was using local Chinese community organisations and ethnic media as part of a " [united front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_front_\(China\) "United front (China)") " strategy and the [One Belt One Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_Belt_One_Road "One Belt One Road") initiative to advance Chinese [soft power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_power "Soft power") influence in New Zealand. Alleged Chinese activities have included using "united front" organisations to promote support for Beijing among the Chinese New Zealand community, monitoring Chinese students academics, cultivating relations with New Zealand's political and business elites, and encouraging Chinese diaspora participation in New Zealand politics. Key "united front" organisations have included the [New Zealand China Friendship Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_China_Friendship_Society "New Zealand China Friendship Society"), Peaceful Reunification of China Association of New Zealand (PRCANZ), the New Zealand Overseas Chinese Service Centre, and local branches of the [Chinese Students and Scholars Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Students_and_Scholars_Association "Chinese Students and Scholars Association").[^97]

According to Brady's research, Chinese state agencies like the [Xinhua News Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinhua_News_Agency "Xinhua News Agency") had forged cooperation agreements with several NZ Chinese media outlets including the *Chinese Herald*, FM 90.6, Panda TV, Channel 37, *Chinese Times*, Kiwi Style, SkyKiwi, World TV, and NCTV, bringing them in line with Beijing's agenda. Brady also raised concerns about Chinese united front efforts to cultivate the support of politicians from the New Zealand National, Labour, and [ACT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ACT_New_Zealand "ACT New Zealand") parties including National [Member of Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_of_Parliament_\(New_Zealand\) "Member of Parliament (New Zealand)") [Jian Yang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jian_Yang_\(politician\) "Jian Yang (politician)"), Labour candidate [Raymond Huo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Huo "Raymond Huo"), and ACT candidate [Kenneth Wang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_Wang_\(politician\) "Kenneth Wang (politician)"). Brady's paper suggested that Yang had once been a Chinese intelligence officer since he had taught at the [People's Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") 's [Air Force Engineering University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_Force_Engineering_University "Air Force Engineering University") and Luoyang PLA University of Foreign Language. In addition, Brady's paper noted that several former National MPs and ministers including [Ruth Richardson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruth_Richardson "Ruth Richardson"), [Chris Tremain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chris_Tremain "Chris Tremain"), [Don Brash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don_Brash "Don Brash"), and former Prime Minister [Jenny Shipley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jenny_Shipley "Jenny Shipley") had joined the boards of several Chinese banks.[^97]

Brady's paper coincided with the [2017 New Zealand general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2017_New_Zealand_general_election "2017 New Zealand general election") and attracted substantial attention from New Zealand politicians, commentators and the media.[^98] The-then Prime Minister [Bill English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_English "Bill English") said he had no concerns about the issues raised by the report while Labour leader [Jacinda Ardern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacinda_Ardern "Jacinda Ardern") said that she did not see a need to follow Australia's lead in scrutinising Chinese influence in domestic affairs but vowed to look at the issue further. Don Brash, who was named in the report for his co-directorship of the [Industrial Bank of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Bank_of_China "Industrial Bank of China") in New Zealand, stated that China was no different than other great powers in wanting to extend its influence and cultivate allies. Huo, who was named in the report, stated "that there was a fine line between what Brady has alleged and the genuine promotion of the NZ-China relationship." Former Prime Minister Helen Clark responded that New Zealand should engage with major powers in the Asia-Pacific region but "should not be naive in its interaction with them." Shipley denied being a "mouthpiece" of the Chinese government.[^99] [^100]

Following a 2024 documentary about Chinese government interference in New Zealand by [Stuff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stuff_\(website\) "Stuff (website)"), the Chinese embassy issued a statement warning New Zealand that further investigative journalism on the issue could "harm" the country.[^101]

### Huawei 5G ban

In late November 2018, the New Zealand Government banned the Chinese telecommunications company [Huawei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huawei "Huawei") from supplying mobile equipment to national telecommunications company [Spark New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spark_New_Zealand "Spark New Zealand") 's 5G network. This was done at the advice of NZ's signals intelligence agency, the [Government Communications Security Bureau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_Communications_Security_Bureau "Government Communications Security Bureau"), which cited a "significant network security risk." The New Zealand ban was linked to similar efforts by other Western governments, including the United States, the United Kingdom, and Australia, to restrict the usage of Huawei products and services in their 5G networks as well as the ongoing [China-United States trade war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China-United_States_trade_war "China-United States trade war").[^102] [^103] GCSB Minister [Andrew Little](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Little_\(New_Zealand_politician\) "Andrew Little (New Zealand politician)") defended the ban, citing China's [National Intelligence Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Intelligence_Law "National Intelligence Law"), which compels Chinese corporates and citizens to co-operate and collaborate with Chinese intelligence.[^104]

Huawei New Zealand managing director Yanek Fan criticised the New Zealand Government for treating the company unfairly, while telecommunications provider [2degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2degrees "2degrees") criticised the ban for harming competition.[^105] [^106] [Chinese Foreign Ministry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the People's Republic of China") spokesperson Geng Shuan called on New Zealand to provide a level playing field for Chinese companies. Meanwhile, the CCP-owned tabloid *[Global Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Times "Global Times")* warned that the Huawei ban would hurt New Zealand's industry and consumers.[^107]

In early 2019, various New Zealand media speculated that the Chinese cancellation of the 2019 New Zealand-China Year of Tourism event at [Te Papa Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Te_Papa_Museum "Te Papa Museum") in Wellington and the decision to deny an [Air New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_New_Zealand "Air New Zealand") flight landing rights were connected to the Huawei ban. Air New Zealand Flight NZ289 had initially been denied landing rights due to references in the flight plan to [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") as an independent state, something at odds with the [One China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China "One China") policy.[^108] [^109] [^110] In response to reportage, [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand "Prime Minister of New Zealand") [Jacinda Ardern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacinda_Ardern "Jacinda Ardern") denied that there had been a breakdown in China–New Zealand relations and said that the denial of the Air New Zealand flight landing rights was the result of administrative errors. [Opposition Leader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_\(New_Zealand\) "Leader of the Opposition (New Zealand)") [Simon Bridges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Bridges "Simon Bridges") has criticised the [Labour-led coalition government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sixth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand "Sixth Labour Government of New Zealand") for allegedly damaging China–New Zealand relations.[^111] [^112] According to clarification posts in Weibo, the plane was never registered in the CCAR part 125 required by Civil Aviation Administration of China, and landing without that registration will result in loss of points, affecting their operations in China. The particular plane registered as ZK-NZQ had a cabin configuration that was not usually used on flights to mainland China, and had never before been to mainland China in its five months of service.

During a press conference held in mid-February 2019, Chinese Foreign Ministry Spokesperson [Geng Shuang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geng_Shuang "Geng Shuang") denied that there was a breakdown in bilateral relations in response to the cancellation of the 2019 China-New Zealand Year of Tourism event and Chinese media reports discouraging Chinese tourists from visiting New Zealand. He clarified that the Chinese Embassy and consulate generals in New Zealand had issued statements advising their nationals to take precautions against theft and robbery while visiting New Zealand. Shuang characterised the Chinese-New Zealand relationship as "sound and steady." [^113] [^114]

In mid-July 2020, GCSB Minister Little confirmed that New Zealand would not ban Huawei equipment in response to similar decisions by the British and United States governments to exclude Huawei from their 5G networks on national security grounds. Telecommunications Users Association chief executive Craig Young welcomed the Government's announcement, saying that a ban would force companies with Huawei equipment to replace expensive equipment due to the integrated nature of the country's 2G, 3G and 4G networks.[^115] [^116] In response to the Government's announcement, Huawei NZ's deputy managing director Andrew Bowater emphasised the company's commitment to helping customers deal with the effects of the [COVID-19 pandemic in New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_New_Zealand "COVID-19 pandemic in New Zealand").[^115] [^117]

### China's Xinjiang policies

In July 2019, the UN ambassadors from 22 nations, including New Zealand, signed a joint letter to the UNHRC condemning China's [mistreatment of the Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution_of_Uyghurs_in_China "Persecution of Uyghurs in China") as well as its mistreatment of other minority groups, urging the Chinese government to close the [Xinjiang internment camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang_internment_camps "Xinjiang internment camps").[^118]

On 20 July, Prime Minister [Jacinda Ardern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacinda_Ardern "Jacinda Ardern") criticised China's treatment of Uyghurs, the new [Hong Kong national security law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Hong_Kong_national_security_law "2020 Hong Kong national security law"), and Chinese opposition to Taiwanese membership of the [World Health Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization "World Health Organization") while addressing the China Business Summit in Auckland.[^119] In response, Chinese Ambassador Wu Xi warned New Zealand not to interfere in Chinese internal affairs, stating that "we should not take our relationship for granted and... should make sure that our bilateral relations are immune from various virus in these trying times." [^120]

In late April 2021, the libertarian [ACT party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ACT_New_Zealand "ACT New Zealand") sponsored motion asking the New Zealand Parliament to debate and vote on whether China's alleged oppression of the Uyghur minority constituted a [genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genocide "Genocide").[^121] This motion was supported by the [Green Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand "Green Party of Aotearoa New Zealand") and the [Māori Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C4%81ori_Party "Māori Party").[^122] [^123] In response, [Minister of Trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_Trade_\(New_Zealand\) "Minister of Trade (New Zealand)") [Damien O'Connor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damien_O%27Connor "Damien O'Connor") warned that the Uyghur genocide motion could have significant repercussions for China–New Zealand relations. The Chinese Ambassador Wu Xi also issued a statement warning against foreign interference and dismissed talk of forced labour and genocide as "lies" fabricated by anti-China elements.[^124]

On 4 May, the ruling Labour Party successfully revised the motion to discuss concerns about human rights abuses in Xinjiang while omitting the term genocide.[^125] [^126] On 5 May, the New Zealand Parliament unanimously accepted a motion stating that "severe human rights abuses" were happening in Xinjiang and called on the Government "to work with all relevant instruments of international law to bring these abuses to an end." [^127] On 5 May, the Chinese Embassy issued a statement claiming that the motion was based on a "groundless accusation on China over human rights abuse" and interfered in Chinese internal affairs.[^128] [^129]

### 2019 Hong Kong protests

In early August 2019, the New Zealand government rebuked Chinese diplomats over recent comments and actions in which they sought to suppress freedom of speech and voiced support for violent opposition to Hong Kong protestors in New Zealand.[^130] Earlier, the [Chinese Consulate-General in Auckland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Consulate-General,_Auckland "Chinese Consulate-General, Auckland") had praised the "patriotic actions" of Chinese students who had confronted a group of pro-Hong Kong democracy student activists at the [University of Auckland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Auckland "University of Auckland").[^131] The Hong Kong student activists had set up a [Lennon Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lennon_Wall "Lennon Wall") to express solidarity with the [2019–20 Hong Kong protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019%E2%80%9320_Hong_Kong_protests "2019–20 Hong Kong protests"). One of the Chinese students had reportedly assaulted a Hong Kong student during an altercation at the university in late July 2019.[^132] [^133] [Ministry of Foreign Affairs and Trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade_\(New_Zealand\) "Ministry of Foreign Affairs and Trade (New Zealand)") officials cautioned Chinese officials about their interference in New Zealand affairs, and Prime Minister Ardern reiterated New Zealand's commitment to free speech on university campuses.[^130] [ACT Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ACT_New_Zealand "ACT New Zealand") leader [David Seymour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Seymour "David Seymour") also sent a letter to the Consulate-General criticising it for interfering in New Zealand internal affairs.[^134]

In response, Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesperson [Hua Chunying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hua_Chunying "Hua Chunying") defended the actions of Chinese international students and the Consulate-General in Auckland, blaming "Hong Kong independence" activists for stirring up anti-China sentiments at the University of Auckland. While sympathising with the patriotic feelings of Chinese students, Hua reminded Chinese students to abide by [New Zealand law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_law "New Zealand law") and the regulations of their university. Hua also defended the actions of the Consulate-General as "fulfilling its duty" and "beyond reproach." She also called on certain New Zealanders to stop condoning "anti-China separatist activities" under the guise of free speech. Hua also defended the response of the Chinese Consulate-General in [Brisbane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brisbane "Brisbane") following similar clashes between pro-Hong Kong and pro-China students at the [University of Queensland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Queensland "University of Queensland").[^135] [^136]

### Taiwan

Since 1973, New Zealand and Taiwan have maintained unofficial trade and economic relations [^79] in accordance with the [One China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China "One China") policy.[^137] In early May 2020, [Foreign Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(New_Zealand\) "Minister of Foreign Affairs (New Zealand)") [Winston Peters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winston_Peters "Winston Peters") expressed support for Taiwan rejoining the [World Health Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization "World Health Organization") during a press conference. Taiwan has been excluded from the international organisation due to the PRC's [One China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_principle "One China principle").[^138] The Taiwanese Government welcomed Peter's remarks,[^139] while the Chinese Embassy objected to them, reiterating that New Zealand should abide by the One China principle.[^140] The New Zealand Government subsequently backed Taiwan's bid to join the WHO, placing New Zealand alongside Australia and the United States, who took similar positions during the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic").[^141]

On 12 May 2020 Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesperson [Zhao Lijian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhao_Lijian "Zhao Lijian") responded by warning that Peters' remarks violated the One China principle and would hurt bilateral relations between the two countries. He claimed that Beijing had made proper arrangements for Taiwan's participation in global health events and accused Taipei of exploiting the COVID-19 pandemic to seek Taiwanese independence.[^142] Peters stood by his remarks.[^143]

In mid-April 2025 a cross-party delegation of New Zealand members of Parliament visited Taiwan. The delegation consisted of [National Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party "New Zealand National Party") MPs [Stuart Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stuart_Smith_\(politician\) "Stuart Smith (politician)"), [Greg Fleming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greg_Fleming_\(politician\) "Greg Fleming (politician)") and [Hamish Campbell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamish_Campbell "Hamish Campbell"), [New Zealand First](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_First "New Zealand First") MP [Jamie Arbuckle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamie_Arbuckle "Jamie Arbuckle"), [ACT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ACT_New_Zealand "ACT New Zealand") MP [Cameron Luxton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameron_Luxton "Cameron Luxton") and [Labour Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party "New Zealand Labour Party") MPs [Tangi Utikere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangi_Utikere "Tangi Utikere") and [Helen White](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helen_White_\(politician\) "Helen White (politician)"). They met with [Taiwanese President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Taiwan "President of Taiwan") [Lai Ching-Te](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lai_Ching-Te "Lai Ching-Te"), who issued a statement thanking the New Zealand Government, "reiterating the importance of peace and stability across the Taiwan Strait." [^144] On 24 April 2025 the Chinese Embassy in New Zealand issued a statement expressing "strong dissatisfaction" with the visit and accusing the participants of "colluding with 'Taiwan independence' separatist forces during their trip." The Embassy warned that China "reserved the right to take further measures in response to the visit." [^145] In November 2025 [HMNZS *Aotearoa*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HMNZS_Aotearoa "HMNZS Aotearoa") transited the [Taiwan Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_Strait "Taiwan Strait"), followed by Chinese naval forces.[^146]

In June 2026, China banned four New Zealand lawmakers– [Maureen Pugh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maureen_Pugh "Maureen Pugh"), [David Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Wilson_\(New_Zealand_First_politician\) "David Wilson (New Zealand First politician)"), [Laura McClure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laura_McClure "Laura McClure") and [Duncan Webb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duncan_Webb "Duncan Webb") –from entering the country for one year after they visited Taiwan in May. New Zealand and Taiwan criticised the move, with McClure and Webb defending their actions.[^147] [^148] The Chinese Foreign Ministry said their visit to Taiwan violated the [One China policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_policy "One China policy") and amounted to foreign interference in China's internal affairs.[^149] New Zealand Prime Minister [Christopher Luxon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Luxon "Christopher Luxon") described the ban on the four lawmakers as "entirely inappropriate" and said that the NZ government would raise the matter with their Chinese counterparts.[^150]

### Hong Kong national security law, 2020

On 9 July, Foreign Minister [Winston Peters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winston_Peters "Winston Peters") announced that New Zealand would review "the setting of its relationship" with Hong Kong in response to the introduction of the [Hong Kong national security law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Hong_Kong_national_security_law "2020 Hong Kong national security law") earlier that month.[^151] [^152] On 28 July, New Zealand suspended its extradition treaty with Hong Kong, with Foreign Minister Peters stating that the new law "eroded rule-of-law principles" and undermined the "one country, two systems" rule. Prime Minister Ardern also criticised the new law for violating New Zealand's principles of freedom of association and the right to take a political view.[^153] [^154] In response, the Chinese Embassy criticised the New Zealand Government for violating international law and norms, and interfering in China's internal affairs.[^155] On 3 August, China suspended its extradition agreement with New Zealand in retaliation to NZ's suspension of its extradition agreement with Hong Kong. In response, Foreign Minister Peters' office countered that New Zealand lacked an extradition agreement with China.[^156] [^157]

On 6 October, New Zealand joined a group of 39 mainly US-aligned countries alongside [Albania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albania "Albania"), [Bosnia-Herzegovina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosnia-Herzegovina "Bosnia-Herzegovina"), Canada, [Haiti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haiti "Haiti") [Honduras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honduras "Honduras"), and [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan") in issuing a joint statement at the United Nations to denounce China for its treatment of ethnic minorities and for curtailing freedoms in Hong Kong.[^158]

On 18 November, the New Zealand Foreign Minister [Nanaia Mahuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanaia_Mahuta "Nanaia Mahuta") joined her Australian, Canadian, British and United States counterparts in issuing a joint statement condemning the disqualification of pro-democracy legislators as a breach of Hong Kong's autonomy and rights under the framework of the [Sino-British Joint Declaration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-British_Joint_Declaration "Sino-British Joint Declaration").[^159] [^160] In response, the [Chinese Foreign Ministry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the People's Republic of China") 's spokesperson [Zhao Lijian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhao_Lijian "Zhao Lijian") issued a warning to the [Five Eyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Eyes "Five Eyes") countries, stating that "No matter if they have five eyes or 10 eyes, if they dare to harm China's sovereignty, security and development interests, they should beware of their eyes being poked and blinded." [^161] [^162] In response, Mahuta defended New Zealand's commitment to free speech, free media, and democracy.[^160]

### Brereton Report

On 1 December 2020, Prime Minister Ardern expressed concern about a doctored Chinese Foreign Ministry official Zhao Lijian's [Twitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twitter "Twitter") post showing an Australian soldier holding a bloodied knife against the throat of an [Afghan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afghans "Afghans") child, describing the post as "un-factual." Foreign Minister Nanaia Mahuta had also described the tweet as "inflammatory disinformation" and conveyed New Zealand's concerns to Beijing.[^163] The Australian Government had earlier condemned the tweet as "offensive" and "outrageous" and demanded an apology from Beijing. The Chinese post had occurred against the backdrop of the [Brereton Report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brereton_Report "Brereton Report") and recent tensions in [Australia–China relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia%E2%80%93China_relations "Australia–China relations").[^164] [^165] In response, Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesperson [Hua Chunying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hua_Chunying "Hua Chunying") expressed surprise at New Zealand's concern and defended the accuracy of the picture and posts.[^166]

### Disagreements with Five Eyes partners

In mid-April 2021, Foreign Minister Nanaia Mahuta stated that New Zealand would not let the United States–led [Five Eyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Eyes "Five Eyes") alliance dictate its bilateral relationship with China and that New Zealand was uncomfortable with expanding the remit of the intelligence grouping. In that statement Mahuta also described the relationship between New Zealand and China as the relationship between a [taniwha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taniwha "Taniwha") and a dragon. Mahuta's statements came amidst rising disagreements between Wellington and Canberra on how to manage relations with Beijing. The Australian Government has expressed concern about the New Zealand Government's perceived efforts to undermine collective attempts to push back against what it regards as "increasingly aggressive behaviour from Beijing." [^167] [^168]

In response to Mahuta's remarks, Prime Minister Ardern claimed that New Zealand was still committed to the Five Eyes alliance but would not use the group as its first point for messaging on non-security matters. While *[The Telegraph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph "The Daily Telegraph")* 's defence editor [Con Coughlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Con_Coughlin "Con Coughlin") criticised New Zealand for undermining the Five Eyes' efforts to put a united front against Beijing, the *Global Times* praised New Zealand for putting its own national interests over the Five Eyes.[^169] [^170]

### Chinese cyber attacks

On 20 July 2021, the Minister in charge of the [Government Communications Security Bureau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_Communications_Security_Bureau "Government Communications Security Bureau") (GCSB) [Andrew Little](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Little_\(New_Zealand_politician\) "Andrew Little (New Zealand politician)") confirmed that the spy agency had established links between Chinese state-sponsored actors known as " [Advanced Persistent Threat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advanced_persistent_threat "Advanced persistent threat") 40" ([APT40](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/APT40 "APT40")) and malicious cyber activity in New Zealand. In addition, Little confirmed that New Zealand was joining other Western governments including the United States, United Kingdom, Australia and the European Union in condemning the [Chinese Ministry of State Security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_State_Security_\(China\) "Ministry of State Security (China)") and other Chinese state-sponsored actors for their involvement in the [2021 Microsoft Exchange Server data breach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Microsoft_Exchange_Server_data_breach "2021 Microsoft Exchange Server data breach").[^171] [^172] In response, the Chinese Embassy in New Zealand rejected these allegations and lodged a "solemn representation" with the New Zealand Government.[^173]

On 21 July, Foreign Minister Nanaia Mahuta confirmed that New Zealand Foreign Ministry officials had met with Chinese Embassy officials at the request of the Chinese Embassy in response to the cyber attack allegations. The Embassy urged the New Zealand Government to abandon its so-called " [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") mentality." New Zealand exporters have expressed concerns that an escalation of diplomatic tensions could have serious implications for China-New Zealand trade.[^174]

In March 2024 the GCSB's Director-General Andrew Clark, GCSB Minister [Judith Collins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judith_Collins "Judith Collins"), Prime Minister [Christopher Luxon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Luxon "Christopher Luxon") and Foreign Minister [Winston Peters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winston_Peters "Winston Peters") accused the Chinese government of using hacker group [APT40](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/APT40 "APT40") to breach the [New Zealand Parliamentary Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_Parliament "New Zealand Parliament") and the [Parliamentary Council Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_Counsel_Office_\(New_Zealand\) "Parliamentary Counsel Office (New Zealand)") 's computer systems in 2021. In response, a Chinese Embassy spokesperson dismissed the complaint as "groundless and irresponsible accusations" and confirmed it would lodge a complaint with New Zealand officials. Reports of the data hack accompanied reports that the United States, British and Australian governments had sanctioned APT40 for similar activities in their countries.[^175] [^176] Former Prime Minister and Labour leader [Chris Hipkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chris_Hipkins "Chris Hipkins") confirmed he had also been briefed about the attempted cyber attacks back in 2021 but had not publicly disclosed the attack due to procedures around protecting sources, navigating loopholes and managing diplomatic relations with China.[^176]

### 2021 Hong Kong legislative election

Following the [2021 Hong Kong legislative election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Hong_Kong_legislative_election "2021 Hong Kong legislative election") held in December 2021, Foreign Minister Mahuta joined New Zealand's Five Eyes partners in issuing a joint statement criticising the exclusion of opposition candidates and urging China to respect human rights and freedoms in Hong Kong in accordance with the [Sino-British Joint Declaration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-British_Joint_Declaration "Sino-British Joint Declaration").[^177] In response, the Chinese Embassy in Wellington issued a statement claiming the elections were "politically inclusive and fair" and urging the Five Eyes alliance to respect Chinese sovereignty over Hong Kong.[^178]

### 2022 Sino-Solomon Islands defence pact

In late March 2022, Prime Minister Ardern and Foreign Minister Mahuta joined the Australian Government in voicing concerns about a proposed Solomon Islands security agreement with China. This agreement would allow China to deploy military and security forces in the Solomon Islands and to establish a military base.[^179] [^180] [^181]

### AUKUS Pillar 2

On 1 February 2024, Australia agreed to brief New Zealand on [AUKUS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AUKUS "AUKUS") Pillar 2 developments following a joint bilateral meeting between Australian and New Zealand foreign and defence ministers [Penny Wong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penny_Wong "Penny Wong"), [Winston Peters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winston_Peters "Winston Peters"), [Richard Marles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Marles "Richard Marles") and [Judith Collins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judith_Collins "Judith Collins") in [Melbourne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melbourne "Melbourne"). Australian Defence Minister Marles confirmed that Australia would send officials to brief their New Zealand counterparts on Pillar 2, which would focus on the exchange of non-nuclear military technology.[^182] [^183] The four ministers also issued a joint statement expressing concerns about human rights violations in [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang"), [Tibet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibet "Tibet") and [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong").[^184] In response to the joint Australian-NZ meeting, the Chinese Embassy in Wellington issued a statement condemning alleged interference in China's domestic affairs and describing AUKUS as inimical to international nuclear non-proliferation efforts.[^185] [^184] On 12 February, the [New Zealand Labour Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party "New Zealand Labour Party") also abandoned its previous support for the Pillar Two component of AUKUS, with associate foreign spokesperson [Phil Twyford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phil_Twyford "Phil Twyford") describing AUKUS as an "offensive warfighting alliance against China." [^186]

### 2025 China-Cook Islands partnership agreement

In early February 2025, New Zealand Foreign Minister [Winston Peters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winston_Peters "Winston Peters") criticised the [Cook Islands Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_Cook_Islands "Prime Minister of the Cook Islands") [Mark Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Brown_\(Cook_Islands\) "Mark Brown (Cook Islands)") for not consulting New Zealand about plans to sign a strategic partnership agreement with China in mid-February 2025. As an [associated state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_state "Associated state") in a [free association relationship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cook_Islands%E2%80%93New_Zealand_relations "Cook Islands–New Zealand relations") with New Zealand, the Cook Islands and New Zealand are obliged to consult each other on foreign policy and security issues.[^187] [^188] Brown has rejected New Zealand's position on the grounds that the partnership agreement did not involve foreign affairs and defence.[^189] Brown's relations with New Zealand had also been strained after Peters blocked his plans to introduce a separate Cook Islands passport while retaining [New Zealand citizenship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_nationality_law "New Zealand nationality law").[^190]

On 7 February 2025, the Chinese Embassy in New Zealand issued a statement that China and the Cook Islands "have treated each other as equals and pursued mutually beneficial relations since the establishment of diplomatic relations in 1997." [^191] On 10 February, [Chinese Foreign Ministry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(China\) "Ministry of Foreign Affairs (China)") spokesperson [Guo Jiakun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guo_Jiakun "Guo Jiakun") stated that "the relationship between China and the Cook Islands does not target any third party, and should not be disrupted or restrained by any third party." [^192] The Cook Islands and Chinese governments signed the partnership agreement on 14 February. In response, a spokesperson for Peters said that New Zealand would review the agreement in accordance to its national interests and constitutional relationship with the Cook Islands.[^193]

### 2025 Chinese naval exercises

On 20 February 2025, New Zealand Defence Minister [Judith Collins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judith_Collins "Judith Collins") and [Australian Defence Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_for_Defence_\(Australia\) "Minister for Defence (Australia)") [Richard Marles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Marles "Richard Marles") confirmed that the [Australian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_Defence_Force "Australian Defence Force") and [New Zealand Defence Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_Defence_Force "New Zealand Defence Force") were monitoring three Chinese [naval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army_Navy "People's Liberation Army Navy") warships that were sailing through [international waters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_waters "International waters") near [Sydney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sydney "Sydney").[^194] The Chinese warships carried out three live-fire exercises, disrupting several flights across the [Tasman Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tasman_Sea "Tasman Sea").[^195] [^196] On 26 February, Foreign Minister Peters met with Chinese Foreign Minister [Wang Yi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Yi_\(politician\) "Wang Yi (politician)") to raise New Zealand's concerns about ongoing Chinese naval exercises in the Tasman Sea and China's [recent partnership agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Cook_Islands_relations#2025_partnership_agreement "China–Cook Islands relations") with the [Cook Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cook_Islands "Cook Islands").[^83] Wang agreed to consider New Zealand and Australian concerns that its military did not give enough notice before staging live-fire exercises in the Tasman Sea.[^197]

### Censorship by China

In July 2025, a diplomatic incident emerged between China and New Zealand after the Chinese Embassy attempted to pressure the [DocEdge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doc_Edge "Doc Edge") Festival in Auckland to cancel the screening of a documentary critical of China's activities in the [South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Sea "South China Sea"). The festival organisers received a formal letter from the embassy objecting to the film's portrayal of maritime disputes and reiterating China's historical claims in the region. In response, DocEdge publicly defended its programming choices, affirmed its commitment to curatorial independence and free expression, and published the embassy's letter in full. The festival encouraged viewers to watch the film and draw their own conclusions. The incident highlighted growing concerns over foreign diplomatic efforts to influence cultural and media events in New Zealand.[^198] [^199] The Chinese Consulate General in Auckland had requested cancellation of further screenings of the documentary which had already premiered on 30 June. DocEdge published the consulate's statement on its website, citing a "commitment to transparency and fairness".[^199] The documentary in question was *[Food Delivery: Fresh From the West Philippine Seas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_Delivery:_Fresh_from_the_West_Philippine_Sea "Food Delivery: Fresh from the West Philippine Sea")*.

### Air and maritime operations

On 18 April 2026, representatives from the [Chinese Ministry of National Defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_National_Defense_\(China\) "Ministry of National Defense (China)") and [Chinese Foreign Ministry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(China\) "Ministry of Foreign Affairs (China)") lodged a diplomatic complaint with the New Zealand Government. They alleged that an [RNZAF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNZAF "RNZAF") [Boeing P-8 Poseidon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_P-8_Poseidon "Boeing P-8 Poseidon") had violated Chinese airspace over the [Yellow Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yellow_Sea "Yellow Sea") and [East China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_China_Sea "East China Sea"). The [New Zealand Defence Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_Defence_Force "New Zealand Defence Force") has denied breaching Chinese airspace, stating that it was monitoring shipping near [North Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Korea "North Korea") for violations of [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") sanctions as part of the multinational Pacific Security Maritime Exchange.[^200] [^201] [Massey University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massey_University "Massey University") senior defence and security studies fellow John Battersby attributed the stern Chinese response to tensions in the Pacific over aid, policing agreements and the Chinese-Cook Islands partnership.[^201]
