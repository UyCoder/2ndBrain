The **history of Sino-Korean relations** dates back to prehistoric times, which has been marked by both periods of cooperation and antagonism. The [Han dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_dynasty "Han dynasty") [conquered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_conquest_of_Gojoseon "Han conquest of Gojoseon") [Gojoseon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Chos%C5%8Fn "Old Chosŏn") in the 2nd century and the established of the [Four Commanderies of Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_Commanderies_of_Han "Four Commanderies of Han") in the northern portion of the Korean Peninsula. The Korean kingdom of [Goguryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo "Goguryeo") aided [Sima Yi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sima_Yi "Sima Yi") of [Cao Wei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Wei "Cao Wei") led a successful [campaign against his rival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sima_Yi%27s_Liaodong_campaign "Sima Yi's Liaodong campaign") [Gongsun Yuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gongsun_Yuan "Gongsun Yuan"). The alliance later broke down, starting the [Goguryeo–Wei War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo%E2%80%93Wei_War "Goguryeo–Wei War"), which ended in Cao Wei victory. Later, taking advantage of Wei weakness, Goguryeo eventually reconquered the former territories of Gojoseon. The [Sui dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sui_dynasty "Sui dynasty") launched [four unsuccessful campaigns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo%E2%80%93Sui_War "Goguryeo–Sui War") in the 7th century to subdue Goguryeo; Sui's defeats in Korea contributed to the collapse of the dynasty. Goguryeo fought [further wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo%E2%80%93Tang_War "Goguryeo–Tang War") with the [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty"), which allied with the Korean kingdom of [Silla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silla "Silla"). Tang and Silla eventually prevailed over Goguryeo, leading Tang to establish the [Protectorate General to Pacify the East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protectorate_General_to_Pacify_the_East "Protectorate General to Pacify the East"). [Unified Silla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unified_Silla "Unified Silla") and Tang fought a [war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silla%E2%80%93Tang_War "Silla–Tang War") subsequently, in which Silla prevailed. Silla fell to [Goryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goryeo "Goryeo") in 935.

The [Khitan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khitan_people "Khitan people") -led [Liao dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liao_dynasty "Liao dynasty") of China fought a [war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goryeo%E2%80%93Khitan_War "Goryeo–Khitan War") with Goryeo, in which Goryeo prevailed, gaining parts of the northern Korean peninsula from Liao in exchange for submitting tribute. The [Song](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty "Song dynasty") regarded Goryeo as a potential military ally and maintained friendly relations as equal partners. In 1115 the Jurchen founded the [Jin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jin_dynasty_\(1115%E2%80%931234\) "Jin dynasty (1115–1234)"), and in 1125 Jin annihilated Liao and started [invasion of Song](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jingkang_incident "Jingkang incident"), leading Goryeo to declare itself to be a tributary state of Jin in 1126. During the 13th century, the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire") [invaded China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_invasion_of_China "Mongol invasion of China") and defeated the Jin and Song dynasties; the Mongol-led [Yuan dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_dynasty "Yuan dynasty") [invaded Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_invasions_of_Korea "Mongol invasions of Korea") from 1231 to 1259, with the Yuan prevailing. After Yuan rule began to destabilize, [King Gongmin of Goryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gongmin_of_Goryeo "Gongmin of Goryeo") seized the opportunity to rebel and declared Goryeo's independence. Following the fall of the Yuan and the emergence of the [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") in 1368, a proposed attack against the Ming resulted in [a coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wihwado_Retreat "Wihwado Retreat") led by General [Yi Sŏnggye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yi_S%C5%8Fnggye "Yi Sŏnggye") that ended the Goryeo dynasty and the establishment of the [Joseon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseon "Joseon"); Ming gave up competing territorial claims while Joseon accepted tributary status.

The Ming had assisted Joseon during [Toyotomi Hideyoshi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi "Toyotomi Hideyoshi") 's [invasion of Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_invasions_of_Korea_\(1592%E2%80%931598\) "Japanese invasions of Korea (1592–1598)"), in which the [Wanli Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wanli_Emperor "Wanli Emperor") sent a total of 221,500 troops. Joseon had also used [Classical Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Chinese "Classical Chinese") as a common script alongside [Korean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_language "Korean language"), and its central government was modelled after the Chinese system. The [Later Jin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Later_Jin_\(1616%E2%80%931636\) "Later Jin (1616–1636)") and its successor [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty"), which replaced the Ming, invaded Joseon twice, in [1627](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Later_Jin_invasion_of_Joseon "Later Jin invasion of Joseon") and [1636](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_invasion_of_Joseon "Qing invasion of Joseon"), forcing [Injo of Joseon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injo_of_Joseon "Injo of Joseon") into submission. Following the [First Sino-Japanese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Sino-Japanese_War "First Sino-Japanese War"). the Qing dynasty lost tributary relations with Korea diplomatic relations between the Qing dynasty and the [Korean Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_Empire "Korean Empire") were established in 1899, but relations were halted in 1905 as a result of the [Eulsa Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan%E2%80%93Korea_Treaty_of_1905 "Japan–Korea Treaty of 1905") that made Korea a Japanese protectorate and which was then annexed by the [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_Japan "Empire of Japan") in 1910. Later, the Chinese government recognized the formation of the [Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_the_Republic_of_Korea "Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea") in 1919. Following [Japan's surrender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surrender_of_Japan "Surrender of Japan"), Korea was split into [North Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Korea "North Korea") and [South Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Korea "South Korea"). Bilateral diplomatic relations between South Korea and the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") were established in 1948, making the ROC the first country to recognize the Republic of Korea as the sole legitimate government of Korea. The People's Republic of China, established in 1949, recognized only the Democratic People's Republic of Korea during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), and backed the country against South Korea during the [Korean War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_War "Korean War"). In the 1980s, relations between the PRC and South Korea warmed, and the two countries established diplomatic relations in 1992.

## Han and Gojoseon

According to *[Samguk yusa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samguk_yusa "Samguk yusa")*, [Dangun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dangun "Dangun") Joseon was the first state that represented Korean cultural identity.[^1] Although controversial, a legend tells that in around 1100 BC a Chinese sage named [Jizi (Gija)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jizi "Jizi") and his intellectuals fled from the [Shang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shang_dynasty "Shang dynasty") to avoid political turmoil and sought asylum in [Gojoseon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Chos%C5%8Fn "Old Chosŏn"), and active cultural trades ensued after. The "Monograph on Geography" (*Dili zhi* 地理志) of the *[Book of Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Han "Book of Han")* (1st century AD) claims that Jizi had taught the people of Joseon [agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture"), [sericulture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sericulture "Sericulture"), and [weaving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weaving "Weaving"), as well as proper [ceremony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_\(Confucian\) "Li (Confucian)").[^2] Jae-hoon Shim interprets the following sentence in that section of the *Hanshu* as claiming that Jizi also introduced the law of " [Eight Prohibitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eight_Prohibitions "Eight Prohibitions") " (犯禁八條) in Joseon.[^3] The *[Records of Three Kingdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Records_of_Three_Kingdoms "Records of Three Kingdoms")* (first published in the early fifth century) claims that the descendants of Jizi reigned as kings of Joseon for forty generations until they were overthrown by [Wei Man](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wei_Man "Wei Man"), a man from the [state of Yan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_of_Yan "State of Yan"), in 194 BC.[^4]

According to his commentary to the *Shiji*, [Du Yu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Du_Yu "Du Yu") (first half of the 3rd century) states that the tomb of Jizi was located in Meng Prefecture of the State of Liang (modern-day Henan). This suggests that the story of Jizi's association with Joseon was not necessarily prevailing although the narrative seen in the *[Hanshu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanshu "Hanshu")* later became common.

As historian Jae-hoon Shim concludes, only during the Han dynasty (206 BC – 220 AD) did Jizi begin to be associated with Joseon, and only after the Han were his descendants identified as the Joseon royal family.[^5] Jizi remains a controversial figure, due in part to the difficulty of verifying his existence. Only in the thirteenth century did Korean texts start to integrate Gija more fully into Korean history. The *[Samguk yusa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samguk_yusa "Samguk yusa")* (1281) explained that after being enfeoffed by [King Wu of Zhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Wu_of_Zhou "King Wu of Zhou"), Gija replaced [Dangun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dangun "Dangun") 's descendants as the ruler of [Joseon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Chos%C5%8Fn "Old Chosŏn"), whereas *[Jewang Ungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewang_Ungi "Jewang Ungi")* (1287) identified Dangun and Gija as the first rulers of former and latter Joseon respectively.[^6] Most premodern Korean historians after that accepted that Jizi had replaced another indigenous power (represented by Dangun) in [Old Joseon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gojoseon "Gojoseon").[^6] Nonetheless, the Jizi Mythology plays a defining role in explaining the pre-modern relationship between Korea and China.

In 194 BC, [Wei Man](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wi_Man_of_Gojoseon "Wi Man of Gojoseon"), a Chinese general from [Yan state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yan_\(state\) "Yan (state)"), sought refugee along with Yan immigrants in Gojoseon following [Qin's wars of unification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin%27s_wars_of_unification "Qin's wars of unification") that ended the centuries long [Warring States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warring_States "Warring States") period and established the first highly centralized, unified state in Chinese history. Wi Man was given a duty to guard borders in the [Liaodong Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liaodong_Peninsula "Liaodong Peninsula") by Gojoseon. Wei Man eventually became a leader of new immigrants and indigenous population as his Chinese refugee population introduced Chinese technology into Gojoseon while adopting Korean traditions and clothing.[^7] Eventually his growing popularity allowed him to usurp the throne of [King Jun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Jun "King Jun") who fled south to [Jin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jin_\(Korean_state\) "Jin (Korean state)").[^8] The expanding military might and technological growth of Wiman Joseon soon threatened Han China, and the diplomatic relationship between the two countries deteriorated quickly. Wi Man's grandson, King [Ugeo of Gojoseon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ugeo_of_Gojoseon "Ugeo of Gojoseon"), sparked conflict when he refused to let Gojoseon's southern neighboring state, [Jin](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jin_Confederacy_\(Korean_state\)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Jin Confederacy (Korean state) (page does not exist)"), send its ambassadors through Gojoseon's territory in order to reach the Han dynasty.

The [Gojoseon–Han War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_conquest_of_Gojoseon "Han conquest of Gojoseon") occurred around 109 BC(2nd Century ADE). When Emperor [Wu of Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wu_of_Han "Wu of Han") sent an ambassador She He (涉何) to [Wanggeom-seong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wanggeom-seong "Wanggeom-seong") to negotiate Jin's right of passage through Gojoseon. King Ugeo refused and had his son, Prince [Wi Jang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wi_Jang "Wi Jang") (長降) whose task was to escort the ambassador back home. However, when they got close to Han's borders, the ambassador assassinated [Wi Jang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wi_Jang "Wi Jang") (長降) and claimed to Emperor Wu that he had defeated Joseon in battle. Emperor Wu, unaware of this deception, made him the military commander of the Commandery of Liaodong. The outraged King Ugeo made a raid on Liaodong(province in Northeast China) and killed She He. Scholars also hypothesize that the initiation of war may also have been because the Han dynasty was concerned that Gojoseon would ally with the [Xiongnu against the Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han%E2%80%93Xiongnu_War "Han–Xiongnu War").[^9]

As a result, the Han dynasty launched a two-pronged attack, one by land and one by sea, against Gojoseon which totaled to at least 50,000 soldiers.[^7] The two forces attacking Gojoseon were unable to coordinate well with each other and the majority of the 50,000 strong force was destroyed.[^7] When Emperor Wu received news of these defeats, he wished to reestablish peaceful relations between Han and Gojoseon which King Ugeo agreed to. King Ugeo sent his son and a gift of 5,000 horses to the Han court to pay tribute and bring about peaceful negotiations. However, when the prince and his escort of 10,000 soldiers reached the Yalu River, Han General Xun Zhi reasoned that they should lay down arms. The prince suspected that Xun Zhi was planning on murdering him and so went back to Wanggeom-seong, resuming the war.[^10]

The second Han invasion of Gojoseon fared much better, and by 108 BC Wanggeom fell to Han forces after a months long siege with most of the Gojoseon leadership either surrendering to the Han or killed. Han took over the Gojoseon lands and established [Four Commanderies of Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_Commanderies_of_Han "Four Commanderies of Han") in the western part of former Gojoseon.[^11]

The Han dynasty's ultimate victory resulted in the defeat of Gojoseon and the establishment of the [Four Commanderies of Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_Commanderies_of_Han "Four Commanderies of Han"). Chinese now found control over its northeast frontier and northern Korea, which resulted in provoked unity among the local tribes and indigenous population, and the establishment of [Goguryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo "Goguryeo") on the Korea Peninsula, which took advantage of Chinese conflict with the [Xiongnu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiongnu "Xiongnu") to expand into Liaodong.[^12] Goguryeo eventually reconquered the former territories of Gojoseon(those territories being the province of Liaodong in Northeastern China), then proceeded to keep expanding in all directions.

## Cao Wei and Goguryeo

In 238, [Sima Yi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sima_Yi "Sima Yi") of [Cao Wei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Wei "Cao Wei") led a successful [campaign against his rival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sima_Yi%27s_Liaodong_campaign "Sima Yi's Liaodong campaign") [Gongsun Yuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gongsun_Yuan "Gongsun Yuan"), with the aid of [Goguryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo "Goguryeo"). This led to the Chinese recapture of Liaodong as well as establishing great contacts with Korean kingdoms. By 242, the alliance between Cao Wei and Goguryeo broke down when [Dongcheon of Goguryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dongcheon_of_Goguryeo "Dongcheon of Goguryeo") initiated a raid into Wei territory. In response, the [Goguryeo–Wei War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo%E2%80%93Wei_War "Goguryeo–Wei War") erupted in 244, and the Cao Wei launched two successful campaigns against Goguryeo. During the first campaign, Chinese General [Guanqiu Jian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanqiu_Jian "Guanqiu Jian") led 10,000 infantry and cavalry into Goguryeo and was met by King Doncheon's army of 20,000 at the Battle of Liangkou. Sources differ on the details of the battle itself. Korean sources such as the [Samguk sagi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samguk_sagi "Samguk sagi") claiming that Goguryeo initially won the first two of the three battles at Liangkou and inflicting 6,000 Chinese losses before the Cao Wei counterattacked and inflicted 18,000 Korean losses.[^13] Meanwhile, Chinese sources claim that the Korean forces repeatedly lost the engagements before being routed.[^14] Regardless of the details, all sources agree that the Cao Wei decisively triumphed over Goguryeo forces by the end of the first campaign, which finally ended with the sacking of the Goguryeo capital and resettlement of many of its inhabitants. Goguryeo fared little better during the second campaign, which was largely marked by King Dongcheon desperately fleeing deeper into the Korean peninsula to evade Cao Wei forces. Although Goguryeo forces and their allied tribes were unable to defeat the Cao Wei in battle, Chinese forces overextended themselves deep inside Korea, and finally decided to withdraw despite multiple victories over Goguryeo and their allies such as Okjeo.

So great was the defeat that Goguryeo faded from Chinese historical records for nearly half a century before it finally recovered its strength.[^15] According to the Korean source 15 years later Goguryeo defeated Wei at Yangmaenggok.[^16]

The history of Goguryeo in the latter half of the 3rd century was characterized by Goguryeo's attempts to consolidate nearby regions and restore stability as it dealt with rebellions and foreign invaders that resulted from Wei's victorious campaign against Goguryeo,[^17] including Wei again during 259 in which Goguryeo defeated Wei at Yangmaek, and the Sushen during 280 in which Goguryeo launched a counterattack on the Sushen and occupied their capital.[^18]

According to the *[Samguk sagi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samguk_sagi "Samguk sagi")*, during the Wei invasion in 259, [King Jungcheon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jungcheon_of_Goguryeo "Jungcheon of Goguryeo") assembled 5,000 of his elite cavalry and defeated the invading Wei army at a valley in Yangmaek, killing 8,000 enemies. Goguryeo's fortunes rose again during [King Micheon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micheon_of_Goguryeo "Micheon of Goguryeo") 's rule (300–331), when the king took advantage of the weakness in Wei's successor the [Jin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jin_dynasty_\(265%E2%80%93420\) "Jin dynasty (265–420)") and wrestled the commanderies of Lelang and Daifang from central Chinese control.[^19] After more than seventy years of recovery, Goguryeo had finally transformed into a proper Korean Empire as it integrated various tribal communities of the Okjeo and Dongye.

In terms of [historiography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historiography "Historiography"), the expeditions of the second campaign are significant for providing detailed information on the various Korean tribes and peoples of the Korean Peninsula and Manchuria, such as Goguryeo, Buyeo, Okjeo, Ye, and Yilou. The expedition, unprecedented in scale in those regions, brought first-hand knowledge about the topography, climate, population, language, manners, and customs of these areas to Chinese cognizance, and was duly recorded into the *[Weilüe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weil%C3%BCe "Weilüe")* by the contemporary historian [Yu Huan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yu_Huan "Yu Huan").[^20] Though the original *Weilüe* is now lost, its contents were preserved in the *Records of Three Kingdoms*, where the reports from the Goguryeo expedition are included in the "Chapter on Eastern Barbarians" (東夷傳, *[Dongyi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dongyi "Dongyi") Zhuan*) — considered the most important single source of information for the culture and society of early states and peoples on the Korean Peninsula.[^18]

The [Xianbei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xianbei "Xianbei") -ruled [Former Yan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Former_Yan "Former Yan") dynasty sacked the Goguryeo capital and made the king pay tribute. However, Goguryeo triumphed over the [Xianbei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xianbei "Xianbei") decades later when [Gwanggaeto the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gwanggaeto_the_Great "Gwanggaeto the Great") defeated [Later Yan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Later_Yan "Later Yan") and conquered Liaodong and most of Manchuria.[^12]

## Sui, Tang, Goguryeo and Balhae

[Sui dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sui_dynasty "Sui dynasty") launched [four unsuccessful campaigns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo%E2%80%93Sui_War "Goguryeo–Sui War") to subdue Goguryeo. The [Emperor Yang of Sui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_Yang_of_Sui "Emperor Yang of Sui") wanted to establish a Sui-led order in East Asia after unifying [China proper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_proper "China proper"). Goguryeo opposed this and started to prepare for defense against Sui dynasty.[^21] The [Sui dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sui_dynasty "Sui dynasty") 's reunification of China for the first time in centuries was met with alarm in Goguryeo, and [Pyeongwon of Goguryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyeongwon_of_Goguryeo "Pyeongwon of Goguryeo") began preparations for a future war by augmenting military provisions and training more troops.[^22] Although Sui was far larger and stronger than Goguryeo, the Baekje-Silla Alliance which had previously driven Goguryeo from the Han Valley in the mid 6th century had fallen apart, and thus Goguryeo's southern border was secure. Initially, Goguryeo tried to appease Sui by offering tribute as Korean kingdoms had done under the [Tributary system of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tributary_system_of_China "Tributary system of China"). However, Goguryeo continued insistence on an equal relationship with Sui, its reinstatement of the imperial title "Taewang" (Emperor in Korean) of the East and its continued raids into Sui territory greatly angered the Sui Court. Furthermore, Silla and Baekje, both under threat from Goguryeo, requested Sui assistance against Goguryeo as all three Korean kingdoms had desired to seize the others' territories to rule the peninsula, and attempted to curry Sui's favor to achieve these goals.

Goguryeo's expansion and its attempts to equalize the relationship conflicted with [Sui China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sui_dynasty "Sui dynasty") and increased tensions. In 598, Goguryeo made a preemptive attack on [Liaoxi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liaoxi_Province "Liaoxi Province") which led to the [Battle of Linyuguan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Linyuguan "Battle of Linyuguan"), but was beaten back by Sui forces.[^23] This caused [Emperor Wen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_Wen_of_Sui "Emperor Wen of Sui") to launch a counterattack by land and sea that ended in disaster for Sui.[^24] As a result, the Sui launched a series of invasions that took place in the years 598, 612, 613, and 614.

Sui's most disastrous campaign against Goguryeo was in 612, in which Sui, according to the History of the Sui Dynasty, mobilized 30 division armies, about 1,133,800 combat troops. Pinned along Goguryeo's line of fortifications on the [Liao River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liao_River "Liao River"), a detachment of nine division armies, about 305,000 troops, bypassed the main defensive lines and headed towards the Goguryeo capital of Pyongyang to link up with Sui naval forces, who had reinforcements and supplies.

However, Goguryeo was able to defeat the Sui navy, thus when the Sui's nine division armies finally reached Pyongyang, they didn't have the supplies for a lengthy siege. Sui troops retreated, but General [Ŭlchi Mundŏk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C5%AClchi_Mund%C5%8Fk "Ŭlchi Mundŏk") led the Goguryeo troops to victory by luring the Sui into an ambush outside of Pyongyang. At the [Battle of Salsu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Salsu "Battle of Salsu"), Goguryeo soldiers released water from a dam, which split the Sui army and cut off their escape route. Of the original 305,000 soldiers of Sui's nine division armies, it is said that only 2,700 escaped to Sui China.

The 613 and 614 campaigns were aborted after launch—the 613 campaign was terminated when the Sui general [Yang Xuangan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_Xuangan "Yang Xuangan") rebelled against [Emperor Yang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_Yang_of_Sui "Emperor Yang of Sui"), while the 614 campaign was terminated after Goguryeo offered a truce and returned Husi Zheng (斛斯政), a defecting Sui general who had fled to Goguryeo, Emperor Yang later had Husi executed. Emperor Yang planned another attack on Goguryeo in 615, but due to Sui's deteroriating internal state he was never able to launch it. Sui was weakened due to rebellions against Emperor Yang's rule and his failed attempts to conquer Goguryeo. They could not attack further because the provinces in the Sui heartland would not send logistical support.

Emperor Yang's disastrous defeats in Korea greatly contributed to the collapse of the Sui dynasty.[^24] [^25] [^26]

Goguryeo also engaged in many wars with the [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty"), but it eventually lost as [Silla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silla "Silla") took side with the Tang in the [Goguryeo–Tang War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo%E2%80%93Tang_War "Goguryeo–Tang War"). In the winter of 642, [King Yeongnyu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yeongnyu_of_Goguryeo "Yeongnyu of Goguryeo") was apprehensive about [Yŏn Kaesomun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y%C5%8Fn_Kaesomun "Yŏn Kaesomun"), one of the great nobles of Goguryeo,[^27] and plotted with other officials to kill him. However, Yŏn Kaesomun caught news of the plot and killed Yeongnyu and 100 officials, initiating a [coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "Coup d'état"). He proceeded to enthrone Yeongnyu's nephew, Go Jang, as [King Bojang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bojang_of_Goguryeo "Bojang of Goguryeo") while wielding de facto control of Goguryeo himself as the [generalissimo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalissimo "Generalissimo") ([Korean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_language "Korean language"): 대막리지; [Hanja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanja "Hanja"): 大莫離支). At the outset of his rule, Yŏn Kaesomun took a brief conciliatory stance toward Tang China. For instance, he supported [Taoism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoism_in_Korea "Taoism in Korea") at the expense of [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_Buddhism "Korean Buddhism"), and to this effect in 643, sent emissaries to the Tang court requesting Taoist sages, eight of whom were brought to Goguryeo. This gesture is considered by some historians as an effort to pacify Tang and buy time to prepare for the Tang invasion Yŏn thought inevitable given his ambitions to annex Silla.

However, Yŏn Kaesomun took an increasingly provocative stance against [Silla Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silla "Silla") and [Tang China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty"). Soon, Goguryeo formed an alliance with Baekje and invaded Silla, Daeya-song (modern Hapchon) and around 40 border fortresses were conquered by the Goguryeo-Baekje alliance.[^28] Since the early 7th century, Silla had been forced on the defensive by both Baekje and Goguryeo, which had not yet formally allied but had both desired to erode Sillan power in the Han Valley. During the reign of King [Jinpyeong of Silla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jinpyeong_of_Silla "Jinpyeong of Silla"), numerous fortresses were lost to both Goguryeo and the continuous attacks took a toll on Silla and its people.[^29] During Jinpyeong's reign, Silla made repeated requests beseeching Sui China to attack Goguryeo.[^29] Although these invasions were ultimately unsuccessful, in 643, once again under pressure from the Goguryeo–Baekje alliance, Jinpyeong's successor, [Queen Seondeok of Silla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queen_Seondeok_of_Silla "Queen Seondeok of Silla"), requested military aid from Tang. Although Taizong had initially dismissed Silla's offers to pay tribute and its requests for an alliance on account of Seondeok being a woman, he later accepted the offer due to Goguryeo's growing belligerence and hostile policy towards both Silla and Tang. In 644, Tang began preparations for a major campaign against Goguryeo.[^27]

In 645, [Emperor Taizong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_Taizong_of_Tang "Emperor Taizong of Tang"), who had a personal ambition to defeat Goguryeo and was determined to succeed where [Emperor Yang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_Yang_of_Sui "Emperor Yang of Sui") had failed, personally led an attack on Goguryeo. The Tang army captured a number of Goguryeo fortresses, including the important [Yodong/Liaodong Fortress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yodong_Fortress "Yodong Fortress") (遼東城, in modern [Liaoyang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liaoyang "Liaoyang"), [Liaoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liaoning "Liaoning")). During his first campaign against Goguryeo, Taizong famously showed generously to the defeated inhabitants of numerous Goguryeo fortresses, refusing to permit his troops to loot downs and enslave inhabitants and when faced with protest from his commanders and soldiers, rewarded them with his own money.[^30] [Ansi City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ansi_City "Ansi City") (in modern [Haicheng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haicheng,_Liaoning "Haicheng, Liaoning"), Liaoning), which was the last fortress that would clear the [Liaodong Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liaodong_Peninsula "Liaodong Peninsula") of significant defensive works and was promptly put under siege. Initially, Taizong and his forces achieve great progress, when his numerically inferior force smashed a Goguryeo relief force at the [Battle of Mount Jupil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Mount_Jupil "Battle of Mount Jupil"). Goguryeo's defeat at Mount Jupil had significant consequences, as Tang forces killed over 20,000 Goguryeo soldiers and captured another 36,800, which crippled Goguryeo's manpower reserves for the rest of the conflict.[^30] However, the capable defense put up by Ansi's commanding general (whose name is controversial but traditionally is believed to be [Yang Manch'un](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_Manch%27un "Yang Manch'un")) stymied Tang forces and, in late fall, with winter fast approaching and his supplies running low, Tang forces under the command Prince Li Daozong attempted to build a rampart to seize the city in a last ditch effort, but was foiled when Goguryeo troops managed to seize control of it. Afterwards, Taizong decided to withdraw in the face of incoming Goguryeo reinforcements, deteriorating weather conditions and the difficult supply situation. The campaign was unsuccessful for the Tang Chinese,[^31] failing to capture Ansi Fortress after a protracted siege that lasted more than 60 days.[^32] Emperor Taizong invaded Goguryeo again in 647 and 648, but was defeated both times.[^33] [^34] [^35] [^36] [^37]

Emperor Taizong prepared another invasion in 649, but died in the summer, possibly due to an illness he contracted during his Korean campaigns.[^36] [^33] His son [Emperor Gaozong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_Gaozong_of_Tang "Emperor Gaozong of Tang") continued his campaigns. Upon the suggestion of [Kim Ch'unch'u](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muyeol_of_Silla "Muyeol of Silla"), the Silla–Tang alliance first conquered [Baekje](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baekje "Baekje") in 660 to break up the Goguryeo–Baekje alliance, and then turned its full attention to Goguryeo.[^41] However, Emperor Gaozong, too, was unable to defeat Goguryeo led by [Yŏn Kaesomun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y%C5%8Fn_Kaesomun "Yŏn Kaesomun");[^41] [^42] one of Yŏn Kaesomun's most notable victories came in 662 at the *Battle of Sasu* (蛇水), where he annihilated the Tang forces and killed the invading [Nanman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanman "Nanman") general Pang Xiaotai (龐孝泰) and all 13 of his sons.[^43] [^44] Therefore, while Yŏn Kaesomun was alive, Tang could not defeat Goguryeo.[^45]

In the summer of 666, [Yŏn Kaesomun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y%C5%8Fn_Kaesomun "Yŏn Kaesomun") died of a natural cause and Goguryeo was thrown into chaos and weakened by a succession struggle among his sons and younger brother.[^46] He was initially succeeded as *Dae Mangniji*, the highest position newly made under the ruling period of Yŏn Kaesomun, by his oldest son [Yŏn Namsaeng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y%C5%8Fn_Namsaeng "Yŏn Namsaeng"). As Yŏn Namsaeng subsequently carried out a tour of Goguryeo territory, however, rumors began to spread both that Yŏn Namsaeng was going to kill his younger brothers [Yŏn Namgŏn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y%C5%8Fn_Namg%C5%8Fn "Yŏn Namgŏn") and [Yŏn Namsan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y%C5%8Fn_Namsan "Yŏn Namsan"), whom he had left in charge at [Pyongyang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyongyang "Pyongyang"), and that Yŏn Namgŏn and Yŏn Namsan were planning to rebel against Yŏn Namsaeng. When Yŏn Namsaeng subsequently sent officials close to him back to Pyongyang to try to spy on the situation, Yŏn Namgŏn arrested them and declared himself *Tae Mangniji*, attacking his brother. Yŏn Namsaeng sent his son [Ch'ŏn Hŏnsŏng](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ch%27%C5%8Fn_H%C5%8Fns%C5%8Fng&action=edit&redlink=1 "Ch'ŏn Hŏnsŏng (page does not exist)") (泉獻誠), as Yŏn Namsaeng changed his family name from Yŏn (淵) to Ch'ŏn (泉) observe naming taboo for Emperor Gaozu, to Tang to seek aid. [Emperor Gaozong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_Gaozong_of_Tang "Emperor Gaozong of Tang") saw this as an opportunity and sent an army to attack and destroy Goguryeo. In the middle of Goguryeo's power struggles between Yŏn Kaesomun's successors, his younger brother, Yŏn Chŏngto, defected to the Silla side.[^46]

In 667, the Chinese army crossed the [Liao River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liao_River "Liao River") and captured Shin/Xin Fortress (新城, in modern [Fushun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fushun "Fushun"), [Liaoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liaoning "Liaoning")). The Tang forces thereafter fought off counterattacks by Yŏn Namgŏn, and joined forces with and received every possible assistance from the defector Yŏn Namsaeng,[^46] although they were initially unable to cross the Yalu River due to resistance. In spring of 668, Li Ji turned his attention to Goguryeo's northern cities, capturing the important city of Buyeo (扶餘, in modern [Nong'an](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nong%27an_County "Nong'an County"), [Jilin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jilin "Jilin")). In fall of 668, he crossed the [Yalu River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yalu_River "Yalu River") and put [Pyongyang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyongyang "Pyongyang") under siege in concert with the Silla army.

Yŏn Namsan and Bojang surrendered, and while Yŏn Namgŏn continued to resist in the inner city, his general, the Buddhist monk [Sinsŏng](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sins%C5%8Fng&action=edit&redlink=1 "Sinsŏng (page does not exist)") (信誠) turned against him and surrendered the inner city to Tang forces. Yŏn Namgŏn tried to commit suicide, but was seized and treated. This was the end of Goguryeo, and Tang annexed Goguryeo into its territory, with [Xue Rengui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xue_Rengui "Xue Rengui") being put initially in charge of former Goguryeo territory as protector general. The violent dissension resulting from Yŏn Kaesomun's death proved to be the primary reason for the Tang–Silla triumph, thanks to the division, defections, and widespread demoralization it caused.[^47] The alliance with Silla had also proved to be invaluable, thanks to the ability to attack Goguryeo from opposite directions, and both military and logistical aid from Silla.[^47] The Tang established the [Andong Protectorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andong_Protectorate "Andong Protectorate") on former Goguryeo lands after the latter's fall.[^48] [^49]

However, there was much resistance to Tang rule (fanned by Silla, which was displeased that Tang did not give it Goguryeo or Baekje's territory), and in 669, following Emperor Gaozong's order, a part of the Goguryeo people were forced to move to the region between the Yangtze River and the Huai River, as well as the regions south of the Qinling Mountains and west of Chang'an, only leaving old and weak inhabitants in the original land. Some people entered the service of the Tang government, such as [Go Sagye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Go_Sagye "Go Sagye") and his son [Gao Xianzhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gao_Xianzhi "Gao Xianzhi") (Go Seonji in Korean), the famed general who commanded the Tang forces at the [Battle of Talas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Talas "Battle of Talas").[^50] [^51] [^52] [^53] [^54]

Silla thus unified most of the Korean peninsula in 668, but the kingdom's reliance on China's Tang dynasty had its price. Tang set up the [Protectorate General to Pacify the East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protectorate_General_to_Pacify_the_East "Protectorate General to Pacify the East"), governed by [Xue Rengui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xue_Rengui "Xue Rengui"), but faced increasing problems ruling the former inhabitants of Goguryeo, as well as Silla's resistance to Tang's remaining presence on the Korean Peninsula. Silla had to forcibly resist the imposition of Chinese rule over the entire peninsula, which lead to the [Silla–Tang Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silla%E2%80%93Tang_War "Silla–Tang War"), but their own strength did not extend beyond the [Taedong River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taedong_River "Taedong River").

Later, Tang China considered [Balhae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balhae "Balhae") as a [tributary state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tributary_system_of_China "Tributary system of China"), whereas Balhae strongly opposed such view. As the [Emperor of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_of_China "Emperor of China") saw himself as the emperor of the entire civilized world, it was not possible for such an emperor to have equal diplomatic relations with any other regional powers, and as such all diplomatic relations in the region were construed by the Chinese as tributary regardless of the intention of those regions.

## Liao, Jin, Song and Goryeo

During this time North China was ruled by the [Khitan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khitan_people "Khitan people") -led [Liao dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liao_dynasty "Liao dynasty") followed by the [Jurchen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jurchen_people "Jurchen people") -led [Jin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jin_dynasty_\(1115%E2%80%931234\) "Jin dynasty (1115–1234)"). Although Liao initially attempted to maintain friendly relations with Goryeo, the Liao's destruction of Goryeo's predecessor, Balhae, led to lingering resentments within the Korean court in Kaesong. As descendants of Goguryeo, the Balhae people and the Goryeo dynasties were related.[^55] [Taejo of Goryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taejo_of_Goryeo "Taejo of Goryeo"), Goryeo's first King, felt a strong familial kinship with Balhae, calling it his "relative country" and "married country",[^56] and protected the Balhae refugees following the Liao Conquest of Balhae.[^57] This was in stark contrast to Later Silla, which had endured a hostile relationship with Balhae.[^58] Taejo displayed strong animosity toward the Khitans who had destroyed Balhae. The Liao dynasty sent 30 envoys with 50 camels as a gift in 942, but Taejo exiled the envoys to an island and starved the camels under a bridge, in what is known as the "Manbu Bridge Incident".[^59] [^60] Taejo proposed to [Gaozu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shi_Jingtang "Shi Jingtang") of [Later Jin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Later_Jin_\(Five_Dynasties\) "Later Jin (Five Dynasties)") that they attack the Khitans in retribution for Balhae, according to the *[Zizhi Tongjian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zizhi_Tongjian "Zizhi Tongjian")*.[^56] Furthermore, in his *Ten Injunctions* to his descendants, he stated that the Khitans are "savage beasts" and should be guarded against.[^59] [^61] Khitan conquest of Balhae resulted in Goryeo's prolonged hostility towards the Khitan Empire.[^62]

As a result of the hostile relationship, the Liao invaded Goryeo and waged a series of wars from 993 to 1019. Goryeo triumphed over the Liao dynasty in early 11th century and gained parts of the northern Korean peninsula from Liao in exchange for submitting tribute. Following the Goryeo–Khitan War, a balance of power was established in East Asia between Goryeo, Liao, and Song.[^63] [^64] With its victory over Liao, Goryeo was confident in its military ability and no longer worried about a Khitan military threat.[^65] Fu Bi, a grand councilor of the Song dynasty, had a high estimate of Goryeo's military ability and said that Liao was afraid of Goryeo.[^66] [^67] Furthermore, regarding the attitude of the Koreans, he said: "Among the many tribes and peoples which, depending on their power of resistance, have been either assimilated or made tributary to the Khitan, the Koreans alone do not bow their heads." [^68] Song regarded Goryeo as a potential military ally and maintained friendly relations as equal partners.[^69] Meanwhile, Liao sought to build closer ties with Goryeo and prevent a Song–Goryeo military alliance by appealing to Goryeo's infatuation with Buddhism, and offered Liao Buddhist knowledge and artifacts to Goryeo.[^70] During the 11th century, Goryeo was viewed as "the state that could give either the Song or Liao military ascendancy".[^67] When imperial envoys, who represented the emperors of Liao and Song, went to Goryeo, they were received as peers, not suzerains.[^71] [^72] Goryeo's international reputation was greatly enhanced.[^69] [^73] Beginning in 1034, merchants from Song and envoys from various Jurchen tribes and the [Tamna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamna "Tamna") kingdom attended the annual *Palgwanhoe* in Kaesong, the largest national celebration in Goryeo;[^73] the Song merchants attended as representatives of China while the Jurchen and Tamna envoys attended as members of Goryeo's *[tianxia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianxia "Tianxia")*.[^74]

Goryeo had a complex relationship with the Jurchens, who would later go on to form the [Jin dynasty (1115–1234)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jin_dynasty_\(1115%E2%80%931234\) "Jin dynasty (1115–1234)"). Jurchens in the [Yalu River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yalu_River "Yalu River") region were tributaries of Goryeo since the reign of [Wang Geon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taejo_of_Goryeo "Taejo of Goryeo"), who called upon them during the wars of the [Later Three Kingdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Later_Three_Kingdoms "Later Three Kingdoms") period, but the Jurchens switched allegiance between Liao and Goryeo multiple times, taking advantage of the tension between the two nations; posing a potential threat to Goryeo's border security, the Jurchens offered tribute to the Goryeo court, expecting lavish gifts in return.[^75]

General Yun Kwan (1040–1111) and his army

The Jurchens north of Goryeo had traditionally rendered tribute to the Goryeo monarchs and called Goryeo their "parent country" considering past ties between [Goguryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo "Goguryeo") and its [Mohe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohe_people "Mohe people") subjects,[^76] [^77] [^78] but thanks to the defeat of Liao to the Koreans in 1019, the [Wanyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wanyan "Wanyan") tribe of the [Heishui Mohe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heishui_Mohe "Heishui Mohe") unified the Jurchen tribes and gained in might, taking advantage of the power vacuum.

At the peak of its power, Goryeo contested with the rising [Wanyan tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wanyan "Wanyan") of which Goryeo considered them as barbaric vassals descending from the [Mohe people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohe_people "Mohe people") that served their [Goguryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo "Goguryeo") ancestors,[^79] [^80] in the state of total war over former territories of Goguryeo and [Balhae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balhae "Balhae"). This was in sharp contrast with the more positive perceptions the Jurchens had of Goryeo. For example, the annals of [King Yejong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yejong_of_Goryeo "Yejong of Goryeo") (r. 1105–1122) in the *[History of Goryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goryeosa "Goryeosa")* report that Wanyan Wugunai's son [Yingge](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wanyan_Yingge&action=edit&redlink=1 "Wanyan Yingge (page does not exist)") (盈歌; 1053–1103) considered Goryeo as his "parent country" (父母之邦) because his clan's ancestor Hanpu had come from Goryeo.[^81] Although this could have been because of the Jurchens' desire for Goryeo aid against their rivals, as historians are unsure if Hanpu, the progenitor of the Jurchen Jin dynasty's royal family who was from Goryeo, was ethnically Silla, Goryeo, Balhae or Jurchen.

As the geopolitical situation began to shift in turbulence by the start of the 12th century, Goryeo unleashed two major military campaigns from 1104 to 1109 spearheaded by the ambitious [King Yejong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yejong_of_Goryeo "Yejong of Goryeo") with vows of reclaiming former [Goguryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goguryeo "Goguryeo") territories held by Jurchen tribes united under the progenitors of the Jin dynasty, [Wanyan Wuyashu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wuyashu "Wuyashu") and [Aguda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_Taizu_of_Jin "Emperor Taizu of Jin"), with the aims of also preventing potential aggressions from the Jurchen tribes. Led by prominent generals such as [Yun Kwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yun_Kwan "Yun Kwan") and [Ch'ŏk Chun-gyŏng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ch%27%C5%8Fk_Chun-gy%C5%8Fng "Ch'ŏk Chun-gyŏng"), the well-trained [Byeolmuban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byeolmuban "Byeolmuban") (別武班; "Special Warfare Army") of approximately 250,000 [^82] [^83] men initially succeeded in ravaging Jurchen territories and building the strategic "Nine Fortresses" (동북 9성; 東北九城) of which exact locations are still topics of debate. Following the invasion, numerous Jurchen tribes surrendered to the invading Korean forces but many stayed vigilant and resumed fierce resistance led by the Wanyan tribe, complicating the phase of the war. Despite the Koreans of Goryeo having proceeded to utilizing [scorched earth tactics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scorched_earth "Scorched earth"), the Jurchen tribes under the leadership of Wanyan Wuyashu achieved a pyrrhic victory as Goryeo considered securing the Nine Fortresses too costly albeit having the upper hand in the war. Contacted by the Jurchens of the Wanyan tribe that have tasted the bitterness as well of facing Korean forces in their homes for peace, Goryeo would eventually move on to reluctantly signing a peace agreement with the Wanyan tribe and later on cede the Nine Fortresses to Wuyashu, in return for tributes sent by the Jurchens, the full repatriation of Korean settlers, and the guarantee of nonaggression.[^84] Though the objective of reclaiming former ancestral lands failed, Goryeo managed to maintain peaceful relations with the Jin dynasty which progress in conquering the Liao and [Northern Song dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Song_dynasty "Northern Song dynasty") respectively.[^85]

During the reign of Jurchen leader [Wuyashu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wuyashu "Wuyashu") in 1103–1113, the border between the two nations was stabilized and Korean forces withdrew from Jurchen territories, acknowledging Jurchen control over the contested region.[^86] [^87]

In 1115 the Jurchen founded the [Jin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jin_dynasty_\(1115%E2%80%931234\) "Jin dynasty (1115–1234)"), and in 1125 Jin annihilated Liao, which was Goryeo's suzerain, and started [invasion of Song](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jingkang_incident "Jingkang incident"). In response to the circumstantial changes, Goryeo declared itself to be a tributary state of Jin in 1126.[^88] [^89] After that, peace was maintained and Jin never actually did invade Goryeo. As a result, Goryeo paid tribute to the Jin to maintain friendly relations and trade.

## Yuan and Goryeo

During the 13th century, the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire") [invaded China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_invasion_of_China "Mongol invasion of China") and defeated the [Jin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jin_dynasty_\(1115%E2%80%931234\) "Jin dynasty (1115–1234)") and [Song dynasties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty "Song dynasty"). In 1271, [Kublai Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kublai_Khan "Kublai Khan") proclaimed the [Yuan dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_dynasty "Yuan dynasty") of China in the traditional Chinese style.[^90] During the period of 1231–1259, the Yuan dynasty [invaded Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_invasions_of_Korea "Mongol invasions of Korea"). Goryeo endured six devastating invasions at tremendous cost to civilian lives throughout the Korean Peninsula, and numerous cultural artifacts such as the [Tripitaka Koreana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripitaka_Koreana "Tripitaka Koreana") and the [Hwangnyongsa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hwangnyongsa "Hwangnyongsa"). The hardships endured by the Goryeo people and the destruction of the country in the face of the invasions ultimately resulting in the capitulation of [Goryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goryeo "Goryeo") and becoming a tributary state of the Yuan dynasty for 86 years until achieving its independence in 1356. As a stipulation of the peace treaty, the Mongols annexed the northern areas of Korean Peninsula after the invasions and incorporated them into their empire as [Ssangseong Prefectures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ssangseong_Prefectures "Ssangseong Prefectures") and [Dongnyeong Prefectures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dongnyeong_Prefectures "Dongnyeong Prefectures").[^91] Once the treaty was concluded and vassaldom established, intermarriage between the Koreans and Mongols was encouraged by the Mongol Empire.[^92] After the death of [Wonjong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wonjong_of_Goryeo "Wonjong of Goryeo") in 1274, his successor [Chungnyeol of Goryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chungnyeol_of_Goryeo "Chungnyeol of Goryeo") received Kublai's daughter [Qutlugh-Kelmish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princess_Jeguk "Princess Jeguk") as a wife, and his reign began a wholesale [Mongolization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolization "Mongolization") of the Korean court that continued until the middle of the 14th century. On paper, the official protocol for Korea was that of a subordinate principality, and Korean rulers made lengthy stays at the Mongol Yuan court, both before and after their coronation.[^93] In addition, their Mongol wives, and even concubines, exerted great influence over Goryeo politics. For instance, [Bayankhutag, Princess Gyeonghwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princess_Gyeonghwa "Princess Gyeonghwa") selected officials for posts within the Goryeo government.[^94] The Mongols and the Kingdom of Goryeo became linked via marriage and Goryeo became a *quda* ([marriage alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marriage_alliance "Marriage alliance")) state of the Yuan dynasty; monarchs of Goryeo during this period were effectively imperial sons in-law (*khuregen*). The effects of intermarriage on Mongol-Goryeo relations worked both ways: during the reign of Kublai Khan, King Chungnyeol of Goryeo married one of Kublai's daughters; later, a court lady from Korea called the [Empress Gi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empress_Gi "Empress Gi") became an empress through her marriage with [Ukhaantu Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ukhaantu_Khan "Ukhaantu Khan"), and her son, [Biligtü Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biligt%C3%BC_Khan "Biligtü Khan") of the [Northern Yuan dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Yuan_dynasty "Northern Yuan dynasty"), became a Mongol Khan. Furthermore, the kings of Goryeo held an important status within the Mongol imperial hierarchy, much like other important families of conquered or client states of the Mongol Empire (e.g. the [Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghurs "Uyghurs"), the [Oirats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oirats "Oirats"), and [Khongirad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khongirad "Khongirad")).[^95]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/%EC%84%9C%EC%9A%B8_%EB%A7%88%ED%8F%AC_%EA%B3%A0%EB%A0%A4_%EA%B3%B5%EB%AF%BC%EC%99%95_%EB%82%B4%EC%99%B8_%EC%98%81%EC%A0%95.jpg/250px-%EC%84%9C%EC%9A%B8_%EB%A7%88%ED%8F%AC_%EA%B3%A0%EB%A0%A4_%EA%B3%B5%EB%AF%BC%EC%99%95_%EB%82%B4%EC%99%B8_%EC%98%81%EC%A0%95.jpg)

King Gongmin (1330–1374) and Princess Noguk assisted in the peaceful succession of Gegeen Khan.

The Mongols extracted tribute from throughout their empire.[^96] From Goryeo, they received gold, silver, cloth, grain, ginseng, and falcons.[^97] [^98] The tribute payments were a burden on Goryeo and subjugated polities in the empire.[^97] [^98] [^99] As with all parts of the Mongol Empire, Goryeo provided palace women, eunuchs, Buddhist monks, and other personnel to the Mongols.[^100] Korean concubines were procured by the Khan.[^101] One of them was [Empress Gi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empress_Gi "Empress Gi"), who, through her political command and incorporation of Korean females and eunuchs in the court, spread Korean clothing, food, and lifestyle in the [capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khanbaliq "Khanbaliq").[^102] It became prestigious to marry Korean women among members of the Yuan elite.[^103] The entry of Korean women into the Mongol court was reciprocated by the entry of Mongol princesses into the Korean court, beginning with the marriage of [Chungnyeol of Goryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chungnyeol_of_Goryeo "Chungnyeol of Goryeo") and a daughter of Kublai Khan; in total, 9 princesses of the Yuan court married into the Goryeo royal family.[^104]

During Yuan rule, Korea was effectively forced to serve as a Mongol military base. Mongol ambitions on Japan resulted in two [invasions of Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_invasions_of_Japan "Mongol invasions of Japan"). In both efforts, the Mongols directed Korean shipbuilding and militarization towards the amphibious assault of the Japanese coasts and pressed a large proportion of Korean naval and infantry forces into the service of Mongol military objectives. Korea supplied 770 fully manned ships and 5,000 soldiers in 1274 and 900 ships and 10,000 soldiers in 1281.[^93] Yuan officials and envoys took concubines and wives in Korea while they were stationed in Korea for the invasion of Japan.[^105] For a variety of reasons, both invasions failed.

As Yuan rule began to destabilize in China, [King Gongmin of Goryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gongmin_of_Goryeo "Gongmin of Goryeo") seized the opportunity to rebel and declared Goryeo's independence after decades of Mongol rule. Furthermore, Gongmin regained Goryeo's northern territories and Goryeo would even briefly invade Liaodong after it regained its independence. The Yuan dynasty eventually fell in 1368.

## Ming and Joseon

Following the emergence of the [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") in 1368, when it overthrew the Mongol Yuan dynasty, the royal court in Goryeo split into two conflicting factions, one supporting the Ming and the other standing by the Yuan. In 1388, a Ming messenger came to Goryeo to demand that territories of the former [Ssangseong Prefectures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ssangseong_Prefectures "Ssangseong Prefectures") be handed over to Ming China. The tract of land was taken by Mongol forces during [the invasion of Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_invasions_of_Korea "Mongol invasions of Korea"), but had been reclaimed by Goryeo in 1356 as the Yuan dynasty weakened. The act caused an uproar among the Goryeo court, and General [Ch'oe Yŏng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ch%27oe_Y%C5%8Fng "Ch'oe Yŏng") seized the chance to argue for an invasion of the Ming-controlled [Liaodong Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liaodong_Peninsula "Liaodong Peninsula").

General [Yi Sŏng-gye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taejo_of_Joseon "Taejo of Joseon") was chosen to lead the attack; [he revolted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wihwado_Retreat "Wihwado Retreat"), swept back to the capital Gaegyeong (present-day [Kaesong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaesong "Kaesong")) and initiated a [coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "Coup d'état"), overthrowing [King U](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U_of_Goryeo "U of Goryeo") in favor of his son, [Chang of Goryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chang_of_Goryeo "Chang of Goryeo") (1388). He later killed King U and his son after a failed restoration and forcibly placed a royal named Wang Yo on the throne (he became King [Gongyang of Goryeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gongyang_of_Goryeo "Gongyang of Goryeo")). In 1392, Yi eliminated [Chŏng Mong-ju](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ch%C5%8Fng_Mong-ju "Chŏng Mong-ju"), highly respected leader of a group loyal to Goryeo dynasty, and dethroned King Gongyang, exiling him to [Wonju](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wonju "Wonju"), and he ascended the throne himself. The Goryeo kingdom had come to an end after 474 years of rule.

In the beginning of his reign, Yi Sŏng-gye, now ruler of Korea, intended to continue to use of the name Goryeo for the country he ruled and simply change the royal line of descent to his own, thus maintaining the façade of continuing the 500-year-old Goryeo tradition. After numerous threats of mutiny from the drastically weakened but still influential Gwonmun nobles, who continued to swear allegiance to the remnants of the Goryeo and to the now-demoted [Wang clan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_\(surname\)#Korea "Wang (surname)"), the consensus in the reformed court was that a new dynastic title was needed to signify the change. In naming the new kingdom, Taejo contemplated two possibilities – "Hwaryeong" (his place of birth) and "Joseon". After much internal deliberation, as well as endorsement by the neighboring Ming dynasty's emperor, Taejo declared the name of the kingdom to be Joseon, a tribute to the ancient Korean state of [Gojoseon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Chos%C5%8Fn "Old Chosŏn").[^106] He also moved the capital to [Hanseong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_of_Korea#During_Joseon "Capital of Korea") (modern [Seoul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seoul "Seoul")) from [Gaegyeong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_of_Korea#During_Goryeo "Capital of Korea") (modern Kaesong).

[Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") of China shared a close trade and diplomatic relationship with the [Joseon dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseon "Joseon") of Korea. In 1392, the Joseon and Ming settled their border through negotiation, with the Joseon accepting tributary status while the Ming gave up competing territorial claims.[^107]<sup><span title="Page: 98">: 98</span> </sup> Both dynasties shared [Confucian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucianism "Confucianism") ideals in society. Joseon had been a member of the Ming's sinocentric East Asian regional order. Joseon was historically one of the most pro-China Korean dynasties, and the Ming Court traditionally treated Korea alongside Vietnam and Thailand as its most favored tributaries. Relations were close and stable for the 250 years following the 1392 border negotiations.[^107]<sup><span title="Page: 98">: 98</span></sup>

Ming China assisted Joseon Korea during [Toyotomi Hideyoshi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi "Toyotomi Hideyoshi") 's [invasion of Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_invasions_of_Korea_\(1592%E2%80%931598\) "Japanese invasions of Korea (1592–1598)"),[^107]<sup><span title="Page: 96">: 96</span> </sup> in which the [Wanli Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wanli_Emperor "Wanli Emperor") sent a total of 221,500 troops. For this, the Chinese Emperor of that time, the [Wanli Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wanli_Emperor "Wanli Emperor") is remembered fondly by most Koreans of the modern era.

Joseon Korea, in an effort to repay the Ming Chinese assistance during [Toyotomi Hideyoshi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi "Toyotomi Hideyoshi") 's [invasion of Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_invasions_of_Korea_\(1592%E2%80%931598\) "Japanese invasions of Korea (1592–1598)") 's sent 13,000 soldiers to aid the Ming against the [Later Jin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Later_Jin_\(1616%E2%80%931636\) "Later Jin (1616–1636)") at the [Battle of Sarhū](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Sarh%C5%AB "Battle of Sarhū") where nearly two-thirds of the Joseon Expeditionary Force was killed. [^108] Furthermore, Joseon Korea was invaded twice during the [Ming-Qing Transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming-Qing_Transition "Ming-Qing Transition") due to its loyalty to the Ming Government in Beijing.

As a result of Joseon's subjugation and Ming's destruction, Joseon supported the [Little China ideology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Little_China_\(ideology\) "Little China (ideology)"), known as sojunghwa.[^109] According to Youngmin Kim, " it held that the Joseon embodied Chineseness authentically while other neighboring countries failed to do so in the face of the barbarian domination of the center of the civilized world." [^109] A set of standardized rites and unifying symbols were developed in Late Joseon Korea in order to maintain that sense of cultural identity.[^109] Joseon dynasty believed that the Qing dynasty was unworthy of succeeding the politico-cultural orthodoxy of "China". Instead, the [Confucianist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucianism "Confucianism") Joseon dynasty asserted itself as the legitimate heir to the Chinese civilization and termed itself "Little China".[^110]

Long after submitting to the Qing, the Joseon court and many Korean intellectuals kept using Ming [reign periods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_era_name "Chinese era name"), as when a scholar marked 1861 as "the 234th year of [Chongzhen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chongzhen_Emperor "Chongzhen Emperor")." [^111]

## Qing, Joseon and Korean Empire

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/%E4%BA%9C%E6%9D%B1%E5%8D%B0%E7%94%BB%E8%BC%AF_01_150_%22%E5%9C%8B%E5%A2%83%E7%A2%91%EF%BC%88%E7%99%BD%E9%A0%AD%E5%B1%B1%EF%BC%89%22.jpg/250px-%E4%BA%9C%E6%9D%B1%E5%8D%B0%E7%94%BB%E8%BC%AF_01_150_%22%E5%9C%8B%E5%A2%83%E7%A2%91%EF%BC%88%E7%99%BD%E9%A0%AD%E5%B1%B1%EF%BC%89%22.jpg)

Qing–Joseon boundary marker on Mount Paektu (photographed in 1930s)

Under [Hong Taiji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Taiji "Hong Taiji"), the [Later Jin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Later_Jin_\(1616%E2%80%931636\) "Later Jin (1616–1636)") and its successor [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") invaded Joseon twice, in [1627](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Later_Jin_invasion_of_Joseon "Later Jin invasion of Joseon") and [1636](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_invasion_of_Joseon "Qing invasion of Joseon"). Following the 1636 invasion, the Qing claimed victory and forced [Injo of Joseon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injo_of_Joseon "Injo of Joseon") into submission. [Hong Taiji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Taiji "Hong Taiji") set up a platform in [Samjeondo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samjeondo_Monument "Samjeondo Monument") in the upper reach of the [Han River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_River_\(Korea\) "Han River (Korea)").[^112] At the top of the platform he accepted [King Injo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injo_of_Joseon "Injo of Joseon") 's submission. King Injo [kowtowed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kowtow "Kowtow") to [Hong Taiji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Taiji "Hong Taiji"), who allegedly forced Injo to repeat the humiliating ritual many times.[^113] A [monument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samjeondo_Monument "Samjeondo Monument") in honor of the so-called *excellent virtues of the Manchu Emperor* was erected at Samjeondo, where the ceremony of submission had been conducted.

After securing Injo's submission, the Qing forced Joseon to sever its relations with the collapsing Ming dynasty, which eventually fell in 1644. Despite reestablishing economic relations by officially entering the [imperial Chinese tributary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tributary_system_of_China "Tributary system of China"), Joseon leaders and intellectuals remained resentful of the Manchus, whom they regarded as barbarians, and [^114] regarded the Ming dynasty as the center of the civilized world.[^109] Injo's successor, [Hyojong of Joseon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyojong_of_Joseon "Hyojong of Joseon"), after rising to the throne, began to reform and expand the [military of Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_history_of_Korea "Military history of Korea") to avenge the fallen Ming dynasty; first he removed [Kim Chajŏm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kim_Chaj%C5%8Fm "Kim Chajŏm"), who had corrupted politics and had greater power than the king himself. Then, he called [Song Si-yŏl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_Si-y%C5%8Fl "Song Si-yŏl") (송시열; 宋時烈) and [Kim Sang-heon](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kim_Sang-heon&action=edit&redlink=1 "Kim Sang-heon (page does not exist)") to his court, who supported war against the Qing dynasty. His military expansion was massive, and he also built several border fortresses along [Yalu River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yalu_River "Yalu River") where Joseon and Qing shared a border. When a band of Dutch sailors including [Hendrick Hamel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrick_Hamel "Hendrick Hamel") drifted on [Jeju Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeju_Island "Jeju Island"), Hyojong ordered them to build muskets for the army, providing muskets to the Koreans for the first time after the [Seven Year War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_invasions_of_Korea_\(1592%E2%80%931598\) "Japanese invasions of Korea (1592–1598)").

However, the Qing dynasty continued to thrive, expanding quickly into the west after successfully conquering the Ming in 1644. The campaign was unable to be put in action, since the Qing dynasty assimilated the massive [Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_Chinese "Han Chinese") army into their own. The Joseon military, although reformed and expanded, was no match against the combined Manchu and Han forces. Also, the Qing dynasty began to treat Joseon as its friend and closest ally.

The expanded military was first put into action in 1654, when the Qing dynasty called for help to fight against invading [Russians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians "Russians"). 150 Joseon musketeers, along with 3,000 Manchus, met the Russian army at the [Battle of Hutong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Hutong_\(1654\) "Battle of Hutong (1654)") (호통 [Hanja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanja "Hanja"): 好通), present-day [Yilan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yilan_County,_Heilongjiang "Yilan County, Heilongjiang"), which was won by the Qing–Joseon allied forces. Four years later, in 1658, Hyojong sent troops once again to help Qing dynasty against Russia; 260 Joseon musketeers and cannoneers led by [Shin Ryu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shin_Ryu "Shin Ryu") joined the forces of [Ninguta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ninguta "Ninguta") Military Governor [Sarhuda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarhuda "Sarhuda"), the joint force sailed down the [Hurka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mudanjiang_River "Mudanjiang River") and [Sungari Rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sungari_River "Sungari River") and met the Russian forces under command of an Amur [Cossack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cossack "Cossack"), [Onufrij Stepanov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onufrij_Stepanov "Onufrij Stepanov") near the fall of the [Sungari River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sungari_River "Sungari River") into the [Amur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amur_River "Amur River"), killing 270 Russians and driving them out of Qing territory. The battles against Russia proved that Hyojong's reform had stabilized the Joseon army, although they were never put into action again. Despite the campaigns, Russia and Joseon remained on good terms. The Northern campaign is known as [Naseon Jeongbeol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian%E2%80%93Manchu_border_conflicts "Russian–Manchu border conflicts") (나선정벌; 羅禪征伐), or "Suppression of the Russians".

After enjoying two centuries of peace, Joseon Korea and Qing China were confronted with the rising European powers. Qing China's national strength gradually declined after its defeat in the [First](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Opium_War "First Opium War") and [Second Opium Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Opium_War "Second Opium War"). As such, China was forced to sign a series of concessions and " [unequal treaties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unequal_treaty "Unequal treaty") " with the Western powers. At the same time, the [Meiji Restoration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiji_Restoration "Meiji Restoration") occurred in Japan and led to the rise of the [Empire of Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_Japan "Empire of Japan"), which gradually expanded its military power. The [Donghak Peasant Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donghak_Peasant_Revolution "Donghak Peasant Revolution") of Korea in 1894 became a catalyst for the [First Sino-Japanese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Sino-Japanese_War "First Sino-Japanese War"), which saw the defeat of the Qing military. As part of the terms in the post-war [Treaty of Shimonoseki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Shimonoseki "Treaty of Shimonoseki"), China recognized the independence of Korea and ceased its tributary relations as well as Japan annexing the [island of Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Taiwan "Geography of Taiwan"). The [Korean Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_Empire "Korean Empire") established modern diplomatic relationship with Qing, but Korea was eventually annexed, against their will, by Japan under the [Japan–Korea Treaty of 1910](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan%E2%80%93Korea_Treaty_of_1910 "Japan–Korea Treaty of 1910").

## Republic of China and Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea

The anti-Japanese [March First Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_First_Movement "March First Movement") protests erupted in Korea in 1919, in which led to millions of Koreans peacefully protesting against Japanese rule.[^115] Although the protests were brutally suppressed by the Japanese Government, shortly afterwards, the [Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_the_Republic_of_Korea "Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea") was established as a government in exile, based in [Shanghai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai "Shanghai"), with [Syngman Rhee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngman_Rhee "Syngman Rhee") serving as its first president with its subsequent recognition by the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)").[^116] During the [Paris Peace Conference of 1919](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Peace_Conference_of_1919 "Paris Peace Conference of 1919"), many Korean nationalists became optimistic that U.S. President [Woodrow Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson "Woodrow Wilson") 's [Thirteen Points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Points "Thirteen Points") that called for self-determination, right to sovereignty and freedom could apply to them and thus key nationalists [Kim Kyu-Sik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kim_Kyu-Sik "Kim Kyu-Sik") flocked to Paris to petition for Korea's right to self-determination.[^117] The Republic of China was a key ally of the Provisional Korean Government, and a leading advocate for the restoration of Korean independence. The Chinese invited the Korean delegation to the conference with both the hope that Korea could regain its independence but also in hopes that the Korean delegation would embarrass Japan internationally.[^118] Unfortunately, China was at this point far too weak to do anything besides support Korea diplomatically and politically, and all other nations did not have a vested interested in Korea, and thus the Korean delegation ended in failure. Despite this, many Korean politicians as well as independence activists worked in China to restore Korean sovereignty.

The Provisional Government received significant recognition and support by both the founder of the ROC, [Sun Yat-sen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen "Sun Yat-sen") and his successor, [Chiang Kai-shek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek "Chiang Kai-shek"), both of whom led [Kuomintang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuomintang "Kuomintang") regime in China. The Provisional Government coordinated the armed resistance against the Japanese imperial army during the 1920s and 1930s, including the [Battle of Fengwudong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Fengwudong "Battle of Fengwudong") in June 1920, the [Battle of Qingshanli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Qingshanli "Battle of Qingshanli") in October 1920, and [Yoon Bong-Gil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoon_Bong-Gil "Yoon Bong-Gil") 's assassination of Japanese officers in Shanghai in April 1932. The last action in particular greatly influenced Chiang, who began to take a more proactive approach to Korean independence.[^119] Chiang wrote in his diary that the Japanese invaders should "reflect \[on their actions\] and awaken themselves", and praised Yun's patriotic martyrdom as a great contribution to Korean independence.[^119]

China tried to promote the legitimacy of the [Provisional Government of Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_the_Republic_of_Korea "Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea") (KPG), which was established by [Korean exiles in China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koreans_in_China "Koreans in China") after the suppression of the March 1st Movement in Korea. The KPG was ideologically aligned with the Chinese government of the time, as independence leader [Kim Ku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kim_Ku "Kim Ku") had agreed to [Chiang Kai-shek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek "Chiang Kai-shek") 's suggestion to adopt the Chinese [Three Principles of the People](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Principles_of_the_People "Three Principles of the People") program in exchange for financial aid.[^120] At the same time, China supported the leftist independence leader [Kim Won-bong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kim_Won-bong "Kim Won-bong") and convinced the two Kims to form the unified [Korean Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_Liberation_Army "Korean Liberation Army") (KLA). Under the terms in which the KLA was allowed to operate in China, it became an auxiliary of China's [National Revolutionary Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Revolutionary_Army "National Revolutionary Army") until 1945. Furthermore, Chiang even went as far as to establish a special class for Korean recruits at the [Whampoa Military Academy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whampoa_Military_Academy "Whampoa Military Academy"), many of whom would support Chiang's [Northern Expedition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Expedition "Northern Expedition").[^119] China's [National Military Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Military_Council "National Military Council") had also decided that "complete independence" for Korea was China's fundamental Korean policy; otherwise, the government in [Chongqing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chongqing "Chongqing") tried to unify the warring Korean factions.[^120]

Following the outbreak of the [Second Sino-Japanese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War "Second Sino-Japanese War") and the fall of Shanghai, the Korean Provisional Government relocated to [Chongqing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chongqing "Chongqing"), where it formed the [Korean Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_Liberation_Army "Korean Liberation Army") (KLA) in 1941. Originally, the Republic of China placed the [Korean Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_Liberation_Army "Korean Liberation Army") under the supreme authority of the commander-in-chief of the Chinese Army. The regulation was repealed in 1944, after the Provisional Government had achieved had improved its financial standing and achieved greater importance in the eyes of the Chinese government.[^121] The hundreds-strong KLA engaged in [guerrilla warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare "Guerrilla warfare") actions against the Japanese throughout the Asian theater of war until Japanese surrender in 1945.[^121]

The Republic of China was one of the participants of the [Cairo Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cairo_Conference "Cairo Conference"), which resulted in the [Cairo Declaration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1943_Cairo_Declaration "1943 Cairo Declaration"). One of the main purposes of the Cairo Declaration was to create an independent Korea, free from [Japanese Colonial Rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule "Korea under Japanese rule") and restoration of Formosa (Taiwan) and Manchuria to China.[^122] Initially, American negotiators were suspicious of Chinese intentions and potential territorial over Korea. American diplomat [Stanley Hornbeck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanley_Hornbeck "Stanley Hornbeck") asked then Minister of Foreign Affairs [T.V. Soong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T.V._Soong "T.V. Soong") how China viewed Korea "geographically, historically and politically" to which Soong replied:

> "\[The\] Chinese in no sense think of Korea as a part of, or a lost part, of an existing or a once having existed Chinese empire. Nor...do they so think of Indo-China." [^123]

Although Chiang wanted an immediate restoration of Korean independence, he ultimately had to compromise with President [Franklin D. Roosevelt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt "Franklin D. Roosevelt") who wanted an international trusteeship.[^119] Despite this, in a show of gratitude, the Provisional Government of Korea issued a statement the day after the Cairo Declaration thanking both the Republic of China and the Cairo Conference for guaranteeing the restoration of Korean sovereignty.[^124] Historian Bae Kyoung-han from Silla University notes that it was because of Chiang and the ROC that Korean independence, which had been lost since 1910, was mentioned and supported by the Cairo Conference.[^119]

Although the Cairo Conference led to Allied support for Korean independence, it did not lead to recognition of the Korean Provisional Government. Chiang and Korean leaders like [Syngman Rhee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngman_Rhee "Syngman Rhee") tried to influence the [U.S. State Department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State "United States Department of State") to support Korean independence and recognize the KPG, but the Far Eastern Division was skeptical. Its argument was that the [Korean people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_people "Korean people") "were emasculated politically" after decades of Japanese rule, and showed too much disunity, preferring a [condominium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condominium_\(international_law\) "Condominium (international law)") solution for Korea that involved the Soviets.[^120] China was adamantly opposed to Soviet influence in Korea after hearing about [Soviet atrocities in Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_repressions_of_Polish_citizens_\(1939%E2%80%931946\) "Soviet repressions of Polish citizens (1939–1946)") since its liberation.[^120] It should be emphasized that Chinese support for Korean independence was not entirely altruistic, as leading reasons behind Chinese support for Korean independence rested on the idea that a strong and independence Korea would prevent future Japanese aggression and also Chiang feared growing Soviet influence in Korea as the war drew to a close.[^125]

By the [Cairo Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cairo_Conference "Cairo Conference"), the US and China came to agree on Korean independence "in due course", with China still pressing for immediate recognition of the exile government and a tangible date for independence. After Soviet-American relations deteriorated, on August 10, 1945, the [United States Department of War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_War "United States Department of War") agreed that China should land troops in [Pusan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pusan "Pusan"), Korea from which to prevent a Soviet takeover. However, this turnaround was too late to prevent the [division of Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_of_Korea "Division of Korea"), as the [Red Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Army "Red Army") quickly [occupied northern Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_invasion_of_northeast_China "Soviet invasion of northeast China") that same month.[^120]

## Cultural relations

The [Chinese character system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_characters "Chinese characters"), known as [Hanja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanja "Hanja") in Korean, was introduced into Korea through the spread of Buddhism during the [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty"). Hanja was used as the sole means of writing Korean until [Sejong the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sejong_the_Great "Sejong the Great") promoted the invention of [Hangul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hangul "Hangul") during the 15th century.

[Confucianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucianism "Confucianism") also became a fundamental part of Korean society, rising into prominence under the [Goryeo dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goryeo "Goryeo") and prospered under the [Joseon dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseon "Joseon"). Examples included [civil service examinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_examinations "Imperial examinations"), known as *[gwageo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gwageo "Gwageo")*, and educational institutions ([Gukjagam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gukjagam "Gukjagam") and [Sungkyunkwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sungkyunkwan "Sungkyunkwan")), promoting the studies of [Chinese classical texts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_classics "Chinese classics").

The [imperial examination system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_examination "Imperial examination") implemented by China was introduced into Korea, which was not only inseparable from the close exchanges and deepening commercial relations between the two countries, but also had an important relationship with the ruling class of Korea at that time. The reason why the imperial examination system became popular in Korea was because of the strong support and advocacy of the Korean government at the time. The imperial examination system is in line with the idea of the ruling class to manage the country, incorporates the idea of self-development in Korea, encourages more Korean scholars and intellectuals to safeguard the interests of the Korean ruling government, and has caused the pursuit of equality in Korean culture and academic excellence of booming thought.

The thought process made people, even generals, like to write something to express their ideas. That is why in that period, at beginning, the sijo is only to be written by yangban, who went through a long and difficult process of education, but in the later period, there were many anonymous sijos to come out. Instead of using Chinese words, they used their own language and made it with more mordane but with a deep heart-touched sijo. The adoption of the civil service examination system is first helpful for the emperor to regulate the country and pick up the talented people, but then it transits into the path that made people's minds free. No more could they only obey rules, but try to battle the constriction of a hard language, be creative, active, and likely to express themselves.
