Official diplomatic relations between the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC) and [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand") began in July 1975 after years of negotiations.[^1] [^2] Historically, Thailand had good relations with governments in China; however, after [Plaek Phibunsongkhram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plaek_Phibunsongkhram "Plaek Phibunsongkhram") attempted to erase and prohibit Chinese culture and influence in the country, relations with the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)") were damaged.

In modern times, China has significant influence in Thailand.[^3] [^4] [^5] [^6] [^7] China is Thailand's leading trading partner, with bilateral trade reaching $153 billion in 2025. China is also the leading source of tourists to Thailand. Especially since the [2014 Thai coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Thai_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "2014 Thai coup d'état"), China has also been a leading exporter of weapons to Thailand.

## History

### Medieval and Early modern period

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Entree_de_l%27Ambassadeur_de_Siam_a_la_Cour_de_Pekin_18th_Century.jpg/250px-Entree_de_l%27Ambassadeur_de_Siam_a_la_Cour_de_Pekin_18th_Century.jpg)

Entry of the Siamese ambassador to the Court of Beijing, 18th century

Sino-Thai relations date back to as late as the early 15th century, when the [Ming treasure voyages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_treasure_voyages "Ming treasure voyages") of [Zheng He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zheng_He "Zheng He") stopped in [Ayutthaya Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayutthaya_Kingdom "Ayutthaya Kingdom"). Despite the Chinese support of [Malacca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malacca_Sultanate "Malacca Sultanate"), a rival of Thailand, the Thai were still considered one of the more loyal [Chinese tributaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tributary_system_of_China "Tributary system of China"); when [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azuchi%E2%80%93Momoyama_period "Azuchi–Momoyama period") [invaded Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_invasions_of_Korea_\(1592%E2%80%931598\) "Japanese invasions of Korea (1592–1598)"), the Thai offered to invade Japan for the Chinese. The relations between the two countries continued into the [Qing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") period. In the 1760s, the [Qing invasions of Burma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Burmese_War "Sino-Burmese War") alleviated the Burmese pressure on Thailand. Imports of [Thai rice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jasmine_rice "Jasmine rice") helped sustain the Qing Chinese population, while the [Thai Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_Chinese "Thai Chinese") wielded significant influence over the political affairs of early modern Thailand.

### Modern times

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Belt_and_Road_Initiative_participant_map.svg/250px-Belt_and_Road_Initiative_participant_map.svg.png)

Countries that have signed co-operation documents related to the Belt and Road Initiative

In 1932, [Siam's People's Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Party_\(Thailand,_1927\) "People's Party (Thailand, 1927)") overthrew the absolute monarchy and established a constitutional monarchy.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 138">: 138</span> </sup> It intensified assimilationist policies towards the Chinese community in Siam.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 138">: 138</span> </sup> From 1938 to 1939, it passed a series of laws to eliminate the economic influence of Chinese people, including prohibiting Chinese from certain professions, dealing in certain goods, and living in specified residential areas.[^8]<sup><span title="Pages: 138–139">: 138–139</span> </sup> The country's *Nationality Law* was amended in 1939 to require that all Chinese seeking to become citizens had to adopt Thai names, enroll their children in Thai schools, speak Thai, and renounce any allegiance to China.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 139">: 139</span></sup>

During World War II, Thailand further restricted the political activities of Chinese.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 139">: 139</span> </sup> In 1943, Thailand prohibited Chinese people from purchasing land.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 139">: 139</span></sup>

After the [establishment of the People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Proclamation of the People's Republic of China"), Thailand imposed immigration quotas that virtually prohibited immigration to Thailand from the PRC.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 139">: 139</span> </sup> During the [Korean War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_War "Korean War"), [Thai forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand_in_the_Korean_War "Thailand in the Korean War") under the [United Nations Command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Command "United Nations Command") fought the PRC's [People's Volunteer Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Volunteer_Army "People's Volunteer Army") in multiple battles.[^9] After 1955, Thailand began easing its naturalization policies.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 139">: 139</span></sup>

Under [Thai Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_Prime_Minister "Thai Prime Minister") [Plaek Phibunsongkhram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plaek_Phibunsongkhram "Plaek Phibunsongkhram"), relations with the Chinese were tense during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"). However, Phibunsongkhram sent the children of his advisor [Sang Phathanothai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sang_Phathanothai "Sang Phathanothai") to live in China as a goodwill gesture and for informal backdoor diplomacy. The book *[The Dragon's Pearl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Dragon%27s_Pearl "The Dragon's Pearl")* by Phathanothai's daughter Sirin recounts her experience in growing up during the [Cultural Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_Revolution "Cultural Revolution") in China. Thailand supported [Prince Boun Oum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boun_Oum "Boun Oum") 's government while the [Maoist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maoism "Maoism") government backing of China supported [Prince Souphanouvong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Souphanouvong "Souphanouvong") and the communist movement of [Pathet Lao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathet_Lao "Pathet Lao") with [Marxist-Leninist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism-Leninism "Marxism-Leninism") roots that inspired by [Chairman Mao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") which was funded by the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party"). In Cambodia, under [Sihanouk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norodom_Sihanouk "Norodom Sihanouk") 's government, the revolutionary [Pol Pot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pol_Pot "Pol Pot") (*Saloth Sar*) secretly formed an infamous [Khmer Rouge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Rouge "Khmer Rouge") which will later become the dictator of his [four-year reign of terror](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodian_genocide "Cambodian genocide") of what will be known as [Year Zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Year_Zero_\(political_notion\) "Year Zero (political notion)") from 1975 to 1979.

In June 1963, [Thai King](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy_of_Thailand "Monarchy of Thailand") [Bhumibol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhumibol_Adulyadej "Bhumibol Adulyadej") and his wife, [Sirikit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirikit "Sirikit"), visited [Taipei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taipei "Taipei") in the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China") (ROC). In 1969, [Minister of National Defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_National_Defense_\(Republic_of_China\) "Ministry of National Defense (Republic of China)") [Chiang Ching-kuo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Ching-kuo "Chiang Ching-kuo") visited [Bangkok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangkok "Bangkok") as a special envoy of the [government of the Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_the_Republic_of_China "Government of the Republic of China") to meet with Thai King. Thailand switched diplomatic relations from to the People's Republic of China in July 1975.[^10]

Until 1975, relations were of mutual suspicion, as the PRC supported left-leaning factions within the Thai political circle, and Thailand was wary of Chinese involvement with [Cambodian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia "Cambodia") and [Laotian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laos "Laos") conflicts during the civil war for the communist movements of the Khmer Rouge and Pathet Lao.[^1]

Relations developed positively in 1978, when China continued to back Thailand during Cambodia's internal conflict in which [Marxist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxist "Marxist") forces from [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam") ousted the Maoist Khmer Rouge from power in early 1979 and threatened the security of Southeast Asia.

Relations continue to develop as trade became the dominant theme in bilateral relations.[^2] Thailand continues to support the [One China Policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_Policy "One China Policy") and maintains unofficial relations with [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan"), which helps Thailand gain access to capital and the huge Chinese mainland market.[^2] Thai-Chinese businesses are part of the larger [bamboo network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamboo_network "Bamboo network").[^11] The [Charoen Pokphand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charoen_Pokphand "Charoen Pokphand") (CP Group), a prominent Thai conglomerate founded by the Thai-Chinese [Chearavanont](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chearavanont "Chearavanont") family, has been the single largest foreign investor in China.[^12]

In 1994, Taiwanese leader [Lee Teng-hui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Teng-hui "Lee Teng-hui") made a private visit to Thailand and met with Thai King to discuss projects on economic co-operation.[^10]

Thai Princess [Sirindhorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirindhorn "Sirindhorn") has received China's [Medal of Friendship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medal_of_Friendship "Medal of Friendship") and [Chinese Language and Culture Friendship Award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friendship_Award_\(China\) "Friendship Award (China)") for her work in promoting closer relations between the two countries. She speaks fluent [Mandarin Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese "Mandarin Chinese") and has translated several Chinese novels into Thai.[^13]

Thailand adopted a policy of improving relations with China since the [2014 Thai coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Thai_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "2014 Thai coup d'état"), when relations with the West worsened.[^14] In the Thai House of Representatives, concerns had been raised regarding increasing Chinese influence over the country, and some dubbed Thailand as a Chinese province, with the Chinese acquisition of land and real estate allowing China to build dams in the Mekong, and a Chinese private company to invest in the Thai high-speed rail megaproject.[^15]

#### 2020s

In 2024, Thailand and China signed a visa-waiver program that lifted the visa requirement for citizens of both nations, effective since March 1, 2024.[^16]

During Thai PM Paetongtarn Shinawatra's visit to Beijing, concerns arose over the potential deportation of 48 [Uyghur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghurs "Uyghurs") detainees in Thailand, despite warnings of abuse if sent back to China. The visit also focused on strengthening bilateral ties in areas like infrastructure and technology.[^17]

In July 2025, officials from the Chinese Embassy in Bangkok demanded that the [Bangkok Art and Culture Centre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangkok_Art_and_Culture_Centre "Bangkok Art and Culture Centre") (BACC) censor pieces from an exhibit titled "Constellation of Complicity: Visualising the Global Machinery of Authoritarian Solidarity", which included art critical of China.[^18] The BACC was forced to remove pieces by the Bangkok Metropolitan Administration.[^19] [^20]

## Economic relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Thai_Consulate_General%2C_Kunming.JPG/250px-Thai_Consulate_General%2C_Kunming.JPG)

Thai Consulate in Kunming

China is Thailand's top trading partner.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 32">: 32</span> </sup> Bilateral trade relations grow each year.[^21] Bilateral trade in 1999 was worth US$4.22 billion.[^21] That reached $25.3 billion in 2006, US$31.07 billion in 2007, and US$36.2 billion in 2008.[^22] In 2025, trade between the two countries was worth US$153 billion.[^23] The 21st-century [Chinese transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reform_and_opening_up "Reform and opening up") into a [major economic power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Century "Chinese Century") has led to an increase of foreign investments in the [bamboo network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamboo_network "Bamboo network"). The a network of [overseas Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_Chinese "Overseas Chinese") businesses operating in the markets of Southeast Asia shares family and cultural ties.[^24] [^11]

China is Thailand's second-largest export market. China is also Thailand's largest importer of goods into the country in 2010.[^25] China's exports to Thailand computer components, electrical motors, consumer electronics, machinery, metal products, chemicals, and clothing.[^22] Thailand's exports to China computer components, rubber, refined oil, plastic pellets, chemical electronics, crude oil, wood products, and food.[^22]

China and Thailand signed a [free trade agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_trade_agreement "Free trade agreement") 2003 that covered agricultural products.[^26] It was also known as an early harvest agreement on agricultural products.[^27] China takes advantage of the [ASEAN–China Free Trade Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN%E2%80%93China_Free_Trade_Area "ASEAN–China Free Trade Area"), which came into effect January 1, 2010 and will allow its goods to be exported through ASEAN countries with zero or reduced [trade barriers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade_barriers "Trade barriers").[^28]

In 2011, China announced its plan to invest US$1.51 billion to establish the China City Complex in Bangkok for trading in Chinese-made goods like garments, ornaments, and household items.[^29]<sup><span title="Page: 160">: 160</span> </sup> On 6 June 2013, Thailand and China signed a five-year plan on strengthen their maritime cooperation.[^29]<sup><span title="Page: 162">: 162</span></sup>

Thailand was the first country able to export fresh [durian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durian "Durian") to China.[^30]

The public in Thailand view China's [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative") projects in the country, such as railways, positively.[^8]<sup><span title="Pages: 34–35">: 34–35</span></sup>

## Military relations

Military ties increased following the [2014 Thai coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Thai_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "2014 Thai coup d'état"). Between 2016 and 2022 Thailand purchased US$394 million worth of Chinese weapons, compared to US$207 million from the United States.[^44] Thai Prime Minister [Prayuth Chan-ocha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prayuth_Chan-ocha "Prayuth Chan-ocha") ordered 49 Chinese [VT-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VT-4 "VT-4") [main battle tanks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_battle_tanks "Main battle tanks") and 3 submarines, which cost more than US$1 billion.[^45] [^46] China and Thailand agreed to open a joint commercial arms factory in [Khon Kaen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khon_Kaen "Khon Kaen").[^45] It will be responsible for the assembly, production, and maintenance of land weapon systems for the [Royal Thai Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Thai_Army "Royal Thai Army"). Specific details were subject to further discussions between the ministry and [Norinco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norinco "Norinco"), which makes tanks, weapons, and other heavy equipment. In May 2017, the [Royal Thai Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Thai_Navy "Royal Thai Navy") signed a contract with the [China Shipbuilding Industry Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Shipbuilding_Industry_Corporation "China Shipbuilding Industry Corporation") for a S26T diesel-electric submarine, which is derived from the [Type 039A submarine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_039A_submarine "Type 039A submarine").[^46] The submarine is expected to be delivered in 2023.[^46] Chinese Military Commentator Zhou Chenming stated that China will also likely provide technical guidance to Thailand.[^46]

### Military exercises

In November 2015, Thailand and China held joint air exercises.[^29]<sup><span title="Page: 167">: 167</span> </sup> In March to April of 2025, the Royal Thai Navy and People's Liberation Army Navy conducted the Blue Strike-2025 joint exercises in [Zhanjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhanjiang "Zhanjiang").[^47] [^48]

## Educational relations

In 2006, Thailand's [Ministry of Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Education_\(Thailand\) "Ministry of Education (Thailand)") signed an agreement with [Hanban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanban "Hanban") to promote Chinese language education in Thailand.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 63">: 63</span> </sup> Thailand is one of the biggest promoters of Chinese language education in southeast Asia.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 62">: 62</span> </sup> In 2009, China and Thailand signed an agreement on education cooperation.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 63">: 63</span></sup>

Thailand was one of the first countries in southeast Asia to open a [Confucius Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius_Institute "Confucius Institute").[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 63">: 63</span> </sup> As of 2024, it has 16 Confucius Institutes, the most of any country in Asia.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 63">: 63</span> </sup> China built the Sirindhorn Chinese Language and Culture Center at Mae Fah Luang University in Thailand as a gift.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 61">: 61</span> </sup> It was named after [Princess Sirindhorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirindhorn "Sirindhorn") in honor of her role in cultural exchange between Thailand and China.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 61">: 61</span> </sup> The center hosts a Confucius Institute, which is operated in partnership with [Xiamen University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiamen_University "Xiamen University").[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 61">: 61</span></sup>

## Cultural relations

In 2012, China established a cultural center in Bangkok, Thailand.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 64">: 64</span> </sup> It is the largest cultural center established by China in southeast Asia.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 64">: 64</span></sup>

## Public opinion

According to a 2026 [feeling thermometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeling_thermometer "Feeling thermometer") poll by the [Carter Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carter_Center "Carter Center") and [Emory University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emory_University "Emory University"), Chinese opinion of Thailand was on average 38 out of 100.[^49]
