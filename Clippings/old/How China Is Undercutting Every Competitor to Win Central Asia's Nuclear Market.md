Kazakhstan just rejected Russia's nuclear monopoly. Here's who's building its future instead.  
  
For thirty years, Rosatom was the default option for any Central Asian country that wanted nuclear power. Then Kazakhstan awarded two of its three planned plants to China's CNNC — and quietly signaled that the era of automatic Russian dominance over the region's most strategic infrastructure may be over.  
  
In this panel, we break down the nuclear buildout underway across Central Asia — in Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, and Kyrgyzstan. Who's building, who's financing, who controls the fuel supply, and what happens when the loans come due.  
  
Featuring Sapargali Shalgimbayev, President of CFIVE (Astana) and former diplomat at the Kazakhstani Ministry of Foreign Affairs, and Andrew D'Anieri, Associate Director of the Atlantic Council's Eurasia Center.  
  
📌 Topics covered:  
  
— Why Central Asia is going nuclear when wind, solar, and hydro are all available  
— The BOOT financing model — and the risk countries never reach the Transfer stage  
— Fuel sourcing: Kazatomprom, the Ulba plant, and the Chinese supply chain  
— How CNNC is building plants for $5.5 billion and what that price actually means  
— Uzbekistan's hybrid NPP complex and Kyrgyzstan's small reactor plans  
— Western bidders — Westinghouse, EDF, KEPCO — and Kazakhstan's unawarded fourth plant  
— Is nuclear a permanent feature of Central Asia's energy future?
