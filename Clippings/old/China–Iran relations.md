**China–Iran relations** ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters "Simplified Chinese characters"): 中国–伊朗关系, [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_language "Persian language"): روابط ایران و چین) refer to the [bilateral relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilateralism "Bilateralism") between the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") and [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran "Iran"). The relationship between the two civilizations is one of the oldest continuous contacts in history, stretching back more than 2,500 years. Their earliest interactions began when the [Achaemenid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") and the [Zhou dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_dynasty "Zhou dynasty") had indirect contact in the 6th century BC.[^1] The first direct and official contact started between the [Parthian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parthian_Empire "Parthian Empire") and [Han dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_dynasty "Han dynasty") around 200 BC, when the Chinese envoy [Zhang Qian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhang_Qian "Zhang Qian") encountered the Parthian court.[^2] In 115 BC, Emperor [Wu of Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han "Emperor Wu of Han"), seeking to expand trade—especially in his search for high-quality horse herds—sent envoys with gifts to the court of [Mithridates II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mithridates_II_of_Parthia "Mithridates II of Parthia").[^3]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Zhang_Qian.jpg/250px-Zhang_Qian.jpg)

The 138–126 BC travels of Zhang Qian to the West, Mogao Caves, 618–712 AD mural.

Persian merchants became the main carriers of goods and culture across Central Asia, bringing their religions, music, and ideas into China.[^4] Ambassadors were exchanged, and Chinese travelers visited Iran regularly. Each side introduced new goods to the other, the most famous of which was [Chinese silk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_industry_in_China "Silk industry in China"). The trade route carrying this precious commodity from China to Europe passed through Iran, becoming known as the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road"), with Iran controlling much of the trade that moved silk, metals, glass, and textiles between East and West.[^5] [^6]

During the [Sassanid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sasanian_Empire "Sasanian Empire"), royal marriages took place.[^7] Several Sassanid kings and nobles married Chinese princesses or women from influential Chinese families to strengthen ties. These unions symbolized the close relationship between the two sides. Persian communities [lived in Chinese cities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iranians_in_China "Iranians in China") during the [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty"), and after the fall of Sassanid Empire, some Sassanid princes even took refuge in China.[^8] Trade also expanded by sea, with Persian sailors linking the [Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf") to Chinese ports.[^9] [^10] In the 13th century during the [Mongol era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire"), Iran (under [Ilkhanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ilkhanate "Ilkhanate")) and China (under [Yuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_dynasty "Yuan dynasty")) were part of a single empire.

In modern times, official diplomatic relations were established in 1937 between the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)") and [Pahlavi Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pahlavi_Iran "Pahlavi Iran"). During the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") before the mid-1960s, Iran was generally aligned against the People's Republic of China until both countries' relations with the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") and the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") shifted. After the [Iranian Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iranian_Revolution "Iranian Revolution"), China and the U.S. were both distrusted, but subsequent sanctions by the west pushed Iran closer to the east.[^11] Cooperation gradually expanded, with China purchasing more than 80 percent of [Iran's shipped oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_in_Iran "Energy in Iran") by 2025 and becoming a leading investor in transportation and industrial projects.[^12] Relations deepened through long-term economic agreements and [China's Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative"), making Iran one of China's most important [strategic partners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_partnerships_of_China "Diplomatic partnerships of China"). In 2021, the two nations signed a 25-year cooperation agreement.[^13]
