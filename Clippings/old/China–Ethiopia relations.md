**People's Republic of China–Ethiopia relations** were established in 1970. [Ethiopia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopia "Ethiopia") has an embassy in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") [^1] and the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC) has an embassy in Addis Ababa.[^2]

China's bilateral relationship with Ethiopia is one of its most prioritized in Africa and China believes Ethiopia is particularly significant in peace and security within east Africa.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 109">: 109</span> </sup> Relations are longstanding, with Chinese direct investment (FDI) in Ethiopia reaching US$4 billion and [bilateral trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilateral_trade "Bilateral trade") growing to $5.4 billion by 2016-2018.[^4] [^5] [^6] [^7]

## History

It is not precisely known when China and Ethiopia first made direct contact. The sinologist A. Hermann believed that a live rhinoceros that arrived at the court of the Chinese [Emperor Ping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_Ping_of_Han "Emperor Ping of Han") from the country of the "Agazi" or "Agazin" between AD 1 and 6 came from the [Horn of Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horn_of_Africa "Horn of Africa"). Ethiopian expert [Richard Pankhurst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Pankhurst_\(academic\) "Richard Pankhurst (academic)") is certain that by the [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty") (618–907) "the Chinese were certainly acquainted with the Horn of Africa." From that period onwards, China traded with not only Ethiopia and the Horn, but with the peoples of the Eastern African coast, obtaining elephants' tusks, rhinoceros horns, pearls, the musk of the [civet cat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_civet "African civet"), ambergris, and slaves. Starting in the [Yuan dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_dynasty "Yuan dynasty") the Chinese began to increasingly trade directly with Africans, which is attested not only in contemporary documents, but from archeological finds of Chinese coins and porcelain.[^8]

Despite this early commercial contact, neither side showed much interest in diplomatic activity with one another until the twentieth century. China was one of only five governments which refused to recognize [Italy's conquest of Ethiopia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Italo-Ethiopian_War "Second Italo-Ethiopian War").[^9] Relations were poor during the [Haile Selassie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haile_Selassie "Haile Selassie") era, when Ethiopia was allied with the western powers in the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"). During the [Korean War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_War "Korean War"), Ethiopia's [Kagnew Battalion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kagnew_Battalion "Kagnew Battalion") under the [United Nations Command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Command "United Nations Command") fought the PRC's [People's Volunteer Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Volunteer_Army "People's Volunteer Army") at the [Battle of Pork Chop Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Pork_Chop_Hill "Battle of Pork Chop Hill").[^10] [^11] PRC support with arms and military training for the [Eritrean Liberation Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eritrean_Liberation_Front "Eritrean Liberation Front") also contributed to tension between the countries.[^12]

### Establishment of diplomatic relations

The two countries established diplomatic relations on 24 November 1970 [^3]<sup><span title="Page: 347">: 347</span> </sup> when the People's Republic of China under [Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party") [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") agreed to recognize Eritrea as Ethiopian, in exchange for Haile Selassie's recognition of Taiwan (controlled by the Republic of China) as Chinese, although Ethiopia was among the signatories of the [Treaty of San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_San_Francisco "Treaty of San Francisco") in 1951.

In 1971, Ethiopia voted to admit representatives from the Beijing government and expel those representing the government in Taipei. Relations improved for a short period after the [Ethiopian revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopian_Civil_War "Ethiopian Civil War") of 1974, but became strained as the Ethiopian military junta developed increasingly close ties with the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"). After the [Ethiopian People's Revolutionary Democratic Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopian_People%27s_Revolutionary_Democratic_Front "Ethiopian People's Revolutionary Democratic Front") took power in 1991, relations have steadily improved, with increasing diplomatic contacts and growing trade and Chinese investment in the Ethiopian economy.[^13]

As a part of China's diplomacy, the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) conducted significant party-to-party relations with the Ethiopian People's Revolutionary Democratic Front (EPRDF).[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 111">: 111</span> </sup> The EPRDF's disintegration in 2019 disrupted China's knowledge and understanding of the domestic situation in Ethiopia.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 111">: 111</span> </sup> Although the CCP has also conducted party-to-party relations with the [Prosperity Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prosperity_Party "Prosperity Party"), as of 2023 it has yet to reach the same level of relations it had with the EPRDF and the [Tigray People's Liberation Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tigray_People%27s_Liberation_Front "Tigray People's Liberation Front").[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 112">: 112</span></sup>

## Official contacts, agreements, and treaties

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/1964-04_1964%E5%B9%B41%E6%9C%8830%E6%97%A5_%E4%B8%AD%E5%9B%BD%E8%AE%BF%E9%97%AE%E5%9F%83%E5%A1%9E%E4%BF%84%E6%AF%94%E4%BA%9A_%E5%91%A8%E6%81%A9%E6%9D%A5%E4%B8%8E%E6%B5%B7%E5%B0%94%C2%B7%E5%A1%9E%E6%8B%89%E8%A5%BF%E4%B8%80%E4%B8%96.jpg/250px-1964-04_1964%E5%B9%B41%E6%9C%8830%E6%97%A5_%E4%B8%AD%E5%9B%BD%E8%AE%BF%E9%97%AE%E5%9F%83%E5%A1%9E%E4%BF%84%E6%AF%94%E4%BA%9A_%E5%91%A8%E6%81%A9%E6%9D%A5%E4%B8%8E%E6%B5%B7%E5%B0%94%C2%B7%E5%A1%9E%E6%8B%89%E8%A5%BF%E4%B8%80%E4%B8%96.jpg)

Zhou Enlai and Haile Selassie in 1964

[Chinese premier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_China "Premier of China") [Zhou Enlai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Enlai "Zhou Enlai") visited Ethiopia in January 1964.[^13] The Ethiopian emperor [Haile Selassie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haile_Selassie "Haile Selassie") visited [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") in October 1971, where he was received by [CCP Chairman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party") [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong").[^14] [Qian Qichen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qian_Qichen "Qian Qichen"), China's vice-premier and minister of foreign affairs, visited Ethiopia in July 1989, January 1991 and January 1994. [CCP General Secretary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") [Jiang Zemin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiang_Zemin "Jiang Zemin") visited in May 1996.[^15]

In June 2001, the Ethiopian deputy foreign minister visited Beijing, where he expressed support for the " [One China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China "One China") " policy in the dispute with [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan").[^16] In December 2003, Chinese premier [Wen Jiabao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wen_Jiabao "Wen Jiabao") visited Ethiopia to attend the opening of the [Forum on China–Africa Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forum_on_China%E2%80%93Africa_Cooperation "Forum on China–Africa Cooperation"). In December 2004, the heads of the Ethiopian and Chinese legislatures met in Beijing and in a joint statement said that the two counties wish to expand all aspects of cooperation.[^17] In May 2007, China's Assistant Minister of Commerce Wang Chao visited [Addis Ababa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addis_Ababa "Addis Ababa") and signed a debt relief agreement worth US$18.5 million.[^18] In February 2008, the Chinese minister of construction met his counterpart in Addis Ababa, and reemphasized the commitment of the two governments to cooperation. The Ethiopian minister welcomed the involvement of Chinese construction companies in improving Ethiopian infrastructure.[^19] In November 2008, [Wu Bangguo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wu_Bangguo "Wu Bangguo"), the [chairman of the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Standing_Committee_of_the_National_People%27s_Congress "Chairman of the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress") visited Ethiopia where he met senior Ethiopian officials and political leaders including President [Girma Wolde-Giorgis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Girma_Wolde-Giorgis "Girma Wolde-Giorgis") and discussed ways to strengthen economic cooperation.[^20] [^21]

Agreements between the two countries include Agreement for Economic and Technological Cooperation (1971, 1988 and 2002); Trade Agreement (1971, 1976); Trade Protocol (1984,1986,1988); Agreement for Trade, Economic and Technological Cooperation (1996) and Agreement for Mutual Promotion and Protection of Investment (1988).[^15] In May 2009 the two countries signed an agreement to eliminate double taxation, expected to boost trade and investment.[^22]

Ethiopia and China signed a military cooperation agreement in 2005 which covers military training, technology exchange, and joint peacekeeping missions.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 239">: 239</span> </sup> As part this military cooperation agreement, Ethiopian field-grade officers routinely receive training in China.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 239">: 239</span></sup>

In 2014, China and Ethiopia signed a [strategic partnership agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_partnerships_of_China "Diplomatic partnerships of China").[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 211">: 211</span></sup>

China has an [extradition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extradition "Extradition") treaty with Ethiopia.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 188">: 188</span></sup>

## Economic relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Ethiopian_Embassy_in_Beijing.JPG/250px-Ethiopian_Embassy_in_Beijing.JPG)

Ethiopian Embassy in Beijing

The economic relationship is multi-faceted. Between 2000 and 2014, China provided over $12 billion in loan finance (usually tied to infrastructure projects undertaken by Chinese firms). There is growing Chinese investment in the Ethiopian economy, while imports of cheap consumer goods from China ($3.4 billion in 2015) greatly exceeding exports from Ethiopia to China ($380 million in 2015). The Chinese appear to be interested in Ethiopia for political reasons (among African countries, its governance and developmental orientation is closest to that of China, and it hosts the African Union headquarters), and as a business partner. Ethiopia's focus on infrastructure has created numerous opportunities for Chinese construction firms. Ethiopia is also a significant market for Chinese exports that will expand as Ethiopia's rapid economic growth continues. For Ethiopia, Chinese finance provides critical support for the government's legitimacy, as electricity, transport, and employment opportunities continue to expand, stimulating economic growth and helping promote exports to other countries. China's "business is business" approach is welcome by comparison to western aid providers who often link their contributions to changes in the Ethiopian legal and political structure.[^13] [^24]

As China began a [new town](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planned_community "Planned community") construction boom around 2010, the Chinese government and [state-owned enterprises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State-owned_enterprises_of_China "State-owned enterprises of China") began developing new towns with African governments, including Ethiopia.[^25]<sup><span title="Page: 316">: 316</span></sup>

### Direct aid

Chinese aid to Ethiopia has included dispatch of medical teams and teachers, and educational scholarships for Ethiopian students studying in China.[^15] [^26] Former Ethiopian President [Mulatu Teshome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mulatu_Teshome "Mulatu Teshome") was educated in China.[^27] China's aid program funded the construction of a vocational school in Ethiopia. Although the plan was to use Chinese vocational instructions, the school eventually switched to German instructors, with students learning skills including engineering, automobile, architecture and construction.[^28] In June 2009, the Chinese ambassador assisted in laying the foundation stone for the Tirunesh Dibaba Beijing Hospital, planned as a modern hospital 6000 square meters in size with 100 beds.[^29] The Chinese government is funding construction and will provide medical instruments and equipment.[^30] The hospital is named after Ethiopian runner [Tirunesh Dibaba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tirunesh_Dibaba "Tirunesh Dibaba"), who won two gold medals at the [2008 Beijing Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Beijing_Olympics "2008 Beijing Olympics").[^31] The hospital was completed in November 2011 and handed over to the Ethiopian government. Fifteen Chinese medical personnel were sent to help their Ethiopian counterparts.[^32]

### Infrastructure Loans

Chinese government has also provided significant loans to Ethiopia, most of which are [concessional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loan#Concessional "Loan").[^33]<sup><span title="Page: 229">: 229</span> </sup> These are nearly all tied to construction projects to be undertaken by Chinese enterprises such as the Addis Ababa ring road opened in 2003.[^34] [^35]

Some Chinese-built projects are financed by the Ethiopian government. In 2002, for example, the [Sino Hydro Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino_Hydro_Corporation "Sino Hydro Corporation") started work on the estimated US$224 million [Tekeze hydroelectric project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tekez%C3%A9_Dam "Tekezé Dam") with a 607-foot dam on the [Tekezé River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tekez%C3%A9_River "Tekezé River") due for completion in 2007.[^36] After delays due in part to problems with massive landslides, the project was completed for a final cost of US$365 million in July 2009 and should deliver 300 megawatts of power.[^37] [^38] In July 2009, Ethiopia signed further agreements with China for the Sino Hydro Corporation to build 2,150 megawatts of hydro-electric capacity with the Gibe IV ([Omo River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omo_River_\(Ethiopia\) "Omo River (Ethiopia)")) and Halele Werabesa dams, in a deal worth US$2.67 billion. China will cover 85% of the project costs through preferential buyer's credit and concessionary loans.[^39] [^40] The environmentalist [Richard Leakey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Leakey "Richard Leakey") has expressed concerns about the possible impact of the Omo river dams on [Lake Turkana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Turkana "Lake Turkana").[^41] The [Export-Import Bank of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Export%E2%80%93Import_Bank_of_China "Export–Import Bank of China") funded a railway from Addis Ababa to landlocked Ethiopia's main port in neighboring Djibouti, completed in 2016.[^42]

### Investment and trade

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Belt_and_Road_Initiative_participant_map.svg/250px-Belt_and_Road_Initiative_participant_map.svg.png)

Countries which signed cooperation documents related to the Belt and Road Initiative

Ethiopia is a particularly emphasized place where Chinese companies should " [go out](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Go_Out_policy "Go Out policy") " to, with Chinese top leadership describing Ethiopia as a "bridge" between the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative") and Africa's development, as well as "a pilot country for [China-Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa%E2%80%93China_economic_relations "Africa–China economic relations") production capacity cooperation." [^33]<sup><span title="Pages: 222–223">: 222–223</span> </sup> Chinese firms investing in Ethiopia are generally [greenfield investors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenfield_project#Greenfield_investment "Greenfield project").[^33]<sup><span title="Page: 233">: 233</span></sup>

By 2009, direct Chinese investment in Ethiopia had reached US$900 million.[^43]

Exports from Ethiopia to China have grown from negligible levels before 2000 to around US$130 million in 2006, primarily in raw materials such as sesame seeds and partially finished leather. Meanwhile, exports from China to Ethiopia have grown from under US$50 million in 1996 to US$430 million in 2006, including low-priced clothing, machinery and electronics equipment.[^13] The Ethiopian government has encouraged imports, purchasing Chinese equipment and supplying it to local construction and manufacturing firms on a lease-to-buy basis.[^44] Trade continues to grow rapidly. By 2015 bilateral trade had grown to $3.8 billion, with Ethiopian exports encouraged through special quota and tariff arrangements on many goods.[^43] In a paper prepared for the [OECD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OECD "OECD"), economist Tegegne Gebre Egziabher of Addis Ababa University notes that in the short term, cheap Chinese imports may have damaged local producers. However, the longer term benefit may be to stimulate improvements in efficiency and quality. Chinese investment in local infrastructure may assist towards this outcome.[^45]

[Sinopec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinopec "Sinopec") is a part owner of the joint venture POLY-GCL Petroleum, which as of 2023 is developing a $4 billion natural gas project in Ethiopia and which will include a pipeline to the Djiboutian coast and an export terminal.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 165">: 165</span> </sup> According to [David H. Shinn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_H._Shinn "David H. Shinn") and academic Joshua Eisenman, the project underscore China's commitment to expanding its import of liquified natural gas from African countries.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 165">: 165</span></sup>

## Space cooperation

The China Academy of Space Technology and Ethiopian engineers partnered to develop a remote-sensing microsatellite for Ethiopia, which China launched from the [Taiyuan Satellite Launch Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiyuan_Satellite_Launch_Center "Taiyuan Satellite Launch Center") in 2019.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 304">: 304</span> </sup> China provided a grant covering 75% of the project, financing training for Ethiopian engineers and the microsatellite itself.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 304">: 304</span> </sup> Ethiopia funded the construction of ground station facilities for the project.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 304">: 304</span> </sup> Ethiopia uses the satellite for environmental protection, weather forecasting, crop monitoring, natural resources applications, and earth observatory tasks.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 304">: 304</span></sup>

China launched a second satellite for Ethiopia in 2020, this one a [remote sensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_sensing "Remote sensing") satellite for natural disaster monitoring.[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 304">: 304</span></sup>

## Political relations

Ethiopia follows the [one China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_principle "One China principle"). It recognizes the People's Republic of China as the sole government of China and [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") as an integral part of China's territory, and supports all efforts by the PRC to "achieve [national reunification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_unification "Chinese unification") ". It also considers [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong"), [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang") and [Tibet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region "Tibet Autonomous Region") to be China's internal affairs.[^46]

## The Abole incident

In April 2007 fighters from the [Ogaden National Liberation Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ogaden_National_Liberation_Front "Ogaden National Liberation Front"), a group of ethnic [Somalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalis "Somalis") seeking independence from Ethiopia attacked workers at an exploratory oilfield in [Abole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abole "Abole"), a small town about 120 km south of [Jijiga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jijiga "Jijiga") in eastern Ethiopia. They killed about 65 Ethiopian and nine Chinese workers, and kidnapped another seven Chinese.[^47] The oilfield is run by a subsidiary of the government-owned [China Petroleum and Chemical Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Petroleum_and_Chemical_Corporation "China Petroleum and Chemical Corporation") (Sinopec). A Sinopec spokesperson said that the incident would not discourage the firm from further exploration.[^48] The Chinese Foreign Ministry avoided discussing the attackers' motives beyond stating that they were attempting to sabotage China's relationship with Ethiopia.[^49] A Chinese team was dispatched to investigate what had happened and look into ways of improving safety in the future.[^50]
