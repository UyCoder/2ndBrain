**China–Germany relations**, also called **Sino-German relations** [^1], are the [international relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_relations "International relations") between China and Germany. Until 1914, the Germans [leased](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leased "Leased") concessions in China, including little parts of [Yantai City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yantai_City "Yantai City") and [Qingdao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qingdao "Qingdao") on [Shandong Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shandong_Peninsula "Shandong Peninsula"). After [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), during which the Germans lost all their [leased](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leased "Leased") territories in China, Sino-German relations gradually improved as [German military advisers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-German_cooperation_1926%E2%80%931941 "Sino-German cooperation 1926–1941") assisted the [Kuomintang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuomintang "Kuomintang") government's [National Revolutionary Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Revolutionary_Army "National Revolutionary Army"), though this would change during the 1930s as [Adolf Hitler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Hitler "Adolf Hitler") gradually [allied himself with Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German-Japanese_relations#Rapprochement,_Axis_and_World_War_II_\(1920%E2%80%931945\) "German-Japanese relations").

During the aftermath of the [Eastern Front (World War II)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Front_\(World_War_II\) "Eastern Front (World War II)"), Germany was divided in two states: a liberal and democratic [West Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Germany "West Germany") and a communist [East Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Germany "East Germany"). [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") tensions led to a West German alliance with the United States against communism and thus allied against the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") (PRC). The Eastern part was allied through the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") with the PRC. After [German reunification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_reunification "German reunification"), relations between Germany and China improved.

## History

### Early contacts

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Shanghai-J-S-Bach-2626.jpg/250px-Shanghai-J-S-Bach-2626.jpg)

A statue of German composer J. S. Bach outside the entrance of Shanghai's main railway station; Bach never visited China.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Eduard_Gaertner_-_The_Chinese_Room_in_the_Royal_Palace%2C_Berlin_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/250px-Eduard_Gaertner_-_The_Chinese_Room_in_the_Royal_Palace%2C_Berlin_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg)

The Chinese Room in the Berlin Palace was decorated and furnished in Chinese styles. This is Eduard Gaertner 's 19th century painting of the room.

Unlike [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portugal "Portugal") or the [Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands"), German states were not involved, on the state level, in the early (16th-17th centuries) in contacts between Europe and China. Nonetheless, a number of individual Germans reached China at that time, in particular as [Jesuit missionaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesuit_China_missions "Jesuit China missions"). Some of them played a significant role in China's history, as did [Johann Adam Schall von Bell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Adam_Schall_von_Bell "Johann Adam Schall von Bell") (in China in 1619–1666), who was in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") when it was taken by the [Manchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchus "Manchus") in 1644, and soon became a trusted counselor of the early [Qing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_Dynasty "Qing Dynasty") leaders. Meanwhile, in [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rome "Rome") another German Jesuit, [Athanasius Kircher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athanasius_Kircher "Athanasius Kircher"), who never got to go to China himself, used reports of other Jesuits in China to compile *China Illustrata*, a work that was instrumental in popularizing knowledge about China among the 17th-century European readers.

The earliest Sino-German trade occurred overland through [Siberia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberia "Siberia"), and was subject to transit taxes by the [Russian government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"). To make trading more profitable, [Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia "Kingdom of Prussia") decided to take the sea route, and the first German merchant ships arrived in [Qing China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty"), as part of the [Royal Prussian Asian Trading Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Prussian_Asian_Trading_Company "Royal Prussian Asian Trading Company") of [Emden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emden "Emden") in the 1750s. In 1861, after China's defeat in the [Second Opium War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Opium_War "Second Opium War"), the [Treaty of Tientsin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Tientsin "Treaty of Tientsin") was signed, which opened formal commercial relations between various European states, including Prussia, with China.[^2]

### Early diplomatic relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/LiHungTschang.jpg/250px-LiHungTschang.jpg)

Qing trade minister Li Hongzhang and German chancellor Otto von Bismarck in Bismarck's mansion at Friedrichsruh in 1896.

In 1859, following the [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") 's defeat in the [Second Opium War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Opium_War "Second Opium War"), Prussia sent the [Eulenburg Expedition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eulenburg_Expedition "Eulenburg Expedition") to negotiate commercial treaties with the Qing, the [Empire of Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_Japan "Empire of Japan") and [Siam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siam "Siam"). On 2 September 1861, [Friedrich Albrecht zu Eulenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Albrecht_zu_Eulenburg "Friedrich Albrecht zu Eulenburg") and a representative from the [Zongli Yamen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zongli_Yamen "Zongli Yamen") signed the [Treaty of Tianjin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E4%B8%AD%E5%BE%B7%E9%80%9A%E5%95%86%E6%9D%A1%E7%BA%A6 "zh:中德通商条约"), which opened formal commercial relations between China and [Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussia "Prussia"), which represented the [German Customs Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Customs_Union "German Customs Union"). Prussia would later on become the dominant and leading part of the newly founded [German Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Empire "German Empire"). The treaty would govern Sino-German relations until [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), when the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)") repudiated the treaty unilaterally.

During the late 19th century, Sino-foreign trade was dominated by the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire"), and [Otto von Bismarck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck "Otto von Bismarck") was eager to establish German footholds in China to balance the [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland "United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland") dominance. In 1885, Bismarck had the [Reichstag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reichstag_\(German_Empire\) "Reichstag (German Empire)") pass a steamship subsidy bill which offered direct service to China. In the same year, he sent the first German banking and industrial survey group to evaluate investment possibilities, which led to the establishment of the [Deutsch-Asiatische Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deutsch-Asiatische_Bank "Deutsch-Asiatische Bank") in 1890. Through these efforts, Germany was second to Britain in trading and shipping in China by 1896.

Due to the decisiveness of steam-powered fleets over the [junks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Junk_\(ship\) "Junk (ship)") of the small [Imperial Chinese Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Chinese_Navy "Imperial Chinese Navy") during China's conflicts with European powers in the mid-nineteenth century, the Chinese began a naval construction program in the 1880s to meet these threats more effectively. They enlisted British and [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Empire "German Empire") assistance, and two [*Dingyuan* -class ironclads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dingyuan-class_ironclad "Dingyuan-class ironclad") were ordered from Germany, the *[Dingyuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_ironclad_Dingyuan "Chinese ironclad Dingyuan")* and the *[Zhenyuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_ironclad_Zhenyuan "Chinese ironclad Zhenyuan")*.[^3] [^4]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/20240730_Former_Site_of_Kiautschou_Governor%27s_Hall_02.jpg/250px-20240730_Former_Site_of_Kiautschou_Governor%27s_Hall_02.jpg)

Being colonized and influenced by Germany, Qingdao and Shandong Province have many German remnants, like Tsingtao Brewery, European townhouses, Qingdao–Jinan railway, and many factories and coal mines.

In 1897, the [German empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_empire "German empire") took advantage of the [murder of two German missionaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juye_Incident "Juye Incident") to invade [Qingdao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qingdao "Qingdao") and founded the [Jiaozhou Bay colony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kiautschou_Bay_concession "Kiautschou Bay concession"). Germany took control of key points in the Shandong Peninsula. In 1898, it leased for 99 years (or until 1997, as the British did in Hong Kong's New Territories) Jiaozhou Bay and its port of Qingdao under threat of force. Development was a high priority for Berlin. Over 200 million marks were invested in world-class harbor facilities such as berths, heavy machinery, rail yards, and a floating dry dock. Private enterprise worked across the Shandong Province, opening mines, banks, breweries, factories, shops and rail lines.[^5]

In 1900, Germany took part in the [Eight-Nation Alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eight-Nation_Alliance "Eight-Nation Alliance") that was sent to relieve the [Siege of the International Legations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_the_International_Legations "Siege of the International Legations") in Beijing during the [Boxer Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxer_Rebellion "Boxer Rebellion"). The Kaiser called for very harsh punishment of the Chinese, but his soldiers arrived after the fighting was largely over.[^6]

In 1907–1908, Kaiser Wilhelm II sent [Prince of Bülow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_von_B%C3%BClow "Bernhard von Bülow"), Chancellor at the time, to discuss a potential treaty of triple alliance with the Qing high-ranking official Yuan and President [Theodore Roosevelt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt "Theodore Roosevelt").[^7] But it was overturned in favor of the [Gentlemen's Agreement of 1907](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gentlemen%27s_Agreement_of_1907 "Gentlemen's Agreement of 1907") and due to the passing of Grand Empress Dowager, [Cixi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empress_Dowager_Cixi "Empress Dowager Cixi").

During the [Xinhai revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinhai_revolution "Xinhai revolution"), revolutionaries killed a German arms dealer in Hankou as he was delivering arms to the Qing.[^8] Revolutionaries killed 2 Germans and wounded 2 other Germans at the battle of Hanyang, including a former colonel.[^9]

### Early 20th century

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Wang_Jingwei_and_Nazis.jpg/250px-Wang_Jingwei_and_Nazis.jpg)

Wang Jingwei with German diplomats as head of the state of Japanese puppet China

The German military had a major role in Republican China.[^10] The German Navy's [East Asia Squadron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asia_Squadron "East Asia Squadron") was in charge of Germany's concessions at [Qingdao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qingdao "Qingdao"), and spent heavily to set up modern facilities that would be a showcase for Asia. The Empire of Japan seized the German operations in 1914 after sharp battles. After World War I, the [Weimar Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_Republic "Weimar Republic") provided extensive advisory services to the Republic of China, especially training for the Chinese army. As well as studying and visiting Japan, Germans visited and studied China in between the two World Wars.

Colonel General [Hans von Seeckt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_von_Seeckt "Hans von Seeckt"), the former commander the German army, organized the training of China's elite army units and the beginning civil war that included military activities against the Chinese Communists from 1933 to 1935.[^11] All military academies had German officers, as did most army units. In addition, German engineers provided expertise and bankers provided loans for China's railroad system. Trade with Germany flourished in the 1920s, with Germany as China's largest supplier of government credit. According to some not confirmed sources in 1937, [H.H. Kung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H.H._Kung "H.H. Kung") visited Germany in an attempt " *to convince Hitler to side with China against Japan* ".[^12] [^13] With assurances of the contrary, Nazi Germany sided with the Japanese after they invaded China the following month and the last important German advisor left in 1938.[^14] [^15]

However, at the same time, the exiled German Communist [Otto Braun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Braun_\(communist\) "Otto Braun (communist)") was in China as a [Comintern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comintern "Comintern") agent, probably sent in 1934, to advise the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) on military strategy and taking a major part in The [Long March](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_March "Long March") under a Chinese name, Li De ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"): [李德](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9D%8E%E5%BE%B7 "wikt:李德"); [pinyin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin "Pinyin"): *Lǐ Dé*); it was only many years later that Otto Braun and "Li De" came to be known as the same person.[^16]

### World War II (1941–1945)

Sino-German cooperation collapsed in 1939 due to the start of [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") in Europe, forcing many Chinese nationals to leave Germany due to increased government surveillance and coercion. The example the Japan set in the [Second Sino-Japanese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War "Second Sino-Japanese War") forced Hitler to replace China with Japan as the Nazi's strategic ally in East Asia.[^17] Following the Japanese [Attack on Pearl Harbor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor "Attack on Pearl Harbor") in 1941, the Chinese declared war on Germany, which resulted in the [Gestapo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gestapo "Gestapo") launching mass arrests of Chinese nationals across Germany. The very few Chinese in [German-occupied Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupation_of_Poland_\(1939%E2%80%931945\) "Occupation of Poland (1939–1945)") were also victims of Nazi Germany, with 13 deported from [Warsaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warsaw "Warsaw") to the [Gross-Rosen concentration camp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross-Rosen_concentration_camp "Gross-Rosen concentration camp") in 1944.[^18] At the end of the war, the Chinese communities in cities such as [Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin "Berlin"), [Hamburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamburg "Hamburg"), and [Bremen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bremen "Bremen") that existed before the war were destroyed.

### Division of Germany and the Cold War (1945–1990)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-57000-0388%2C_Berlin%2C_V._SED-Parteitag%2C_4.Tag.jpg/250px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-57000-0388%2C_Berlin%2C_V._SED-Parteitag%2C_4.Tag.jpg)

China's Dong Biwu (second from the right) with Otto Grotewohl (third from the right) and Walter Ulbricht (fourth from the right) in East Berlin (1958)

The [Federal Republic of Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Germany "Federal Republic of Germany") or [West Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Germany "West Germany") initially did not recognize the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") primarily because of its hard-line anti-communist foreign policy of the [Hallstein Doctrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallstein_Doctrine "Hallstein Doctrine"). West Germany formally supported the [One-China policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-China_policy "One-China policy"), in hopes of finding Chinese backing of the reunification of Germany. In October 1972, West Germany officially established diplomatic contacts with the PRC, although unofficial contacts had been in existence since 1964, three years after the beginning of the Sino-Soviet Split.[^19] [^20] [^21]

The [German Democratic Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Democratic_Republic "German Democratic Republic") or [East Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Germany "East Germany") also managed to have good relations with the PRC, despite the [Sino-Soviet Split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_Split "Sino-Soviet Split") that occurred for most of the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") until the [1989 Sino-Soviet Summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1989_Sino-Soviet_Summit "1989 Sino-Soviet Summit").[^22] Since the March 1982 speech on Sino-Soviet rapprochement by General Secretary [Leonid Brezhnev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev "Leonid Brezhnev") to the [Communist Party of Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Uzbekistan "Communist Party of Uzbekistan") in [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent"), Sino-East German relations began to steadily improve. In June 1986, Foreign Minister [Wu Xueqian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wu_Xueqian "Wu Xueqian") visited [East Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Berlin "East Berlin") in the highest-level Chinese delegation to [Eastern Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Europe "Eastern Europe") since the 1961 split.[^23] Moreover, Chairman [Erich Honecker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_Honecker "Erich Honecker") visited [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") in early October 1986, where he was met by Chinese President [Li Xiannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_Xiannian "Li Xiannian") with in a welcoming ceremony on [Tiananmen Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiananmen_Square "Tiananmen Square") a [military band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_band "Military band") and a marchpast by the [People's Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") [honor guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honor_guard "Honor guard"). The visit became the first official visit by an [Eastern Bloc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Bloc "Eastern Bloc") leader to the PRC.[^24] [^25]

### Reunified Germany (1990–present)

#### Early post-communism in united Germany

The frequent high-level diplomatic visits are acknowledged to have helped guarantee the smooth development of Sino-German relations. From 1993 to 1998, German and Chinese leaders met face-to-face 52 times: Among those Chinese leaders who visited Germany were [Jiang Zemin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiang_Zemin "Jiang Zemin"), former [General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party"); [Qiao Shi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qiao_Shi "Qiao Shi"), former [Chairman of the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Standing_Committee_of_the_National_People%27s_Congress "Chairman of the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress") (NPC); and [Li Peng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_Peng "Li Peng"), former [Premier of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Premier of the People's Republic of China") and Chairman of the NPC Standing. Meanwhile, German leaders who visited China included President [Roman Herzog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Herzog "Roman Herzog"), Chancellor [Helmut Kohl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmut_Kohl "Helmut Kohl"), Foreign Minister [Klaus Kinkel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klaus_Kinkel "Klaus Kinkel") and Minister of State at the German [Federal Foreign Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Foreign_Office "Federal Foreign Office") [Ludger Volmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ludger_Volmer&action=edit&redlink=1 "Ludger Volmer (page does not exist)"). Among these leaders, Chancellor Kohl visited China twice in 1993 and 1995. Since the new German government came into power in October 1998, Chancellor [Gerhard Schröder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerhard_Schr%C3%B6der "Gerhard Schröder") has paid three visits to China. One after another from Germany came Vice Prime Minister and Foreign Minister [Joschka Fischer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joschka_Fischer "Joschka Fischer"), Defense Minister [Rudolf Scharping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Scharping "Rudolf Scharping"), and Minister of Economics and Technology [Werner Müller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_M%C3%BCller_\(politician\) "Werner Müller (politician)"). At the same time, Germany welcomed Chinese Primer [Zhu Rongji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhu_Rongji "Zhu Rongji"), Foreign Minister [Tang Jiaxuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_Jiaxuan "Tang Jiaxuan"), State Councilor [Wu Yi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wu_Yi_\(politician\) "Wu Yi (politician)"), member of the [Political Bureau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Politburo of the Chinese Communist Party") of the CCP [Central Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Central Committee of the Chinese Communist Party") [Wei Jianxing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wei_Jianxing "Wei Jianxing") as well as member of the [CCP Politburo Standing Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Politburo Standing Committee of the Chinese Communist Party") [Hu Jintao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hu_Jintao "Hu Jintao").

#### 2000s

Relations would continue to improve even more after 1998. For instance, [Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin "Berlin") and [Peking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") (at that time yet called Peking), and fervently opposed [the invasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq_War "Iraq War") of [Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq "Iraq") made by the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") in 2003, and in 2006 Germany (the largest economy and the most populous country of the European Union) and the Chinese Republic further enhanced their foreign political, economic and diplomatic relations and even ties within one of an EU-Sino strategic partnerships. For example, Germany and Republic of China also opposed direct military involvement of US in the [2011 Libyan civil war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_Libyan_civil_war "2011 Libyan civil war") that had been made after the liberation of the accused Libean medical sisters and doctors, that were accused of crimes against children there, and liberated by the French President. [Huawei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huawei "Huawei"), a major Chinese tech company, has collaborated with [Porsche Design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porsche_Design "Porsche Design"), a German design company in developing their Porsche Design Huawei Smartwatch GT 2 [^26] [^27]

#### 2010s

Before the 2011 visit of China's PM Wen Jiabao, the Chinese government issued a "White Book on the accomplishments and perspective of Sino-German cooperation", the first of its kind for a European country. The visit also marked the first Sino-German government consultations, an exclusive mechanism for Sino-German communications. In 2018, [Mercedes-Benz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercedes-Benz "Mercedes-Benz") apologized to China for quoting the [Dalai Lama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/14th_Dalai_Lama "14th Dalai Lama") on Instagram.[^28] In September 2019, China's ambassador to Germany stated that the meeting between Germany's foreign minister and Hong Kong activist [Joshua Wong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joshua_Wong "Joshua Wong") will damage relations with China.[^29]

#### 2020s

On 22 April 2020, Germany's [Interior Ministry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Ministry_of_the_Interior,_Building_and_Community "Federal Ministry of the Interior, Building and Community") released a letter revealing that Chinese diplomats had contacted German Government officials "to encourage them" "to make positive statements on how China was handling the coronavirus pandemic". The German government did not comply with these requests.[^30] In December 2020, with Germany ending its two-year term on the [United Nations Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council"), Chinese Ambassador [Geng Shuang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geng_Shuang "Geng Shuang") responded "Out of the bottom of my heart: good riddance" in response to the German Ambassador [Christoph Heusgen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoph_Heusgen "Christoph Heusgen") 's appeal to free two Canadians [Michael Kovrig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Kovrig "Michael Kovrig") and [Michael Spavor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Spavor "Michael Spavor") detained in China.[^31]

In October 2021, a tweet from the *[Global Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Times "Global Times")* called for a "final solution to the Taiwan question" which was condemned by German politician [Frank Müller-Rosentritt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_M%C3%BCller-Rosentritt "Frank Müller-Rosentritt") for its similarity to the “final solution to the Jewish question” which resulted in [the Holocaust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Holocaust "The Holocaust").[^32] In December 2021, as a result of a diplomatic spat between [Lithuania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithuania "Lithuania") and China over Taiwan and [human rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights_in_China "Human rights in China") China pressured [Continental AG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_AG "Continental AG") and other German companies to stop doing business with Lithuania.[^33] [^34] The [Bundesverband der Deutschen Industrie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bundesverband_der_Deutschen_Industrie "Bundesverband der Deutschen Industrie") described the expansion of the ban on importing Lithuanian goods to components in integrated supply chains as a "devastating own goal." [^35] The German government approved a quarter ownership for China shipper, [COSCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COSCO "COSCO"), in the Hamburg container terminal port in May 2023.[^36] 2023 also brought the Federal Government Strategy on China, which laid out the German government's stance on China.[^37]

In September 2023, German Foreign Minister [Annalena Baerbock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annalena_Baerbock "Annalena Baerbock") named [CCP General Secretary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") a dictator next to [Vladimir Putin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Putin "Vladimir Putin").[^38] In April 2024, German authorities arrested three German nationals for [spying for China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_intelligence_activity_abroad "Chinese intelligence activity abroad") and arranging illicit military [technology transfers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technology_transfer "Technology transfer").[^39] [^40] The same month, authorities arrested a suspected spy, Jian Guo, working for [Maximilian Krah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximilian_Krah "Maximilian Krah").[^41] [^42] The spy was later sentenced to four years in prison.[^43] In July 2024, Germany blocked the sale of a gas turbine business to a subsidiary of [China State Shipbuilding Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_State_Shipbuilding_Corporation "China State Shipbuilding Corporation") for national security reasons.[^44] The same month, the German government announced a deal with telecommunication companies in the country to remove Chinese 5G equipment by 2029.[^45]

In July 2024, Germany summoned the Chinese ambassador over a 2021 cyber-attack against the [Federal Agency for Cartography and Geodesy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Agency_for_Cartography_and_Geodesy "Federal Agency for Cartography and Geodesy") attributed to "Chinese state actors" for the "purpose of espionage." [^46] In August 2024, Germany's IT sector trade association reported that 45% of German businesses had suffered cyberattacks or [industrial espionage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_espionage "Industrial espionage") traced to China.[^47] In October 2024, a Chinese woman was arrested in [Leipzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leipzig "Leipzig") on suspicion passing information on arms deliveries to Chinese intelligence.[^48] [^49] In November 2024, German authorities investigated a Chinese shipping vessel, the *[Yi Peng 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yi_Peng_3 "Yi Peng 3")*, in the [Baltic Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_Sea "Baltic Sea") after it was found be in the vicinity of two severed undersea fiber-optic data cables and suspected of sabotage.[^50] [^51]

In January 2025, German prosecutors indicted three German nationals for illegally obtaining information on military technology for the [Ministry of State Security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_State_Security_\(China\) "Ministry of State Security (China)") (MSS).[^52] In July 2025, Germany's Foreign Office summoned China's ambassador to Germany after a [PLAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army_Navy "People's Liberation Army Navy") warship targeted a German aircraft, a specially configured [Beechcraft King Air 350](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beechcraft_King_Air_350 "Beechcraft King Air 350") [^53] that was flying towards [Djibouti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djibouti "Djibouti"), with a laser during [Operation Aspides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Aspides "Operation Aspides") in the [Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea").[^54] [^55] According to *[Der Spiegel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Der_Spiegel "Der Spiegel")*, the aircraft aborted its mission and returned to Djibouti after being laser-targeted by a Chinese PLAN frigate. A German Foreign Ministry spokesperson stated the ship had been seen in the area before and “laser-targeted the aircraft with no reason or prior communication during a routine mission flight.” [^53] The PLAN ship accused of carrying out the laser attack was believed to be from the three-ship PLAN 47th Chinese Naval Escort Taskforce, comprising destroyer CNS *Baotou* (133), frigate CNS *Honghe* (523) and fleet oiler [CNS *Gaoyouhu*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNS_Gaoyouhu "CNS Gaoyouhu") (904).[^56]

In November 2025, German Vice Chancellor [Lars Klingbeil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lars_Klingbeil "Lars Klingbeil") visited China as part of the biannual financial dialogue talks between the two countries. During the visit, he met with Vice Premier [He Lifeng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/He_Lifeng "He Lifeng") and [Wang Huning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Huning "Wang Huning"), the [chairman of the National Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_National_Committee_of_the_Chinese_People%27s_Political_Consultative_Conference "Chairman of the National Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference").[^57] He also met with [Liu Haixing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liu_Haixing "Liu Haixing"), the [head of the International Department of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_the_International_Department_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Head of the International Department of the Chinese Communist Party"), in his capacity as the co-leader of the Social Democratic Party as part of the party dialogue between the SPD and the CCP established in 1984.[^58]

## Political relations

Germany follows the one China policy. According to its national strategy, Germany "has close and good relations with Taiwan in many areas and wants to expand them. As part of the EU's One-China policy, we support issue-specific involvement on the part of democratic Taiwan in international organisations. The status quo of the Taiwan Strait may only be changed by peaceful means and mutual consent". According to Foreign Minister [Frank-Walter Steinmeier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank-Walter_Steinmeier "Frank-Walter Steinmeier") in 2008, Germany considers Taiwan to be a part of China.[^59] [^60]

In July 2019, the UN ambassadors from 22 nations, including Germany, signed a joint letter to the [United Nations Human Rights Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council "United Nations Human Rights Council") condemning [China's persecution of the Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution_of_Uyghurs_in_China "Persecution of Uyghurs in China") as well as its mistreatment of other minority groups, urging the [CCP General Secretary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") 's [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_under_Xi_Jinping "China under Xi Jinping") to close the [Xinjiang internment camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang_internment_camps "Xinjiang internment camps").[^61] [^62] In 2019, [Volkswagen Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volkswagen_Group "Volkswagen Group") came under pressure for cooperating with the Chinese government in the region of [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang").[^63] In June 2020, Germany opposed the [Hong Kong national security law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Hong_Kong_national_security_law "2020 Hong Kong national security law").[^64] On 6 October 2020, Germany's ambassador to the UN, on behalf of the group of 39 countries including Germany, the U.K. and the U.S., made a statement to denounce China for its treatment of ethnic minorities and for curtailing freedoms in Hong Kong.[^65]

## Economic relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/BMW_Tiexi_Factory.jpg/250px-BMW_Tiexi_Factory.jpg)

BMW assembly factory in Tiexi, Shenyang

Chinese markets are important for [German industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_industry "German industry"), particularly the [German automotive industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automotive_industry_in_Germany "Automotive industry in Germany").[^66]<sup><span title="Page: 24">: 24</span> </sup> Germany is China's biggest trading partner and technology exporter in Europe.[^67] During much of the 2000s and 2010s, China and Germany had highly complementary economies, with Germany supplying industrial machinery to China. This changed during the 2020s, when Chinese manufacturers grew increasingly advanced, leading them to be direct competitors to German companies as well as China to be less dependent on German industrial products overall.[^68] The sales of German automotive brands in China have declined rapidly in the 2020s due to the increasing competitiveness of Chinese brands and the quick adoption of [electric vehicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_vehicle_industry_in_China "Electric vehicle industry in China") technology. From 2020 to 2025, German carmakers have seen their market share in China decline from 24 percent to 12 percent.[^69] [^70] In 2025, for the first time, Germany imported more capital goods from China than it exported.[^68]

The trade volume between China and Germany surpassed 100 billion U.S. dollars in 2008.[^71] By 2014, [German Chancellor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Chancellor "German Chancellor") [Angela Merkel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angela_Merkel "Angela Merkel") had visited China on trade missions seven times since assuming office in 2005; this underlines the importance of China to the German economy.[^72] In February 2024, [Volkswagen Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volkswagen_Group "Volkswagen Group") and Chinese [electric vehicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_vehicle "Electric vehicle") manufacturer [XPeng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XPeng "XPeng") signed a technology cooperation and joint development agreement on platform and software.[^73]

## Public opinion

A survey published in 2025 by the [Pew Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pew_Research_Center "Pew Research Center") found that 67% of German people had an unfavorable view of China, while 29% had a favorable view.[^74]
