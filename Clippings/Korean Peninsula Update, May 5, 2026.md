## Toplines

**North Korea will likely significantly expand its arsenal of nuclear warheads in the next decade. This expansion could force the United States and South Korea to reevaluate their ability to intercept a North Korean nuclear attack or neutralize the nuclear threat through preemptive strikes.** *Bloomberg* reported on April 28 that, based on a January statement from South Korean President Lee Jae Myung, North Korea can produce enough fissile material for as many as 20 nuclear warheads annually.\[1\] This production rate would allow North Korea to field 290 nuclear warheads by 2035, an equivalent nuclear arsenal to France, the world’s fourth-largest nuclear power by warhead count.\[2\] ISW-CDOT has previously assessed that North Korea is likely increasing its nuclear warhead production capacity through expanded enrichment facilities.\[3\]

Workers’ Party of Korea (WPK) General Secretary Kim Jong Un tasked North Korea’s nuclear program with developing large nuclear warheads capable of threatening the United States and smaller tactical nuclear warheads to deter South Korea during the 9th Party Congress in February.\[4\] Expanding the production of fissile material needed for nuclear warheads is a critical step towards achieving this goal. An annual production of fissile material for 20 nuclear warheads would likely allow North Korea to enhance the survivability of its nuclear deterrent by ensuring it does not rest solely on a handful of critical systems. North Korea could also use an increased fissile material production rate to improve its ability to threaten the United States with long-range intercontinental ballistic missiles (ICBM). North Korean ICBMs can already reach the US mainland, but it is unclear if North Korea can currently field them at a sufficient scale to penetrate US missile defenses.\[5\] Advances in fissile material production capacity could allow North Korea to pursue both a more survivable and more threatening nuclear armament that can deter its regional adversaries and the United States.

**Russian regional authorities are seeking to increase economic cooperation with North Korea, which may support the development of the Rason Special Economic Zone (SEZ) and North Korean efforts to diversify trade relations beyond the People’s Republic of China (PRC).** The government of Primorsky Krai, a Russian region bordering North Korea across the Tumen River, announced plans on April 30 to establish a working group to expand commerce with North Korea.\[6\] Seoul-based media outlet *NK News* reported that the working group would support Russian businesses entering the North Korean market and increasing export volumes.\[7\] The group listed shipments of organic fertilizers and fish products as export priorities. *NK News* separately reported that multiple Russian businesses have expanded exports and launched joint ventures with North Korea since 2025, including plans to launch a joint plastics factory in Vladivostok and North Korean construction of a meat-processing plant in Russia.\[8\]

Increased economic cooperation between Primorsky Krai and North Korea could develop the Rason SEZ. Sanctions imposed by the UN Security Council (UNSC), the United States, and South Korea have historically constrained the growth of North Korean SEZs.\[9\] Russia has expanded trade with North Korea in violation of UNSC sanctions since its 2022 invasion of Ukraine, however. The North Korean-Russian trade volume increased nine-fold the year after the invasion, going from approximately 3.78 million US dollars in 2022 to 34.4 million US dollars in 2023.\[10\] North Korea and Russia are set to launch a road bridge across the Tumen River by June 19.\[11\] The bridge would supplement the existing Korea-Russia Friendship Bridge, a rail bridge with limited capacity for commercial road transport.\[12\] The bridge would accommodate up to 300 vehicles per day, enabling Russian businesses to ship goods directly to the Rason SEZ.\[13\]

Russian investment in the Rason SEZ would allow North Korea to receive foreign capital without risking broader market liberalization. North Korea could also seek to reduce trade dependence on the PRC by establishing an independent trade channel with Russia. North Korea and the PRC have recovered bilateral trade to pre-COVID-19 levels, reaching 2.73 billion US dollars in 2025, alongside the resumption of passenger train and air routes in March 2026.\[14\]

**The South Korean Ministry of Unification (MOU) will likely expand public discussions on whether to prioritize coexistence with North Korea over reunification.** South Korean Minister of Unification Chung Dong-young referred to inter-Korean relations as “ROK-Joseon relations” on April 25.\[15\] Chung referred to North Korea as the “Democratic People’s Republic of Korea (DPRK)” and characterized inter-Korean relations as “ROK-Joseon relations” on March 25.\[16\] This was the first time a high-ranking South Korean official used this terminology to refer to North Korea.\[17\] “Joseon” \[조선\] is the term that North Korea uses to refer to itself. South Korea typically uses “Bukhan” \[북한\], which means Northern Korea, to refer to North Korea. South Korea’s terminology reflects its official position that does not recognize North Korea as a separate state.\[18\] The MOU hosted an academic conference on April 29 regarding changing the official name of North Korea to “Joseon,” following Chung’s statement.\[19\] Vice Unification Minister Kim Nam-jung stated that “recognizing each other’s realities with respectful language and institutions” would help the two Koreas move towards peaceful coexistence.\[20\] An unnamed MOU official stated on April 28 that the ministry intends to hold public discussions to decide on the official term for North Korea.\[21\] North Korea began referring to South Korea as the “Republic of Korea (ROK)” instead of “South Joseon” in 2023 following WPK General Secretary Kim Jong Un’s announcement of “hostile two-state relations.”\[22\] North Korea‘s terminology change was intended to express that the ROK and DPRK are separate nations, and no longer a single ethnic community.

The MOU’s desire for this terminology shift appears to be a conciliatory reciprocal measure to promote dialogue with North Korea. The MOU is showing respect for North Korea by referring to it by its official title, as Pyongyang now does for Seoul. This measure reflects broader MOU efforts to treat North Korea as a separate state. The MOU is also likely to prioritize short-term peaceful coexistence over long-term reunification. Chung emphasized the current impracticality of reunification on April 29 and argued that the immediate necessity for inter-Korean relations is the “institutionalization of peace,” reiterating the Lee Jae Myung administration’s policy of peaceful coexistence with North Korea.\[23\] North Korea could perceive these measures as South Korea accepting North Korean demands and its preferred regional order, however. This attempt may also cause internal opposition within the Lee administration and the public. The South Korean Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MOFA) and South Korean security officials have often been misaligned with Chung’s MOU on North Korea policy. Chung argued for South Korea to not participate as a co-sponsor of the United Nations Human Rights Council’s North Korea human rights resolution in March to avoid provoking Pyongyang.\[24\] South Korean National Security Advisor Wi Sung-lac and MOFA opposed the move.\[25\] A Presidential civilian advisory group, the Peaceful Unification Advisory Council, stated that 51.6 percent of its April poll respondents do not recognize the North Korean regime despite the administration’s coexistence policy.\[26\] This internal opposition underscores a significant gap between public opinion and MOU measures to respect the North Korean regime, a core principle of the peaceful coexistence policy.

## Key Takeaways

1. **North Korean Nuclear Modernization:** North Korea will likely significantly expand its arsenal of nuclear warheads, possibly to 290 warheads by 2035, according to South Korean estimates. An expanded North Korean nuclear force could require the United States and South Korea to reevaluate their ability to counter North Korean nuclear capabilities.
2. **Russian-North Korean Relations:** Russian regional authorities are working to expand economic cooperation with North Korea. North Korea likely seeks to diversify its trade relations and lessen its economic dependence on the PRC.
3. **Inter-Korean Relations:** The MOU proposed changing the official name of North Korea to “Joseon,” reflecting North Korea’s preferred terminology. This terminology change implicitly recognizes North Korea as a separate state, which some MOU officials believe would encourage “peaceful coexistence” between the Koreas.

## North Korean Domestic Politics

**North Korea appeared to strengthen its political control over North Korean youth while urging greater mobilization of youth labor forces in key economic projects at the 11th Congress of the Socialist Patriotic Youth League.\[27\]** North Korean state media reported on May 1 that the 11th Congress of the Youth League—presided over by senior WPK officials—reviewed its five-year performance, revised the organization’s charter, and elected new leadership from April 28 to 30.\[28\] The conference centered WPK General Secretary Kim Jong Un’s “love and trust” of young people and the portrayal of the youth as North Korea’s frontline force in fulfilling state programs decided at the WPK 9th Congress.\[29\] North Korea’s Socialist Constitution mandates that North Koreans age 14 to 30 join the Youth League. The Youth League has approximately 5 million members and maintains organizational units at the provincial, district, and school levels.\[30\] Youth League Membership serves as a political requirement for North Koreans in securing social status and pursuing pathways to provincial or central party positions.

Members of the Youth League have been central to Kim’s economic development efforts in recent years. North Korean state media reported that around 20,000 Youth League members “self-enlisted” to serve in construction sites, coal mines, factories, and rural farms. Members of the Youth League also contributed to the building of 50,000 housing units in Pyongyang and other regional development projects.\[31\] The 11th Congress also highlighted the deployment of North Korean troops to Russia’s Ukraine war.

North Korea appears to be institutionalizing political oversight of younger generations through the Youth League at a time when the demand for able-bodied, younger personnel is increasing across manufacturing sectors and military operations overseas. Former North Korean leaders Kim Il Sung and Kim Jong Il convened youth league congresses irregularly: the 5th Congress in 1964, the 6th in 1971, the 7th in 1981, and the 8th in 1993.\[32\] Kim Jong Un has established a five-year interval for the congresses since 2016, convening the 9th, 10th, and 11th congresses in 2016, 2021, and 2026, respectively. North Korea’s adoption of a narrative elevating the role of the youth has coincided with stricter information control laws in 2020 and 2023, aimed at denying access to foreign culture and media, however.\[33\] North Korea likely intends to galvanize youth support and participation in state-led projects through tighter political control and ideological training.

## Counter-North Korean Coalition Building Efforts

**The United Nations Command (UNC) implicitly rejected the Lee administration’s offer to alter the current management plan for the Demilitarized Zone (DMZ). The Lee administration’s proposal would allow South Korea to control more territory.** UNC Deputy Commander Lieutenant General Scott Winter stated that any change to the current DMZ management system must be approached with “extreme caution,” following the South Korean Ministry of National Defense’s (MND) proposal for joint DMZ management.\[34\] The Act on the Support for the Peaceful Use of the Demilitarized Zone, also known as the DMZ law, would grant the MOU authority within the South Korean part of DMZ in areas where the UNC currently holds lawful jurisdiction.\[35\] The MOU previously expressed support for the DMZ Law on January 28. The UNC expressed concern over the bill on the same day.\[36\] The MND proposed a joint management plan to the US-South Korea defense consultative body and other related bodies in February, incorporating both the MOU and UNC’s positions.\[37\] The MND’s proposal would allow the UNC to maintain management of territory north of the frontline fences in the DMZ, while South Korea would manage territory south of the fences.\[38\] South Korea’s frontline fences do not perfectly align with the actual DMZ boundaries, a 2km space from the Military Demarcation Line (MDL) on both north and south, due to security and terrain issues.\[39\] South Korea would manage approximately 30 percent of the South Korean DMZ area under the MND’s plan.\[40\] MND Minister Ahn Gyu-baek reportedly met with UNC and US Forces Korea (USFK) Commander Xavier Brunson in February to explain this joint management concept.\[41\]

UNC Deputy Commander Winter’s statement appears to be an indirect rejection of the MND proposal. The UNC’s expression of concern over the DMZ law on January 28 was highly unusual, as the UNC tends to avoid direct comments on South Korean legislation to stay out of political disputes.\[42\] The UNC Military Armistice Commission currently maintains jurisdiction over the DMZ and MDL pursuant to Paragraph 9, Article 1 of the Korean War Armistice Agreement of 1953.\[43\] ISW-CDOT previously assessed that the MOU’s attempt to shift jurisdiction of the DMZ reflects the MOU’s efforts to bolster its control over Seoul’s North Korea policy.\[44\] The MOU has also attempted to open “peace trails” in the DMZ and restore a no-fly zone near the MDL, both of which require consultation with the UNC.\[45\] The MOU may seek to gain greater jurisdiction over the area to use the DMZ as a space for expanding exchanges with North Korea and forming a “peace zone.”

**South Korea’s Army is planning to integrate unmanned systems into its frontline combat units. South Korea is likely learning from battlefield developments in Ukraine and looking to unmanned systems to offset concerns surrounding the future size of South Korea’s military.** South Korean Army Chief of Staff General Kim Gyu-hastated on April 29 that the army sought to deploy suicide and reconnaissance drones to army battalions “in a timely manner.”\[46\] The army also aims to introduce 11,000 drones for training and have 50,000 drones operational with frontline units by 2029, according to South Korean media.\[47\] General Kim stated that drones would become “like personal firearms” for the army under the future operating concept, and suggested that all ground forces personnel should be familiar with drone operation.\[48\] General Kim pointed to the war in Ukraine as the driving force in South Korean military modernization related to unmanned systems, referencing the key role of unmanned platforms in modern conflicts.\[49\]

Introducing suicide and reconnaissance drones to army battalions is an important step in integrating unmanned systems into South Korean army operations. The battalion is the smallest formation capable of independent combined arms operations in the South Korean army, and attaching unmanned systems warfare assets to battalions will allow their flexible application at the tactical level.\[50\] ISW-CDOT has previously reported that South Korea is turning to unmanned systems and remote weapons systems on the frontline with North Korea to address South Korea’s declining birthrate and resulting future manpower shortages.\[51\] Greater integration of unmanned systems in lower-level army units could mitigate future manpower problems by increasing the area of the frontline that each unit can cover, although it is unlikely to have this effect in the near-term.\[52\]

The war in Ukraine has seen the use of millions of drones ranging from small reconnaissance drones to sophisticated strike drones capable of hitting targets hundreds of kilometers from the frontline.\[53\] The proliferation of such drones has rendered the Ukrainian battlefield largely transparent, complicating both sides’ ability to concentrate force at any point on the frontline without incurring significant casualties.\[54\] Both Russia and Ukraine have created separate formations dedicated to unmanned systems warfare and have integrated unmanned systems into frontline units.\[55\] South Korea will likely have to substantially increase the number and type of drones that it fields with frontline units if it is to effectively adapt to changes in modern war and mitigate future manpower shortages through unmanned systems as a result.
