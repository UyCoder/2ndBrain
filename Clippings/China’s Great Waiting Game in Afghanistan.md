Afghanistan is one of China’s thorniest security and foreign policy problems today. Rich in natural resources and strategically situated at the nexus of South, Central, and West Asia, the country is a natural target for Chinese investment and influence. For decades, however, Afghanistan’s domestic instability and proximity to China’s own restive Xinjiang region have inhibited economic cooperation and led Beijing to treat its neighbor primarily as a source of security threats, including terrorism as well as drugs and migration. In the 2010s, China voiced support for stronger economic and security ties with the U.S.-backed government of the Islamic Republic of Afghanistan, but it remained wary of the perceived risks of greater involvement and therefore did not deliver on many of its commitments. The complete withdrawal of U.S. forces and return of Taliban control in August 2021 have exacerbated China’s long-standing challenges in Afghanistan and produced some new ones as well.

As the Islamist government in Kabul contends with crippling sanctions, international isolation, and weak state capacity, Beijing’s priority is to mitigate the threat posed by Uighur militants and other armed groups based in Afghanistan against China and Chinese assets in the region. Its approach to Afghanistan under the Taliban involves three elements. The first is eschewing sticks: China is unwilling to take on the security role previously performed by the United States and indeed juxtaposes its conduct in the international arena against U.S. interventionism. The second element is dangling carrots: China is offering the Taliban valuable economic support but making it contingent on cooperation in neutralizing the threat from Uighur fighters and other China-designated terrorist groups. The third is building fences: China is tightening security along its border with Afghanistan and working with other countries in the region to contain any “spillover.”

China is playing a waiting game with Afghanistan, betting that Kabul will eventually meet its core demands. In the meantime, even if the country’s economic potential goes largely unrealized, its political value to Beijing continues to appreciate. Beijing denounces the United States for the damage done by both the prolonged U.S. presence in and chaotic U.S. withdrawal from Afghanistan. It casts the country’s dire situation as a sign of the United States’s unreliability and irresponsibility, sending a pointed message to U.S. partners in Central, East, and Southeast Asia and attempting to discredit the United States as part of a broader competition with Washington.

## China’s Goals and Interests in Afghanistan

Afghanistan has long been a source of anxiety for Chinese officials and security analysts. Following the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in 1979, the country became yet another example of China’s perceived “encirclement” by hostile forces. Shared concern about the extension of Soviet power pushed Beijing as well as Washington to support the Mujahideen resistance, foreshadowing the uneasy alignment between the two powers that would emerge in later decades. After the collapse of the Soviet-backed government and the onset of civil war in the early 1990s, Beijing’s primary concerns shifted from the nearby presence of a hostile power to the dangers of a power vacuum in which drug and arms trafficking and other nontraditional security threats could flourish. The destabilization of Afghanistan coincided with the intensification of Beijing’s crackdown on unrest in Xinjiang, including the so-called Baren incident in April 1990 and the Ghulja incident in 1997.[^1] As the Chinese state committed to “strike hard” (严打) against so-called separatist forces, Uighur fighters fled west into Central Asia, where the collapse of the Soviet Union had given way to a new tide of Islamist politics and militancy. Loosely organized groups of Uighur fighters established footholds in Afghanistan (as well as Pakistan, Tajikistan, and other countries) and partnered with local insurgent and jihadi forces. This regionalization of Uighur militancy matched and further motivated China’ growing role in the region. Originally established in 1996 to resolve border disputes, the Shanghai Five—the predecessor of the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO) (established in 2001), comprising China, Russia, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, and Tajikistan—expanded and evolved over the following years to target the “three evil forces” (三股势力) of terrorism, extremism, and separatism and coordinate counterterrorism and counternarcotics operations among member states.[^2]

Since the 1990s, Beijing has been keen to manage the risk of the spillover of these forces in Afghanistan as well as the drug trade that sustains them in two dimensions: the direct route via the Wakhan Corridor and the indirect route via surrounding countries. The former is potentially quite dangerous because it opens directly into southern Xinjiang—ground zero for the Uighur separatist threat in Beijing’s eyes—but arguably easier to control. The border has been effectively sealed for decades, with direct overland trade and travel passing through Pakistan or Tajikistan first.[^3] The latter problem is more complicated and spurred Beijing to adopt a multilateral, regional approach. Containing the spread of narcotics, arms, and terrorism was one of the core functions of the Shanghai Five.

From Beijing’s perspective, the September 11 attacks by al Qaeda and the subsequent U.S.-led invasion and occupation of Afghanistan focused global attention on dangerous instability on China’s western border and provided an opportunity to link its security goals to those of the United States and the nascent “global war on terror.” In January 2002, a regional foreign ministers meeting at the China-led SCO issued a joint declaration condemning the September 11 attacks and affirming that “as Afghanistan’s neighbors…we have long been directly threatened by terrorism and drugs from that country.” [^4] Beijing also successfully lobbied Washington to designate the Afghanistan-based East Turkistan Islamic Movement (ETIM) a terrorist organization and freeze its assets.[^5]

Developments within China also informed Beijing’s assessment of the security threat posed from Afghanistan. Building on earlier initiatives to develop the frontier and secure China’s borders, Beijing launched in 2000 the “great western development” campaign aimed at reducing the economic disparity and promoting social, cultural, and political integration between the relatively wealthy and urbanized coastal and central regions and the poorer, rural west. Though it is credited with promoting development in China’s western region, including Xinjiang, the campaign also exacerbated ethnic and political tensions as the gains of development were not evenly distributed. At the same time, intensifying repression aggravated Uighur grievances and motivated anti-China militancy. Even as the U.S.-led NATO coalition brought a degree of political stability to Afghanistan, the problem of Uighur separatism—and thus the latent security threat emanating from Afghanistan—became more acute within China. Sporadic attacks by Uighur militants within Xinjiang and other provinces were in turn invoked by Beijing to legitimize an increasingly harsh state crackdown on Uighur culture and Islamic practice more broadly.

While China continued to regard Afghanistan as a source of security threats, it also became increasingly aware of and interested in the country’s economic potential. This was part of a broader trend in two respects. First, Beijing pushed its state-owned enterprises and private firms to “go out” and extend the country’s economic and geopolitical reach around the world. Second, following several Western and regional countries, Beijing sought to exploit Afghanistan’s much-touted and in some cases newly discovered natural resource deposits. The late 2000s and early 2010s were a period of renewed and intensifying global attention on Afghanistan’s natural wealth. Surveys conducted under the auspices of the U.S.-led occupation elevated already high expectations for the country’s resources, sparking “frenzied speculation” in the words of one scholar.[^6] In 2007 an article in a petrochemicals-focused Chinese magazine ran a piece entitled “Afghanistan: An Oil and Gas Opportunity at China’s Doorstep” that lamented China’s miniscule investment in its western neighbor. Citing a 2006 exploration by the U.S. Geological Survey, the article also noted the vast increase in Afghanistan’s estimated oil reserves compared with the Soviet era.[^7] In 2011 the China National Petroleum Corporation signed a 25-year production-sharing agreement with the government of Afghanistan to develop fields in the Amu Darya Basin, which at the time were estimated to contain as much as 87 million barrels.[^8] A consortium headed by the China Metallurgical Corporation likewise won a 30-year concession to develop the copper industry around Mes-i Aynak in 2007–8. However, these ambitious agreements proved to be mostly aspirational.

Over the 2010s, China gradually increased its diplomatic engagement with Afghanistan in venues such as the Istanbul Process on Afghanistan, which was hosted by China in 2014. However, this engagement did not deliver any major ventures. Several factors explain China’s limited commitment. First, Beijing did not view Afghanistan’s political and security environment as conducive to large-scale investment. The risks remained high and the prospects for stable returns low. Second, China had little incentive to support a political project that—if successful—might be interpreted as a vindication of the U.S.-led intervention. Third, given the history of Uighur militants finding support or sanctuary in Afghanistan, China did not want a strong and successful Afghan government that could not guarantee Beijing’s security concerns, particularly those related to Xinjiang. At the same time, China declined to take action that might have contributed to greater stability, such as by pressuring its partner Pakistan to curb its ties with the Haqqani network.[^9]

## After the Fall

The fall of Kabul to the Taliban and the total withdrawal of U.S. forces in August 2021 transformed the political landscape in which China has continued to pursue its long-standing security interests. Beijing’s challenge is fundamentally the same: pursuing its own military-security goals (neutralizing the threat from Uighur fighters and other designated terrorists) without actually taking military-security action. But the dilemma has become more acute. U.S.-China counterterrorism cooperation ceased by late 2020 with the removal of ETIM from the list of U.S.-designated terrorist groups. The new regime in Kabul is avowedly Islamist and politically as well as ideologically resistant to the denunciation and containment of what Beijing considers “religious extremism.” Moreover, the main rival to the Taliban, Islamic State–Khorasan Province (IS-KP), has aligned itself with Uighur groups. It seeks to further destabilize Afghanistan and see strikes on Chinese targets as an effective means of doing so.[^10]

China’s already limited economic presence in Afghanistan was further thinned as the U.S.-backed government weakened and eventually lost Kabul to the Taliban. Country-specific investment guides put out by the Chinese government to support overseas Chinese entrepreneurs offer snapshots of China’s shrinking economic foothold. The 2020 guide claims there were fourteen “major Chinese capital enterprises” in the country.[^11] No investment guide was published for Afghanistan for 2021–23,[^12] which was likely a result of domestic instability, and the 2024 update listed only six enterprises.” [^13] The main organization for Chinese in Afghanistan, the Association of Chinese Businesspeople in Afghanistan (effectively China’s in-country chamber of commerce), was already a small-scale operation in 2010, when one interviewee lamented it had dwindled to fewer than two hundred people—less than half of its membership size when it was established in the early 2000s.[^14] A video posted on Douyin and YouTube suggests that the organization, recently renamed the Afghanistan Chinese Businesspeople and Overseas Chinese Association, has been reduced to a small, informal group of expatriates.[^15]

The continued presence of even a small number of businesspeople in Afghanistan has had ambiguous consequences. The 2024 investment guide put out by the Ministry of Commerce recommended for the first time that Chinese citizens venturing out to “tribal regions” and other areas far beyond the embassy’s reach hire local security for protection and, when necessary, use an armored vehicle and install a signal jammer—a tacit indication that the government is aware that private citizens are venturing out despite the security situation. On the one hand, informal informational channels through merchant networks are even more useful for the Chinese embassy and state in light of the Taliban government’s contested control over the country. On the other hand, these entrepreneurs are also vulnerable to attacks from insurgent and terrorist groups that have targeted Chinese people in recent years. In January 2025 a Chinese national was killed in Takhar Province in northern Afghanistan, for which IS-KP claimed responsibility.[^16] In July, four Chinese were among eight people killed in a fight at a gold mine in Badakhshan.[^17] In late October, two Chinese were among four killed in a clash at another mine in Badakhshan by the Tajikistan border.[^18] Even more recently, three Chinese citizens were killed in Tajikistan’s Khatlon Province in a cross-border assault involving small-arms fire and a drone equipped with grenades, reflecting the evolving nature of the threat to Chinese targets.[^19]

Facing a persistent and dynamic security challenge in Afghanistan, Beijing remains cautious in its engagement with the Taliban government while highlighting damage done by the United States and the alleged unreliability of Washington as a security partner. Its strategy can be understood as comprising three elements, elaborated below.

*Eschewing sticks*. Beijing remains unwilling to put “boots on the ground” or strike targets within Afghanistan. Indeed, Chinese officials proclaim their opposition to any sort of intervention and juxtapose their conduct against that of the United States and NATO. China criticizes the United States for the presence of its bases in Afghanistan and for its withdrawal from the country. In the weeks following the Taliban takeover of Kabul, Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesperson Wang Wenbin declared that “the United States is an important cause of and the biggest external factor in the Afghanistan problem” and that “what the U.S. did in Afghanistan over the past two decades is a textbook example which shows us the consequences of wanton military intervention and attempts to impose one’s own ideology and values on others.” [^20]

Such criticism of allegedly Western-imposed democracy is of a piece with Beijing’s more general assertions of each country’s right to pursue its own development path in keeping with its particular conditions. Nevertheless, the question of ideology is a particularly sensitive one with respect to Afghanistan. Especially in light of the presence of Uighur fighters and their sympathizers in Afghanistan, Beijing must walk the rather awkward line between, on the one hand, defending its own efforts to achieve the “Sinicization of Islam” (伊斯兰教的中国化) and “de-radicalization” in China through harsh measures, including mass internment, forced labor, and assimilationist ethnic policies, and, on the other hand, building a working relationship with the Islamist regime in Kabul.

Accordingly, China has organized carefully curated visits for Taliban representatives to Xinjiang, showcasing the Grand Bazaar of Urumqi, the Xinjiang Islamic Institute, and the Kashgar Old City—controlled settings designed to project an image of religious harmony and modernity.[^21] Since 2022, China has repeatedly invited Taliban delegations to visit China for learning, training, and exchange purposes. From April 28 to 30, 2025, a delegation of media officials from Afghanistan was hosted in Xinjiang. One member of the delegation, Uzeer Barialay, the director of the Government Dailies Office at Afghanistan’s Ministry of Information and Culture, remarked that Xinjiang is nothing like what some Western media portray and pledged to share what he witnessed to a much wider audience in Afghanistan.[^22] In another case, from July 15 to 18, 2025, a delegation of young Afghan Taliban diplomats, primarily second-generation Taliban, visited Xinjiang, where Chinese officials highlighted counter-extremism policies and the benefits of state-led modernization.[^23]

These Xinjiang-specific narrative measures have been complemented by a broader appeal to civilizational pluralism in keeping with China’s Global Civilization Initiative. A key part of China’s stance on Afghanistan is the “three respects and three nevers” (三个尊重，三个从不): “respecting Afghanistan’s independence, sovereignty, and territorial integrity; respecting the independent choices made by the Afghanistan people; and respecting Afghanistan’s religious beliefs and national customs. China never interferes in Afghanistan’s domestic affairs, never seek selfish gains, and never seek to pursue spheres of influence.” [^24] This is an important lesson that China learned from other great powers in Afghanistan.

On June 17, 2025, Chinese ambassador to Afghanistan Zhao Xing published an article in the English-language *Heart of Asia* newspaper of Afghanistan, which outlined China’s civilizational approach and pledged to “harness the power of civilization” and work with the Afghan people to build “a closer community with a shared future for mankind.” [^25] According to an interviewee speaking under the Chatham House Rule in late October 2025, China reportedly provided Chinese-language scholarships for Taliban members from both the Haqqani and Kandahar factions to visit China, learn the Chinese language, and familiarize themselves with Chinese culture. Such scholarships and cultural exchanges for the Taliban accomplish several things for Beijing. They enhance China’s image by exposing the Taliban to the country’s economic development and technology (and by extension, military) prowess. These trips could be especially effective for Taliban members who have been isolated for two decades or more and are eager to get international experience. Second, they build networks and help train local personnel for future Chinese diplomatic, economic, and security projects in Afghanistan. Third, they give Beijing an opportunity to directly counter Western media narratives on the Uighurs, Xinjiang, and Islam in China more generally.

*Dangling carrots.* Beijing is offering Kabul attractive economic partnerships and assistance on the condition of security cooperation against Uighur and other designated terrorist groups. In March 2022, China convened high-ranking officials from the Taliban and Afghanistan’s neighboring countries as well as Russia and the United States to discuss the Tunxi Initiative (屯溪倡议) for Afghanistan issues. At the conference, China promised to develop and invest in Afghanistan, including the Mes-i Aynak copper mine and Amu Darya oil fields, but with the caveat of “security circumstances permitting” (安全形势允许条件下).[^26] In response, the Taliban government promised that it would not permit any force to use Afghanistan’s territory to conduct activities against China, but it stopped short of mentioning any specific measures against Uighur militants.[^27] China’s official “position on the Afghanistan question,” issued in April 2023, states that “security is the foundation and precondition of development” and expresses the “hope that Afghanistan earnestly fulfill its promise and adopt even more effective measures and strike with even greater resolve all terrorist forces, including the ETIM.” [^28] On a recent trip to Kabul in August 2025, Chinese foreign minister Wang Yi pointed out that “China supports Afghanistan’s pursuit of an ‘economy-centered’ policy and is willing to contribute to Afghanistan’s development and revitalization in order to enhance its capacity for independent growth.” He pledged that China “will continue to uphold justice for Afghanistan within multilateral mechanisms” and stated that it “opposes the unjust suppression of and freezing of assets belonging to Afghanistan,” alluding to threats made by the United States. He added that “China hopes that the Afghan side will take strong measures to combat international terrorist forces such as the ‘East Turkistan Islamic Movement’ so as to provide strong guarantees and the requisite environment for bilateral cooperation.” [^29]

Thus far these incentives have had limited effect. Soon after the U.S. withdrawal and the initial round of Chinese overtures to the new government in Kabul, it was reported that the Taliban had relocated Uighur militants from Badakhshan Province, which borders Xinjiang, to “other parts of the country,” though there was no indication that they were to be repatriated to China.[^30] Moreover, Chinese people and assets in Afghanistan have been targeted by IS-KP and other groups opposed to the Taliban. The resulting security concerns have in turn led to further stalling and cancellation of Chinese projects. In January 2023, Beijing revived the defunct Amu Darya project through a new 25-year deal between the Xinjiang Central Asia Petroleum and Gas Company and the Taliban government. An Afghanistan-focused investment guide published by China’s Ministry of Commerce in 2024 reported “steady progress” on the project, but Afghan authorities canceled the deal in June 2025, claiming that the Chinese side had breached the contract.[^31] The Mes-i Aynak copper mine project likewise remains stalled. In August 2023 the Taliban government announced it had reached a deal with a Chinese company to extract gold in Takhar.[^32] But the prospects for the deal appear uncertain given the deteriorating security situation in the province, which has witnessed multiple attacks on Chinese nationals (including the aforementioned January 2025 fatality). In October 2025 the Chinese embassy in Afghanistan issued an alert specifically warning Chinese nationals and companies involved in goldmining there and in neighboring Badakhshan to “avoid operating in the affected regions,” raising further doubts about the viability of the project.[^33]

*Building fences*. Beijing continues to invest in preventing any “spillover” of terrorist activity, extremist influence, and drug trafficking out of Afghanistan. The primary concern in this respect remains the security of the Wakhan Corridor and the Afghanistan-China border. Beijing has withheld support for a Taliban proposal to build a road through the corridor to facilitate cross-border transit.[^34] Meanwhile, Chinese state media continue to emphasize the importance of the Wakhjir Pass for border security and national defense.[^35] At an August 2025 meeting with Sirajuddin Haqqani in Kabul, Wang Yi stated that “China supports the early resumption of bilateral patrols along the Wakhan Corridor to maintain peace and tranquility in the border area.” [^36] Beijing has also increased its security presence near the corridor in neighboring Tajikistan, reportedly constructing a “secret” military facility (which Chinese and Tajikistani authorities both deny) and strengthening an already close security partnership with Dushanbe.[^37] The People’s Liberation Army takes responsibility for military bases,[^38] and the People’s Armed Police patrols the Wakhan Corridor.[^39]

Beyond the Wakhan Corridor, Beijing is also working to prevent “spillover” from Afghanistan into other countries. It has leveraged the SCO as well as dialogue mechanisms with the Central Asian countries and with Afghanistan, Pakistan, and Tajikistan to institutionalize its security agenda. Another tool for cooperation is the “Action Plan between the Member States of the Shanghai Cooperation Organization and the Islamic Republic of Afghanistan on Combating Terrorism, Drug Trafficking, and Organized Crime.” [^40] The China-Afghanistan-Pakistan trilateral dialogue, which was created in 2017, is another platform from which China leverages its diplomatic influence to address its own security concerns. Following the fall of the Afghan Republic, the focus of this dialogue shifted from viewing the Afghan Taliban as a destabilizing actor to concentrating on the Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan (TTP). This shift, however, has little to do with Afghanistan’s national interests under the Taliban. Instead, the dialogue has increasingly become a mechanism through which China and Pakistan exert pressure on Afghanistan to respond to their security priorities.

## The Waiting Game

Underlying China’s approach to Afghanistan is a basic tension between goals and means: Beijing’s priority is to neutralize the threat of Uighur militants and other designated terrorists, but it is unwilling to use direct military strikes to do so and prefers instead to leverage economic and political influence to incentivize Kabul to cooperate. This dynamic was already present before 2021 but has become even more acute since the Taliban took control—both because of the ideological particularities and international isolation of that regime and because insurgents such as IS-KP seek to use terrorist attacks and the Uighur issue to drive a wedge between Kabul and Beijing and weaken the Taliban’s grip on power.

China is therefore playing a waiting game: as the most powerful player in the region with a record of delivering other countries economic development in exchange for political and security cooperation, Beijing believes it can afford to wait for Kabul to meet its demands before following through on the investment and resource exploitation agreements that would bring the government much-needed revenue and capacity. Yet Kabul can play the waiting game, too. The Taliban government has courted several other countries for investment, most notably India, China’s top regional rival. India recently upgraded its diplomatic representation in Kabul and hosted Taliban representatives for high-level talks in New Delhi. Kabul has likewise invited Indian firms to invest in Afghanistan’s mining sector.[^41] This move is primarily meant to counterbalance Pakistan but nevertheless sends a signal to Beijing that it is not Kabul’s only potential partner. Moreover, in the context of China’s intensifying rivalry with the United States over critical minerals, Afghanistan’s deposits of an estimated $1 trillion worth of lithium may have lent the Taliban government even more confidence in its ability to extract concessions without providing additional security cooperation. In April 2025, it declined an offer from China to invest $10 billion in the lithium sector.[^42]

Afghanistan’s vast and valuable resource reserves thus remain largely untapped. For now, China is not getting much from its western neighbor, but neither is anyone else. Moreover, terrorism and instability are problems for other countries too and offer a common enemy against which Beijing can rally the region. Indeed, much of the strategic value China derives from Afghanistan comes from its problems. By invoking the “Afghanistan question,” Beijing can coordinate regional security and diplomacy in a way that serves its interests in strengthening partnerships with other Central Asian countries, broadcasting its commitment to nonintervention, and discrediting the United States as a destructive, irresponsible, and unreliable power.
