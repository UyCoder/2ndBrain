Diplomatic relations between the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") and the [Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines "Philippines") were established in June 1975. Relations peaked during the Philippine presidencies of [Gloria Macapagal Arroyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidency_of_Gloria_Macapagal_Arroyo "Presidency of Gloria Macapagal Arroyo") and [Rodrigo Duterte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidency_of_Rodrigo_Duterte "Presidency of Rodrigo Duterte"). However, they have deteriorated due to [territorial disputes in the South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_disputes_in_the_South_China_Sea "Territorial disputes in the South China Sea"), particularly since the 2012 [Scarborough Shoal standoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scarborough_Shoal_standoff "Scarborough Shoal standoff"); in 2013, the Philippine government under President [Benigno Aquino III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benigno_Aquino_III "Benigno Aquino III") in 2013 [filed an arbitration case](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Sea_Arbitration "South China Sea Arbitration") against China over China's maritime claims. The policy of current Philippine president [Bongbong Marcos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bongbong_Marcos "Bongbong Marcos") aims for distancing relations between the Philippines and China in favor of the country's [relationship with the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines%E2%80%93United_States_relations "Philippines–United States relations"). The current [policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy_of_Xi_Jinping "Foreign policy of Xi Jinping") of the [general secretary of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") aims for greater influence over the Philippines, and the region in general, while combating American influence.

During [Rodrigo Duterte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodrigo_Duterte "Rodrigo Duterte") 's presidency, the Philippines improved its relations and cooperation with China on various issues, developing a stronger and stable ties with the country, as well as a successful [Code of conduct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_of_conduct "Code of conduct") with China and the rest of [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN").[^1] [^2] China is the Philippines' top trading partner.[^3] However, average trust view of [Filipinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filipinos "Filipinos") towards China is negative.[^4] Relations deteriorated during the presidency of Bongbong Marcos due to increasing tensions over the South China sea dispute,[^5] resulting in the Philippines' withdrawal from the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative") in 2023 and allegations of election interference in 2025.[^6] [^7]

### Imperial China and Precolonial Philippine States

Before [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Empire "Spanish Empire") colonized the [Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines "Philippines"), [Imperial China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_China "Imperial China") acknowledged the existence of several Precolonial Philippine kingdoms and the Chinese Emperor received embassies from [Filipino Datus, Rajahs, and Sultans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_\(900%E2%80%931565\)#Emergence_of_Independent_polities "History of the Philippines (900–1565)").[^8]

### Spanish colonial period

During the [Spanish colonial period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_\(1565%E2%80%931898\) "History of the Philippines (1565–1898)"), several notable armed confrontations occurred between the authorities and overseas Chinese in the Philippines such as the [1603 Sangley Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sangley_Rebellion_\(1603\) "Sangley Rebellion (1603)") and the [1639 Sangley Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sangley_Rebellion_\(1639\) "Sangley Rebellion (1639)").[^9]

### American occupation (1899 – 1946)

During the [Philippine-American War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine-American_War "Philippine-American War"), Chinese nationalist figures such as [Sun Yat-sen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen "Sun Yat-sen") expressed support for the Philippine revolutionaries under [Emilio Aguinaldo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emilio_Aguinaldo "Emilio Aguinaldo"). In 1899, Sun arranged a stealthy shipment of Japanese arms to the Philippines.[^10]

Between 1921 and 1934, relations between China and the Philippines were marked by cooperation.[^11] In 1927, Philippine Senate President [Manuel L. Quezon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manuel_L._Quezon "Manuel L. Quezon") urged the Filipinos to distance themselves from Chinese nationalists due to fears of offending the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"). He believed that having links with China would hinder the Philippines' independence campaign.[^12] By the 1930s, there were debates in the US government on granting the Philippines its independence. [Newton W. Gilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_W._Gilbert "Newton W. Gilbert"), who formerly served as the Philippine Vice [Governor-General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governor-General_of_the_Philippines "Governor-General of the Philippines"), sent a letter to the [US Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Senate "United States Senate") stating the importance of keeping the Philippines while also noting the consequences of a possible Chinese invasion.[^13]

### Cold War

After the Philippines became independent in 1946, it established diplomatic relations with the [Nationalist government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalist_government "Nationalist government") of China and continued on after it lost the mainland to the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) in the [Chinese Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War "Chinese Civil War") that forced the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China") to [retreat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retreat_of_the_government_of_the_Republic_of_China_to_Taiwan "Retreat of the government of the Republic of China to Taiwan") to the [island of Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Taiwan "Geography of Taiwan") in 1949, formerly a [Japanese colony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_under_Japanese_rule "Taiwan under Japanese rule") that the ROC received in 1945. During the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), the two countries were part of the [anti-communist camp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Bloc "Western Bloc") that view the Chinese Communists as a security threat.[^14] During the [Korean War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_War "Korean War"), the [Philippine Expeditionary Forces to Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_Expeditionary_Forces_to_Korea "Philippine Expeditionary Forces to Korea") under the [United Nations Command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Command "United Nations Command") fought the PRC's [People's Volunteer Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Volunteer_Army "People's Volunteer Army") in multiple battles.[^15]

### Establishment of official diplomatic relations

It began considering normalizing relations with the People's Republic at the start of the 1970s and the Philippines recognized the PRC on 9 June 1975, with the signing of the Joint Communiqué by leaders of the two countries.[^16]

### Major agreements

In 2009, the Philippines and China signed the *Joint Action Plan for Strategic Cooperation*, a five-year [agreement to increase cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_partnerships_of_China "Diplomatic partnerships of China") in all areas.[^17]<sup><span title="Page: 158">: 158</span></sup>

In 2016, Philippine president [Rodrigo Duterte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodrigo_Duterte "Rodrigo Duterte") and Chinese leader [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") created the biannual Bilateral Consultation Mechanism on the South China Sea, a process allowing the two nations to peacefully manage disputes and strengthen their relations.[^18] [^19]

On 16 November 2017, the Philippines and China signed fourteen deals on a variety of issues, including transportation and military aid, worth approximately US$21.6 million.[^17]<sup><span title="Page: 169">: 169</span></sup>

In July 2019, UN ambassadors of 37 countries, including Philippines, signed a joint letter to the [United Nations Human Rights Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council "United Nations Human Rights Council") defending China's policies of [persecution of Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution_of_Uyghurs_in_China "Persecution of Uyghurs in China") in [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang").[^20] The Philippines was one of 16 countries that defended China's policies in Xinjiang in 2019 but did not do so in 2020.[^21]

### Belt and Road Initiative

During the respective visits of Philippine President Rodrigo Duterte to the People's Republic of China and the Chinese Communist Party general secretary Xi Jinping's visit to the Philippines, the two agreed to a significant increase of Official Development Assistance (ODA) from the People's Republic of China as part of Xi's [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative").[^22] However, concerns were soon raised over the terms and conditions of the ODA funding and the lack of transparency into the details.[^23] As of 2018, the delivery of that aid had also stalled, with few firm commitments put in place by the Xi administration.[^22] [^23] Despite this, the Duterte administration continued to make the relationship a major part of its economic agenda.[^23] During the [Marcos Jr. administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidency_of_Bongbong_Marcos "Presidency of Bongbong Marcos") in 2023, the Philippines withdrew from the [Belt and Road initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_initiative "Belt and Road initiative").[^6]

## Trade and commerce

Since [Song dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty "Song dynasty") times in China and [precolonial times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_\(900%E2%80%931565\) "History of the Philippines (900–1565)") in the Philippines, evidence of trade contact can already be observed in the [Chinese ceramics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_ceramics "Chinese ceramics") found in archaeological sites, like in [Santa Ana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Ana,_Manila "Santa Ana, Manila"), [Manila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manila "Manila").[^24] During [Ming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") and [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") times in China and [Spanish colonial era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_\(1565%E2%80%931898\) "History of the Philippines (1565–1898)") in the Philippines, the Philippines through Manila has had centuries-long trade contacts with cities such as [Quanzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quanzhou "Quanzhou"), [Zhangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhangzhou "Zhangzhou"), [Xiamen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiamen "Xiamen") in [Fujian province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fujian "Fujian") and [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou "Guangzhou") and [Macau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macau "Macau") in [Guangdong province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangdong "Guangdong"), especially as part of the [Maritime Silk Road trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maritime_Silk_Road "Maritime Silk Road"), then connected with the [Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manila_galleon "Manila galleon") that ensured the export of Chinese trade goods, such as chinaware, across [Spanish America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hispanic_America "Hispanic America") and [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe") in the Spanish colonial empire and the constant supply of Spanish silver into the [economy of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_China "Economy of China") as observed in the later dominance and widespread use of the [Spanish silver dollar coins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_dollar#Asia "Spanish dollar") in the [Ming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty_coinage "Ming dynasty coinage") and [Qing dynasty coinage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty_coinage "Qing dynasty coinage") and its general acceptance as a de facto standard of trade across the [Far East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far_East "Far East") around the 16th to 19th century. In 1567, the Spanish trade port in the city of Manila in the [Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captaincy_General_of_the_Philippines "Captaincy General of the Philippines") as part of the Spanish colonial empire was opened which until the fall of the Ming dynasty brought over forty million Kuping Taels of silver to China with the annual Chinese imports numbering at 53,000,000 pesos (each peso being 8 [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_real "Spanish real")) or 300,000 Kuping Taels. During the Ming dynasty the average [Chinese junk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Junk_\(ship\) "Junk (ship)") which took the voyage from the [Spanish East Indies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_East_Indies "Spanish East Indies") to the city of Guangzhou took with it eighty thousand pesos, a number which increased under the Qing dynasty as until the mid-18th century the volume of imported Spanish pesos had increased to 235,370,000 (or 169 460,000 Kuping Tael). The Spanish mention that around 12,000,000 pesos were shipped from [Acapulco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acapulco_de_Ju%C3%A1rez "Acapulco de Juárez") to Manila in the year 1597 as part of the Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade while in other years this usually numbered between one and four million pesos.[^25]

[Bilateral trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilateral_trade "Bilateral trade") volume in 2007 was US$30.62 billion. From January to October 2008, bilateral trade volume reached US$25.3 billion, an increase of 1.4% as compared with the same period last year. By the end of September 2008, the actually utilized value of accumulative investment from the Philippines to China reached US$2.5 billion. China's transformation into a major economic power in the 21st century has led to an increase of foreign investments in the [bamboo network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamboo_network "Bamboo network"), a network of [overseas Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_Chinese "Overseas Chinese") businesses operating in the markets of Southeast Asia that share common family and cultural ties.[^26] [^27]

The Philippines and China signed an air rights agreement in 2010, significantly increasing flights between the two countries.[^17]<sup><span title="Page: 159">: 159</span></sup>

In August 2011, the Philippines and China signed a five-year trade development program worth US$6 billion.[^17]<sup><span title="Page: 160">: 160</span></sup>

On 17 September 2014, the Philippines and China signed a [memorandum of understanding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memorandum_of_understanding "Memorandum of understanding") for the promotion of investment cooperation.[^17]<sup><span title="Page: 164">: 164</span></sup>

In 2022 the Philippines became able to export fresh [durian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durian "Durian") to China. Market access is sometimes used as a tool of Chinese diplomacy; [banana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banana "Banana") exports from the Philippines have previously been restricted during times of geopolitical tension.[^28]

A 2024 study by [AidData](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AidData "AidData") found that from 2000 to 2022, China's investments in the Philippines exceeded US$9 billion.[^29] [^30] Between 2010 and 2023, Chinese [foreign direct investment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_direct_investment "Foreign direct investment") (FDI) in the Philippines reached US$21.9 billion.[^30] By 2023, China became the second largest market for Philippine exports,[^29] which at some point became the largest during the first semester amounting to US$5.58 billion or equivalent to a share of 16 percent followed by the US and Japan.[^31] However, since 2024, the Philippines has been urging the US and its allies to increase their inward investments to the country in an attempt to counter China.[^32]

## Chinese Filipinos

Chinese Filipinos constitute one group of overseas Chinese. Chinese Filipinos are present within several commerce and business sectors in the Philippines and a few sources estimate companies which comprise a majority of the Philippine economy are owned by Chinese Filipinos, if one includes Chinese mestizos.[^33] [^34] [^35] [^36]

In view of the ongoing territorial dispute of China and the Philippines (such as Scarborough Shoal), Chinese-Filipinos prefer a peaceful solution through diplomatic talks while some view that China should not extend its claims to other parts of South China Sea.[^37]

According to a 2023 report by the [Philippine Center for Investigative Journalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_Center_for_Investigative_Journalism "Philippine Center for Investigative Journalism"), Chinese Filipino tycoon George Siy's think tank, the Integrated Development Studies Institute, has been a major promoter of pro- [Chinese government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_China "Government of China") narratives in the Philippines.[^38]

Sealight Foundation global security analyst Ray Powell took note of the recent attempts by the Chinese government to exploit the Chinese Filipino community. In a March 21, 2026 forum, he particularly mentioned the so-called " [blood transfusion plan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Filipinos#Influence_operations_from_China "Chinese Filipinos") " for Chinese Filipino schools in the country. He clarified that their organization's research did not target the entire community as a whole. Instead, it sought to prevent the "hostile foreign state" from taking over these Chinese Filipino institutions.[^39]

## Territorial disputes

### Spratly Islands and the South China Sea

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/South_China_Sea_claims_map.svg/330px-South_China_Sea_claims_map.svg.png)

Territorial claims in the South China Sea

The two countries have disputes over the sovereignty of some [islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island "Island") and [shoals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoal "Shoal") in the [Spratly Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spratly_Islands "Spratly Islands").[^55] These disputes are linked to other disputes in the South China Sea. China conducts [grey-zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grey-zone_\(international_relations\) "Grey-zone (international relations)") operations in these waters. Rising tensions has led the Philippines to invest further in its military forces, and to deepen cooperation with the United States and Japan.[^56]

In 1734, the Spanish colonial government in the Philippines published the first edition of the [Velarde map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velarde_map "Velarde map"), detailing the territories under full sovereign control of Spanish Philippines, which included [Scarborough Shoal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scarborough_Shoal "Scarborough Shoal") (called *Panacot* in the indigenous language in the map) and the Spratly Islands (referred in the map as *Los Bajos de Paragua*). The 1734 Velarde map is the earliest map to showcase the sovereignty of a nation over Scarborough Shoal and the Spratly Islands.[^57] [^58] In 1792, the Spanish colonial government of the territory of the Philippines launched the first ever survey of Scarborough Shoal on 4 May 1792. The survey, *Plano de la Navigacion*, was taken by Alessandro Malaspina aboard the Sta. Lucia, with Filipino comrades.[^59] The official territories of the Philippines was again published in the 1808 *Carita General del Archipelago Filipino* and again in the 1875 *Carita General del Archipelago Filipino*.[^57] [^58] [^60]

After the [Spanish-American War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish-American_War "Spanish-American War"), Spain lost and ceded the territory of the Philippines to the United States through the [1898 Treaty of Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_\(1898\) "Treaty of Paris (1898)"). The 1898 Treaty of Paris created a treaty line, where Scarborough Shoal, the Spratly Islands, and parts of Tawi-tawi continued to be under Spanish sovereignty. This led to talks between Spain and the United States, which ended upon the signing of the [1900 Treaty of Washington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Washington_\(1900\) "Treaty of Washington (1900)"), which rectified retroactively the 1898 Treaty of Paris. Under the 1900 Treaty of Washington, "all islands belonging to the Philippine Archipelago, lying outside the lines described in Article III" were also ceded to the United States as part of the territory of the Philippines, where Scarborough Shoal, the Spratly Islands, and the rest of Tawi-tawi was included.[^57] [^61] [^62] From 1899 to 1902, the United States war department in the territory of the Philippines republished and reissued four times the 1875 Carita General del Archipelago Filipino with the addition of military telegraph lines, military cable lines, eastern cable company lines, and military department boundaries. The official map of the entire Philippine territory under Spanish rule was effectively adopted as the entire Philippine territory under American rule.[^63] During the 1928 Islas Palmas international case, the United States, as representative of the territory of the Philippines, reiterated in a court memorandum that the 1875 Carta General del Archipielago Filipino "is both an American official and a Spanish official map" of Philippine territory, bounding the United States on its recognition of the Scarborough Shoal and the Spratly Islands as Philippine territory.[^64] [^65] In 1930, the United States and the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") signed a treaty, where the United Kingdom recognized the territory of Philippines which included Scarborough Shoal and the Spratlys, effectively bounding the United Kingdom's successor countries as well, such as [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia") and [Brunei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brunei "Brunei") but there has been disagreement with a Philippines view that the treaty covers the waters within the Treaty Limits by at least the United States and Britain.[^66] In 1932, China sent a [Note Verbale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_correspondence "Diplomatic correspondence") to France, declaring that China's southernmost territory was the Paracels.[^67] [^68] [^69]

After rounds of consultations to address territory disputes, both sides agreed to strive for a solution through bilateral friendly consultation. In October 2004, [Chinese Maritime Safety Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Maritime_Safety_Administration "China Maritime Safety Administration") and [Philippine Coast Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_Coast_Guard "Philippine Coast Guard") conducted a joint sand table rescue exercise for the first time. [China National Offshore Oil Corp.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_National_Offshore_Oil_Corporation "China National Offshore Oil Corporation") and [Philippine National Oil Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_National_Oil_Company "Philippine National Oil Company") signed the "Agreement for Joint Marine Seismic Undertaking on Certain Areas in the South China Sea" on 1 September 2004. In May 2005, [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam") agreed to join the Sino-Philippine cooperation. Oil companies from three countries signed the "Agreement for Joint Marine Seismic Undertaking on Certain Areas in the South China Sea" in March 2005.

Due to the [2012 Scarborough Shoal standoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Scarborough_Shoal_standoff "2012 Scarborough Shoal standoff"), relations between the two countries significantly worsened.[^70] On 8 April 2012, a Philippine navy vessel cornered several Chinese fishing vessels in the Scarborough Shoal lagoon, suspecting the fishing vessels of [illegal fishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illegal,_unreported_and_unregulated_fishing "Illegal, unreported and unregulated fishing").[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 267">: 267</span> </sup> Two China Maritime Surveillance vessels arrived shortly thereafter, placing themselves between the Chinese fishing vessels and the [Philippine navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_Navy "Philippine Navy") vessel to prevent the Philippine navy from making an arrest.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 267">: 267</span> </sup> A two-month standoff followed.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 267">: 267</span> </sup> During the initial phase of the standoff, the Philippines withdrew the navy vessel and replaced it with a coast guard vessel and a [Bureau of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureau_of_Fisheries_and_Aquatic_Resources "Bureau of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources") Vessel.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 268">: 268</span> </sup> China responded by reinforcing its presence, first with the advanced fisheries patrol ship *Yuzheng 310* and later other ships to reach a total of 14 vessels in the area by 3 May.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 268">: 268</span> </sup> An approaching typhoon caused all participants in the standoff to disperse.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 268">: 268</span> </sup> Chinese ships returned to the area shortly thereafter and maintained a regular presence there until October 2016.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 268">: 268</span></sup>

In October 2012, Chinese Vice Foreign Minister [Fu Ying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fu_Ying "Fu Ying") visited Manila in an effort to persuade the Philippines to continue bilateral discussions to address the South China Sea territorial disputes and to avoid involving the United States or taking the dispute to an international forum.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 122">: 122</span> </sup> The Philippines responded by initiating an arbitration against China.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 122">: 122</span></sup>

On April 24, 2026, China sent navy ships and aircraft to patrol near [Scarborough Shoal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scarborough_Shoal "Scarborough Shoal") in the South China Sea, claiming it was protecting its territorial claims. The patrols came after the joint [Balikatan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balikatan "Balikatan") military exercises by the Philippines, the United States, Australia, and New Zealand.[^72] [^73]

On May 6–7, 2026, the [Philippine Coast Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_Coast_Guard "Philippine Coast Guard") reported that a Chinese research vessel, Xiang Yang Hong 33, was conducting unauthorized operations near [Reed Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reed_Bank "Reed Bank") in the South China Sea, accompanied by Chinese Coast Guard and militia ships. The Philippines said this violated its sovereignty and international law, while China claimed it was a legitimate scientific mission.[^74]

On 31 May 2026, China deployed military and coast guard patrols around the disputed [Scarborough Shoal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scarborough_Shoal "Scarborough Shoal") in the [South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Sea "South China Sea"), a day after the Philippines reaffirmed concerns over Chinese actions in the region. The move followed joint Philippine-US maritime exercises near the shoal and was part of Beijing's ongoing efforts to enforce its territorial claims.[^75] [^76]

#### 2013–2016 South China Sea arbitration

In January 2013, the Philippines formally initiated arbitration proceedings against China's claim on the territories within the [nine-dash line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nine-dash_line "Nine-dash line") that includes Spratly Islands, which it contended was unlawful under the [United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea "United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea") (UNCLOS).[^77] [^78] An arbitration tribunal was constituted under Annex VII of [UNCLOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNCLOS "UNCLOS") and it was decided in July 2013 that the [Permanent Court of Arbitration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permanent_Court_of_Arbitration "Permanent Court of Arbitration") (PCA) would function as registry and provide administrative duties in the proceedings.[^79]

On 12 July 2016, the arbitrators agreed with the Philippines on most of its contentions.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 123">: 123</span> </sup> They concluded in the award that there was no evidence that China had historically exercised exclusive control over the waters or resources, hence there was "no legal basis for China to claim historic rights" over the nine-dash line.[^80] Accordingly, the PCA tribunal decision is ruled as final and non-appealable by either countries.[^81] [^82] The tribunal also criticized China's land reclamation projects and its construction of artificial islands in the Spratly Islands, saying that it had caused "severe harm to the coral reef environment".[^83] It also characterized [Taiping Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiping_Island "Taiping Island") and other features of the Spratly Islands as "rocks" under UNCLOS, and therefore are not entitled to a 200 nautical mile [exclusive economic zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone "Exclusive economic zone").[^84] China however rejected the ruling, calling it "ill-founded".[^85] Taiwan, which currently administers Taiping Island, the largest of the Spratly Islands, also rejected the ruling.[^86]

China's response was to ignore the arbitration result and to continue pursuing bilateral discussions with the Philippines.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 128">: 128</span> </sup> The day after the ruling, China's Vice Foreign Minister [Liu Zhenmin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liu_Zhenmin "Liu Zhenmin") stated at a press conference that China had "taken note of the positive attitude of the new Philippine government under President Duterte toward resuming dialogue with China and progressing bilateral relationships from various aspects. We welcome this initiative with open arms." [^71]<sup><span title="Page: 128">: 128</span></sup>

On 8 August 2016, the Philippines dispatched former president [Fidel V. Ramos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fidel_V._Ramos "Fidel V. Ramos") to Hong Kong to mitigate tensions following the arbitration result.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span> </sup> Ramos met with Fu Ying (chair of China's [Foreign Affairs Committee for the National People's Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_Affairs_Committee_of_the_National_People%27s_Congress "Foreign Affairs Committee of the National People's Congress")) and Wu Shicun (president of the National Institute for South China Sea Studies).[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span> </sup> Ramos conveyed the Philippines' willingness to engage in formal discussions with China.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span> </sup> The three issued a statement in their personal capacities emphasizing cooperation and dialogue between the two countries.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span></sup>

On 20 October 2016, Duterte visited China where he and Xi Jinping resumed bilateral discussions on the South China Sea dispute.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span> </sup> In an effort to demonstrate its cooperative approach, following the visit China allowed Filipino fisherman to return to the area of Scarborough Shoal.[^71]<sup><span title="Page: 129">: 129</span></sup>

#### Other incidents

In April 2019, international satellites and local reports revealed that Chinese ships have swarmed Philippine-controlled areas in the South China Sea through a [cabbage strategy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabbage_tactics "Cabbage tactics").[^87] [^88] [^89] Later reports showed that endangered [giant clams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_clam "Giant clam") under Philippine law protection were illegally being harvested by Chinese ships.[^90] [^91] The swarming continued for the entirety of April, with the Philippine foreign affairs secretary, Teddy Locsin Jr., expressing dismay over the incident and calling it an intentional "embarrassment" aimed against the Philippines.[^92] A few days before the 2019 Philippine independence day, President Duterte stated that the country may go to war with China if China claims disputed resources.[^93]

On 9 June 2019, a Chinese ship, *Yuemaobinyu 42212*, [rammed and sank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_Reed_Bank_incident "2019 Reed Bank incident") a Philippine fishing vessel, *F/B Gem-Ver*, near Reed Bank, west of [Palawan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palawan "Palawan"). The fishermen were caught by surprise as they were asleep during the said event. The Chinese ship afterwards left the sank Philippine vessel, while the Filipino fishermen were adrift in the middle of sea and left to the elements, in violation of a rule under UNCLOS.[^93] [^94] The 22 Filipino fishermen were later rescued by a ship from Vietnam.[^95] [^96]

The government responded a day later, stating that they may cut ties with China if the culprits are not punished by the Chinese.[^97] [^98] China has stated that the event was an ordinary maritime accident,[^99] which was later backed up by investigations from the [Armed Forces of the Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_Philippines "Armed Forces of the Philippines").[^100]

The Chinese crew was later criticized for failing to undertake measures to avoid colliding with the *F/B Gem-Ver* and abandoning the stricken boat's crew, in violation of maritime laws.[^101] [^102]

In 2020, [Facebook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facebook "Facebook") took down a Chinese network which was part of a disinformation campaign against the Philippines. The campaign used fake profiles in an attempt to influence public opinion, particularly related to politics and the South China Sea.[^103] [^104]

#### Increasing escalations during the Marcos presidency

The election of Philippine president [Bongbong Marcos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bongbong_Marcos "Bongbong Marcos") in 2022 saw the onset of worsening Philippine-China relations and frequent skirmishes in the South China Sea.[^105] [^106] Marcos' decision to increase the number of Philippine bases the United States military troops can use under the [Enhanced Defense Cooperation Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enhanced_Defense_Cooperation_Agreement "Enhanced Defense Cooperation Agreement") from five to nine met vehement opposition from the Chinese government.[^107] Marcos made overt signals that the Philippines would inevitably get involved in the event of a Taiwan-China conflict,[^108] prompting China to warn the Philippines against "playing with fire".[^109] Under Marcos, the Philippine Coast Guard began a strategy of publicizing China's aggressive actions in the South China Sea by inviting journalists during territorial encounters and releasing the photographs and videos thereafter in an attempt to gain international support.[^105] [^106] Among the escalations since 2023 include [China Coast Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Coast_Guard "China Coast Guard") 's firing on Philippine military ships in the Spratly Islands waters;[^110] the Philippines accusing China of parking its navy and coast guard vessels near Scarborough Shoal in an apparent attempt to block Philippine vessels from passing through the area;[^111] the Philippine Coast Guard accusing China Coast Guard ships of jamming their [automatic identification system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automatic_identification_system "Automatic identification system") (AIS);[^112] and, amidst Chinese and Philippine military operations around the disputed Scarborough Shoal in August 2024, the Armed Forces of the Philippines accusing [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") of "dangerous and provocative actions" after two Chinese Air Force aircraft dropped flares in the path of a Philippines' plane that China claimed was "illegally intruding" into its airspace.[^113] Additionally, in May 2024, the Philippines' [Department of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Department_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(Philippines\) "Department of Foreign Affairs (Philippines)") said it would probe Chinese diplomats' activities around wiretapping after reports surfaced of a recording of an agreement between a Philippine military official and Chinese officials over the South China Sea.[^114]

In April 2024, the Philippine Coast Guard deployed one of its key patrol ships, the [BRP *Teresa Magbanua*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BRP_Teresa_Magbanua "BRP Teresa Magbanua"), to the [Sabina Shoal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabina_Shoal "Sabina Shoal") area after Filipino scientists discovered submerged piles of crushed corals in its shallows, raising suspicions that the Chinese may be preparing to build a structure on the site. In August 2024, two separate collisions involving ships from the [Chinese Coast Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Coast_Guard "Chinese Coast Guard") and the Philippine Coast Guard occurred near the Sabina Shoal, resulting in significant damage to Philippine vessels, including the BRP *Teresa Magbanua*. In response, Commodore [Jay Tarriela](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jay_Tarriela "Jay Tarriela") of the Philippine Coast Guard announced that the Magbanua would not withdraw from the Sabina Shoal area "despite the harassment, bullying activities, and escalatory action of the Chinese coast guard".[^115] Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesperson [Mao Ning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Ning_\(diplomat\) "Mao Ning (diplomat)") described the Philippines' anchoring of the vessel in the shoal as a strategy to "permanently occupy the area".[^116]

The United States' deployment of the [Typhon missile system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typhon_missile_launcher "Typhon missile launcher") in northern Philippines in April 2024 prompted condemnation from China and Russia, which respectively accused the US of threatening regional peace and stoking an arms race. Initially, the Philippine government reassured the public that the missile system shall remain only in the Philippines until September 2024; nearing the specified deadline, however, plans on extending its stay in the country were announced, with a senior Philippine government official remarking it would help deter China and "give them sleepless nights".[^117] [^118]

On 18 February 2025, a Chinese military helicopter flew within 3 meters of a Philippine patrol plane over Scarborough Shoal, leading to a 30-minute standoff. The Philippines warned the helicopter pilot of the danger posed to the crew.[^119] On 21 February 2025, China accused the Philippines of illegally flying three aircraft over the disputed Spratly Islands. The Chinese military said it drove away two [Cessna 208 planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cessna_208_Caravan "Cessna 208 Caravan") and an N-22 aircraft.[^119]

On June 7, 2025, a [Chinese maritime militia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maritime_Militia "Maritime Militia") vessel with hull number 16838 deployed a nylon [parachute anchor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_anchor "Sea anchor") during a period of 3 hours when apparently stuck over Pag-asa Reef 1, about 2.6 km (1.6 mi) from Pag-asa Island within Philippine territorial waters. The vessel was escorted away by China Coast Guard ships. About 30% of the area of the reef, being over 300 m <sup>2</sup> (3,200 sq ft) of coral was damaged, with broken hard and soft coral fragments, that was attributed to the anchor's drag rather than a hull impact upon a later joint assessment by the Palawan Council for Sustainable Development, [Western Philippines University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Philippines_University "Western Philippines University"), and the Philippine Coast Guard. The anchor was still present at 9 m (30 ft) underwater, blocking sunlight to the reef so threatening further damage. The environmental loss was estimated to be ₱11.1 million, within a maximum protection zone under Republic Act No. 7611, where all human activity is banned. These findings may result in legal and diplomatic action under the Philippine Fisheries Code. This is the first well documented case of such damage by a parachute anchor from a Chinese militia vessel.[^120] [^121]

On 12 July 2025, the Philippine Coast Guard cutter [BRP Teresa Magbanua (MRRV-9701)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BRP_Teresa_Magbanua_\(MRRV-9701\) "BRP Teresa Magbanua (MRRV-9701)") intercepted the Chinese Type 815G-class electronic surveillance ship Tianwangxing (793), accompanied by the China Coast Guard Zhaojun-class cutter 4203, approximately 70 nautical miles west of the Philippine archipelago within the country's exclusive economic zone. The Philippine vessel issued radio challenges, which were ignored by the Chinese Navy ship, while the escorting cutter responded with a counter-challenge asserting China's jurisdiction over the disputed waters. Philippine Coast Guard spokesperson Commodore Jay Tarriela described the interception as a “proactive response” and urged Chinese vessels to respect Philippine jurisdiction, respond to communications, and cease unauthorized activities in the EEZ. The PLA Navy later deployed the 052D-class destroyer Guilin (164) to escort the spy ship, which was observed conducting flight operations with a Z-9 utility helicopter. The incident, which took place during the U.S.-Philippines Cope Thunder military exercises, underscored continuing tensions in China–Philippines maritime relations and followed similar encounters involving Chinese military and civilian vessels operating near or within Philippine-claimed waters.[^122]

In September 2025, China announced plans to establish a nature reserve on the disputed Scarborough Shoal, known as [Huangyan Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huangyan_Island "Huangyan Island") in China and Bajo de Masinloc in the Philippines, prompting Manila to condemn it as a "pretext for occupation" that violates Philippine sovereignty, amid ongoing South China Sea territorial disputes and overlapping claims with several Southeast Asian nations.[^123]

#### 2026 public statements and diplomatic exchanges

On 21 January 2026, the Chinese Embassy in Manila issued a series of public statements and social media posts responding to remarks by Philippine government officials and lawmakers concerning disputes in the [West Philippine Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Philippine_Sea "West Philippine Sea"). The embassy published multiple posts on its official platforms disputing statements made by Philippine officials and asserting China's position on maritime and legal issues related to the dispute.[^127]

The statements named several Philippine officials, including Commodore [Jay Tarriela](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jay_Tarriela "Jay Tarriela") of the Philippine Coast Guard, Senators [Risa Hontiveros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risa_Hontiveros "Risa Hontiveros") and [Kiko Pangilinan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kiko_Pangilinan "Kiko Pangilinan"), and Representatives [Leila de Lima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leila_de_Lima "Leila de Lima") and [Chel Diokno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chel_Diokno "Chel Diokno"). Embassy posts accused these officials of spreading misinformation, misinterpreting international law, or politicizing maritime issues. In particular, the embassy's deputy spokesperson publicly responded to remarks by Hontiveros, describing them as political rhetoric and stating that freedom of expression should not be used to defame foreign leaders.[^128]

On 23 January 2026, [1Sambayan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1Sambayan "1Sambayan") called on the [Philippine government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_government "Philippine government") to take a stronger approach against what it described as problematic rhetoric from the Chinese Embassy in Manila, including considering the declaration of certain embassy personnel [persona non grata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persona_non_grata "Persona non grata").[^129]

Philippine officials and lawmakers reacted by criticizing the embassy's statements. Senator Hontiveros urged the Department of Foreign Affairs (DFA) to address what she described as attacks by the Chinese mission, while other lawmakers defended their statements as part of their constitutional duties and expressions of concern over national sovereignty. Senator Pangilinan also publicly condemned the tone of the embassy's responses and called on the DFA to lodge formal diplomatic protests.[^130]

Other Philippine officials similarly criticized the embassy's remarks. Senator [Erwin Tulfo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erwin_Tulfo "Erwin Tulfo") stated that foreign diplomatic missions should respect freedom of speech in the Philippines and refrain from publicly admonishing Philippine officials for statements made in defense of national interests.[^131]

The DFA reiterated support for Philippine officials defending the country's sovereignty and stated that differences between states are best addressed through established diplomatic channels rather than public exchanges.[^132]

On 14 March 2026, in response to recent remarks by Jay Tarriela, the Chinese Embassy in Manila presented a 1990 letter from [Bienvenido Tan Jr.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ambassadors_of_the_Philippines_to_Germany "List of ambassadors of the Philippines to Germany"), former Philippine ambassador to [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany"), addressed to German radio operator [Dieter Löffler](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dieter_L%C3%B6ffler&action=edit&redlink=1 "Dieter Löffler (page does not exist)"), which it said supports China's claims on the Scarborough Shoal. According to the embassy, the letter stated that "According to the [Philippine National Mapping and Resource Information Authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Mapping_and_Resource_Information_Authority "National Mapping and Resource Information Authority"), the Scarborough Reef or Huangyan Dao does not fall within the territorial sovereignty of the Philippines." [^133] Two days later, the Foreign Ministry of the Philippines spokesperson, [Rogelio Villanueva](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rogelio_Villanueva&action=edit&redlink=1 "Rogelio Villanueva (page does not exist)"), rejected the assertion of the Chinese embassy that the then-diplomat Tan admitted that Scarborough Shoal was not part of Philippine territory.[^134] In April 2026, the Philippines discovered [cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide") on Chinese boats seized near Second Thomas Shoal, which they believe was used to kill local fish populations.[^135]

In April 2026, satellite imagery showed that China had deployed vessels and installed a floating barrier at the entrance to Scarborough Shoal in the South China Sea, a disputed area also claimed by the Philippines. Philippine authorities stated that the move restricted access for their fishermen, amid ongoing tensions over control of the shoal.[^136] On 3 May 2026, China and the Philippines got into a dispute over [Sandy Cay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandy_Cay_\(South_China_Sea\) "Sandy Cay (South China Sea)") in the South China Sea. China said Filipino personnel had no right to be there, while the Philippines said Chinese ships were operating in its waters without permission.[^137]

### Benham Rise

In March 2017, Chinese ships were spotted in the [Benham Rise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benham_Rise "Benham Rise"), a protected food supply exclusive zone of the Philippines. The Philippines, through its ambassador to Beijing has officially asked China to explain the reported presence of one of its vessels in Benham Rise in the Pacific.[^138] [^139] A week later, the Chinese Ministry of Foreign Affairs released a statement saying that China is honoring the Philippines' sovereign rights over Benham Rise, and that the ship was passing by. However, the ship was revealed to have been on the area for about three months.[^140] In May 2017, Philippine president Duterte revealed that the Chinese Communist Party leader Xi Jinping made an unveiled threat of war against the Philippines over the islands in the South China Sea during a meeting in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing").[^141]

In January 2018, the Department of Foreign Affairs approved the Chinese Institute of Oceanology of the [Chinese Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Academy_of_Sciences "Chinese Academy of Sciences") to perform a scientific survey of the Rise, with the approval of President Rodrigo Duterte.[^142] In February, Duterte ordered the halting of all foreign researches in the Benham Rise;[^143] however, the research being conducted by the Chinese Academy of Sciences was already finished before the halt order. Duterte later signed an executive order renaming the Benham Rise to Philippine Rise and stressing that the rise is subject to sovereign rights and jurisdiction of the Philippines.[^144]

The International Hydrographic Organization (IHO) and the Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission (IOC) of the UNESCO have rules wherein the entity that first discovers unnamed features underwater have the right to name those features, prompting Filipino officials to realize that China was after, not just research, but also the naming rights over the underwater features of the Philippine Rise which will be internationally recognized through UNESCO.[^145] It was later clarified by the Philippine government that all researches ongoing at the time the halting was made were officially cancelled, but the government still allows research activities in the Rise. Foreign researchers may still do research within the Rise if they apply for research activities through the Philippine government.[^146] The government is also maintaining that the Rise belongs to the Philippines.[^147] On 12 February 2018, the International Hydrographic Organization approved the names proposed by China for five features in the Philippine Rise after China submitted to the organization its research findings on the area. The Chinese naming of the features met public protests in the Philippines.[^148] [^149]

### Censorship

In July 2025, tensions between China and the Philippines deepened after China attempted to block the screening of *[Food Delivery: Fresh from the West Philippine Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_Delivery:_Fresh_from_the_West_Philippine_Sea "Food Delivery: Fresh from the West Philippine Sea")*, a documentary highlighting the challenges faced by Filipino fishermen and the Philippine Coast Guard amid Chinese interference in the West Philippine Sea. Originally set to premiere in the Philippines, the film was reportedly withdrawn due to "external forces" and later debuted at the [Doc Edge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doc_Edge "Doc Edge") Festival in Auckland, where China's embassy and consulate requested the cancellation of future screenings, calling the film a distortion of facts and a tool of "Philippine propaganda".[^151] [^152] [^153] The Philippine government condemned the move, viewing it as an act of censorship.[^152] [^151] [^153]

## COVID-19 pandemic

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/LOZANO_02282021-2.jpg/250px-LOZANO_02282021-2.jpg)

Philippine President Rodrigo Duterte (center) with Chinese Ambassador Huang Xilian during the ceremonial turnover of CoronaVac vaccines in Villamor Air Base, Pasay City on 28 February 2021.

China played an important role in the early months of the [Philippines' response to COVID-19](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_vaccination_in_the_Philippines "COVID-19 vaccination in the Philippines").[^154] In February 2021, China became the first country to send the Philippines COVID-19 vaccines,[^155] following President Rodrigo Duterte's request for assistance from Chinese leader Xi Jinping in securing vaccines.[^156]

A June 2024 [Reuters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuters "Reuters") investigative report later revealed that the United States allegedly launched at the height of the pandemic [a clandestine campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ChinaAngVirus_disinformation_campaign "ChinaAngVirus disinformation campaign") to undermine China's growing influence in the Philippines and to erode Filipinos' public trust on China's [Sinovac vaccine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CoronaVac "CoronaVac"), face masks, and testing kits, thereby causing economic damage and putting innocent lives at risk.[^157]

## National security concerns in the Philippines

Amid escalating tensions in the South China Sea during the Bongbong Marcos administration in April 2024, the [National Intelligence Coordinating Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Intelligence_Coordinating_Agency "National Intelligence Coordinating Agency") and the [National Bureau of Investigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Investigation_\(Philippines\) "National Bureau of Investigation (Philippines)") announced that they would investigate an influx of over a thousand Chinese students enrolled in private universities in [Cagayan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cagayan "Cagayan"), a province facing Taiwan, most of whom were not attending classes, according to the [Bureau of Immigration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureau_of_Immigration_\(Philippines\) "Bureau of Immigration (Philippines)").[^158] [^159] [^160] Several universities and colleges in Cagayan rejected the allegations, and called the national security concerns regarding the Chinese students influx as a "blatant display of racism and [Sinophobia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinophobia "Sinophobia") ".[^161] The [Commission on Higher Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commission_on_Higher_Education_\(Philippines\) "Commission on Higher Education (Philippines)") later stated that most foreign students in the Philippines were Indians and not Chinese, adding that most Chinese students in the country as of Academic Year 2022–2023 were in the [National Capital Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Capital_Region "National Capital Region"), followed by [Cagayan Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cagayan_Valley "Cagayan Valley").[^162] [Bloomberg News](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloomberg_News "Bloomberg News") reported that the Chinese government has invested heavily in Cagayan and attempted to woo local political elites.[^163]

In May 2024, the mayor of [Bamban, Tarlac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamban,_Tarlac "Bamban, Tarlac"), [Alice Guo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alice_Guo "Alice Guo"), faced a senate inquiry after she was linked to an earlier [Philippine Offshore Gaming Operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_Offshore_Gaming_Operator "Philippine Offshore Gaming Operator") (POGO) raid;[^164] she was also suspected by Senator [Risa Hontiveros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risa_Hontiveros "Risa Hontiveros") of being a "Chinese asset",[^165] and was described by President Bongbong Marcos as not being known to most politicians in Tarlac.[^166] [^167] In June 2024, the National Bureau of Investigation confirmed that the fingerprints of Alice Guo matches with Chinese national Guo Hua Ping.[^168] In July 2024, the [Senate of the Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senate_of_the_Philippines "Senate of the Philippines") issued an arrest order for Guo and some of her family members for failing to attend a second consecutive hearing.[^169] Guo subsequently fled the country, but was subsequently captured in Indonesia and returned to the Philippines.[^170] [^171] The same month, [Romeo Brawner Jr.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romeo_Brawner_Jr. "Romeo Brawner Jr.") stated that China is using a [united front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_front_\(China\) "United front (China)") strategy to attempt to "influence various sectors in our society, including the education sector, business, even the media, and local government units." [^172] He cited POGOs as an aspect of that strategy.[^172] On 22 July 2024, President Marcos announced the nationwide ban of POGOs in [his State of the Nation Address](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_State_of_the_Nation_Address_\(Philippines\) "2024 State of the Nation Address (Philippines)") due to numerous security concerns.[^173] [^174] In August 2024, [Rappler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rappler "Rappler") reported that the Manila bureau chief of *[Wenhui Bao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wenhui_Bao "Wenhui Bao")* from 2021 until 2024, Zhang "Steve" Song, was an undercover [Ministry of State Security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_State_Security_\(China\) "Ministry of State Security (China)") (MSS) operative who worked closely with [Huawei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huawei "Huawei") and gathered intelligence about the internal dynamics and politics of key personalities in the country's defense and security sectors.[^175]

In January 2025, Philippine police arrested [Deng Yuanqing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Yuanqing "Deng Yuanqing"), a Chinese national affiliated with the [Army Engineering University of the People's Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_institutions_of_the_armed_forces_of_China "Academic institutions of the armed forces of China") on suspicion of espionage.[^176] China's foreign ministry spokesperson [Mao Ning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Ning_\(diplomat\) "Mao Ning (diplomat)") responded to the allegations of Chinese spies saying that the Philippines should make statements "based on facts" and to stop "making false claims and hyping up so-called Chinese spies". She then urged the country to protect the rights of Chinese citizens in the Philippines.[^177] The same month, Philippine authorities arrested suspected Chinese spies posing as Taiwanese tourists, recording the activities of Philippine Coast Guard ships in Palawan.[^178] In February 2025, the National Bureau of Investigation arrested two Chinese nationals for allegedly spying on [Malacañang Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaca%C3%B1ang_Palace "Malacañang Palace").[^179] In March 2025, Philippine authorities announced they had uncovered an alleged spy ring involving hundreds of Chinese nationals in the country.[^180]

In April 2025, the Philippines expressed concern over China's arrest of three Filipinos on [espionage charges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Espionage "Espionage"), calling the accusations retaliatory and baseless. Chinese authorities alleged the individuals were working for [Philippine intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_intelligence_agencies "List of intelligence agencies") to gather military information, claiming they had confessed. However, the [Philippines' National Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Security_Council_\(Philippines\) "National Security Council (Philippines)") stated the three were former scholars who had studied in [Hainan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hainan "Hainan") under a bilateral program with [Palawan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palawan "Palawan"), and denied any military ties.[^181]

In April 2025, Senator [Francis Tolentino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_Tolentino "Francis Tolentino") presented evidence in a Senate hearing linking the Chinese Embassy in Manila to the operation of troll farms through a contract with Infinitus Marketing Solutions.[^182] [^183] He revealed a signed service agreement and a corresponding payment, alleging the effort was part of a covert disinformation campaign targeting the Philippine government and public.[^182] [^184] On July 1, 2025, China sanctioned former Philippine senator Francis Tolentino, barring him from mainland China, Hong Kong, and Macau over his stance on South China Sea issues.[^185] Beijing cited his “egregious conduct” and opposition to Chinese interests, including authoring the [Philippine Maritime Zones Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_Maritime_Zones_Act "Philippine Maritime Zones Act") and leading a probe into alleged Chinese espionage. The move, announced a day after his term ended, is the first of its kind against a Philippine political figure. Tolentino dismissed the sanction as a “badge of honour” and reaffirmed his commitment to national sovereignty.[^186]

In March 2026, Philippine authorities arrested three Filipino nationals, suspected of spying for China.[^187] In May 2026, at the [Shangri-La Dialogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shangri-La_Dialogue "Shangri-La Dialogue"), Philippine defense secretary [Gilbert Teodoro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilbert_Teodoro "Gilbert Teodoro") said that the Philippines was "under severe threat territory-wise and politically too by China." [^188] The Chinese government subsequently sanctioned Teodoro, his spouse, and child.[^189]

### Cyber crime

On February 27, 2025, Philippine authorities arrested over 400 people, including 207 Chinese nationals, in a raid on a suspected cybercrime hub in [Pasay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasay "Pasay"). The individuals were accused of involvement in illegal online gambling and various scams, including cryptocurrency, romance, and investment frauds. Despite a crackdown ordered by Philippine President Bongbong Marcos in 2024, these operations have continued, with many relocating to other Southeast Asian countries. The arrests highlight the ongoing issue of cybercrime in the region, with China's strict anti-gambling laws fueling some of these illicit operations.[^190]

### Election interference

In 2025, allegations of [election interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_electoral_intervention "Foreign electoral intervention") by the Chinese government were widely reported.[^7] During a hearing of the [Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senate_of_the_Philippines "Senate of the Philippines") 's special panel on maritime and admiralty zones in April 2025, the [National Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Security_Council_\(Philippines\) "National Security Council (Philippines)") said that there are indicators that the Chinese government is interfering in the [2025 Philippine general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Philippine_general_election "2025 Philippine general election") including discrediting candidates critical of it and supporting candidates who sympathize with China.[^191] China's foreign ministry spokesperson [Guo Jiakun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guo_Jiakun "Guo Jiakun") dismissed these reports.[^192]

The [National Intelligence Coordinating Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Intelligence_Coordinating_Agency "National Intelligence Coordinating Agency") (NICA) also claimed that Chinese agents were "amplifying divisive political discourse" in the Philippines in coordination with local proxies. NICA alleged that the Chinese embassy in Manila have paid InfinitUs Marketing Solutions in 2023 for a [troll farm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troll_farm "Troll farm") on [Facebook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facebook "Facebook") and [Twitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twitter "Twitter") to spread disinformation and promote Chinese state interests.[^193] [^194] [^195]

## Safety concerns from China

In 2025, China had made four travel advisories about the Philippines over the course of five months since April.[^196] In April 2025, Anson Que, Chinese-Filipino businessman and CEO of Elison Steel was [kidnapped and murdered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidnapping_and_killing_of_Anson_Que "Kidnapping and killing of Anson Que"), with his body being found in [Rizal province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rizal_\(province\) "Rizal (province)"). The Chinese embassy, that same month, made a safety warning citing "harassment of Chinese individuals and businesses," however, did not specify cases for the advisory.[^197] The safety advisory from the embassy also came at a time during arrests made against Chinese nationals for espionage cases.[^198] The Malacañang Palace later denied the embassy's harassment claims.[^198] In July 2025, [China's Education Ministry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Education_\(China\) "Ministry of Education (China)") issued a safety warning for Chinese nationals studying in the Philippines due to criminal incidents targeting them.[^199] The Education Ministry also issued travel advisories for Chinese students in April and August citing security concerns.[^196] On August 30, [China's Foreign Ministry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(China\) "Ministry of Foreign Affairs (China)") issued a travel warning for its citizens due to "worsening" crime rates in the Philippines. The travel warning was refuted by the Philippines stating that crime rates in the country has declined.[^200] Carl Baker of the [Pacific Forum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Forum_International "Pacific Forum International") stated that China's travel advisories on the Philippines "overstate the risk associated with ordinary tourist and business travel." [^196]

## Public opinions

### Chinese public opinion

According to a 2026 [feeling thermometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeling_thermometer "Feeling thermometer") poll by the [Carter Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carter_Center "Carter Center") and [Emory University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emory_University "Emory University"), Chinese opinion of the Philippines was on average 27 out of 100. The same poll also showed 43% of the Chinese respondents agreed to China "ceding" the South China Sea territories to the Philippines "if Manila ended its [mutual defense treaty with the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutual_Defense_Treaty_\(United_States%E2%80%93Philippines\) "Mutual Defense Treaty (United States–Philippines)") in favor of non-alignment".[^201]

### Filipino public opinion

China remains not trustworthy among the majority of the Filipinos. A December 2025 [OCTA Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OCTA_Research "OCTA Research") survey showed 60% of the respondents did not trust China, as opposed to 13% who answered they did. 79% of the respondents selected China as "the greatest threat to the Philippines", which the polling firm said was "a five-percentage point increase from 74 percent in July 2025".[^202]

In a [Pulse Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_Asia "Pulse Asia") survey conducted in late February 2026, only 3% of the Filipino respondents supported a prospective state visit of Chinese leader [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") to the Philippines. 68% of the respondents supported the continuation of the [transparency initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transparency_initiative "Transparency initiative") against Chinese harassment of the Filipino fishermen and authorities, while 28% opposed it. Support for the initiative was highest among respondents in both Luzon and Metro Manila and lowest among the respondents in the Visayas.[^203]
