**Sino–Third World relations** refers to the general relationship between the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") and the rest of the [Third World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_World "Third World").

China's relations with the rest of the [Third World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_World "Third World") were next in importance to its relations with the superpowers—the Soviet Union and United States—during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"). Chinese leaders have tended to view the [developing nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_nation "Developing nation") of [Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia "Asia"), [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa"), and [Latin America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_America "Latin America") as a major force in international affairs, and they have considered China an integral part of this major Third World force. As has been the case with China's foreign relations in general, policy toward the countries of the developing world has fluctuated over time. It has been affected by China's alternating involvement in and isolation from world affairs and by the militancy or peacefulness of Beijing's views. In addition, China's relations with the Third World have been affected by China's ambiguous position as a developing country that nevertheless has certain attributes more befitting a major power. China has been variously viewed by the Third World as a friend and ally, a competitor for markets and loans, a source of economic assistance, a regional power intent on dominating [Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia "Asia"), and a "candidate [superpower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superpower "Superpower") " with such privileges as a permanent seat on the [UN Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UN_Security_Council "UN Security Council").

In addition to bilateral relations, China conducts international relations with other Third World countries through a variety of multi-lateral bodies or programs, including the Belt and Road Initiative, BRICS, the Forum on Africa-China Cooperation, the China-Arab States Cooperation Forum, and the G77.

## History

China's relations with the Third World have developed through several phases: the Bandung Line of the mid-1950s (named for a 1955 [Bandung Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bandung_Conference "Bandung Conference") of Asian and African nations held in [Bandung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bandung "Bandung"), [Indonesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesia "Indonesia")), support for liberation and world revolution in the 1960s, the pronouncement of the [Theory of the Three Worlds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Worlds_Theory "Three Worlds Theory") and support for a "new international economic order" in the 1970s, and a renewed emphasis on the [Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Principles_of_Peaceful_Coexistence "Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence") in the 1980s. Chinese government provides consistent political support to the [Group of 77](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_of_77 "Group of 77") and has made financial contributions to the Group since 1994.[^1] As a result, official statements of the G77 are delivered in the name of **The Group of 77 and China**.[^2] In addition, the foundation for China's 21st century engagement with Africa as the largest bloc of the Third World is the October 2000 Beijing ministerial conference for China-Africa dialogue (FOCAC) that set the basis for China's aspirations for a new world order, with elevated voice of the Third World.[^3]

### Early years of the People's Republic

China began developing relations with the Third World countries in 1954.[^4]<sup><span title="Page: xxiv">: xxiv</span> </sup> The [Bandung Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bandung_Conference "Bandung Conference") in 1955, at which [Zhou Enlai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Enlai "Zhou Enlai") led the Chinese delegation, was an important milestone for China's foreign relations.[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 80">: 80</span> </sup> China developed its foreign relations with many newly independent and soon-to-be independent countries.[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 80">: 80</span> </sup> China termed this cooperative approach the "Bandung Line." [^6]<sup><span title="Page: 25">: 25</span> </sup> This was the beginning of China's official discourse of [South-South cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South%E2%80%93South_cooperation "South–South cooperation").[^7]<sup><span title="Page: 74">: 74</span> </sup> China's [Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Principles_of_Peaceful_Coexistence "Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence") were incorporated into the Ten Principles of Bandung.[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 80">: 80</span></sup>

### 1960s

During the 1960s China cultivated ties with Third World countries and [insurgent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurgency "Insurgency") groups in an attempt to encourage "wars of national liberation" and revolution and to forge an international united front against both superpowers. China offered economic and technical assistance to other countries and [liberation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberty "Liberty") movements, which, although small in comparison with Soviet and United States aid, was significant considering China's own needs. Third World appreciation for Chinese assistance coexisted, however, with growing suspicions of China's militancy. Such suspicions were fed, for example, by Zhou Enlai's statement in the early 1960s that the potential for revolution in Africa was "excellent" and by the publication of [Lin Biao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lin_Biao "Lin Biao") 's essay "Long Live the Victory of People's War!" in 1965. Discord between China and many Third World countries continued to grow. In some cases, as with Indonesia's charge of Chinese complicity in the [1965 coup attempt in Jakarta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/30_September_Movement "30 September Movement") and claims by several African nations of Chinese [subversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subversion_\(politics\) "Subversion (politics)") during the [Cultural Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_Revolution "Cultural Revolution"), bilateral disputes led to the breaking off of [diplomatic relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_relations "Diplomatic relations"). Although the Third World was not a primary focus of the Cultural Revolution, it was not immune to the chaos this period wrought upon Chinese foreign relations.

### 1970s

In the 1970s, China began to redefine its foreign policy after the isolation and militancy of the late 1960s. China reestablished those of its diplomatic missions that had been recalled during the Cultural Revolution and began the process of [rapprochement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rapprochement "Rapprochement") with the United States. In 1971, the support of Third World nations was crucial in the PRC joining the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") (UN), taking over the seat of the ROC on Taiwan.[^8] China preferred a [United Nations Secretary-General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Secretary-General "United Nations Secretary-General") from the Third World.[^9] [^10]

China's major foreign policy statement during this time was Mao's [Theory of the Three Worlds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Worlds_Theory "Three Worlds Theory"), which was announced at the UN in 1974 by [Deng Xiaoping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping "Deng Xiaoping").[^7]<sup><span title="Page: 74">: 74</span></sup>

According to this theory, the [First World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_World "First World") consisted of the two superpowers—the Soviet Union and the United States—both "imperialist aggressors" whose rivalry was the greatest cause of impending world war. The Third World was the main force in international affairs. Its growing opposition to superpower [hegemony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hegemony "Hegemony") was exemplified by such world events as the [Arab nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_nations "Arab nations") ' control of [oil prices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_price "Oil price"), [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") 's expulsion of Soviet aid personnel in 1972, and the United States withdrawal from [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam"). The [Second World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_World "Second World"), comprising the [developed countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developed_countries "Developed countries") of [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe") plus [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan"), could either oppress the Third World or join in opposing the superpowers. By the second half of the 1970s, China perceived an increased threat from the Soviet Union, and the theory was modified to emphasize that the Soviet Union was the more dangerous of the two superpowers.

The other primary component of China's Third World policy in the early 1970s was a call for radical change in the world [power structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_structure "Power structure") and particularly a call for a " [new international economic order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_International_Economic_Order "New International Economic Order")." Until the late 1970s, the Chinese principles of sovereignty, opposition to hegemony, and self-reliance coincided with the goals of the movement for a new international economic order. Chinese statements in support of the new order diminished as China began to implement the [opening up policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opening_up_policy "Opening up policy"), allow [foreign investment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_direct_investment "Foreign direct investment"), and seek technical assistance and foreign loans. China's critical opinion of international financial institutions appeared to change abruptly as Beijing prepared to join the [International Monetary Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund "International Monetary Fund") and the [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank") in 1980. Chinese support for changes in the economic order stressed the role of collective self-reliance among the countries of the Third World, or " [South-South Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South-South_Cooperation "South-South Cooperation")," in the 1980s.

### 1980s

Also in the 1980s, China reasserted its Third World credentials and placed a renewed emphasis on its relations with Third World countries as part of its independent foreign policy. China stressed that it would develop friendly relations with other nations regardless of their social systems or ideologies and would conduct its relations on the basis of the [Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Principles_of_Peaceful_Coexistence "Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence"). Beijing exchanged delegations with Third World countries regularly, and it made diplomatic use of cultural ties, for example, by promoting friendly links between [Chinese Muslims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Muslim "Chinese Muslim") and [Islamic countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_countries "Islamic countries"). Officially, China denied that it sought a leadership role in the Third World, although some foreign observers argued to the contrary. Beijing increasingly based its foreign economic relations with the Third World on equality and mutual benefit, expressed by a shift toward [trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade "Trade") and [joint ventures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_venture "Joint venture") and away from [grants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grant_\(money\) "Grant (money)") and [interest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interest "Interest") -free [loans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loan "Loan").

By the second half of the 1980s, China's relations with Third World nations covered the spectrum from friendly to inimical. [Bilateral relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilateral_relation "Bilateral relation") ranged from a formal alliance with [North Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Korea "North Korea"), to a near-alliance with [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan"), to hostile relations with [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam") marked by sporadic border conflict. Many relationships have changed dramatically over time: for example, China previously had close relations with Vietnam; its ties with India were friendly during the 1950s but were strained thereafter by border tensions and Pakistan hoped that China would serve as a counterweight to Indian influence. Particularly in [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia"), a legacy of suspicion concerning China's ultimate intentions affected Chinese relations with many countries.

### Present

China has a major role in fostering cooperation among the global south countries in the area of climate change and clean energy.[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 18">: 18</span> </sup> China engaged in South-South climate and clean energy cooperation through: (1) bilateral clean energy agreements, (2) multilateral clean energy cooperation, (3) expanding exports of its clean energy technology to other developing countries, and (4) foreign energy infrastructure development via the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative").[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 221">: 221</span> </sup> Much of China's overall South-South Cooperation is now explicitly linked to the BRI.[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 226">: 226</span> </sup> The BRI is likewise tied to the concepts of the [Chinese Dream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Dream "Chinese Dream") and the [Community of Common Destiny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_of_Common_Destiny "Community of Common Destiny").[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 35">: 35</span></sup>

The "Ten, Hundred, Thousand" program is China's overarching initiative for South-South cooperation in addressing climate change.[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 224">: 224</span> </sup> As of 2023, China had signed partnerships with at least 27 other developing countries as part of this initiative.[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 224">: 224</span> </sup> Through framing BRI and other economic mechanisms as South-South cooperation, China seeks to position itself as a global South leader and draw a contrast with the global North countries.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 125">: 125</span></sup>

China is a major contributor to digital development in the global south.[^14]<sup><span title="Page: 6">: 6</span> </sup> China began to engage in digital initiatives with the global south, such as telecommunications projects, as part of its [Go Out policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Go_Out_policy "Go Out policy") since 1999.[^14]<sup><span title="Page: 5">: 5</span></sup>

[BRICS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BRICS "BRICS") is a further mechanism for China's cooperation with the global south and functions as a forum for policy coordination among its members.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 249">: 249</span></sup>

The [Forum on China-Africa Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forum_on_China%E2%80%93Africa_Cooperation "Forum on China–Africa Cooperation") is the primary multi-lateral coordination mechanism between African countries and China.[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 56">: 56</span> </sup> Along with the [China-Arab States Cooperation Forum (CASCF)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China-Arab_States_Cooperation_Forum "China-Arab States Cooperation Forum"), FOCAC was one of the first regional organizations established by China outside its territorial periphery.[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 56">: 56</span> </sup> As of 2022, the members of FOCAC are 53 African countries (all except Eswatini), China, and the [African Union Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union_Commission "African Union Commission"). A number of North African states are dual members of both CASCF and FOCAC: [Algeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algeria "Algeria"), [Djibouti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Djibouti "Djibouti"), [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt"), [Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya "Libya"), [Mauritania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauritania "Mauritania"), [Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco "Morocco"), [Somalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somalia "Somalia"), [Sudan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudan "Sudan"), and [Tunisia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunisia "Tunisia").[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 57">: 57</span> </sup> Although the African Union has increasingly played a coordinating role since joining CASCF in 2012, each African state represents itself in FOCAC and activities are implemented bilaterally between China and individual African countries.[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 57">: 57</span></sup>

CASCF was established in 2004 during a visit by Chinese President [Hu Jintao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hu_Jintao "Hu Jintao") to the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League") headquarters in Cairo, Egypt.[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 56">: 56</span> </sup> CASCF was the first cooperation forum between the Arab League and any other country or region.[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 59">: 59</span> </sup> CASCF membership consists of China and the Arab League, which officially represents its twenty-two member states as a relatively unified body.[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 57">: 57</span> </sup> CASCF therefore serves as the primary multilateral coordination mechanism between China and the Arab States.[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 56">: 56</span> </sup> This coordination by the Arab League allows Arab states to negotiate actively for collective projects involving multiple states, such as railway projects, nuclear power projects, and Dead Sea initiatives.[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 58">: 58</span></sup>

Effective 1 December 2024, China eliminated [tariffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tariff "Tariff") for goods imported from all of the countries that the United Nations categorizes as [least developed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_developed_countries "Least developed countries") and with which China has [diplomatic relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_China "Foreign relations of China").[^17] Thirty-three of the countries benefiting from the agreement are in [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa%E2%80%93China_economic_relations "Africa–China economic relations") and the non-African countries receiving zero tariff treatment are [Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Yemen_relations "China–Yemen relations"), [Kiribati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Kiribati_relations "China–Kiribati relations"), the [Solomon Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Solomon_Islands_relations "China–Solomon Islands relations"), [Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afghanistan%E2%80%93China_relations "Afghanistan–China relations"), [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh%E2%80%93China_relations "Bangladesh–China relations"), [Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93China_relations "Cambodia–China relations"), [Laos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Laos_relations "China–Laos relations"), [Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Myanmar_relations "China–Myanmar relations"), [Nepal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Nepal_relations "China–Nepal relations"), and [East Timor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93East_Timor_relations "China–East Timor relations").[^17]

## Third World conflicts involving China

One primary motivation for involvement in third world conflicts for PRC and ROC was to gain influence and legitimacy, claiming to be the only 'China' while undermining other side. This was part wider pattern in the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") where the world was divided into spheres of influence. Later the [Sino-Soviet Split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_Split "Sino-Soviet Split") occurred, with the PRC completing against the Soviet Union for influence.
