**Bangladesh–China relations**, also known as **Dragon–Tiger diplomacy**, refers to the bilateral relations between the [People's Republic of Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_Bangladesh "People's Republic of Bangladesh") and the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China"). Both countries established formal diplomatic relations in January 1976. China has an embassy in [Dhaka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhaka "Dhaka") and Bangladesh has one in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") and consulates in [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong") and [Kunming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kunming "Kunming"). Both countries are members of the [BCIM Forum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BCIM_Forum "BCIM Forum") (Bangladesh-China-India-Myanmar Forum for Regional Cooperation). According to Chinese government designations, Bangladesh and China are in a "comprehensive strategic cooperative partnership".[^1]

### Bengal and Imperial China

Chinese monks, scholars and traders began frequenting [Bengal (Tosali Province)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mantriparishad "Mantriparishad") from the [Qin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_dynasty "Qin dynasty") period. Famous ancient Chinese travelers to Bengal included [Faxian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faxian "Faxian"), [Yijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yijing_\(monk\) "Yijing (monk)") and [Xuanzang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xuanzang "Xuanzang").[^2] During the [Pala Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pala_Empire "Pala Empire") of Bengal, [Atisa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atisa "Atisa") of [Bikrampur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bikrampur "Bikrampur") traveled to [Tibet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibet "Tibet") and played an important role in developing Tibetan Buddhism.[^2]

In 648, a companion of [Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad "Muhammad"), [Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sa%27d_ibn_Abi_Waqqas "Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas"), who is credited have introduced [Islam in China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_China "Islam in China"), is said to have followed a route via the [Brahmaputra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmaputra "Brahmaputra"). Evidence remains in [Lalmonirhat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lalmonirhat_District "Lalmonirhat District") of a [masjid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masjid "Masjid") built by himself and is known locally as Abi Waqqas Masjid.[^3] [^4]

Political relations became nonexistent until the Middle Ages after the decline of Buddhism in South Asia.[^5] After the [Muslim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim "Muslim") conquest of Bengal in the 13th century, general [Bakhtiar Khilji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bakhtiar_Khilji "Bakhtiar Khilji") attempted to invade Tibet.[^6] Khilji's army was compelled to retreat due to the harsh conditions of the [Himalayas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himalaya "Himalaya"). [Ming China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_China "Ming China") and the [Bengal Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bengal_Sultanate "Bengal Sultanate") exchanged many embassies during the 15th century. Sultan [Ghiyasuddin Azam Shah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghiyasuddin_Azam_Shah "Ghiyasuddin Azam Shah") began sending envoys to the [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty"). He sent ambassadors in 1405, 1408 and 1409.[^7] Emperor [Yongle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yongle "Yongle") of China responded by sending ambassadors to Bengal between 1405 and 1433, including members of the [Treasure voyages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treasure_voyages "Treasure voyages") fleet led by [Admiral Zheng He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Admiral_Zheng_He "Admiral Zheng He").[^8] The exchange of embassies included the gift of an East African giraffe by Sultan [Shihabuddin Bayazid Shah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shihabuddin_Bayazid_Shah "Shihabuddin Bayazid Shah") to the Chinese emperor in 1414 after the Chinese envoys noticed Bengal's trade with [Malindi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malindi "Malindi").[^8] [^9] [^10] [^11] The treasure voyages led by the Chinese Muslim Admiral Zheng He visited the Bengal Sultanate at least twice. Ming China, under the [Yongle Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yongle_Emperor "Yongle Emperor"), also mediated in the [Bengal Sultanate-Jaunpur Sultanate War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bengal_Sultanate-Jaunpur_Sultanate_War "Bengal Sultanate-Jaunpur Sultanate War") creating peace between the two countries.[^2] [^12]

Experts have also speculated on the possibility of a southwestern [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road") connecting Bengal and China.[^13]

### East Pakistan (or Modern day Bangladesh)

Chinese premier [Zhou Enlai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Enlai "Zhou Enlai") visited [East Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Pakistan "East Pakistan") several times in the 1950s and 60s. The [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") maintained close ties with Bengali nationalist leaders [Maulana Abdul Hamid Khan Bhashani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maulana_Abdul_Hamid_Khan_Bhashani "Maulana Abdul Hamid Khan Bhashani") and [Huseyn Shaheed Suhrawardy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huseyn_Shaheed_Suhrawardy "Huseyn Shaheed Suhrawardy"). Sheikh Mujib in particular, was considered close to [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong"). While serving as the Prime Minister of Pakistan in 1957, Awami League president H. S. Suhrawardy became the first Pakistani leader to make a state visit to the PRC.

## Sectoral relations

### Geopolitical relations

When the [Bangladesh War of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh_War_of_Independence "Bangladesh War of Independence") broke out in 1971, complex geopolitical rivalries erupted in South Asia. The Bangladeshis elicited the help of India in their freedom struggle. China had earlier fought a war with India in 1962 and became an ally of Pakistan. Crucially, it was using Pakistan as a conduit for [rapprochement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rapprochement "Rapprochement") with [Richard Nixon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Nixon "Richard Nixon") and [Henry Kissinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Kissinger "Henry Kissinger") in the United States. The PRC also replaced [Taiwan (Republic of China)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") as a permanent member of [UN Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UN_Security_Council "UN Security Council") in 1971. Its first veto was used to support Pakistan during the [Indo-Pakistani War of 1971](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1971 "Indo-Pakistani War of 1971"). In the early years of Bangladesh's independence, Dhaka was also close to the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"), which was a rival of China following the [Sino-Soviet split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split "Sino-Soviet split"). Despite the support of most countries in the world, Bangladesh's UN membership was vetoed by China until 1974. The situation dramatically changed after [military coups in Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_coups_in_Bangladesh "Military coups in Bangladesh") began in 1975, causing Bangladesh to distance itself from the Indo-Soviet [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") axis in South Asia. China and Bangladesh established diplomatic relations in January 1976.[^14] The period coincided with the recognition of the PRC as the sole legitimate government of China by most of the world. The period also saw the Chinese begin to embrace market socialism. President [Ziaur Rahman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ziaur_Rahman "Ziaur Rahman"), who restored [free markets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_market "Free market") in Bangladesh, visited Beijing and the laid the groundwork for the relationship; while numerous Chinese leaders visited Dhaka in the late 1970s.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Ziaur_Rahman_1979.jpg/250px-Ziaur_Rahman_1979.jpg)

Ziaur Rahman was a key architect of modern Bangladesh-China relations

By the mid-1980s, China had forged close military, commercial and cultural ties with Bangladesh and also supplied it with military aid and equipment.[^14] The then-president of Bangladesh [Hossain Mohammad Ershad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hossain_Mohammad_Ershad "Hossain Mohammad Ershad") was warmly received in Beijing in July 1987. A Bangladesh-China friendship bridge was constructed and inaugurated over river Buriganga connecting [Dhaka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhaka "Dhaka") and [Munshiganj](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munshiganj "Munshiganj") by the Chinese as a token of this newly advancing diplomatic and military relationship. On 4 October 2000, the Government of Bangladesh issued a postal stamp marking the 25th anniversary of the establishment of Bangladesh-China diplomatic relations. By this time, China had provided economic assistance totaling US$300 [million](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Million "Million") to Bangladesh and the bilateral trade had reached a value mounting to a billion dollars.[^15] In 2002, Chinese Premier [Wen Jiabao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wen_Jiabao "Wen Jiabao") made an official visit to Bangladesh and both countries declared 2005 as the "Bangladesh-China Friendship Year." [^16]

The two countries signed nine different bilateral agreements to further their mutual relationship.[^17]

On [Bangladesh Nationalist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh_Nationalist_Party "Bangladesh Nationalist Party") PM Begum [Khaleda Zia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khaleda_Zia "Khaleda Zia") 's invitation China was added as an observer in the [South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Asian_Association_for_Regional_Cooperation "South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation") (SAARC).[^18] Sri Lanka, Maldives, Nepal, and Pakistan joined Bangladesh in strongly supporting China's application for observer status, which was accepted.[^19]

After [Cyclone Sidr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclone_Sidr "Cyclone Sidr") hit Bangladesh in 2007, China donated US$1 million for relief and reconstruction in cyclone-hit areas.

#### Tibet

The Chinese embassy in Bangladesh has twice intervened during local art shows to censor coverage of the plight of [Tibetan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetans "Tibetans") refugees in South Asia. In 2009, police canceled a planned photo exhibit on Tibetan exiles at the [Drik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drik "Drik") gallery in Dhaka, after requests from the Chinese embassy.[^20] In 2016, the Chinese ambassador protested against Tibetan exhibits at the [Dhaka Art Summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhaka_Art_Summit "Dhaka Art Summit"), which led the organizers to censor coverage of Tibet.[^21]

#### Uyghurs

In July 2019, UN ambassadors of 37 countries, including Bangladesh, signed a joint letter to the [United Nations Human Rights Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council "United Nations Human Rights Council") defending [China's persecution of Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution_of_Uyghurs_in_China "Persecution of Uyghurs in China").[^22] [^23] Bangladesh was one of 16 countries that defended China in 2019 but did not do so in 2020.[^24]

### Economic relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Bangabandhu_International_Conference_Center.jpg/250px-Bangabandhu_International_Conference_Center.jpg)

The Bangabandhu International Conference Center in Dhaka was built with Chinese assistance

Shah Amanat Bridge in Chittagong is one of many funded by Chinese development assistance in Bangladesh

As a part of a strengthened bilateral trade and investment relationship, China has given Duty-Free (DF) access to 97 percent of Bangladeshi products which came into force from 1 July 2020. According to the notice of the Tariff Commission of the State Council of China on 16 June 2020, zero-tariff has been applied to 8,256 products originating from Bangladesh among the total 8,549 products recognized in the Chinese tariff-line.[^25] Utilizing this Duty Free (DF) facility, Bangladesh can seize greater market share as this DF Facility covers [Bangladesh-originating 132 knitwear items](https://www.textiletoday.com.bd/decoding-chinas-duty-free-facility-knitwear-bangladesh-tapping-provisions-product-tariff/) and [117 woven items](https://www.textiletoday.com.bd/decoding-chinas-duty-free-facility-for-knitwear-sector-of-bangladesh-tapping-unexploited-export-potentials-part-3/) at HS Code- 8 Digit Level, at which segment of items, Bangladesh is already highly competitive exporting to China.[^26] Bangladesh-China bilateral trade is highly tilted in favour of Beijing,[^27] [^28] [^29] and Bangladesh's bilateral trade deficit with China has increased 1600% in last 20 years (c. 2019).[^27] 25% of Bangladesh's total imports are from China, in 2018-19 China's export to Bangladesh was US$13.6 billion whereas Bangladesh's export to China was only $0.56 billion.[^27] China has given several loans to Bangladesh, which compared to India \[with which Bangladesh shares land border on 3 sides\] are on [less favorable terms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predatory_lending "Predatory lending"), and could lead Bangladesh into [debt-trap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt-trap_diplomacy "Debt-trap diplomacy").[^30] China has also offered to construct [nuclear power plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_plants "Nuclear power plants") in Bangladesh to help meet the country's growing energy needs, while also seeking to aid the development of Bangladesh's natural gas resources.[^31] [^32] China's mainly imports raw materials from Bangladesh like leather, cotton textiles, fish, etc. China's major exports to Bangladesh include textiles, machinery and electronic products, cement, fertiliser, tyre, raw silk, maize, etc.[^33]

In 2005, Chinese premier [Wen Jiabao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wen_Jiabao "Wen Jiabao") visited Bangladesh on an official visit on 7 and 8 April. Various agreements were signed during this visit. On transportation side, China and Bangladesh have agreed to start a direct air transport route between Dhaka and Beijing via Kunming. Also [Kunming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kunming "Kunming") -Chittagong road link through [Myanmar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar "Myanmar") is also considered.[^28] The Chinese premier readily agreed to constructing the Di-Aluminium Phosphate (DAP) [fertiliser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertiliser "Fertiliser") factory in Chittagong entirely on concessional lending instead of on supplier's credit.[^34] In 2007, Chinese Assistant Minister of Commerce, Wang Chao visited Bangladesh with the 39-member purchase delegation. It is the biggest purchase delegation ever to Bangladesh with over 10 companies listed in China's top 500 and some of them in world's top 500. Delegation is reported to have purchased Bangladeshi goods worth over US$50 million.[^35] Both countries accepted to build a "Bangladesh-China Friendship Exhibition Center" in Dhaka.[^35] the amount of foreign trade between Bangladesh and China is about 10 billion dollars. Bangladesh import about 8 billion dollars of goods from China while it exports 2 billion dollars of goods. However, China has recently waived tariffs of 97% Bangladesh's products. This will reduce the trade deficit between Bangladesh and China.

In October 2016, Bangladesh became a participant in [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative") (BRI) while both countries elevated their relationship to Strategic Partnership of Cooperation. Most of the BRI projects in Bangladesh are related to transport and energy infrastructure. As of 2023, out of the $40 billion in investments promised by China, only $4.45 billion was disbursed according to the then Chinese Ambassador to Bangladesh Yao Wen. As of 2026, the projects were numbered around 40 although the exact number remained unknown due to lack of official data. Many of the BRI projects were eventually cancelled. The Sonadia Deep Sea Port project was cancelled in August 2020 over environmental concerns and a strong opposition from the United States, Japan, and India. In 2021, China informed Bangladesh that it would no longer undertake projects involving high pollution and high energy consumption. In the same year, Bangladesh had to shelve 6 BRI projects including Payra deep seaport.[^36] [^37] [^38]

Effective 1 December 2024, China eliminated [tariffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tariff "Tariff") for goods imported from all of the countries that the United Nations categorizes as [least developed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_developed_countries "Least developed countries") and with which China has diplomatic relations, including Bangladesh.[^39]

In 2016, the Chinese Economic and Industrial Zone (CEIZ) in Anwara, Chattogram, was launched during Chinese President Xi Jinping's visit to Dhaka. Despite its potential, the project faced prolonged delays of over nine years due to disagreements with the initial Chinese developer, slow approvals, and extended planning procedures, preventing significant progress until recent years.[^40]

In May 2026, Bangladesh Foreign Minister Khalilur Rahman visited Beijing to talk with Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi, focusing on trade development, regional security and mutual cooperation. They also discussed about infrastructure project, [Belt and Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative") cooperation, energy financing.[^41]

### Defence cooperation

Defence cooperation between China and Bangladesh began after China recognized Bangladesh in 1975.[^42] China has provided military personnel training and military hardware, emerging over the years as the number one provider of such material to Bangladesh.[^42] The [Bangladesh Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh_Army "Bangladesh Army") has been equipped with Chinese tanks, [its navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navy_of_Bangladesh "Navy of Bangladesh") has Chinese frigates and missile boats and the [Bangladesh Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh_Air_Force "Bangladesh Air Force") flies Chinese fighter jets. In 2002, China and Bangladesh signed a "Defence Cooperation Agreement" which covers military training and defence production. In 2006, a Chinese report to the United Nations revealed that Dhaka is emerging as a major buyer of Chinese-made weapons. China sold 65 large calibre artillery systems, 16 combat aircraft and 114 [missile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Missile "Missile") and related equipment to Bangladesh in 2006. Bangladesh also bought roughly 200 small arms and regular artillery pieces from China.[^43]

In 2008, Bangladesh set up an anti-ship missile launch pad near the [Chittagong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chittagong "Chittagong") Port with assistance from China. The maiden missile test was performed on 12 May 2008 with active participation of Chinese experts. It successfully test-fired anti-ship missile [C-802](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-802 "C-802") A with a strike range of 120 km from the frigate [BNS *Osman*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BNS_Osman "BNS Osman") near [Kutubdia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kutubdia "Kutubdia") Island in the [Bay of Bengal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal "Bay of Bengal"). BNS *Osman*, which was commissioned in 1989, is a 1500-ton Chinese built Jianghu class [frigate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frigate "Frigate"), and the C-802A missile is a modified version of Chinese [Ying Ji-802](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ying_Ji-802 "Ying Ji-802") with weight reduced from 815 kg to 715 kg to increase the strike range from 42 km to 120 km.

In 2026, [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh") received various [VT5 tanks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_15_tank "Type 15 tank") from [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China"), which is believed to be the first of a second army order for the Chinese light tank.[^44] [^45]

### Water security: River water sharing

Bangladesh and India have signaled concerns over Chinese plans to dam and divert water from the [Brahmaputra River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmaputra_River "Brahmaputra River") in Tibet.[^46]

Bangladesh has expressed concern particularly over China's [Medog Hydropower Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medog_Hydropower_Station "Medog Hydropower Station") Project due to its potential impact on downstream water flow, particularly on the [Jamuna River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamuna_River_\(Bangladesh\) "Jamuna River (Bangladesh)"), which is fed by the Brahmaputra. In February 2025, Bangladeshi officials formally requested more information from Beijing, citing worries about reduced water availability, ecological disruptions, and the lack of transparency in the dam's planning. The project's scale and potential to alter river flow have heightened fears of adverse effects on agriculture, fisheries, and livelihoods in Bangladesh's flood-prone regions.[^47]

Although China claims the project is a run-of-the-river hydropower initiative that will not affect downstream flow, Bangladesh has requested technical transparency and access to hydrological data to independently assess potential impacts. Malik Fida Khan, executive director of the [Center for Environmental and Geographic Information Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre_for_Environmental_and_Geographic_Information_Services "Centre for Environmental and Geographic Information Services"), highlighted that 70 percent of the dry-season flow in the Ganges-Brahmaputra-Meghna basin comes via the Brahmaputra, warning that any disruption upstream could critically affect Bangladesh's already climate-stressed water resources. Sharif Jamil, an environmental activist and coordinator of [Riverkeeper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riverkeeper "Riverkeeper") Bangladesh, described the dam as "unilateral and geographically sensitive," emphasizing that the lack of transparent consultation could lead to ecological, hydrological, and socio-economic consequences.[^48]

### Covid-19 pandemic

In August 2020, Bangladesh approved the final stage testing of the Chinese pharmaceutical firm Sinovac Biotech Ltd's COVID-19 vaccine.[^49] On 4 October 2020, it was reported that Sinovac had sought Bangladesh's co-financing of the trials.[^50] However, on 13 October 2020, the trials were reported to be uncertain after Bangladesh refused to co-fund the vaccine, saying that at the time of seeking approval, Sinovac had said they would run the trials on their own funds and had also promised to provide 100,000 free doses.[^51]

## Diaspora

There is a sizeable [Chinese diaspora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_diaspora "Chinese diaspora") in the Bangladeshi capital, Dhaka; first arriving in the 1940s and a smaller population in Chittagong. Their presence is seen as a result of the [First World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_World_War "First World War"). Many tend to work as [shoemakers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoemakers "Shoemakers") or are involved in the [opium trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium_trade "Opium trade"), while others have opened up many [Chinese restaurants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_restaurant "Chinese restaurant") across the country with its own [Bangladeshi twist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladeshi_cuisine "Bangladeshi cuisine").[^52] Many popular beauty parlours are also run by Chinese Bangladeshis.[^53] Areas densely populated with [Chinese people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_people "Chinese people") in Dhaka include Imamganj and Mitford.[^54]
