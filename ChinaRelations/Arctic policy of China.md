The [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC) published its first official Arctic policy in a [white paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_paper "White paper") released in 2018 and declared itself a "near-Arctic state." [^1] Analysts have also noted the development of [People's Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") (PLA) capabilities that could extend to the Arctic.[^2] [^3] Termed the "Polar Silk Road" of the larger [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative"), China's arctic policy has been understood by analysts to encompass geostrategic and economic objectives, such as providing options to bypass the [Strait of Malacca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca "Strait of Malacca") and the [Suez Canal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Canal "Suez Canal") while accessing the [Northeast Passage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northeast_Passage "Northeast Passage").[^4]

## History

China's links to the Arctic date back to the early 20th century. In 1925, the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)") signed the [Spitsbergen Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spitsbergen_Treaty "Spitsbergen Treaty"), granting access to commercial activity on [Svalbard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Svalbard "Svalbard").[^5] However, for much of the 20th century, China showed limited interest in the region, focusing instead on Antarctica after the founding of the PRC in 1949.[^6]

Scientific engagement grew from the late 1980s with the establishment of the [Polar Research Institute of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_Research_Institute_of_China "Polar Research Institute of China") in Shanghai and the launch of the *[Chinese Journal of Polar Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Journal_of_Polar_Research&action=edit&redlink=1 "Chinese Journal of Polar Research (page does not exist)")* in 1988.[^7] The first expedition to the North Pole organized by Chinese scientists took place in 1995, and the first expedition organized by the Polar Research Institute of China took place in 1999.[^8] China has conducted Arctic expeditions annually since 2016.[^8]

China joined the [International Arctic Science Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Arctic_Science_Committee "International Arctic Science Committee") in 1996,[^9] and since 1999 has operated research icebreakers, including the [*Xue Long*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xue_Long "Xue Long") and [*Xue Long 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xue_Long_2 "Xue Long 2"). In 2004, China established the [Yellow River Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yellow_River_Station "Yellow River Station") in Norway's Svalbard archipelago. While officially devoted to research, analysts have raised concerns about potential [dual-use](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual-use_technology "Dual-use technology") applications.[^10] [^11]

China hosted the [Arctic Science Summit Week](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arctic_Science_Summit_Week&action=edit&redlink=1 "Arctic Science Summit Week (page does not exist)") in 2005—the first Asian country to do so. By 2010, Chinese policymakers pursued a cautious Arctic approach to avoid alarming the [Arctic coastal states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_Five "Arctic Five"), particularly Russia, which had resumed bomber patrols in the region and planted a flag on the seabed in 2007.[^12]

In 2012, the [*Xue Long*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xue_Long "Xue Long") became the first Chinese vessel to traverse the [Northeast Passage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northeast_Passage "Northeast Passage").[^13] China was granted permanent observer status in the [Arctic Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_Council "Arctic Council") in 2013.[^14]

In September 2015, Chinese navy vessels appeared near the coast of Alaska for the first time.[^8] In 2018, the [*Xue Long* 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xue_Long_2 "Xue Long 2") traversed the [Northern Sea Route](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Sea_Route "Northern Sea Route").[^15]

## 2018 white paper

In January 2018, China released its first Arctic Policy white paper, which remains the most detailed official statement of its regional strategy.[^16] The document describes China as a "near-Arctic state," a self-designation that has drawn criticism from Arctic governments and analysts. China argues that its geographic proximity, climatic vulnerability, and economic interests give it a stake in Arctic affairs.[^17]

The paper emphasizes four principles: respect, cooperation, win–win outcomes, and sustainability. It highlights climate change, ecological protection, shipping routes, and resource utilization as areas of focus. China also commits to following international law, including the [UNCLOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea "United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea"), and acknowledges that non-Arctic states lack territorial sovereignty in the region.[^16]

## Strategic objectives

Chinese interests in the Arctic can be grouped into four main areas:

- **Scientific research** – China maintains an active polar research program, operating stations in the Arctic and fielding icebreakers. The Yellow River Station on Svalbard has conducted studies in geology, atmosphere, and space physics since 2004.[^11]
- **Natural resources** – The Arctic is seen as a potential source of oil, gas, and minerals. China has partnered with Russia on liquefied natural gas projects in [Yamal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yamal_Peninsula "Yamal Peninsula"),[^18] and state-owned companies have invested in mining and infrastructure projects.
- **Shipping routes** – Melting sea ice has drawn Chinese interest in the [Northern Sea Route](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Sea_Route "Northern Sea Route") and other Arctic passages. Beijing has promoted these corridors as part of a "Polar Silk Road" that would complement the broader [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative").[^17]
- **Strategic presence** – While official policy emphasizes peaceful use, some Western governments have raised concerns about potential military applications of Chinese research, technology, and infrastructure in the Arctic.[^19]

## Polar Silk Road

The concept of the "Polar Silk Road" (冰上丝绸之路) was introduced in the 2018 white paper and has since become a central theme in China's Arctic policy.[^4] [^17] It envisions Arctic shipping lanes as extensions of the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative"), providing alternative trade routes between Asia, Europe, and North America and also leveraging melting ice for resource extraction. Chinese state-owned enterprises have participated in feasibility studies and infrastructure development along Russia's Arctic coast, including telecommunications projects under the [Digital Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Silk_Road "Digital Silk Road").[^20]
