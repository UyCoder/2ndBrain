Formal diplomatic relations between the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC) and the [Republic of Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Singapore "Republic of Singapore") were established in 1990. Singapore recognised the PRC later than many other countries and the last in [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia") to do so. This delay stemmed from Singapore's preference that its neighbours, particularly [Indonesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesia "Indonesia"), normalise relations with the PRC first. After Indonesia and the PRC announced their decision to establish ties on 8 August 1990, Singapore and the PRC began discussions to formalise relations. Three rounds of talks were held, beginning on 27 July 1990, to finalise the establishment of diplomatic ties.[^1]

Singapore and China have maintained neutral relations, with Singapore recognising China's growing regional importance while ensuring its own national interests are upheld. Singapore supports constructive Chinese engagement in the [Asia-Pacific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia-Pacific "Asia-Pacific"), but does so on the basis of mutual respect and regional stability. Its co-operation with China, including on security and counterterrorism efforts through [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN"), reflects Singapore's proactive and independent approach to regional diplomacy.[^2] [^3] [^4] [^5] Singapore has also participated in China's waves of industrialization by sharing experiences via state level cooperations in the establishment of [China-Singapore Suzhou Industrial Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suzhou_Industrial_Park "Suzhou Industrial Park"), [Sino-Singapore Tianjin Eco-city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Singapore_Tianjin_Eco-city "Sino-Singapore Tianjin Eco-city"), Sino-Singapore Guangzhou Knowledge City, Singapore-Sichuan Hi-Tech Innovation Park and the Sino-Singapore Jilin Food Zone.[^6]

While the relationship between the two countries remains strong, it has been strained during numerous high-profile events, especially during the 2010s and 2020s, including Singapore's stance against China regarding the [South China Sea dispute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Sea_dispute "South China Sea dispute"), Singapore's support for the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") ' military presence and [alliance system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_System "San Francisco System") in Asia and the seizing of [Singapore Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore_Armed_Forces "Singapore Armed Forces") ' (SAF) vehicles by [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong") authorities in 2016.[^7] Singapore also often engages with [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") through unofficial channels, with relations shaped by shared economic and military priorities and limited political alignment especially during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"). Singapore's delayed recognition of the PRC stemmed largely from its sustained ties with Taiwan throughout the latter part of the 20th century.[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 117">: 117</span> </sup> Even after recognising the PRC, Singapore has continued informal cooperation with Taiwan in areas such as trade, education and cultural exchanges. While this has occasionally attracted disapproval from Beijing, it reflects Singapore's longstanding commitment to strategic independence and balanced diplomacy.

Despite occasional tensions, Singapore and China have sustained steady cooperation across a wide range of sectors, including defence, trade, education and infrastructure development. While both sides regularly reaffirm strong bilateral ties, Singapore has pursued these relations from a position of mutual benefit rather than deference. Its participation in initiatives such as the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative") reflects a pragmatic approach focused on national interest rather than alignment with China's geopolitical goals. Singapore has also consistently advocated for [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN") centrality and has sought to mediate between Beijing and the organisation, making clear that its support for regional cooperation does not equate to uncritical endorsement of China's policies.[^9] [^10] [^11]

## History

### Singapore as a British colony and as a part of Malaysia

The historic connections between the peoples of Singapore and China date back well before the establishment of the PRC in October 1949. Many early Chinese migrants came to the region known as [Nanyang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanyang_\(region\) "Nanyang (region)") seeking to escape poverty and conflict during the [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty"). A significant portion of ethnic Chinese Singaporeans trace their ancestral origins to southern Chinese provinces such as [Fujian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fujian "Fujian"), [Guangdong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangdong "Guangdong") and [Hainan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hainan "Hainan").[^12]

During the [Second Sino-Japanese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War "Second Sino-Japanese War"), which later became part of the larger [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), many ethnic Chinese in Malaya and Singapore strongly supported China. This included providing aid to both the [Kuomintang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuomintang "Kuomintang") (KMT) and the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) in their fight against Japanese forces. After Japan's conquest in the [Malayan campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malayan_campaign "Malayan campaign") and the [Fall of Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_Singapore "Fall of Singapore"), the Japanese retaliated with harsh reprisals against the ethnic Chinese population through operations such as [Sook Ching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sook_Ching "Sook Ching"), resulting in tens of thousands of deaths. The [Malayan People's Anti-Japanese Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malayan_People%27s_Anti-Japanese_Army "Malayan People's Anti-Japanese Army") (MPAJA), initially a resistance group, later evolved into the communist-aligned [Malayan National Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malayan_National_Liberation_Army "Malayan National Liberation Army") (MNLA). During the subsequent [Malayan Emergency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malayan_Emergency "Malayan Emergency") and [Second Malayan Emergency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Malayan_Emergency "Second Malayan Emergency"), the communist insurgency under the [Malayan Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malayan_Communist_Party "Malayan Communist Party") (MCP) was viewed as an extension of China's revolutionary influence in Southeast Asia.[^13] However, China ended its support for the MCP following the establishment of [diplomatic ties between Malaysia and China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Malaysia_relations "China–Malaysia relations") in 1974.

Ethnic Chinese in Singapore (and also in Malaya) were early supporters of MCP, which led to suspicion from the British colonial authorities and later from the ruling [People's Action Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Action_Party "People's Action Party") (PAP). Chinese-language secondary schools and [Nanyang University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanyang_University "Nanyang University") were viewed as hotbeds of communist activity. The government's efforts to curb communist influence included major events such as the [Chinese middle schools riots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_middle_schools_riots "Chinese middle schools riots") and [Operation Cold Store](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Cold_Store "Operation Cold Store"). Nanyang University was eventually reorganised and merged with the [University of Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Singapore "University of Singapore") to form the [National University of Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_University_of_Singapore "National University of Singapore") (NUS).[^14] [^15]

### Post–Singapore independence

[Lee Kuan Yew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Kuan_Yew "Lee Kuan Yew"), Singapore's first [Prime Minister of Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Singapore "Prime Minister of Singapore"), was careful to avoid any perception among [Southeast Asian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia") nations that Singapore, with its predominantly ethnic Chinese population, was a "Third China" alongside the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China") (ROC, Taiwan) and the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC). This caution stemmed from Singapore's experiences with [communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communism "Communism"), the ongoing [Vietnam War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_War "Vietnam War") and domestic political factors. Consequently, Singapore delayed establishing diplomatic relations with China until other Southeast Asian countries had done so in order to avoid appearing biased towards China.[^16] [^13] Lee's official visits to China from 1976 onwards were conducted in [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore_English "Singapore English") to reaffirm that he represented Singapore, not a "Third China".[^17]

During this era, Lee firmly believed in [resisting communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-communism "Anti-communism") in line with the [domino theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domino_theory "Domino theory").[^18] Having witnessed the British failure to defend Singapore in [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") and doubting the United States’ willingness to defend Singapore against communism, Lee welcomed the American presence in the region as a necessary counterbalance to the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") and China.[^16] In the 1970s, Singapore and China began unofficial relations,[^19] which led to the exchange of Trade Offices in September 1981.[^20] Commercial air services between Singapore and mainland China commenced in 1985.[^21] Formal diplomatic relations were finally established on 3 October 1990,[^22] making Singapore the last Southeast Asian country to officially recognise the PRC.[^23]

Despite maintaining strong relations with China, Singapore continues to uphold unofficial ties with [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan"), including a military training and facilities agreement dating back to 1975,[^24] with annual official exercises known as Exercise Starlight (星光計畫).[^25] This arrangement exists due to Singapore's limited land area.[^24] During the 2000s, China tried to offer Singapore the option to move its training facilities to [Hainan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hainan "Hainan").[^26] [^27] In 2004, prior to [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Singapore "Prime Minister of Singapore") [Lee Hsien Loong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Hsien_Loong "Lee Hsien Loong") assuming office, he visited Taiwan to understand recent developments there.[^28] China objected strongly to the visit because of Taiwan's sensitive political status.[^29] Later that year, China put bilateral relations on hold.[^30] In his first [National Day Rally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Day_Rally "National Day Rally") speech, Lee expressed concern that Taiwanese leaders and citizens might have overestimated the level of support they would receive from the international community if they were to declare independence, reflecting Singapore's cautious approach towards maintaining regional stability.[^31]

## Diplomatic events and perspectives

China has its embassy on [Tanglin Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanglin "Tanglin") in Singapore, while Singapore's embassy in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") on [Jianguomen Wai Avenue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jianguomen_Outer_Street "Jianguomen Outer Street"), [Chaoyang District](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaoyang_District_\(Beijing\) "Chaoyang District (Beijing)") was established in 1990,[^32] and it has Consulates-General in [Chengdu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chengdu "Chengdu"), [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou "Guangzhou"), [Shanghai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai "Shanghai"), and [Xiamen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiamen "Xiamen"), as well as [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong"), known during the era of [British rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Hong_Kong "British Hong Kong") as the Singapore Commission.[^33]

### South China Sea dispute

While Singapore is not a party to the [territorial disputes in the South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_disputes_in_the_South_China_Sea "Territorial disputes in the South China Sea"), it has an interest in the outcome of these disputes since they have implications for international law, freedom of navigation, and ASEAN unity.[^34] Singapore's outspoken stance on the South China Sea is viewed by Chinese policymakers as taking the side of the US and other claimant states as well as attempting to internationalise the dispute.[^35] In September 2016, [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") tabloid *[Global Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Times "Global Times")* accused Singapore of supporting the *[Philippines v. China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines_v._China "Philippines v. China")* ruling in favour of the Philippines during the [Non-Aligned Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement "Non-Aligned Movement") meeting in Venezuela, based on unnamed sources.[^36] The *Global Times'* account was disputed by Singapore's ambassador to China, Stanley Loh.[^37] Earlier in August, Chinese diplomat [Hua Chunying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hua_Chunying "Hua Chunying") stated: "China hopes that Singapore...can maintain an objective and fair position as the coordinator of China and Asean dialogue relations, so as to advance Sino-Singapore relations and healthy and stable China-Asean ties." [^38]

### 2016 Terrex seizure incident

In November 2016, nine [Singapore Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore_Army "Singapore Army") [Terrex ICV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrex_ICV "Terrex ICV") armoured personnel carriers and associated equipment were seized by the [Hong Kong Customs and Excise Department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customs_and_Excise_Department_\(Hong_Kong\) "Customs and Excise Department (Hong Kong)") at the [Kwai Tsing Container Terminal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kwai_Tsing_Container_Terminals "Kwai Tsing Container Terminals") (formerly Kwai Chung Container Terminal).[^39] The vehicles and equipment were being shipped back to Singapore from Taiwan after a military exercise in Taiwan.[^40]<sup><span title="Pages: 56–57">: 56–57</span> </sup> The shipment was allegedly seized because [American President Lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_President_Lines "American President Lines") (APL), the private shipping company engaged by the Singapore military to handle the shipment, did not have the appropriate permits for the vehicles equipment,[^41] though it has been alleged that China manufactured the incident to protest against the Singaporean Army's use of Taiwan for training against the backdrop of worsening ties between both states then.[^42] The shipment was later moved to the Hong Kong Customs depot at Hong Kong [River Trade Terminal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/River_Trade_Terminal "River Trade Terminal") and kept indoors since 6 December 2016. This was Hong Kong's biggest seizure of such equipment in the past twenty years.[^43] [^44] On 24 January 2017, Hong Kong Customs announced that the military vehicles would be returned to Singapore.[^40]<sup><span title="Page: 56">: 56</span> </sup> Commissioner of the [Customs and Excise Department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customs_and_Excise_Department_\(Hong_Kong\) "Customs and Excise Department (Hong Kong)"), Roy Tang Yun-kwong, said that shipping company [American President Lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_President_Lines "American President Lines") would likely face criminal charges over the incident for suspected breach of the Hong Kong Law.[^45] In 2019, American President Lines and the captain of the involved ship was charged with violating the Import and Export (Strategic Commodities) Regulations, which they both pleaded not guilty to. In April 2019, they were found guilty,[^46] with American President Lines sentenced to 90,000 HKD in fines (10,000 HKD for each vehicle), and the captain sentenced to 9,000 HKD in fines (1,000 HKD for each vehicle) and 3 months of jail time (suspended for 1.5 years).[^47]

### Taiwan

Since 1975, Singapore has conducted regular military training exercises with the [Republic of China Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_Armed_Forces "Republic of China Armed Forces"), a longstanding defence arrangement that continues despite objections from Beijing.[^48] [^49] Ties between Singapore and Taiwan extend beyond the military sphere and include decades of close co-operation in trade, education and technology. In 1993, Singapore hosted the historic [Wang–Koo summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang%E2%80%93Koo_summit "Wang–Koo summit"), the first formal meeting between Chinese and Taiwanese officials since 1949, underscoring its role as a trusted intermediary in cross-strait relations.[^50]<sup><span title="Page: 78">: 78</span> </sup> Singapore also hosted the [First Ma–Xi meeting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Ma%E2%80%93Xi_meeting "First Ma–Xi meeting"), which marked the first encounter between the top political leaders of both sides of the [Taiwan Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_Strait "Taiwan Strait") since the conclusion of the [Chinese Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War "Chinese Civil War") in 1949. It was the first such meeting since [Chiang Kai-shek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek "Chiang Kai-shek") and [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") held talks in Chongqing during the [Double Tenth Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_Tenth_Agreement "Double Tenth Agreement") negotiations in August 1945.[^51]

Despite pressure from China,[^52] Singapore has maintained its unofficial ties with Taiwan as a matter of strategic autonomy and principled engagement. Singapore has consistently emphasised the importance of peace and stability in the [Taiwan Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_Strait "Taiwan Strait"), while avoiding entanglement in cross-strait disputes. On 4 November 2021, Defence Minister [Ng Eng Hen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ng_Eng_Hen "Ng Eng Hen") acknowledged Taiwan's political status as a highly sensitive issue for China, warning of the grave consequences of any military conflict and stating, "I would advise us to stay very far away from that." [^53] Nonetheless, Singapore's engagement with Taiwan continues to draw criticism from Beijing, such as in January 2024 when Singapore extended formal congratulations to President-elect [Lai Ching-te](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lai_Ching-te "Lai Ching-te"), prompting a diplomatic rebuke from China.[^54] Singapore officially opposes [Taiwan independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_independence_movement "Taiwan independence movement").[^55]

## Economics

The bilateral trade between China and Singapore developed rapidly in recent years and Singapore has maintained the first position among ASEAN countries in their trade with China.[^21] China's [transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reform_and_opening_up "Reform and opening up") into a [major economic power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Century "Chinese Century") in the 21st century has led to an increase of foreign investments in the [bamboo network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamboo_network "Bamboo network"), a network of [overseas Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_Chinese "Overseas Chinese") businesses operating in the markets of Southeast Asia that share common family and cultural ties.[^56] [^57]

In 1998, volume of trade was US$8.154 billion [^21] In 1999, the trade value has increased to US$8.56 billion.[^21] In 2000, the amount increased to US$10.821 billion.[^21] In 2009, the total trade volume was SGD 75.1 billion (US$58.4 billion).[^58]

Singapore is China's 9th largest trading partner,[^59] while China is Singapore's 3rd largest trading partner which consisted of 10.1 percent of Singapore's total external trade from the previous year.

China's exports to Singapore were textiles, clothing, agriculture produce, petrochemical, metals, electromechanical equipment, feed, coking coal, shipping, machinery supplier, communication equipment and electronic components.[^21]

Companies such as [Capitaland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitaland "Capitaland") and [Breadtalk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breadtalk "Breadtalk") have made substantial inroads into China's domestic economy.[^60] [^61] [^62]

Singaporean state-owned companies [Temasek Holdings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temasek_Holdings "Temasek Holdings") and [Singapore Airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore_Airlines "Singapore Airlines") attempted to invest in [China Eastern Airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Eastern_Airlines "China Eastern Airlines") in 2007, though the deal was not successful.[^63]

Singapore is a significant destination for Chinese outbound [foreign direct investment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_direct_investment "Foreign direct investment").[^8]<sup><span title="Page: 49">: 49</span> </sup> Singaporean investment in China became particularly important to Singapore during the 1990s, as Singapore sought to adjust its economic model.[^50]<sup><span title="Pages: 79–80">: 79–80</span></sup>

### Cooperation projects

[Suzhou Industrial Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suzhou_Industrial_Park "Suzhou Industrial Park") developed as a result of high-level intergovernmental cooperation between China and Singapore.[^50]<sup><span title="Page: 78">: 78</span> </sup> In the late 1980s, Singapore's export-driven economic model required adjustment for further growth and Singapore's government viewed investment in China as part of its policy adjustment.[^50]<sup><span title="Page: 79">: 79</span> </sup> Through its Regional Industrial Parks program, Singapore proposed to develop industrial manufacturing locations in other Asian countries, modeled after its own [Jurong Industrial Estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jurong "Jurong").[^50]<sup><span title="Page: 80">: 80</span> </sup> In 1993, Prime Minister of Singapore [Lee Kuan Yew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Kuan_Yew "Lee Kuan Yew") contacted Vice Premier [Zhu Rongji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhu_Rongji "Zhu Rongji") and proposed a government-to-government cooperation program to develop an industrial estate on an unbuilt site in [Suzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suzhou "Suzhou").[^50]<sup><span title="Page: 83">: 83</span> </sup> The next year, the two countries signed the Agreement on the Joint Development of Suzhou Industrial Park.[^50]<sup><span title="Page: 83">: 83</span></sup>

In 2007, China and Singapore began the Sino-Singapore Tianjin Eco-City project.[^50]<sup><span title="Page: 209">: 209</span></sup>

## Culture and education

A [Confucius Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius_Institute "Confucius Institute") in Singapore was established in 2005 as a collaboration with [Nanyang Technological University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanyang_Technological_University "Nanyang Technological University") (NTU), the successor to the former Chinese-language [Nanyang University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanyang_University "Nanyang University"). Its stated mission is to provide Singapore with a platform for learning the Chinese language and culture and to enhance communication with other Chinese communities.[^64] In November 2015, China opened the [China Cultural Centre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Cultural_Centre_\(Singapore\) "China Cultural Centre (Singapore)") in Singapore to facilitate exchanges in arts and culture.[^65] Current difficulties notwithstanding, Singapore's economically successful [dominant party system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominant_party_system "Dominant party system") has led its political system to be studied and cited as a potential model for China's government.[^66] [^67] Such a viewpoint has been institutionalised: Nanyang Technological University offers master's degree programs that are well-attended by Chinese public officials.[^68]

Singapore's attractiveness to Chinese students largely stems from its high-quality education system, safe urban environment and regional proximity. While some are drawn by perceived shared language and traditions, others explicitly cite Singapore's meritocratic environment as a key distinction from the mainland's [guanxi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanxi "Guanxi") -oriented norms.[^69] A 2016 study on Chinese students' integration showed generally positive interactions between them and the Singaporeans surveyed, although only about 26% of students were active in local events and activities.[^70] Singapore's Chinese-language *[Lianhe Zaobao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lianhe_Zaobao "Lianhe Zaobao")* has also long served as an outlet of Singapore's regional [soft power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_power "Soft power").[^71] While it is read by Chinese audiences and officials, its periodic censorship inside China, including blackouts in 2009 and 2019 for critical reporting on issues such as [internet controls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_censorship_in_China "Internet censorship in China") and the [2019–2020 Hong Kong protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019%E2%80%932020_Hong_Kong_protests "2019–2020 Hong Kong protests"), further highlights Singapore's independence in managing its media and public discourse.[^72] [^73]

### Immigration and people-to-people relations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Prime_Minister_Lee_Kuan_Yew_of_Singapore_Making_a_Toast_at_a_State_Dinner_Held_in_His_Honor%2C_1975.jpg/250px-Prime_Minister_Lee_Kuan_Yew_of_Singapore_Making_a_Toast_at_a_State_Dinner_Held_in_His_Honor%2C_1975.jpg)

Prime Minister Lee Kuan Yew consistently affirmed Singapore's distinct ethnic identity in both bilateral relations with China and domestic policy. He made clear that Singaporean Chinese are culturally, politically and socially different from the Chinese in China, emphasising that shared ancestry does not translate into shared allegiance or values.

Chinese people have immigrated to what is known as Singapore since [Zheng He's voyages in the 15th century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_treasure_voyages "Ming treasure voyages"); those early immigrants integrated into the host societies, and their descendants are now known as [Peranakans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peranakan "Peranakan").[^74]

While the majority of [Singaporeans are of ethnic Chinese descent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Singapore "Demographics of Singapore") at around 70 to 75%, this demographic fact is often misinterpreted by some in China, who assume that Singapore is culturally aligned and ideologically sympathetic to Chinese interests.[^75] [^76] [^77] In reality, the Chinese community in Singapore has diverged significantly from that in China, having been shaped by centuries of migration, multiculturalism, colonial legacy and independent nation-building.[^78] This misconception was evident in early bilateral exchanges, where Chinese officials referred to visiting Singaporeans as "kinsmen". While Prime Minister [Lee Kuan Yew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Kuan_Yew "Lee Kuan Yew") acknowledged a "special relationship", he firmly rejected the notion of ideological or emotional kinship, stating, "We are different like the [New Zealanders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealanders "New Zealanders") and the [Australians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australians "Australians") are different from the [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_people "British people")... I have no ideological empathy." [^16] His response underscored Singapore's distinct national identity and independent worldview, particularly in economic and political matters.[^79]

In 2012, some Chinese netizens began referring to Singapore as "Po County" (坡县), a label that many Singaporeans found deeply offensive.[^80] While a few Chinese commenters viewed it as a harmless or affectionate nickname, the implication that Singapore is merely a minor appendage of China was widely condemned.[^80] Many Singaporeans, regardless of ethnicity, saw the term as a patronising and dismissive attempt to diminish the country's sovereignty and independence.[^81]

The Singaporean Chinese today are overwhelmingly locally born and raised, with only a small minority comprising first-generation immigrants from China. They possess no allegiance to either the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC) or the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China") (Taiwan), and instead identify firmly with Singapore's national identity and multicultural society.[^82] Singapore's historical experience with racial riots has made social harmony a top national priority,[^83] and the government has invested decades of effort in cultivating a cohesive, inclusive identity that places Singapore first, above all ethnic and foreign affiliations.[^84]

Despite this, misalignment between Singapore's national identity and the attitudes of some newcomers from mainland China has resulted in tension. Incidents such as the curry dispute,[^85] the [2012 Singapore bus drivers' strike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Singapore_bus_drivers%27_strike "2012 Singapore bus drivers' strike"),[^86] and an infamous fatal speeding accident caused by a wealthy Chinese national,[^87] have stoked public frustration. These cases are viewed by many locals as emblematic of certain Chinese immigrants' failure to respect or integrate into Singapore's multicultural society.[^88]

Singaporeans' firm sense of national identity is reflected in how they respond to perceived foreign encroachments, even in symbolic domains like sport. [Olympic medals won by China-born athletes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore_at_the_Olympics "Singapore at the Olympics") who had only recently obtained [Singaporean citizenship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singaporean_citizenship "Singaporean citizenship") were not widely celebrated by the local population compared to native-born winners, who saw them as " [mercenaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercenaries "Mercenaries") " rather than true Singaporean champions.[^89] [^90] This reflects the fact that Singapore's national identity is grounded in historical experience and a state-building process developed independently of China. Efforts to assert cultural affinity or political alignment based solely on ethnic similarity are considered inaccurate and inappropriate by Singaporeans, including by its leadership.[^79] [^90]

## Security and military relations

In 2002, it was revealed that China had officially proposed relocating Singapore's military training operations from Taiwan to [Hainan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hainan "Hainan"). However, Singapore declined the offer, choosing to maintain its long-standing defence arrangements with Taiwan.[^26] [^27] In 2008, both countries signed the Agreement on Defense Exchanges and Security Cooperation, facilitating limited interactions such as official visits, port calls and professional exchanges. In 2009, *Exercise Cooperation*, the first bilateral army exercise, was held in [Guilin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guilin "Guilin"), China, followed by a joint [counterterrorism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterterrorism "Counterterrorism") exercise in 2010 and 2014.[^91] [^40]<sup><span title="Page: 159">: 159</span> </sup> <sup><span title="Page: 164">: 164</span></sup>

In 2019, *Exercise Cooperation* was held in Singapore and involved the [Singapore Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore_Army "Singapore Army") 's 3rd Singapore Division and 1st Commando Battalion alongside the [People's Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") ’s 74th Army Group.[^91] An enhanced Agreement on Defence Exchanges and Security Cooperation was signed in 2019.[^92] Military activities were paused during the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic"), and resumed in 2022 with agreements on defence education and exchanges.[^92]

### Cyber

In July 2025, [K. Shanmugam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K._Shanmugam "K. Shanmugam"), Singapore's [Coordinating Minister for National Security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinating_Minister_for_National_Security_\(Singapore\) "Coordinating Minister for National Security (Singapore)"), stated that the country's critical infrastructure was attacked by [UNC3886](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNC3886 "UNC3886"), a [cyber-espionage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyberwarfare_and_China "Cyberwarfare and China") group previously linked to the Chinese government.[^93] Singapore's [Cyber Security Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyber_Security_Agency "Cyber Security Agency") was deployed in the response to the attacks.[^94]

On 7 June 2026, Singapore ordered YouTube, Facebook and X to block 14 posts that targeted the Indian community in Singapore and questioned the country's multiracial policies. Authorities said the content had likely originated from Chinese online platforms before spreading across social media, and accused it of promoting anti-Indian sentiment and racial division. The government described the posts as an attempt to undermine social harmony.[^95] [^96]

## Public opinion

In July 2020, Singaporean journalist Maria Siow reported that many mainland Chinese, echoing official narratives, tend to view Singapore as a model for China.[^97] This perception stems from Singapore's status as a [one-party dominant state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-party_dominant_state "One-party dominant state"), which is often characterised by political stability, limited dissent and [low corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corruption_in_Singapore "Corruption in Singapore"). Singapore is also credited with having contributed to China's development during the period of [reform and opening up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reform_and_opening_up "Reform and opening up"), and [Lee Kuan Yew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Kuan_Yew "Lee Kuan Yew") is widely regarded by Chinese observers as an exemplary leader.[^98]

Singapore enjoys generally favourable views among Chinese respondents, as shown by multiple surveys conducted over recent years. A November 2020 study by scholars from the [Lee Kuan Yew School of Public Policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Kuan_Yew_School_of_Public_Policy "Lee Kuan Yew School of Public Policy") and the [University of British Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia "University of British Columbia") found that 69% of Chinese participants held positive opinions of Singapore.[^99] Similarly, a March 2022 survey funded by the [European Regional Development Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Regional_Development_Fund "European Regional Development Fund") and conducted by the Central European Institute of Asian Studies reported that most mainland Chinese respondents viewed Singapore positively and regarded Singaporeans as friendly to Chinese travellers.[^100] Furthermore, according to a 2022 survey by [Renmin University of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renmin_University_of_China "Renmin University of China") and the Global Times Research Center on Chinese views of [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN"), Singapore was identified as the most appealing ASEAN member to Chinese respondents.[^101] According to a 2026 [feeling thermometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeling_thermometer "Feeling thermometer") poll by the [Carter Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carter_Center "Carter Center") and [Emory University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emory_University "Emory University"), Chinese opinion of Singapore was on average 50 out of 100.[^102]

From the Singaporean perspective, favourable views of China remain strong but nuanced. Polling data from [Pew Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pew_Research_Center "Pew Research Center") in 2022 and 2024 showed that 67% of Singaporeans expressed a positive view of China.[^103] [^104] A separate 2022 survey by the Central European Institute of Asian Studies similarly showed most Singaporean respondents holding positive views of China.[^105] According to [Jamestown Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamestown_Foundation "Jamestown Foundation") analyst Russell Hsiao, support for China in Singapore is generationally divided, with older Chinese Singaporeans generally expressing stronger affinity, while younger generations tend to feel less connected to China.[^106]
