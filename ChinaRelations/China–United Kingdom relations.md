**Chinese–United Kingdom relations** ([simplified Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters "Simplified Chinese characters"): 中英关系; [traditional Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters "Traditional Chinese characters"): 中英關係; [pinyin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin "Pinyin"): *Zhōng-Yīng guānxì*) are the [interstate relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_relations "International relations") between China (with its various governments through history) and the United Kingdom.

In the 19th century, the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") established several colonies in China, most prominently [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Hong_Kong "British Hong Kong"), which it gained after British victory in the [First Opium War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Opium_War "First Opium War"). Relations between the two nations have gone through ups and downs over the course of the late 20th and early 21st centuries. The People's Republic of China and the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland established diplomatic relations on 17 June 1954. The UK and China were on opposing sides during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), and relations were strained over the issue of [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong"). In 1984, both sides signed the [Sino-British Joint Declaration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-British_Joint_Declaration "Sino-British Joint Declaration"), which eventually led to the [handover of Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handover_of_Hong_Kong "Handover of Hong Kong") to China in 1997.

Following the conclusion of the Cold War and the handover of Hong Kong, a period known as the "Golden Era" of Sino-British relations began with multiple high-level state visits and bilateral trade and military agreements. This roughly 20-year period came to an abrupt end during the [2019–2020 Hong Kong protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019%E2%80%932020_Hong_Kong_protests "2019–2020 Hong Kong protests") and the imposition of the [Hong Kong national security law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Hong_Kong_national_security_law "2020 Hong Kong national security law"). In the years following relations have deteriorated significantly over various security and [human rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights_in_China "Human rights in China") concerns. The relationship started improving during the [premiership of Keir Starmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premiership_of_Keir_Starmer "Premiership of Keir Starmer") starting from 2024, particularly following his [visit to China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_visit_by_Keir_Starmer_to_China "2026 visit by Keir Starmer to China") in 2026. China is the UK's fifth-largest trading partner as of 2025.

### England and the Ming Dynasty

In the 1620s, [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language") ships began arriving at [Macau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macau "Macau"), a port city leased by China to [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portugal "Portugal"). During this period, an English merchant vessel named *The Unicorn* sank near Macau. The Portuguese salvaged several cannons, specifically sakers, from the wreck and sold them to the Chinese around 1620. These cannons were then replicated by the Chinese as the *[Hongyipao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hongyipao "Hongyipao")*, marking an early instance of military technology exchange.

On 27 June 1637, a fleet of four heavily armed English ships commanded by [Captain John Weddell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Weddell "John Weddell") reached Macau in an effort to establish trade relations with China. This venture was not sanctioned by the [East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_India_Company "East India Company") but was instead organized by a private consortium led by [Sir William Courten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Courten "William Courten"), with [King Charles I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_I_of_England "Charles I of England") personally investing £10,000. The Portuguese authorities in Macau, bound by their agreements with the [Ming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") court, opposed the English expedition. This opposition, coupled with the English presence, quickly provoked the Ming authorities.

Later that summer, the English force captured one of the Bogue forts at the mouth of the [Pearl River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearl_River "Pearl River") and engaged in several weeks of intermittent skirmishes and smuggling operations. The situation deteriorated further, leading the English to rely on Portuguese mediation for the release of three hostages. Eventually, the expedition withdrew from the Pearl River on 27 December 1637. The fate of the fleet afterward remained uncertain.[^1] [^2] [^3]

### Great Britain and the Qing

The relationship between [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Britain "Great Britain") and the Qing regime evolved over several centuries, shaped by diplomacy, trade, military conflict, and the broader dynamics of empire.

Early contact included the 1685 visit of [Michael Shen Fu-Tsung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Shen_Fu-Tsung "Michael Shen Fu-Tsung"), a Chinese Jesuit, to Britain, where he met [King James II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_II_of_England "James II of England").[^4] Trade officially began in 1699 when the East India Company was permitted to conduct business in [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou "Guangzhou") ([Canton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantonese "Cantonese")), marking the start of sustained commercial relations.[^5]

In 1784, the [Lady Hughes Affair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lady_Hughes_Affair "Lady Hughes Affair"), where a British gunner's salute led to unintended deaths, heightened tensions. This foreshadowed the cultural and legal misunderstandings that would plague future interactions. High-level diplomatic efforts followed, such as the [Macartney Embassy of 1793](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macartney_Embassy "Macartney Embassy") and the [Amherst Embassy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amherst_Embassy "Amherst Embassy") of 1816, both of which failed to establish equal diplomatic footing with the Qing court.

By the 1820s and 1830s, British merchants had turned [Lintin Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nei_Lingding_Island "Nei Lingding Island") into a hub for the [opium trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium "Opium").[^6] [^7] This illicit commerce contributed directly to the [First Opium War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Opium_War "First Opium War") (1839–42). Prior to the war, the East India Company's monopoly on Chinese trade was abolished (1833–35), prompting efforts by successive British governments to maintain peace. However, figures like [Lord Napier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lord_Napier "Lord Napier") took a more provocative stance, pushing for deeper market access, despite the Foreign Office under [Lord Palmerston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_John_Temple,_3rd_Viscount_Palmerston "Henry John Temple, 3rd Viscount Palmerston") favoring a less confrontational approach [^8]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/View_of_Hong_Kong%2C_and_the_%27River_Bird%27_steamer.jpg/250px-View_of_Hong_Kong%2C_and_the_%27River_Bird%27_steamer.jpg)

British Hong Kong, c. 1855

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Second_Opium_War_British_Beijing_1860.jpg/330px-Second_Opium_War_British_Beijing_1860.jpg)

British taking Beijing during the Second Opium War in 1860

The war culminated in a decisive British victory. British motivations were framed by Palmerston's biographer as a confrontation between a dynamic, modern trading nation and a stagnant [autocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocracy "Autocracy").[^9] However, critics such as the [Chartists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chartism "Chartism") and young [William Ewart Gladstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone "William Ewart Gladstone") condemned the war as morally reprehensible, pointing to the devastation caused by opium addiction.[^10] [^11] [^12]

A temporary peace was brokered with the [Convention of Chuenpi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_of_Chuenpi "Convention of Chuenpi") in 1841, though it was never ratified. The conflict formally ended with the [Treaty of Nanking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Nanking "Treaty of Nanking") in 1842, which ceded [Hong Kong Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong_Island "Hong Kong Island") to [Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") and opened five treaty ports to international trade.[^13] The [Treaty of the Bogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_the_Bogue "Treaty of the Bogue") followed in 1843, granting Britain most-favoured-nation status and legal extraterritoriality.

Throughout the mid-19th century, British influence in China expanded. From 1845 to 1863, the British Concession in [Shanghai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai "Shanghai") was established, later becoming part of the [Shanghai International Settlement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_International_Settlement "Shanghai International Settlement"). The [Second Opium War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Opium_War "Second Opium War") (1856–60) further entrenched British power. Following military successes, including the sack of the [Old Summer Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Summer_Palace "Old Summer Palace") in 1860, the [Convention of Peking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_of_Peking "Convention of Peking") granted Britain control of the [Kowloon Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kowloon_Peninsula "Kowloon Peninsula") and led to the establishment of a British [legation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legation "Legation") in Beijing by 1861.

[British consulates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_diplomatic_missions_of_the_United_Kingdom "List of diplomatic missions of the United Kingdom") soon appeared across Chinese territory, including in [Wuhan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wuhan "Wuhan"), [Kaohsiung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaohsiung "Kaohsiung"), [Taipei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taipei "Taipei"), Shanghai, and [Xiamen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiamen "Xiamen"). Meanwhile, domestic unrest occasionally erupted, such as the [1868 Yangzhou riot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yangzhou_riot "Yangzhou riot") targeting [Christian missionaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_mission "Christian mission"). Despite such challenges, skilled diplomats like [Li Hongzhang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_Hongzhang "Li Hongzhang") (1823–1901) continued efforts to mediate Qing engagement with Western powers.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/LiHungChang.jpg/250px-LiHungChang.jpg)

Skilled diplomat Li Hongzhang negotiated between the West and the Qing Dynasty.

Technological integration followed. From 1870 to 1900, Britain developed and operated a [telegraph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telegraphy "Telegraphy") network linking [London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London "London") to key Chinese ports.[^14] Diplomatic ties were formalized further when China opened a legation in London in 1877, headed by [Guo Songtao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guo_Songtao "Guo Songtao"). Britain also advised on the Ili Crisis (1877–81), reflecting its growing influence in Qing foreign affairs.

The late 19th century saw geopolitical adjustments. After Britain's annexation of [Burma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar "Myanmar") in 1886, the Burma Convention acknowledged British occupation while maintaining China's symbolic suzerainty through continued tribute payments.[^15] [^16] Conflict between Britain and [Tibetan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetans "Tibetans") forces in [Sikkim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sikkim "Sikkim") led to the [Treaty of Calcutta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_of_Calcutta "Convention of Calcutta") (1890), by which China recognized British control over northern Sikkim. A further agreement in 1890 fixed the border between Sikkim and [Tibet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibet "Tibet").[^17]

Britain's global influence was also felt in individual incidents, such as the 1896 detention of revolutionary [Sun Yat-sen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen "Sun Yat-sen") in the Chinese Legation in London. British public pressure led to his release, illustrating the political significance of [diaspora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diaspora "Diaspora") activism.

The 1898 [Convention for the Extension of Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_for_the_Extension_of_Hong_Kong_Territory "Convention for the Extension of Hong Kong Territory") leased the [New Territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Territories "New Territories") to Britain for 99 years, and that same year, Britain secured a lease on [Weihai Harbour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weihai "Weihai") in [Shandong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shandong "Shandong"). An odd footnote occurred in December 1898, when the arrival of four young English women in Shanghai sparked public commentary and minor diplomatic tensions.[^18] [^19] [^20] [^21]

The turn of the century brought renewed conflict during the [Boxer Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxer_Rebellion "Boxer Rebellion") (1900–1901), a violent anti-foreigner uprising suppressed by an allied force led by Britain and [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan"). The resulting [Boxer Protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxer_Protocol "Boxer Protocol") imposed heavy penalties on the Qing regime. Britain continued to assert influence over Tibetan affairs, most notably through the 1906 Anglo-Chinese Treaty on Tibet, which Britain interpreted as limiting China to nominal [suzerainty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suzerainty "Suzerainty").

By 1909, British consulates in [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") were closed following Japan's assertion of sovereignty, marking a shift in [East Asian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asia "East Asia") power dynamics. This period closed with Britain entrenched as a dominant force in [China's foreign relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_China "Foreign relations of China"), trade, and territorial concessions.

### Britain and the Republic of China (1912–1950)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Sunyatsen1.jpg/250px-Sunyatsen1.jpg)

British diplomats rescued Sun Yat-sen from their Qing counterparts in 1896. Sun later founded the Republic of China.

Between 1912 and 1950, relations between Britain and the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)") (ROC) evolved significantly, marked by shifting alliances, conflict, diplomacy, and eventual disengagement.

Although Sun Yat-sen, who later became the founding father of the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)"), was rescued by British diplomats from Qing agents in 1896, early British involvement with Chinese political affairs was often shaped by colonial interests and imperial competition.

During [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), in 1916, Britain recruited tens of thousands of Chinese labourers into the [Chinese Labour Corps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Labour_Corps "Chinese Labour Corps") to support the war effort on the [Western Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Front_\(World_War_I\) "Western Front (World War I)"). On 14 August 1917, China officially joined the [Allies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I "Allies of World War I"), aligning itself with Britain in opposition to the [Central Powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Powers "Central Powers").

However, tensions emerged after the war. On 4 May 1919, the [May Fourth Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/May_Fourth_Movement "May Fourth Movement") erupted in response to the Chinese government's failure to secure benefits from the postwar settlement. Britain had supported its treaty ally Japan over the contentious [Shandong Problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shandong_Problem "Shandong Problem"), contributing to a broader Chinese disillusionment with Western democracies and a turn toward the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") for ideological and political inspiration.

At the [Washington Naval Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington_Naval_Conference "Washington Naval Conference") (November 1921–February 1922), Britain joined other powers in signing the [Nine-Power Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nine-Power_Treaty "Nine-Power Treaty"), which recognised Chinese sovereignty. As part of the agreements, Japan returned control of [Shandong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shandong "Shandong") province to China, resolving the Shandong Problem [^22]

In the years that followed (1922–1929), Britain, the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), and Japan backed various [Chinese warlords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warlord "Warlord"), often working against the revolutionary [Nationalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalism "Nationalism") government in [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou "Guangzhou") ([Canton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantonese "Cantonese")). Britain and the U.S. supported [Chen Jiongming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chen_Jiongming "Chen Jiongming") 's rebellion against the Nationalists, exacerbating tensions. These foreign interventions, and domestic instability, culminated in the [Northern Expedition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Expedition "Northern Expedition") (1926–1927), which eventually brought most of China under [Chiang Kai-shek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek "Chiang Kai-shek") ’s control.[^23]

On 30 May 1925, the killing of nine Chinese protesters by the British-led [Shanghai Municipal Police](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Municipal_Police "Shanghai Municipal Police") triggered the [30 May Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/May_Thirtieth_Movement "May Thirtieth Movement"), a nationwide anti-British campaign. This incident highlighted growing Chinese resentment toward foreign imperialism.

Further unrest in [Hankou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hankou "Hankou") ([Wuhan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wuhan "Wuhan")) led to the Chen–O’Malley Agreement of 19 February 1927, under which Britain agreed to hand over its concession in Hankou to the Chinese authorities.

Between 1929 and 1931, China pursued full sovereignty by regaining control over its tariff rates, previously fixed at just 5% by foreign powers, and seeking to abolish extraterritorial privileges enjoyed by Britain and other nations in treaty ports like Shanghai. These goals were largely achieved by 1931.[^24]

In 1930, Britain returned Weihai Harbour to Chinese control. Further diplomatic progress was marked by Britain's decision, on 17 May 1935, to elevate its Legation in Beijing to an Embassy; addressing longstanding Chinese complaints about the perceived disrespect of a lower diplomatic rank.[^25]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Oppose_the_British_sign_-_Beijing_%28May_1940%29.jpg/250px-Oppose_the_British_sign_-_Beijing_%28May_1940%29.jpg)

A sign displayed in Japanese-occupied Beijing in May 1940

Following the Chinese capital's move to [Nanjing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanjing "Nanjing"), the British Embassy also relocated there in 1936–1937. As Japan launched its invasion of China in 1937, British public opinion and government sympathy tilted in China's favour. Nonetheless, with Britain focused on defending its own empire, especially [Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore "Singapore"), direct support was limited. Britain did assist by training Chinese troops in India and providing airbases for American supply missions to China [^26]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/INF3-331_Unity_of_Strength_Chiang-Kai-Shek_and_Winston_Churchill_heads%2C_with_Nationalist_China_flag_and_Union_Jack.jpg/250px-INF3-331_Unity_of_Strength_Chiang-Kai-Shek_and_Winston_Churchill_heads%2C_with_Nationalist_China_flag_and_Union_Jack.jpg)

Chiang-Kai-Shek and Winston Churchill, as allies against Japan 1941–1945.

During [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") (1941–1945), Britain and China became official allies against Japan. Chinese troops trained in [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") fought alongside British forces in the [Burma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myanmar "Myanmar") campaign. Close coordination continued throughout the war, symbolised by the wartime cooperation between [Chiang Kai-shek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek "Chiang Kai-shek") and [Winston Churchill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winston_Churchill "Winston Churchill"). However, postwar diplomacy shifted dramatically. On 6 January 1950, [His Majesty's Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/His_Majesty%27s_Government_\(term\) "His Majesty's Government (term)") withdrew its recognition from the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)"), now based in Taiwan, following the [Communist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communism "Communism") victory on the mainland. Britain closed its Embassy in Nanjing but maintained a Consulate in [Tamsui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamsui_District "Tamsui District"), nominally for liaison with the [Taiwan Provincial Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_Provincial_Government "Taiwan Provincial Government").

### Between the UK and the People's Republic of China (1949–present)

#### 1949–1999

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Chinese_Warships_Visit_Portsmouth_MOD_45158368.jpg/250px-Chinese_Warships_Visit_Portsmouth_MOD_45158368.jpg)

Union Flag flies from the PLAN ship Changbai Shan during a visit to Portsmouth in 2015

Between 1949 and the present, the relationship between the United Kingdom and the People's Republic of China (PRC) has evolved through a series of pragmatic decisions, diplomatic tensions, and significant historical moments.

During World War II, Britain was aligned with the [anti-Communist Nationalist Chinese government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-communism "Anti-communism"). Following the war, British priorities centred on preserving stability in China to protect over £300 million in investments; far exceeding U.S. interests in the region. While Britain agreed not to interfere in Chinese affairs as per the [1945 Moscow Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow_Conference_\(1945\) "Moscow Conference (1945)"), it remained sympathetic to the [Nationalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalist_government "Nationalist government"), who appeared dominant in the [Chinese Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War "Chinese Civil War") until 1947.[^27]

However, by August 1948, the tide had turned. With the Communists gaining ground, the British government began to prepare for their potential victory. It maintained consular operations in Communist-controlled areas and declined Nationalist appeals for British assistance in defending Shanghai. By December, [Whitehall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitehall "Whitehall") concluded that although nationalisation of British assets was likely, long-term economic engagement with a stable, industrialising China could prove beneficial. Safeguarding [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong") remained paramount, and the UK bolstered its garrison there in 1949, even as the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) offered reassurances on non-interference.

On 20 April 1949, the [People's Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") attacked [HMS *Amethyst* (F116)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HMS_Amethyst_\(F116\) "HMS Amethyst (F116)") travelling to the British Embassy in Nanjing in the [Amethyst incident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amethyst_incident "Amethyst incident"). The CCP do not recognise the [unequal treaties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unequal_treaties "Unequal treaties") and protest the ship's right to sail on the [Yangtze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yangtze "Yangtze").[^28] [^29]

On 1 October 1949, the PRC government announced its willingness to establish diplomatic relations with any nation that severed ties with the Nationalists. After consultation with [Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations") and European partners, Britain formally recognised the PRC in January 1950.[^27]

Following recognition on 6 January 1950, the UK posted a chargé d'affaires in Beijing, expecting swift ambassadorial exchange. However, the PRC insisted on conditions concerning the Chinese seat at the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") and the handling of Nationalist-held foreign assets.

Meanwhile, British commercial interests began adapting to the new reality. In 1950, a consortium of British businesses formed the Group of 48 (now the [China-Britain Business Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Britain_Business_Council "China–Britain Business Council")) to facilitate trade with the PRC.[^25] [^30] This effort was further [institutionalised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institutionalisation "Institutionalisation") with the formation of the Sino-British Trade Committee in 1954.

Military interactions between the two countries also occurred indirectly during the [Korean War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_War "Korean War"). [British Commonwealth Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Commonwealth_Forces_Korea "British Commonwealth Forces Korea") engaged in several key battles against Chinese forces, including the defence of Hill 282 at [Pakchon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakchon_County "Pakchon County") in 1950, clashes at the [Imjin River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imjin_River "Imjin River") in 1951, and successful engagements at [Kapyong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Kapyong "Battle of Kapyong"), Maryang San, and [Yong Dong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dong_Yong "Dong Yong") in 1953.

In a diplomatic breakthrough, a [British Labour Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labour_Party_\(UK\) "Labour Party (UK)") delegation led by [Clement Attlee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clement_Attlee "Clement Attlee") visited China in 1954 at the invitation of [PRC Premier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_China "Premier of China") [Zhou Enlai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Enlai "Zhou Enlai").[^31] Attlee became the first high-ranking western politician to meet [CCP Chairman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party") [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong").[^32] That same year, the [Geneva Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1954_Geneva_Conference "1954 Geneva Conference") paved the way for mutual diplomatic presence: the PRC agreed to post a chargé d’affaires in London, reopen the British office in Shanghai, and issue [exit visas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Travel_visa "Travel visa") for British nationals detained since 1951.[^33] The PRC and the UK established diplomatic relations on 17 June 1954.[^34] In 1961, the UK began to vote in the General Assembly for [PRC membership of the United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_and_the_United_Nations "China and the United Nations"). It had abstained on votes since 1950.[^35]

During the [Suez Crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Crisis "Suez Crisis") in 1956, China condemned the UK and [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93France_relations "China–France relations") and made strong statements in [support of Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Egypt_relations "China–Egypt relations").[^36]<sup><span title="Page: xxxvii">: xxxvii</span></sup>

Bilateral relations soured during China's [Cultural Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_Revolution "Cultural Revolution"). In June 1967, [Red Guards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Guards "Red Guards") [attacked British diplomats in Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arson_attacks_on_the_British_Charg%C3%A9_d%27affaires_Office "Arson attacks on the British Chargé d'affaires Office"), and PRC authorities offered no condemnation.[^37]

[Riots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1967_Hong_Kong_riots "1967 Hong Kong riots") broke out in Hong Kong in June 1967.[^38] The commander of the [Guangzhou Military Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou_Military_Region "Guangzhou Military Region"), [Huang Yongsheng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huang_Yongsheng "Huang Yongsheng"), secretly suggested invading Hong Kong, but his plan was vetoed by PRC Premier Zhou Enlai.[^39] That same month, unrest spread to Hong Kong, with PRC military commanders even contemplating an invasion; though Zhou Enlai vetoed the idea.[^40] In July, Chinese troops fatally shot five Hong Kong police officers.

Hostilities escalated on 23 August 1967, when Red Guards stormed the British Office of the Chargé d'Affaires (predecessor body of British embassy) in Beijing, injuring chargé d'affaires [Sir Donald Hopson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Hopson "Donald Hopson") and others, including [Sir Percy Cradock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percy_Cradock "Percy Cradock"). The attack was a reprisal for British arrests of CCP agents in Hong Kong. Days later, on 29 August, armed Chinese diplomats clashed with British police in London.[^41]

A thaw began in March 1972, when the PRC extended full diplomatic recognition to the UK, allowing for ambassadorial exchange. The UK, in turn, acknowledged the PRC's position on Taiwan.[^42]

In 1982, during negotiations over Hong Kong's future, Chinese [paramount leader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paramount_leader "Paramount leader") [Deng Xiaoping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping "Deng Xiaoping") bluntly told [Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher "Margaret Thatcher") that China could simply take the territory by force; later confirmed as a genuine consideration.[^39] These talks culminated in the [1984 Sino-British Joint Declaration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-British_Joint_Declaration "Sino-British Joint Declaration").[^43]

[Queen Elizabeth II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elizabeth_II "Elizabeth II") made [a historic state visit to China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_visit_by_Elizabeth_II_to_China "State visit by Elizabeth II to China") in October 1986, becoming the first reigning British monarch to do so.[^44] The most symbolic moment in the bilateral relationship came on 30 June–1 July 1997, when Hong Kong was officially handed over from the United Kingdom to the People's Republic of China, marking the end of more than 150 years of British rule. Following the conclusion of the Cold War and the handover of Hong Kong, a period known as the "Golden Era" of Sino-British relations began with multiple high-level state visits and bilateral trade and military agreements.[^45] [^46]

#### 2000–2019

In the 2000s, significant developments shaped China–UK relations. On 29 October 2008, the United Kingdom formally recognised Tibet as an integral part of the People's Republic of China, marking a shift from its earlier position, which had only acknowledged Chinese suzerainty over the region.[^47]

In November 2005, China and the UK signed a series of bilateral agreements, including announcing an initiative to jointly create "the world's first carbon neutral eco-city." [^48]<sup><span title="Page: 161">: 161</span> </sup> The contemplated development, [Dongtan Eco-City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dongtan,_Shanghai "Dongtan, Shanghai"), was not ultimately completed.[^49]<sup><span title="Pages: 163–164">: 163–164</span> </sup> It later influenced other approaches to [Chinese eco-cities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eco-cities_in_China "Eco-cities in China").[^49]<sup><span title="Pages: 163–164">: 163–164</span></sup>

Further strengthening bilateral ties, [General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") and [President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_China "President of China") Xi Jinping and [Peng Liyuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peng_Liyuan "Peng Liyuan") paid [a state visit to the UK](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_visit_by_Xi_Jinping_to_the_United_Kingdom "State visit by Xi Jinping to the United Kingdom") from 20 to 23 October 2015. Their itinerary included stops in London and [Manchester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchester "Manchester"), with engagements involving [Queen Elizabeth II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elizabeth_II "Elizabeth II") and then-Prime Minister [David Cameron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Cameron "David Cameron"). The visit culminated in the signing of trade deals valued at over £30 billion, symbolising deepening economic cooperation between the two nations.[^50] [^51] [^52]

This spirit of engagement continued under [Prime Minister Theresa May](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theresa_May "Theresa May"), who travelled to China in February 2018 for a three-day trade mission. During the visit, she met with Xi Jinping, affirming the continuation of what was described as the "Golden Era" in UK–China relations.[^53]

British Prime Minister Keir Starmer with Chinese President Xi Jinping at a G20 summit in Rio de Janeiro, November 2024.

Both countries share common membership of the [G20](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G20 "G20"), the [UNSC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council") [P5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permanent_members_of_the_United_Nations_Security_Council "Permanent members of the United Nations Security Council"), the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), and the [World Trade Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Trade_Organization "World Trade Organization"). Bilaterally the two countries have a Double Taxation Agreement,[^54] an Investment Agreement,[^55] and the [Sino-British Joint Declaration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-British_Joint_Declaration "Sino-British Joint Declaration").

#### 2020s

Throughout the early 2020s, relations between China and the United Kingdom became increasingly strained, marked by disputes over human rights, national security, and espionage.[^56]

Tensions rose sharply in 2020 when the UK openly opposed China's imposition of the [Hong Kong national security law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Hong_Kong_national_security_law "2020 Hong Kong national security law"). [Lord Patten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chris_Patten "Chris Patten"), the last British governor of Hong Kong, described the move as a breach of the " [one country, two systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_country,_two_systems "One country, two systems") " framework and a violation of the Sino-British Joint Declaration.[^57] [^58] [^59] [Prime Minister Boris Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boris_Johnson "Boris Johnson") echoed this sentiment in Parliament, calling the law a "clear and serious breach" of the joint declaration. In response, the UK government announced a pathway to full British citizenship for around three million Hong Kong residents holding [British National (Overseas) status.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_National_\(Overseas\)_passport "British National (Overseas) passport")[^60] That same year, the UK suspended its extradition treaty with China, citing concerns over the treatment of the [Uyghur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghurs "Uyghurs") minority in Xinjiang.[^61]

Also in 2020, citing security concerns, the UK government banned the use of [Huawei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huawei "Huawei") 's equipment in its 5G infrastructure.[^62] [^63] The following year, the UK implemented a visa scheme for [Hongkongers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hongkongers "Hongkongers") affected by the national security law, resulting in over 200,000 Hong Kong residents relocating to Britain.[^64]

In April 2021, a cross-party group of MPs, led by [Sir Iain Duncan Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iain_Duncan_Smith "Iain Duncan Smith"), passed a parliamentary motion declaring China's mass detention of [Uyghur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution_of_Uyghurs_in_China "Persecution of Uyghurs in China") Muslims in Xinjiang as [genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genocide "Genocide"). This made the UK the fourth country globally to make such a declaration. In retaliation, China's embassy in London condemned the accusation as “the most preposterous lie of the century” and accused the UK of interfering in its internal affairs.[^65]

UK-China relations were further tested in October 2022, when Chinese consulate officials in Manchester allegedly dragged a pro-democracy protester onto consulate grounds and assaulted him.[^66] Six Chinese diplomats, including the [consul-general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consul_\(representative\) "Consul (representative)"), were subsequently recalled by Beijing.[^67]

After becoming prime minister in July 2024, [Keir Starmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keir_Starmer "Keir Starmer") initially signalled a tougher stance toward China, particularly regarding human rights abuses and [China's support for Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_and_the_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "China and the Russian invasion of Ukraine") during its invasion of Ukraine.[^68] However, diplomatic efforts to restore dialogue continued. In November 2024, Starmer met Chinese leader Xi Jinping at the [G20 summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_G20_Rio_de_Janeiro_summit "2024 G20 Rio de Janeiro summit") in an attempt to reset relations, balancing economic cooperation with national security concerns. The meeting was marred by an incident in which British journalists were forcibly removed by Chinese officials as Starmer raised human rights issues.[^69] [^70]

British Prime Minister Keir Starmer in Beijing, China, 29 January 2026

In January 2025, UK Chancellor [Rachel Reeves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rachel_Reeves "Rachel Reeves") visited China in an effort to stabilise economic ties and normalise relations.[^71] Yet friction persisted. In April 2025, the UK government introduced emergency legislation to prevent the closure of [British Steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Steel_\(2016%E2%80%93present\) "British Steel (2016–present)") ’s [Scunthorpe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scunthorpe "Scunthorpe") plant by its Chinese owner, [Jingye Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jingye_Group "Jingye Group"), citing national security reasons.[^72] [^73] In a rare Saturday sitting, Parliament passed the law allowing the government to take control of the site, with Prime Minister Starmer framing the move as essential for national and economic security.[^74] [^75] In May 2025, the [National Health Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Health_Service "National Health Service") launched an investigation into breaches of two NHS hospitals targeted by Chinese state-linked hackers.[^76] In September 2025, the [Eastern Theater Command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Theater_Command "Eastern Theater Command") of the PLA accused Britain of "trouble-making and provocation" when it and the U.S. jointly sailed warships through the [Taiwan Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_Strait "Taiwan Strait").[^77]

In October 2025 the [Director of Public Prosecutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Director_of_Public_Prosecutions_\(England_and_Wales\) "Director of Public Prosecutions (England and Wales)") controversially dropped charges under the [Official Secrets Act 1911](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Official_Secrets_Act_1911 "Official Secrets Act 1911") against Christopher Cash and Christopher Berry. Cash had been a Parliamentary researcher for [Alicia Kearns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alicia_Kearns "Alicia Kearns") [MP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_of_parliament#United_Kingdom "Member of parliament"). He had also been director of the [Conservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_Party_\(UK\) "Conservative Party (UK)") MP's [China Research Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Research_Group "China Research Group").[^78] In December 2025, Foreign Secretary [Yvette Cooper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvette_Cooper "Yvette Cooper") identified China as a national security threat due to its [cyber-attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyberwarfare_and_China "Cyberwarfare and China"), [espionage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_intelligence_activity_abroad "Chinese intelligence activity abroad"), and its [support for Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_and_the_Russo-Ukrainian_war "China and the Russo-Ukrainian war") "in its war against Ukraine".[^79]

In January 2026, Starmer [visited China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_visit_by_Keir_Starmer_to_China "2026 visit by Keir Starmer to China"), becoming the first British prime minister to visit China since 2018.[^80] Starmer's trip primarily focused on broadening trade ties, bringing a delegation heavy on [British banking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banking_in_the_United_Kingdom "Banking in the United Kingdom") executives, including [HSBC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HSBC "HSBC"), [Barclays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barclays "Barclays"), and [Standard Chartered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Chartered "Standard Chartered"), and cultural emissaries representing the arts and sports, as well as manufacturers, including [Airbus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airbus "Airbus"), [AstraZeneca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AstraZeneca "AstraZeneca"), [Brompton Bikes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brompton_Bicycle "Brompton Bicycle"), [Jaguar Land Rover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaguar_Land_Rover "Jaguar Land Rover"), and [McLaren Automotive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/McLaren_Automotive "McLaren Automotive").[^81] During the visit, China removed sanctions on six serving British Parliament members.[^82] The Chinese government also announced it was "actively considering" visa free access under 30 days to British visitors.[^83]

## Security concerns

British counter-terrorism authorities have reported a rise in hostile state activity linked to China within the United Kingdom, with operations allegedly involving threats to life such as planned attacks and covert actions. In July 2025, Dominic Murphy, head of London's [Counter Terrorism Command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counter_Terrorism_Command "Counter Terrorism Command"), stated that the breadth, complexity, and volume of hostile operations from China among other countries had grown at a rate that neither British authorities, their international partners, nor the wider intelligence community had predicted. Officials also highlighted the increasing use of criminal proxies and vulnerable individuals, including minors, in carrying out these activities. Specific details regarding China's involvement were not disclosed.[^85]

In July 2025, the UK [Joint Committee on Human Rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_Committee_on_Human_Rights "Joint Committee on Human Rights") labelled China a "flagrant" perpetrator of [transnational repression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transnational_repression_by_China "Transnational repression by China") and presented a series of recommended responses to the UK government.[^86]

### Espionage

The [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_the_People%E2%80%99s_Republic_of_China "Government of the People’s Republic of China") of the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC) and various organs of the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) have conducted surveillance and espionage in the United Kingdom, according to top UK national security officials such as the head of its domestic intelligence agency [MI5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MI5 "MI5").[^87] [^88] UK officials, including experts at its MI5, have long been fearful that the PRC could shut down businesses in the nation with [cyberattacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyberattacks "Cyberattacks") and spy equipment embedded in computer and telecommunications equipment.[^89] [^90] [^91]

It is also been alleged that intelligence operatives from China might have been using platforms like [LinkedIn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LinkedIn "LinkedIn") to reach out to UK parliamentarians and conduct espionage.[^92] [^93]

On May 7, 2026, a London court found Chung Biu "Bill" Yuen and Chi Leung "Peter" Wai guilty of spying for Hong Kong and China, targeting pro-democracy activists living in Britain.[^94]

### Academic freedom

Chinese intelligence agencies have threatened [academic freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_freedom "Academic freedom") at British universities. [Sheffield Hallam University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheffield_Hallam_University "Sheffield Hallam University") was threatened and pressured to stop the publication of research on Uyghur forced labor by [Laura Murphy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laura_Murphy_\(academic\) "Laura Murphy (academic)").[^95] [^96] [^97]

Academics in British universities teaching on Chinese topics have been warned by the Chinese government to support the Chinese Communist Party or be refused entry to the country. Professors who disregarded the warnings to speak more positively about the CCP have had their visas cancelled which prevents them from doing fieldwork in China. Academics are warned to avoid [The Three Ts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Three_Ts "The Three Ts").[^98] In March 2021, British Uyghur expert Joanne Smith Finley was sanctioned by China after she referred to the situation in Xinjiang as a genocide in comments given to the [Associated Press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_Press "Associated Press").[^99] [^100]

In November 2025, it was reported that [Sheffield Hallam University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheffield_Hallam_University "Sheffield Hallam University") faced pressure from the Chinese government to halt research by Professor Laura Murphy on alleged forced labour of Uyghur Muslims in Xinjiang. Threats to staff and restrictions on the university's websites in China aimed to protect access to Chinese students. After legal action and scrutiny, the university lifted the ban on the research in October 2025 and issued an apology, while UK authorities condemned foreign interference in academic freedom.[^101]

## Transport

### Air transport

All three major Chinese airlines, [Air China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_China "Air China"), [China Eastern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Eastern_Airlines "China Eastern Airlines") & [China Southern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Southern_Airlines "China Southern Airlines") fly between the UK and China, principally between [London-Heathrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heathrow_Airport "Heathrow Airport") and the three major air hubs of [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing_Capital_International_Airport "Beijing Capital International Airport"), [Shanghai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Pudong_International_Airport "Shanghai Pudong International Airport") and [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou_Baiyun_International_Airport "Guangzhou Baiyun International Airport"). China Southern also flies between Heathrow and [Wuhan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wuhan_Tianhe_International_Airport "Wuhan Tianhe International Airport"). Among China's other airlines; [Hainan Airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hainan_Airlines "Hainan Airlines") flies between [Manchester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchester_Airport "Manchester Airport") and Beijing, [Beijing Capital Airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing_Capital_Airlines "Beijing Capital Airlines") offers Heathrow to [Qingdao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qingdao_Liuting_International_Airport "Qingdao Liuting International Airport"), while [Tianjin Airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianjin_Airlines "Tianjin Airlines") offers flights between [Tianjin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianjin_Binhai_International_Airport "Tianjin Binhai International Airport"), [Chongqing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chongqing_Jiangbei_International_Airport "Chongqing Jiangbei International Airport") and [Xi'an](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi%27an_Xianyang_International_Airport "Xi'an Xianyang International Airport") to [London-Gatwick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gatwick_Airport "Gatwick Airport"). Hong Kong's flag carrier [Cathay Pacific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathay_Pacific "Cathay Pacific") also flies between [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong_International_Airport "Hong Kong International Airport") to Heathrow, Gatwick and Manchester. The British flag carrier [British Airways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Airways "British Airways") flies to just three destinations in China; Beijing, Shanghai and Hong Kong, and in the past [Chengdu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chengdu_Shuangliu_International_Airport "Chengdu Shuangliu International Airport"). Rival [Virgin Atlantic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgin_Atlantic "Virgin Atlantic") flies between Heathrow to Shanghai and Hong Kong. British Airways has mentioned that it is interested in leasing China's new [Comac C919](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comac_C919 "Comac C919") in its pool of aircraft of Boeing and Airbus.[^102]

### Rail transport

In January 2017, [China Railways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Railway "China Railway") and [DB Cargo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DB_Cargo_UK "DB Cargo UK") launched the [Yiwu-London Railway Line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yiwu-London_railway_line "Yiwu-London railway line") connecting the city of [Yiwu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yiwu "Yiwu") and the London borough of [Barking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barking,_London "Barking, London"), and creating the longest railway freight line in the world. Hong Kong's [MTR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MTR_Corporation "MTR Corporation") runs the London's [TfL Rail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TfL_Rail "TfL Rail") service and has a 30% stake in [South Western Railway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Western_Railway_\(train_operating_company\) "South Western Railway (train operating company)"). In 2017, train manufacturer [CRRC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CRRC "CRRC") won a contract to build 71 engineering wagons for [London Underground](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Underground "London Underground"). This is the first time a Chinese manufacturer has won a railway contract.[^103]

### Press

The weekly-published Europe edition of *[China Daily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Daily "China Daily")* is available in a few newsagents in the UK, and on occasions a condensed version called *China Watch* is published in the *[Daily Telegraph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph "The Daily Telegraph")*.[^104] The monthly *NewsChina*,[^105] the North American English-language edition of *China Newsweek (中国新闻周刊)* is available in a few branches of [WHSmith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WHSmith "WHSmith"). Due to local censorship, British newspapers and magazines are not widely available in mainland China, however the *[Economist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economist "Economist")* and *[Financial Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_Times "Financial Times")* are available in Hong Kong.

## British in China

### Sports

[Table tennis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Table_tennis "Table tennis"), originating from the United Kingdom, became one of the most iconic sports in China in the 20th century.[^107]

In [2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basketball_at_the_2012_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_tournament#Group_B "Basketball at the 2012 Summer Olympics – Men's tournament"), the [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Britain_men%27s_national_basketball_team "Great Britain men's national basketball team") managed to beat [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_men%27s_national_basketball_team "China men's national basketball team") in the basketball olympics event with a score of 90–58, the first win for the host nation.
