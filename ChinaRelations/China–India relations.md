[China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") and [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") maintained peaceful relations for thousands of years, but their relationship has varied since the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP)'s victory in the [Chinese Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War "Chinese Civil War") in 1949 and the [annexation of Tibet by the People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annexation_of_Tibet_by_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Annexation of Tibet by the People's Republic of China"). The two nations have sought economic cooperation with each other, while frequent border disputes and [economic nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_nationalism "Economic nationalism") in both countries are major points of contention.

Cultural and economic relations between China and India date back to ancient times. The [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road") not only served as a major [trade route](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade_route "Trade route") between India and China, but is also credited for facilitating the spread of [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") from India to East Asia.[^1] During the 19th century, the British [East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_India_Company "East India Company") increasingly [exported opium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium_trade_in_China "Opium trade in China"), grown in India, leading to the [Opium Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium_Wars "Opium Wars").[^2] [^3] During [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), both [British India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Raj "British Raj") and the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)") (ROC) played a crucial role in halting the progress of [Imperial Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Japan "Imperial Japan").[^4] After India became independent in 1947, it established relations with the ROC. The modern Sino-Indian diplomatic relationship began in 1950, when India was among the first noncommunist countries to end formal relations with the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China") and recognise the PRC as the legitimate government of both [Mainland China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland_China "Mainland China") and [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Taiwan "Geography of Taiwan"). China and India are two of the major [regional powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power") in Asia, and are the two [most populous countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_population "List of countries by population") and among the [fastest growing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_real_GDP_growth_rate "List of countries by real GDP growth rate") major economies in the world.

Growth in diplomatic and economic influence has increased the significance of their bilateral relationship. Between 2008 and 2021, China was India's largest trading partner, and the two countries have extended their strategic and military relations.[^5] [^6] [^7] [^8] India has a large [trade deficit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade_deficit "Trade deficit") that is favoured towards China. The [Sino-Indian border dispute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Indian_border_dispute "Sino-Indian border dispute"), manifested by the [Line of Actual Control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_of_Actual_Control "Line of Actual Control") (LAC), remains a central issue, resulting in the 1962 [Sino-Indian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Indian_War "Sino-Indian War"), the [clashes in 1967](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nathu_La_and_Cho_La_clashes "Nathu La and Cho La clashes"), and the [a standoff in 1987](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1987_Sino-Indian_skirmish "1987 Sino-Indian skirmish").[^9] Since the late 1980s, both countries have rebuilt diplomatic and economic ties; a 1996 agreement forbids firearms and explosives near the LAC.[^10]

Border deconfliction mechanisms emerged, beginning with the 2003 [Special Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_Representative_mechanism_on_the_India%E2%80%93China_boundary_question "Special Representative mechanism on the India–China boundary question"). Renewed conflicts included [standoffs in 2013](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2013_Depsang_standoff "2013 Depsang standoff"), and [in 2017](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2017_China%E2%80%93India_border_standoff "2017 China–India border standoff"); [major skirmishes in 2020-2021](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020%E2%80%932021_China%E2%80%93India_skirmishes "2020–2021 China–India skirmishes") resulted in dozens of deaths on both sides; military infrastructure near the LAC continues to grow.[^11] [^12] India's concerns include China's [close relations with Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Pakistan_relations "China–Pakistan relations"),[^13] and with [separatist groups in Northeast India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurgency_in_Northeast_India "Insurgency in Northeast India").[^14] China's concerns include Indian military and economic activities in the disputed [South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Sea "South China Sea") [^15] and hosting of [Tibetan exiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_exile "Tibetan exile").[^16] [^17] Today, South Asia is the primary site of [great power competition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_power_competition "Great power competition") between China and India.[^18]

## Geopolitical overview

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Sino-Indian_Geography.png/330px-Sino-Indian_Geography.png)

Eastern and Southern Asia. (The border between the People's Republic of China and the India over Arunachal Pradesh / South Tibet reflects actual control, without dotted line showing claims.)

The China–India border straddles the [Himalayas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himalayas "Himalayas"), with [Nepal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nepal "Nepal") and [Bhutan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhutan "Bhutan") acting as [buffer states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffer_state "Buffer state"). Parts of the disputed [Kashmir region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashmir_region "Kashmir region") claimed by India ([J&K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jammu_and_Kashmir_\(union_territory\) "Jammu and Kashmir (union territory)") and [Ladakh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ladakh "Ladakh")) are claimed and administered by either [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan") ([Azad Kashmir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azad_Kashmir "Azad Kashmir") and [Gilgit Baltistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilgit_Baltistan "Gilgit Baltistan")) or by the PRC ([Aksai Chin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aksai_Chin "Aksai Chin")). The Government of Pakistan, on its maps, shows the Aksai Chin area as mostly within China and labels the boundary "Frontier Undefined", while India holds that Aksai Chin is illegally occupied by the PRC. China and India also dispute most of [Arunachal Pradesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arunachal_Pradesh "Arunachal Pradesh").[^19]

Not only is China's India policy shaped by [greater competition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_foreign_policy_toward_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "United States foreign policy toward the People's Republic of China") with the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), but there are also real structural issues in India-China relations that exacerbate discord. These stem largely from China's attempts to keep India at arm's length in the [Indo-Pacific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Pacific "Indo-Pacific") region. There are clear differences in the regional order in Asia that the two countries desire—India seeks a multipolar order, of which India is one of the main poles, while China seeks a single pole, of which India is not a pole at all.[^20]

As India strengthens its security ties with the United States and takes a leading role in organizing the [Global South](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_South "Global South"), tensions with China persist. China's engagement with India's neighbors and growing military cooperation with Pakistan further strain India's geopolitical landscape. In this volatile landscape, Sino-Indian relations seek a delicate balance between pragmatic economic interests and escalating geopolitical tensions.[^19]

## History

### Antiquity

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Etched_carnelian_beads_after_Mackay.jpg/250px-Etched_carnelian_beads_after_Mackay.jpg)

Etched carnelian beads, Harappa Culture. 21 Such beads were imported from India to China in the early half of 1st millennium BCE. 22

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Echecs_chinois.JPG/250px-Echecs_chinois.JPG)

Xiangqi, or Chinese chess, which, like Western Chess is believed to be descended from the Indian chess game of chaturanga. 23 The earliest indications reveal the game may have been played as early as the third century BCE.

China and India have also had some contact before the transmission of Buddhism. References to a people called the *[Chinas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinas "Chinas")*, are found in ancient [Indian literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_literature "Indian literature"). The [Indian epic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_epic_poetry "Indian epic poetry") *[Mahabharata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahabharata "Mahabharata")* (c. 5th century BCE) contains references to " [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_in_the_Mah%C4%81bh%C4%81rata "China in the Mahābhārata") ", which may have been referring to the [Qin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_\(state\) "Qin (state)") state which later became the [Qin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_dynasty "Qin dynasty"). [Chanakya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chanakya "Chanakya") (c. 350–283 BCE), the prime minister of the [Maurya Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurya_Empire "Maurya Empire"), refers to Chinese silk as "cinamsuka" (Chinese silk dress) and "cinapatta" (Chinese silk bundle) in his *[Arthashastra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthashastra "Arthashastra")*.[^24]

[Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") was transmitted from India to China in the 1st century CE.[^25] Trade relations via the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road") acted as economic contact between the two regions.

### Middle Ages

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Ambassador_from_Zhong_Tianzhu_%28%E4%B8%AD%E5%A4%A9%E7%AB%BA_Central_India%29_in_The_Gathering_of_Kings_%28%E7%8E%8B%E4%BC%9A%E5%9B%BE%29_circa_650_CE.jpg/250px-Ambassador_from_Zhong_Tianzhu_%28%E4%B8%AD%E5%A4%A9%E7%AB%BA_Central_India%29_in_The_Gathering_of_Kings_%28%E7%8E%8B%E4%BC%9A%E5%9B%BE%29_circa_650_CE.jpg)

Ambassador from Central India (中天竺 Zhong Tianzhu ) to the court of the Tang dynasty. Wanghuitu (王会图) circa 650 CE.

From the 1st century onwards, many Indian scholars and monks traveled to China, such as [Batuo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batuo "Batuo") ([fl.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floruit "Floruit") 464–495 CE)—first abbot of the [Shaolin Monastery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shaolin_Monastery "Shaolin Monastery") —and [Bodhidharma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bodhidharma "Bodhidharma") —founder of [Chan/Zen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zen "Zen") Buddhism—while many Chinese scholars and monks also traveled to India, such as [Xuanzang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xuanzang "Xuanzang") (b. 604) and [I Ching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I_Ching_\(monk\) "I Ching (monk)") (635–713), both of whom were students at [Nalanda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nalanda "Nalanda") University in [Bihar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bihar "Bihar").

#### Tang and Harsha dynasties

During the 7th century, [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty") China gained control over large portions of the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road") and Central Asia. In 649, the Chinese general [Wang Xuance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Xuance "Wang Xuance"), along with thousands of recruited Tibetan and Nepalese troops, briefly invaded North India and won the [Battle of Chabuheluo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Chabuheluo "Battle of Chabuheluo").

During the 8th century, the astronomical [table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aryabhata%27s_sine_table "Aryabhata's sine table") of [sines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions "Trigonometric functions") by the [Indian astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_astronomy "Indian astronomy") and [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_mathematics "Indian mathematics"), [Aryabhatta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aryabhatta "Aryabhatta") (476–550), were translated into the [Chinese astronomical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_astronomy "Chinese astronomy") and [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_mathematics "Chinese mathematics") book of the *[Treatise on Astrology of the Kaiyuan Era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treatise_on_Astrology_of_the_Kaiyuan_Era "Treatise on Astrology of the Kaiyuan Era")* (*Kaiyuan Zhanjing*), compiled in 718 CE during the Tang dynasty.[^26] The *Kaiyuan Zhanjing* was compiled by [Gautama Siddha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gautama_Siddha "Gautama Siddha"), an astronomer and astrologer born in Chang'an, and whose family was originally from India. He was also notable for his translation of the [Navagraha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navagraha "Navagraha") calendar into Chinese.

#### Yuan dynasty

Tamil Hindu Indian merchants traded in [Quanzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quanzhou "Quanzhou") during the Yuan dynasty.[^27] [^28] [^29] Hindu statues were found in Quanzhou dating to this period.[^30]

According to [Badauni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Badauni "Badauni") and [Ferishta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firishta "Firishta"), the [Delhi Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delhi_Sultanate "Delhi Sultanate") under [Muhammad bin Tughluq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_bin_Tughluq "Muhammad bin Tughluq") had ambitions to invade China. There existed a direct trade relationship between China and the Delhi Sultanate. Ibn Battuta mentions that the [Yuan Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_dynasty "Yuan dynasty") had sent an embassy to Muhammad for reconstruction of a sacked temple at [Sambhal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sambhal "Sambhal").

### Ming dynasty

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Treasure_Boat_Shipyard_-_stele_pavilion_-_Calicut_stele_-_P1080100.JPG/250px-Treasure_Boat_Shipyard_-_stele_pavilion_-_Calicut_stele_-_P1080100.JPG)

Stele installed in Calicut by Zheng He (modern replica)

Between 1405 and 1433, [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") China sponsored a [series of seven naval expeditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_treasure_voyages "Ming treasure voyages") led by Admiral [Zheng He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zheng_He "Zheng He"). Zheng He visited numerous Indian kingdoms and ports, including the [Malabar coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malabar_coast "Malabar coast"), [Bengal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bengal "Bengal"), and [Ceylon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceylon "Ceylon"), the [Persian Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf"), [Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabia "Arabia"), and later expeditions ventured down as far as [Malindi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malindi "Malindi") in what is now [Kenya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenya "Kenya"). Throughout his travels, Zheng He liberally dispensed Chinese gifts of silk, [porcelain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porcelain "Porcelain"), and other goods. In return, he received rich and unusual presents, including African zebras and giraffes.[^31] After the Ming treasure voyages, private Chinese traders continued operating in the eastern Indian Ocean. Chinese junks could frequently be seen in the ports of the [Vijayanagara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vijayanagara "Vijayanagara") Empire, carrying silks and other products.[^32]

### Qing dynasty

The [Bhois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhoi_dynasty "Bhoi dynasty") of [Orissa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odisha "Odisha") maintained minor maritime trade links with China. Many of the [gosains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gosains "Gosains") entering Tibet from China passed through the territory of Birakisore Deva I of Khurda (1736–1793) who styled himself as Gajapati, the ruler of Utkala, when visiting the Jagannath temple at Puri.[^33]

The reign of [Tipu Sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tipu_Sultan "Tipu Sultan") in [Mysore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Mysore "Kingdom of Mysore") saw Chinese technology used for sugar production,[^34] and sandalwood was exported to China.[^35] Tipu's and Mysore's tryst with silk began in the early 1780s when he received a silk cloth from an ambassador from the Qing dynasty-ruled China at his court. Tipu was said to be enchanted by the item to such an extent that he resolved to introduce its production in his kingdom. He sent a return journey to China, which returned after twelve years.[^36]

After the Qing expansion into the Himalayas, there was increased contact with South Asia, which often manifested in the form of tributary relations. The Qing were obliged to defend their subservient state, [Badakhshan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Badakhshan "Badakhshan"), against the [Afghans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durrani_Empire "Durrani Empire") and [Marathas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maratha_Empire "Maratha Empire"), though no major clash with the Marathas ever took place. The Afghans gained the initiative and defeated the Marathas at [Panipat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_Battle_of_Panipat "Third Battle of Panipat") in 1761. The battle's outcome was used by the Afghans to intimidate the Qing.[^37]

#### Sino-Sikh War

In the 18th to 19th centuries, the [Sikh Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sikh_Empire "Sikh Empire") expanded into neighbouring lands. It had annexed [Ladakh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ladakh "Ladakh") into the state of [Jammu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jammu "Jammu") in 1834. In 1841, they had some conflicts with Chinese forces; neither side wished to continue the conflict, as the Sikhs were embroiled in tensions with the British that would lead up to the [First Anglo-Sikh War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Anglo-Sikh_War "First Anglo-Sikh War"), while the Chinese were in the midst of the [First Opium War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Opium_War "First Opium War"). The Sikhs claimed victory. The two parties signed a treaty in September 1842, which stipulated no transgressions or interference in the other country's frontiers.[^38]

#### British Raj

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Epitaph_of_Major_Hsiao_Chu_Ching_Second_World_War.JPG/250px-Epitaph_of_Major_Hsiao_Chu_Ching_Second_World_War.JPG)

Epitaph of Major Hsiao Chu Ching at Jairampur cemetery 39 40

Indian soldiers, known as " [sepoys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sepoy "Sepoy") ", who were in [British service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Indian_Army "British Indian Army") participated in the [First](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Opium_War "First Opium War") and [Second Opium Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Opium_War "Second Opium War") against [Qing China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty"). Indian sepoys were also involved in the suppression of the [Boxer Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxer_Rebellion "Boxer Rebellion") in 1900, in addition to serving as guards in the [British colony of Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Hong_Kong "British Hong Kong") and foreign concessions such as the [Shanghai International Settlement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_International_Settlement "Shanghai International Settlement"). The Chinese slur " *Yindu A San* " (印度阿三 - Indian number three) was used to describe Indian soldiers in British service, with some of the popular sentiment rejecting the possibility for Sino-Indian fraternity.[^41]

### Republic of China

In 1924, on his major tour of several major Chinese cities, giving lectures about using their shared Asian values and traditional spirituality to help together promote world peace, [Rabindranath Tagore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore "Rabindranath Tagore") was invited to [Canton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou "Guangzhou") by Sun Yat-sen, an invitation which he declined. There was considerably mixed reception to Tagore from the Chinese students and intellectuals.[^42]

[Hu Shih](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hu_Shih "Hu Shih"), the Chinese ambassador to the United States from 1938 to 1942, commented, albeit critically, on India's Buddhism almost completely subsuming Chinese society upon its introduction.[^43]

> ASIA is one. The [Himalayas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himalayas "Himalayas") divide, only to accentuate, two mighty civilizations, the Chinese with its [communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communism "Communism") of [Confucius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius "Confucius"), and the Indian with its [individualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individualism "Individualism") of the [Vedas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vedas "Vedas"). But not even the snowy barriers can interrupt for one moment that broad expanse of love for the [Ultimate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_\(philosophy\) "Absolute (philosophy)") and [Universal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_\(metaphysics\) "Universal (metaphysics)"), which is the common thought-inheritance of every Asiatic race, enabling them to produce all the great religions of the world and distinguishing them from those maritime peoples of the [Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean "Mediterranean") and the [Baltic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_Sea "Baltic Sea"), who love to dwell on the [Particular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particular "Particular"), and to search out the means, not the end, of life.[^44]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Nehru_Chiang_Gandhi_Madame_Chiang_10_Feb_1942_India.jpg/250px-Nehru_Chiang_Gandhi_Madame_Chiang_10_Feb_1942_India.jpg)

Chiang Kai-shek and his wife Song Meiling with Mahatma Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru

While never having actually visited India in his lifetime, [Sun Yat-sen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen "Sun Yat-sen"), founder of the Republic of China, occasionally spoke and wrote of India as a fellow Asian nation that was likewise subject to harsh Western exploitation, and frequently called for a [Pan-Asian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan-Asian "Pan-Asian") united front against all unjust imperialism.[^45] [^46] Similarly, believing that then- [Republican China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalist_Government "Nationalist Government") and [British India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Raj "British Raj") were "sister nations from the dawn of history" who needed to transform their "ancient friendship into a new camaraderie of two freedom-loving nations", [Jawaharlal Nehru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru "Jawaharlal Nehru") visited China in 1939 as an honored guest of the government. Highly praising both [Chiang Kai-shek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek "Chiang Kai-shek") and his wife [Song Meiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_Meiling "Song Meiling"), Nehru referred to Chiang as "not only a great Chinese, but a great Asiatic and world figure...one of the top most leaders of the world...a successful general and captain in war", and Song as "full of vitality and charm...a star hope for the Chinese people...a symbol of China's invincibility". Chiang recorded a favorable impression of Nehru in his diary, and the Chiangs also regularly wrote Nehru during his time in prison and even after their 1942 visit to India.[^47] [^48]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Chiang_Couple_Gandhi_India_10_Feb_1942.jpg/250px-Chiang_Couple_Gandhi_India_10_Feb_1942.jpg)

The Chiangs with Mahatma Gandhi in Calcutta in 1942

Partially to enlist India's aid against both [Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_Japan "Empire of Japan") and [Western imperialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_imperialism "Western imperialism") in exchange for China's support for [Indian independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_independence_movement "Indian independence movement"), the Chiangs visited [British India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_India "British India") in 1942 and met with Nehru, [Mahatma Gandhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi "Mahatma Gandhi"), and [Muhammad Ali Jinnah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_Ali_Jinnah "Muhammad Ali Jinnah"). The Chiangs also sought to present their nation as a potential third option for the Indian people to ally themselves with, with public sympathies at the time sharply split between the British and the Japanese, who actively tried to sway India's population with pledges to liberate Asia if they would help their efforts against the British. While the public reception to the Chiangs was mostly positive, some reacted less favorably to the Chiangs' presence in India, with Jinnah believing that Chiang Kai-shek lacked proper understanding of Indian society and feeling he was biased in favor of Nehru and Gandhi while neglecting the demands of other religious communities.[^49] For his part, Chiang apparently believed none of the major Indian leaders could help his government meaningfully. Nevertheless, the Chiangs continued to commit themselves to supporting the [Indian independence movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_independence_movement "Indian independence movement") from afar, mostly via diplomacy, with Song Meiling writing to Nehru encouragingly: "We shall leave nothing undone in assisting you to gain freedom and independence." [^50]

In 1942, a division of the [Kuomintang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuomintang "Kuomintang") 's armies entered India as the [Chinese Army in India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Army_in_India "Chinese Army in India") in their struggle against Japanese expansion in [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia").[^51] [^52]

## Post independence

On 15 August 1947, India gained independence from the United Kingdom. The newly independent India established diplomatic relations with the ROC. On 1 October 1949, the Chinese [People's Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") defeated the Kuomintang (the ROC's ruling Nationalist Party) and took over [Mainland China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland_China "Mainland China"), establishing the PRC. Soon afterwards, India became a federal, democratic republic after its constitution came into effect on 26 January 1950.

### 1950s

> Their last telegrame to us is an act of gross discourtesy \[...\] It looks as though it is not a friend speaking in that language but a potential enemy \[...\] for the first time, after centuries, India’s defence has to concentrate itself on two fronts simultaneously. \[...\] In our calculations we shall now have to reckon with communist China in the north and in the north-east, a communist China which has definite ambitions and aims and which does not, in any way, seem friendly disposed towards us.

— Excerpt from Home Minister [Sardar Patel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vallabhbhai_Patel "Vallabhbhai Patel") 's letter to Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru, November 7, 1950 [^53]

India [established diplomatic relations with the PRC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dates_of_establishment_of_diplomatic_relations_with_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Dates of establishment of diplomatic relations with the People's Republic of China") on 1 April 1950, the first non-communist/socialist nation in Asia to do so.[^54] Pakistan continued to recognize the ROC until 1951.[^55]

The relationship between India and a newly communist China started out on an optimistic note. Jawaharlal Nehru, the first prime minister of India, and [PRC premier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_China "Premier of China") [Zhou Enlai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Enlai "Zhou Enlai") articulated a vision of an internationalist foreign policy governed by the ethics of the Panchsheel ([Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Principles_of_Peaceful_Coexistence "Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence")).[^56] However, there was notable skepticism on the Indian side from the very beginning about Chinese intentions. For example, [Bhimrao Ambedkar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B._R._Ambedkar "B. R. Ambedkar") was surprised that Nehru took Panchsheel seriously,[^57] while [Acharya Kriplani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acharya_Kriplani "Acharya Kriplani") said that Panchsheel was "born in sin".[^57] Nehru himself was disappointed when it became clear that the two countries had a conflict of interest in Tibet, which had traditionally served as a [buffer zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffer_zone "Buffer zone"). From the Chinese side, strategists were concerned about the possibility of India continuing to spread its dominance in Asia and the Indian Ocean in the manner of British India.[^58]

China viewed Tibet as a part of its territory. The preceding government of the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)") under [Chiang Kai-shek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek "Chiang Kai-shek") also [claimed Tibet as Chinese territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibet_Area_\(administrative_division\) "Tibet Area (administrative division)"). However, he was unable to re-assert control. [Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party") [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") saw Indian concern over Tibet as a manifestation of interference in the internal affairs of the PRC. The PRC reasserted control over Tibet and to end [Tibetan Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism "Tibetan Buddhism") and [feudalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism "Feudalism"), which it did by force of arms in 1950. To avoid antagonizing the PRC, Nehru informed Chinese leaders that India had no political ambitions or territorial ambitions and did not seek special privileges in Tibet, but that traditional trading rights must continue. With Indian support, Tibetan delegates signed an agreement in May 1951 recognizing PRC sovereignty but guaranteeing that the existing political and social system of Tibet would continue.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Prime_Minister_Nehru_and_Chinese_Chairman_Mao_Zedong_in_Beijing.jpg/250px-Prime_Minister_Nehru_and_Chinese_Chairman_Mao_Zedong_in_Beijing.jpg)

Chairman Mao Zedong of the Chinese Communist Party and Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru. In April 1954, India and the PRC signed an eight-year agreement on Tibet that became the Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence (or Panchsheel ). Jawaharlal Nehru of India, during the latter's state visit to China, October 1954.

In October 1954, China and India signed an [agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Indian_Agreement_1954 "Sino-Indian Agreement 1954") regarding Tibet, whereby India recognised Tibet as part of China, with China accepting the continuance of the previous trade arrangements. Observers noted the agreement heavily favored China.[^59]

It is the popular perception that the catchphrase of India's diplomacy with China in the 1950s was *Hindi-Chini bhai-bhai*, which means, in [Hindi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindi "Hindi"), "Indians and Chinese are brothers".[^60] Nehru sought to initiate a more direct dialogue between the peoples of China and India in culture and literature. Around then, the Indian artist (painter) [Beohar Rammanohar Sinha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beohar_Rammanohar_Sinha "Beohar Rammanohar Sinha"), who had earlier decorated the pages of the original [Constitution of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_India "Constitution of India"), was sent to China in 1957 on a Government of India fellowship to establish a direct cross-cultural and inter-civilization bridge. Noted Indian scholar [Rahul Sankrityayan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rahul_Sankrityayan "Rahul Sankrityayan") and diplomat [Natwar Singh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natwar_Singh "Natwar Singh") were also there, and [Sarvapalli Radhakrishnan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarvapalli_Radhakrishnan "Sarvapalli Radhakrishnan") paid a visit to the PRC.

After signing the 1954 agreement, India published new maps showing defined borders, as it became apprehensive that China might make claims on Indian territory.[^61] Two major territorial disputes existed between China and India, which remained dormant until 1959. In the northeast, Indian territory included the Assam Himalayan region up to the McMahon Line, which China did not recognise as a legal boundary. In the western sector, Indian territory inherited from the British Raj included the Aksai Chin plateau, which Chinese maps started showing as Chinese territory in the 1940s, if not earlier.[^62] When India discovered that China built a road through the region, border clashes and Indian protests became more frequent. In January 1959, PRC premier [Zhou Enlai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Enlai "Zhou Enlai") wrote to Nehru, pointing out that no government in China had accepted as legal the [McMahon Line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/McMahon_Line "McMahon Line"), which the 1914 [Simla Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simla_Convention "Simla Convention") defined as the eastern section of the border between India and Tibet.

In March 1959, the [Dalai Lama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalai_Lama "Dalai Lama"), spiritual and temporal head of the [Tibet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Tibet_\(1950%E2%80%93present\) "History of Tibet (1950–present)"), sought sanctuary in [Dharmsala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dharamsala,_Himachal_Pradesh "Dharamsala, Himachal Pradesh"), Himachal Pradesh, where he established the [Tibetan government-in-exile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_government-in-exile "Tibetan government-in-exile"). Thousands of Tibetan refugees settled in northwestern India. Relations between India and China deteriorated after India provided the Dalai Lama sanctuary.[^63]

### 1960s

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/India_disputed_areas_map.svg/250px-India_disputed_areas_map.svg.png)

Disputed territories of India

Border disputes resulted in a short border war between the People's Republic of China and India on 20 October 1962.[^63] The border clash resulted in an overall defeat of India as the PRC pushed the Indian forces to within 48 km of the [Assam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assam "Assam") plains in the northeast. It also occupied strategic points in the [Aksai Chin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aksai_Chin "Aksai Chin") and Demchok regions of [Ladakh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ladakh "Ladakh"), before declaring a unilateral [ceasefire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceasefire "Ceasefire") on 21 November. It claimed that it withdrew to 20 km behind its contended line of control. India disagreed with the claim.

> "I don't want to know what happened in the past. All I want to know is who are my commanders, where are the Chinese, how much ammunition have I got…"

— Field Marshal [Sam Manekshaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sam_Manekshaw "Sam Manekshaw") recalling 1962 when he was appointed to take charge of NEFA following the resignation of [Kaul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brij_Mohan_Kaul "Brij Mohan Kaul") and [Menon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V._K._Krishna_Menon "V. K. Krishna Menon") in the aftermath of the China India war.[^64]

During the [Sino-Indian border conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Indian_War "Sino-Indian War"), India's [Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_India "Communist Party of India") was accused by the Indian government of being pro-PRC, and many of its political leaders were jailed. Subsequently, [the Communist Party of India (CPI) split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1964_split_in_the_Communist_Party_of_India "1964 split in the Communist Party of India") with the leftist section, forming the [Communist Party of India (Marxist)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_India_\(Marxist\) "Communist Party of India (Marxist)") in 1964.

Relations between the PRC and India deteriorated during the rest of the 1960s and the early 1970s, while [China–Pakistan relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93Pakistan_relations "China–Pakistan relations") improved and [Sino-Soviet relations worsened](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split "Sino-Soviet split"). The PRC backed Pakistan in its [1965 war with India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1965 "Indo-Pakistani War of 1965"), and issued "ultimatums" threatening military action at its own border. The threats only served to damage Pakistan's relations with the western powers.[^65] In late 1967, there were two more conflicts between Indian and Chinese forces at their contested border, in [Sikkim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sikkim "Sikkim"), known as the [Nathu La and Cho La clashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nathu_La_and_Cho_La_clashes "Nathu La and Cho La clashes"). Both sides suffered heavy casualties, but India came out in a better position than the PRC.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/1967-09_1967%E5%B9%B4_%E5%8C%97%E4%BA%AC%E4%B8%BE%E8%A1%8C%E6%8A%97%E8%AE%AE%E5%8D%B0%E5%BA%A6%E7%9A%84%E7%A4%BA%E5%A8%81%E6%B8%B8%E8%A1%8C.jpg/330px-1967-09_1967%E5%B9%B4_%E5%8C%97%E4%BA%AC%E4%B8%BE%E8%A1%8C%E6%8A%97%E8%AE%AE%E5%8D%B0%E5%BA%A6%E7%9A%84%E7%A4%BA%E5%A8%81%E6%B8%B8%E8%A1%8C.jpg)

Chinese demonstrations against Indian " reactionary factions" in Beijing in 1967.

The PRC continued an active propaganda campaign against India and supplied ideological, financial, and other assistance to dissident groups, especially to [tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribes_of_India "Tribes of India") in northeastern India. The PRC accused India of assisting the [Khampa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kham "Kham") rebels in Tibet. Sri Lanka played the role of chief negotiator for the withdrawal of Chinese troops from the Indian territory. Both countries agreed to Colombo's proposals.[^66] [^67]

### 1970s

In August 1971, India signed a [Treaty of Peace, Friendship, and Co-operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Soviet_Treaty_of_Friendship_and_Cooperation "Indo-Soviet Treaty of Friendship and Cooperation") with the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"). The PRC sided with Pakistan in its [December 1971 war with India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1971 "Indo-Pakistani War of 1971"). Although China strongly condemned India, it did not carry out its veiled threat to intervene on Pakistan's behalf. By this time, the PRC had [replaced the Republic of China in the UN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_and_the_United_Nations "China and the United Nations"), where its representatives denounced India as being a "tool of Soviet expansionism."

India and the PRC renewed efforts to improve relations after Indian Prime Minister [Indira Gandhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indira_Gandhi "Indira Gandhi") 's [Congress party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_National_Congress "Indian National Congress") lost the 1977 elections to [Morarji Desai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morarji_Desai "Morarji Desai") 's [Janata Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janata_Party "Janata Party"). In 1978, the Indian Minister of External Affairs [Atal Bihari Vajpayee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atal_Bihari_Vajpayee "Atal Bihari Vajpayee") made a landmark visit to Beijing, an impetus to both countries to officially re-establish diplomatic relations in 1979. The PRC modified its pro-Pakistan stand on [Kashmir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashmir "Kashmir") and appeared willing to remain silent on India's absorption of Sikkim and its special advisory relationship with [Bhutan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhutan "Bhutan"). The PRC's leaders agreed to discuss the boundary issue, India's priority, as the first step to a broadening of relations. The two countries hosted each other's news agencies, and [Mount Kailash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Kailash "Mount Kailash") and [Mansarowar Lake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Manasarovar "Lake Manasarovar") in Tibet, the home of the [Hindu pantheon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_pantheon "Hindu pantheon"), were opened to annual [pilgrimages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilgrimage "Pilgrimage").

### 1980s

In 1981, the Minister of Foreign Affairs of the People's Republic of China, [Huang Hua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huang_Hua_\(politician\) "Huang Hua (politician)"), made a landmark visit to New Delhi.[^68] PRC Premier [Zhao Ziyang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhao_Ziyang "Zhao Ziyang") concurrently toured [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan"), [Nepal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nepal "Nepal"), and [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh").

In 1980, Indian Prime Minister [Indira Gandhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indira_Gandhi "Indira Gandhi") approved a plan to upgrade the deployment of forces around the Line of Actual Control. India also undertook infrastructural development in disputed areas.[^69] [^70] In 1984, squads of Indian soldiers began actively patrolling the [Sumdorong Chu Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumdorong_Chu_Valley "Sumdorong Chu Valley") in [Arunachal Pradesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arunachal_Pradesh "Arunachal Pradesh"). In the winter of 1986, the Chinese deployed their troops to the Sumdorong Chu before the Indian team could arrive and built a helipad at Wandung.[^71] Surprised by the Chinese occupation, India's then Chief of Army Staff, [General K.Sundarji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sundarji "Sundarji"), airlifted a brigade to the region.[^70] [^72] Chinese troops could not move any further into the valley and were forced to move away from the valley.[^73] By 1987, Beijing's reaction was similar to that in 1962 and this prompted many Western diplomats to predict war. However, Indian foreign minister N.D. Tiwari and Prime Minister [Rajiv Gandhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rajiv_Gandhi "Rajiv Gandhi") travelled to Beijing to negotiate a mutual de-escalation.[^70]

India and the PRC held eight rounds of border negotiations between December 1981 and November 1987. In 1985, the PRC insisted on mutual concessions without defining the exact terms of its "package proposal" or where the actual line of control lay. In 1986 and 1987, the negotiations achieved nothing, given the charges exchanged between the two countries of military encroachment in the [Sumdorung Chu Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumdorong_Chu_Valley "Sumdorong Chu Valley"). China's construction of a military post and helicopter pad in the area in 1986 and India's grant of statehood to [Arunachal Pradesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arunachal_Pradesh "Arunachal Pradesh") (formerly the [North-East Frontier Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North-East_Frontier_Agency "North-East Frontier Agency")) in February 1987 caused both sides to deploy troops to the area. The PRC relayed warnings that it would "teach India a lesson" if it did not cease "nibbling" at Chinese territory. By the summer of 1987, however, both sides had backed away from conflict and denied military clashes had taken place.

A warming trend in relations was facilitated by [Rajiv Gandhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rajiv_Gandhi "Rajiv Gandhi") 's visit to China in December 1988. The two sides issued a joint communiqué that stressed the need to restore friendly relations on the basis of the Panchsheel. India and the People's Republic of China agreed to achieve a "fair and reasonable settlement while seeking a mutually acceptable solution" to the border dispute. The communiqué also expressed China's concern about agitation by [Tibetan separatists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_separatism "Tibetan separatism") in India and reiterated that anti-China political activities by expatriate Tibetans would not be tolerated. Rajiv Gandhi signed bilateral agreements on science and technology co-operation, established direct air links, and on cultural exchanges. The two sides also agreed to hold annual diplomatic consultations between foreign ministers, set up a joint committee on economic and scientific co-operation, and a joint working group on the boundary issue. The latter group was to be led by the Indian foreign secretary and the Chinese vice minister of foreign affairs.

### 1990s

Top-level dialogue continued with the December 1991 visit of [PRC premier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Premier of the People's Republic of China") [Li Peng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_Peng "Li Peng") to India and the May 1992 visit to China of Indian president [R. Venkataraman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._Venkataraman "R. Venkataraman"). Six rounds of talks of the Indian-Chinese Joint Working Group on the Border Issue were held between December 1988 and June 1993. Progress was also made in reducing tensions on the border via mutual troop reductions, regular meetings of local military commanders, and advance notification about military exercises. In July 1992, [Sharad Pawar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharad_Pawar "Sharad Pawar") visited Beijing, the first Indian Minister of Defence to do so. Consulates reopened in [Bombay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bombay "Bombay") (Mumbai) and Shanghai in December 1992.

In 1993, the sixth-round of the joint working group talks was held in New Delhi but resulted in only minor developments. Prime Minister [Narasimha Rao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P._V._Narasimha_Rao "P. V. Narasimha Rao") and Premier Li Peng signed a border agreement dealing with cross-border trade, cooperation on environmental issues (e.g. Pollution, [Animal extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered_Species "Endangered Species"), [Global warming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_warming "Global warming"), etc.), and radio and television broadcasting. A senior-level Chinese military delegation made a goodwill visit to India in December 1993 aimed at "fostering confidence-building measures between the defence forces of the two countries." The visit, however, came at a time when China was providing greater military support to Burma. The presence of Chinese radar technicians in Burma's [Coco Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coco_Islands "Coco Islands"), which border India's [Andaman and Nicobar Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andaman_and_Nicobar_Islands "Andaman and Nicobar Islands"), caused concern in India.

In January 1994, Beijing announced that it not only favored a negotiated solution on Kashmir, but also opposed any form of independence for the region. Talks were held in New Delhi in February aimed at confirming established "confidence-building measures", discussing clarification of the "line of actual control", reduction of armed forces along the line, and prior information about forthcoming military exercises. China's hope for settlement of the boundary issue was reiterated.

In 1995, talks by the India-China Expert Group led to an agreement to set up two additional points of contact along the 4,000 km border to facilitate meetings between military personnel. The two sides were reportedly "seriously engaged" in defining the McMahon Line and the line of actual control vis-à-vis military exercises and prevention of air intrusion. Talks were held in Beijing in July and in New Delhi in August to improve border security, combat cross-border crimes, and on additional troop withdrawals from the border. These talks further reduced tensions.[^74]

There was little notice taken in Beijing of the April 1995 announcement of the opening of the Taipei Economic and Cultural Centre in New Delhi. The centre serves as the representative office of the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China") (Taiwan) and is the counterpart of the [India-Taipei Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India-Taipei_Association "India-Taipei Association") located in Taiwan. Both institutions share the goal of improving India-ROC relations, which have been strained since New Delhi's recognition of Beijing in 1950.

China-Indian relations hit a low point in 1998 following [India's nuclear tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pokhran-II "Pokhran-II"). Indian Defence Minister [George Fernandes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Fernandes "George Fernandes") declared that "“in my perception of national security, China is enemy No 1....and any person who is concerned about India's security must agree with that fact",[^75] hinting that India developed nuclear weapons in defence against [China's nuclear arsenal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction "China and weapons of mass destruction"). During the 1999 [Kargil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kargil_War "Kargil War"), China voiced support for Pakistan, but also counseled Pakistan to withdraw its forces.

### 2000s

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Officers_at_Nathu_La.JPG/250px-Officers_at_Nathu_La.JPG)

Indian and Chinese officers at Nathu La. Nathu La was re-opened in 2006 following numerous bilateral trade agreements. The opening of the pass is expected to bolster the economy of the region and play a key role in the growing China-Indian trade.

In a major embarrassment for China, the 17th [Karmapa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karmapa "Karmapa"), [Urgyen Trinley Dorje](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urgyen_Trinley_Dorje "Urgyen Trinley Dorje"), who was proclaimed by China, made a dramatic escape from Tibet to the [Rumtek Monastery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rumtek_Monastery "Rumtek Monastery") in Sikkim. Chinese officials were in a quandary on this issue as any protest to India on the issue would mean an explicit endorsement on India's governance of Sikkim, which the Chinese still hadn't recognised. In 2003, China officially recognised Indian sovereignty over Sikkim as the two countries moved towards resolving their border disputes.

In 2004, the two countries proposed opening up the [Nathula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nathula "Nathula") and [Jelepla Passes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jelepla_Pass "Jelepla Pass") in Sikkim. 2004 was a milestone in China-Indian bilateral trade, surpassing the US$10 billion mark for the first time. In April 2005, Chinese Premier [Wen Jiabao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wen_Jiabao "Wen Jiabao") visited [Bangalore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangalore "Bangalore") to push for increased China-Indian cooperation in high-tech industries. Wen stated that the 21st century will be "the Asian century of the IT industry." Regarding the issue of India gaining a permanent seat on the [UN Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UN_Security_Council "UN Security Council"), Wen Jiabao initially seemed to support the idea, but had returned to a neutral position.

In the [South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Asian_Association_for_Regional_Cooperation "South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation") (SAARC) Summit in 2005, China was granted observer status. While other countries in the region are ready to consider China for permanent membership in the SAARC, India seemed reluctant.

In 2005, China and India signed the ' [Strategic and Cooperative Partnership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_partnerships_of_China "Diplomatic partnerships of China") for Peace and Prosperity'.[^76] However, there has been very little, if any, strategic convergence between the two countries.[^77]

Issues surrounding energy have risen in significance. Both countries have growing energy demand to support economic growth. Both countries signed an agreement in 2006 to envisage [ONGC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ONGC "ONGC") Videsh Ltd (OVL) and the [China National Petroleum Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_National_Petroleum_Corporation "China National Petroleum Corporation") (CNPC) placing joint bids for promising projects.

In 2006, China and India re-opened the [Nathula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nathula "Nathula") pass for trading; Nathula had been closed for 44 years prior to 2006. The re-opening of border trade helps to ease the economic isolation of the region.[^78] In November 2006, China and India had a verbal spat over the claim of the north-east Indian state of [Arunachal Pradesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arunachal_Pradesh "Arunachal Pradesh"). India claimed that China was occupying 38,000 square kilometres of its territory in [Kashmir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashmir "Kashmir"), while China claimed the whole of Arunachal Pradesh as its own.[^79]

In 2007, China denied the application for visa from an [Indian Administrative Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Administrative_Service "Indian Administrative Service") officer in Arunachal Pradesh. According to China, since Arunachal Pradesh is a territory of China, he would not need a visa to visit his own country.[^80] Later in December 2007, China reversed its policy by granting a visa to Marpe Sora, an Arunachal-born professor in computer science.[^81] [^82] In January 2008, Prime Minister [Manmohan Singh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manmohan_Singh "Manmohan Singh") visited China to discuss trade, commerce, defence, military, and various other issues.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Burning_effigies_of_Chinese_leaders.jpg/250px-Burning_effigies_of_Chinese_leaders.jpg)

Hundreds of Tibetans protested against the Chinese government in New Delhi, 2008.

Until 2008, the British Government's position remained the same as it had been since the [Simla Accord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simla_Accord_\(1914\) "Simla Accord (1914)") of 1913: that China held [suzerainty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suzerainty "Suzerainty") over Tibet but not [sovereignty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereignty "Sovereignty"). Britain revised this view on 29 October 2008, when it recognized Chinese sovereignty over Tibet through its website.[^83] [^84] [^85] [The Economist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economist "The Economist") stated that although the British Foreign Office's website does not use the word sovereignty, officials at the Foreign Office said "it means that, as far as Britain is concerned, 'Tibet is part of China. Full stop.'" [^86] This change in Britain's position affects India's claim to its North Eastern territories, which rely on the same Simla Accord that Britain's prior position on Tibet's sovereignty was based upon.[^87]

In October 2009, the [Asian Development Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_Development_Bank "Asian Development Bank") formally acknowledged Arunachal Pradesh as part of India and approved a loan to India for a development project there. Earlier, China had exercised pressure on the bank to cease the loan.[^88] However, India succeeded in securing the loan with the help of the United States and Japan. China expressed displeasure at the [ADB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_Development_Bank "Asian Development Bank").[^89] [^90]

## Triangular relations

The United States and Russia (previously Soviet Union) have been a consistent part of developments in Chinese and Indian relations.

As a major power, Japan has also been part of China–India relations, with initiatives such as the [Quadrilateral Security Dialogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral_Security_Dialogue "Quadrilateral Security Dialogue"). Pakistan and China share warm relations that drive Chinese infrastructure projects in the disputed territory of northern Kashmir. The Middle East, Latin America, and Africa are places where both India and China engage and compete. The Middle East is important to both countries in terms of their energy security. In Africa, China and India seem most engaged across a wide variety of issues, from development to peacekeeping. In South Asia and South-East Asia, a power balance struggle between China and India is seen in triangular relations.[^173]

### Nepal

[Nepal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nepal "Nepal") 's geostrategic situation during periods of heightened tensions between China and India, such as during the 1962 war, resulted in varying shades of cautionary neutrality when making statements. Nepal's [Permanent Representative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permanent_representative "Permanent representative") stated, "we do not want to pass judgement on the merits of Sino-Indian border dispute. As a country friendly to both...". [Tulsi Giri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulsi_Giri "Tulsi Giri"), then the Nepalese foreign minister, stated, "Nepal's relations with India will remain close \[...\] Yet, Nepal has a 600-mile boundary with China, China has now emerged as a great power. Friendly relations with our northern neighbour (China) should be the natural aim of the country's foreign policy." [^174] During the time Tibet was independent, Nepal played the role as an important buffer state and was central to influence between the regions.[^175]

The China-Nepal-India Economic Corridor (CNIEC) was proposed by China in April 2018.[^176] It is an extension of the agreed upon China-Nepal [Trans-Himalayan Multi-dimensional Connectivity Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Himalayan_Multi-dimensional_Connectivity_Network "Trans-Himalayan Multi-dimensional Connectivity Network") into India. While China and Nepal have shown favourable reactions towards CNIEC, India is "indifferent".[^177] This indifference is postulated to stem from CNIEC being a part of [BRI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative"), China's growing influence over Nepal, and an end to "India's monopoly over Nepal's transit points and Nepal's attempt to end its dependency on India".[^177]

Earlier in the meeting, Ghosh recorded Kennedy's reaction to a letter from Nehru, "He indignantly said that only a few months earlier when Mr Nehru was overwhelmed by the power of Communist China, he made desperate appeal to him for air protection, and non-alignment or no non-alignment, the President had to respond. Unlike the left-wing diplomatic leanings of the Indian Congress Party, the [BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bharatiya_Janata_Party "Bharatiya Janata Party") administration has strengthened its strategic partnership with the United States.[^179] China faces combined challenges to its influence with its neighbors, and because it sees a growing threat from India and the United States' cooperation, it has exaggerated the discord between the two countries to [divide and conquer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divide_and_conquer "Divide and conquer") its rivals.[^20]

India participated in the restart of Quadrilateral Security Dialogue,[^180] and has strengthened the military, diplomatic, and economic cooperation with the United States, Japan, and Australia.[^180] More recently, after the deadly Chinese-initiated clash at Galwan Valley in June 2020, preceded by the 2017 Doklam standoff, the U.S.-based think tank, the Center for Foreign Relations, explained that there was a significant ramp-up in QUAD activity.[^181] [^180] [^182] The group cites how, since 2020, the group has looked beyond regional cooperation and have focused on checking Chinese regional behavior, with the four navies performing their first joint exercise in over a decade in November 2020. This was specifically initiated by India in response to the border skirmishes.[^182]

### Indo-Pacific

Pakistan, and more importantly, Myanmar, other than India itself, are potential land routes into the Indian Ocean. Pan Qi, Vice Minister for Communications, wrote in 1985 that China would need to find an outlet for its landlocked provinces. At the time, he suggested routes to the Indian Ocean through Myanmar.[^184]

In the same vein of invigorating relationships with geopolitical allies, India has also strengthened their [Look East Policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Look_East_policy_\(India\) "Look East policy (India)"), transforming it into the Act East Policy under the Modi administration.[^185] [^186] The rebranded Act East Policy has an increased focus on infrastructure projects, such as the Agartala-Akhaura Rail Project and the Asian Trilateral Highway.[^187] Such geopolitical connectivity measures improved India's alliances with the Philippines, Malaysia, and Vietnam to check Chinese dominance.[^186] [^188] These soft power measures also enhance Indian border security.[^185] [^188]

### Western Indian Ocean

The Western Indian Ocean has been characterised as an emerging "geopolitical theatre" [^189] marked by interstate conflict between India, China and the US.[^190] [^189] The regions holds key geostrategic importance to all three states.[^191] Roughly 40 percent of world trade passes through the region as well as two thirds of global oil and gas supplies. It is a main gateway for global energy supply and two of the world's most important maritime chokepoints, the Bab-el Mandeb and Hormuz strait, are both located in the Western Indian Ocean region.[^192] India and China are both dependent on access and free passage in the Western Indian Ocean as it is crucial to secure the increasing energy demand by the two growing economies/ [^192] The US is also dependent on the free passage of oil from the Middle East to the trans-Atlantic world through the region.[^191] [^193]

India and China are both motivated by defensive interests in securing their supplies of energy and trade in the region. Beyond this both states are attempting to increase their relative power positions, economically as well as militarily and naval. This is commonly understood through the terms [String of Pearls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_of_Pearls_\(Indian_Ocean\) "String of Pearls (Indian Ocean)") regarding China [^192] and India's [Act East Policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Act_East_policy "Act East policy").[^194] This could potentially align the two countries against the US, but this does not seem to be the case. India and China continue to strive for similar power positions and power rivalry between the two states continue.[^193] [^195] [^196]

Beijing is refusing to see India as a regional power [^197] and from an Indian perspective the Indian Ocean is largely seen as “India’s Ocean”. Any Chinese encroachment on India's sphere of interest is seen as a potential threat.[^193] [^197] To counter increased Chinese naval and economic presence India has joined the [Quad-alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral_Security_Dialogue "Quadrilateral Security Dialogue") between the US, Japan and Australia and is carrying out naval missions with the US, China's core strategic rival. As such Sino-Indian relations in the Western Indian Ocean can be understood in terms of a triangular relationship of continuous counterbalancing between the three states.

Both India and China have interests in containing the influence of the other, yet none of them has the capacity to fully do this. India's aspirations to become a great regional power are constrained for several reasons. New Delhi's focus has traditionally been directed towards the continent and its naval development is lagging behind both China and the US.[^197] [^194] Scholars also point towards the lack of a coherent maritime strategy despite increased political attention to the issue, for example through its [Act East policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Act_East_policy "Act East policy").[^193] [^190] Political protectionist tendencies have allowed China to increase its relative economic influence overtaking India's position as main trading partner in various countries in the region.[^194] In 2005 China surpassed India and became Bangladesh's main trading partner.[^198] Despite these constraints China does not have the capacity to fully contain India. India continues to hold a special position in the region due to its dominating geographical position, history and interstate relations.[^195] Furthermore, it is in US interests to counterbalance China's increased naval strength through cooperation with India.

As such geopolitical rivalry and strategic counterbalancing remains unsettled in the Western Indian Ocean and intensified rivalry between India and China seems to be the most likely trajectory some time to come.[^195] [^190] [^197] This has substantial security implications as the increased militarisation decreases trust between the states and thus complicates cooperation over persisting security problems such as piracy.[^191] A potential risk of militarisation at sea is that offence-defence tactics are often difficult to distinguish. Even if India and China followed solely defensive strategies, as it has been claimed, it might be perceived as offensive tactics within the maritime domain.[^199] Furthermore, both states are deploying military and naval means beyond what is commonly seen as necessary to defend global shipping routes.[^191] [^189] This includes nuclear-powered submarines send by China to fight piracy in the region.[^191]

## Military relations

### Border clashes

Since the 1950s, during the term of India's first prime minister Jawaharlal Nehru, India and China have repeatedly encountered border disputes at the Line of Actual Control, delineating the border between the two nations.[^200] The first Sino-India border war broke out in 1962 at the Aksai Chin region, escalating into a month-long military conflict.[^200] Initiating the conflict, China took advantage of global concern about the ongoing Cuban missile crisis to time their regional invasion in hopes of building a military road in India's Ladakh region.[^201]

During the Narendra Modi administration, bloody clashes erupted again on the Sino-Indian border.[^181] The strategic alliance between China and Pakistan, and the vigorous arming of the [Pakistani forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistani_forces "Pakistani forces"), make India face the threat of a two-front war.[^202] Indian officials said the huge differences between the two sides on the border issue "eroded the entire basis" of their relationship.[^203]

### Maritime concerns

According to [Center for Strategic and International Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_for_Strategic_and_International_Studies "Center for Strategic and International Studies") (CSIS), since 2020, Chinese research vessels with dual-use capabilities have expanded operations in the Indian Ocean Region, raising concerns. While framed as scientific missions, these surveys—conducted by ships linked to state and military institutions—collect oceanographic data critical for submarine operations and undersea warfare. Vessels such as *Xiang Yang Hong 06* and *Shiyan 06* have mapped the seafloor, deployed sensors, and operated near Indian waters, sometimes while concealing their presence by disabling identification systems.[^204] Despite the evidence presented in a 2024 study conducted by the CSIS, Chinese state-media Global Times dismissed the concerns as a "China Threat Narrative".[^205]

Chinese vessels have operated near sensitive maritime zones and within the EEZs of regional countries, prompting India to increase naval surveillance and diplomatic engagement. In early 2024, Sri Lanka, imposed a temporary moratorium on Chinese research vessels docking at its ports, reflecting growing regional resistance to Beijing's opaque maritime activities.[^204] [^206]

In July 2025, a Chinese research vessel was caught operating covertly in the Bay of Bengal near Indian waters, according to a French maritime intelligence firm. The ship had switched off its [Automatic Identification System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automatic_identification_system "Automatic identification system") (AIS) but was tracked via radio frequency signals. It was likely conducting strategic activities such as seafloor mapping, acoustic analysis, and identifying submarine transit routes—operations linked to surveillance and anti-submarine warfare.[^207] [^208]

China and India conduct a joint military exercise called 'Exercise Hand–in–Hand'.[^209] The exercise began in 2007, with the second edition taking place in 2008.[^210] The third, fifth, and seventh editions of Hand-in-Hand were conducted in China in 2013, 2015, and 2018, respectively, while the fourth edition and sixth editions were held in India in 2014 and 2016.[^211] The eighth edition was held in India in 2019.[^212] While bilateral military exercises between India and China have ceased over the past few years, India is still accepting invitations from Russia for multilateral armed forces exercises that also happen to host Chinese soldiers.[^213]

## Water sharing and hydro–politics

A total of seven rivers that start in Tibet flow through India — [Indus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_River "Indus River"), [Satlej](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sutlej "Sutlej"), [Karnali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karnali_River "Karnali River") (Ghaghara), [Subansiri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subansiri_River "Subansiri River"), [Brahmaputra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmaputra_River "Brahmaputra River"), and [Lohit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lohit_River "Lohit River") (and its tributary Dulai).[^214]

[Brahma Chellaney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahma_Chellaney "Brahma Chellaney") has written that "India's formal recognition of Chinese sovereignty over Tibet constitutes the single biggest security blunder with lasting consequences for Indian territorial and river-water interests".[^215]

India has concern with China's water–diversion, dam–building, and inter–river plans. More so, in a conflict, India fears that China can use the rivers as leverage. China has already constructed ten dams on the Bhramaputra and its tributaries, such as the [Zangmu Dam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zangmu_Dam "Zangmu Dam"), and there has been talk of China building a mega–dam at the "great bend" called the Motuo Dam. India's concerns also stem from the fact that China does not cooperate with regard to timely sharing of information related to projects which would impact water sharing, nor does China allow Indian experts to visit dam sites. There are a number of memoranda of understanding on hydrological data sharing between the two countries with regard to the Brahmaputra, including emergency management.[^216]

An alternate view is also presented in relation to "misinformation spread by some (Indian) newspapers" and the waters of Brahmaputra — "80 percent of the waters of the Brahmaputra emanate from the north side of the Himalayas in China and that that country cannot be the sole arbiter of its international waters. This is not right. Eighty percent of the waters of the mighty Brahmaputra are picked up after it enters India" and that Chinese activities have helped India by reducing annual flood intensity in the north-east.[^214]

India formally “registered its concern with China” in December 2024 over construction of Medog Dam, also known as the [Motuo Hydropower Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motuo_Hydropower_Station "Motuo Hydropower Station"), the country's external affairs minister told lawmakers in March 2025, adding that the project figured prominently in talks between the nations in January 2025.[^217]

In 2025, while India expressed significant concerns over China's construction of the [Motuo Hydropower Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motuo_Hydropower_Station "Motuo Hydropower Station") on the Yarlung Tsangpo River in Tibet, citing potential threats to water security, environmental stability, and regional livelihoods, Indian officials and experts warned that the project could enable China to control or divert river flows into India, affecting the Siang and Brahmaputra rivers, which are vital for millions in the northeastern states. Arunachal Pradesh Chief Minister [Pema Khandu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pema_Khandu "Pema Khandu") described the dam as an "existential threat" to indigenous communities such as the [Adi tribe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adi_people "Adi people"), highlighting risks of water scarcity or catastrophic flooding due to sudden water releases. The Indian government formally urged China to ensure transparency, consult downstream countries, and safeguard the interests of riparian states, framing the issue as a matter of regional cooperation and stability.[^218] Some experts, observers, and individuals from journalistic fraternity from India have also expressed concerns. Dr. Jogendranath Sharma, an eminent geologist and retired professor from [Dibrugarh University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dibrugarh_University "Dibrugarh University"), cautioned that China's proposed 60,000 MW Medog Hydropower Project, located in a seismically active and ecologically sensitive region, could trigger severe natural disasters in downstream Assam. He cited potential risks including erosion from “hungry water,” disruption of groundwater systems, altered monsoon patterns, and threats to biodiversity and livelihoods, and advocated for a joint Indo-China environmental impact assessment.[^219]

On 19 July 2025, the [Yarlung Zangbo hydropower project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yarlung_Zangbo_hydropower_project "Yarlung Zangbo hydropower project") of China officially commenced construction.[^220] India and Bangladesh have raised concerns about the impact in their countries when the dam becomes operational after 2030.[^221]

In August 2025, India reiterated its concerns over China's Medog Hydropower Station, citing risks to regional water security, ecology, and downstream livelihoods. The Ministry of External Affairs stated that the project was being closely monitored and that India had repeatedly urged Beijing to ensure transparency and consult downstream countries. External Affairs Minister [S. Jaishankar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S._Jaishankar "S. Jaishankar") raised the issue during his July 2025 visit to China for the [Shanghai Cooperation Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Cooperation_Organisation "Shanghai Cooperation Organisation") Foreign Ministers’ Meeting, also calling for the resumption of suspended hydrological data sharing.[^222] [^223]

Indian observers have raised concerns about the legality of China's unilateral actions in asserting upstream control over transboundary rivers. Aravind Yelery, associate professor at [Jawaharlal Nehru University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru_University "Jawaharlal Nehru University"), described China's approach as neglecting its responsibility to preserve river flows, suggesting it is driven by geopolitical ambitions and poses environmental and diplomatic risks. He noted similar patterns in China's management of the Mekong River, where multiple dams have been built without formal riparian agreements. Atul Kumar, a fellow at the [Observer Research Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observer_Research_Foundation "Observer Research Foundation"), highlighted that China has not entered into water-sharing treaties with its neighbors and often withholds hydrological data during periods of strained relations.[^224]

## Economic relations

India-China economic relations have been institutionalized through the Joint Economic Group and Business Council, as well as more focused efforts such as the "Agreement on the Avoidance of Double Taxation".[^225]

China and India have developed their own complementary skills following a period in which they had cut themselves off from each other. By 2007, while China excelled at cost-effective manufacturing, India was skilled in cost effective designing and development. In 2007, [Tarun Khanna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarun_Khanna_\(academic\) "Tarun Khanna (academic)") wrote in [Harvard Business Review](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Business_Review "Harvard Business Review") that "The simplest, and most powerful, way of combining China and India is to focus on hardware in China and on software in India." [^226] In the 2009 book "Getting China and India Right", the authors suggest a China plus India strategy so as to strategically benefit from both India's and China's scale, complementary strengths, and reducing the risk of being unilaterally present.[^227]

There are cases when Indian companies have gone to China and done well, such as [Mahindra and Mahindra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahindra_%26_Mahindra "Mahindra & Mahindra"), while Chinese companies such as [Huawei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huawei "Huawei") have done well in India. Huawei set up its Indian unit in 1999 and by 2007, had 1500 engineers. Huawei's Bangalore unit, already one of Huawei's most important research and development centres, was [Capability Maturity Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capability_Maturity_Model "Capability Maturity Model") Level 5 certified in 2003.[^226]

In the oil sector, there is competition and engagement — China's [Sinopec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinopec "Sinopec") and [China National Petroleum Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_National_Petroleum_Corporation "China National Petroleum Corporation") and India's [Oil and Natural Gas Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_and_Natural_Gas_Corporation "Oil and Natural Gas Corporation") fight over oil assets in some regions, while winning bids as joint ventures in others such as Syria, Colombia, Angola, and Venezuela.[^226]

Since the deterioration of relations between the two countries in the 2010s, India has banned a large number of Chinese technology companies and software, including Huawei, [TikTok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TikTok "TikTok"), [WeChat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WeChat "WeChat"), etc., from entering the Indian market.[^228] In order to reduce Western countries' previous dependence on Chinese products, India plays an integral role in boosting supply chain resilience through [friendshoring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friendshoring "Friendshoring").[^229]

On 18 January 2024, at the [World Economic Forum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Economic_Forum "World Economic Forum") 2024 in [Davos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davos "Davos"), Switzerland, top Indian industrial policy bureaucrat Rajesh Kumar Singh told *[Reuters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuters "Reuters")* that due to easing of border tensions between India and China in 2023, India could reduce its heightened scrutiny of Chinese investments in India although timeframe for it was not given.[^230] [^231]

In July 2025, China reportedly recalled over 300 engineers from Foxconn's iPhone plant in India. India responded by partnering with Taiwan, the US, South Korea, and Japan to secure engineers and critical manufacturing equipment.[^232] In December 2025, India imposed [anti-dumping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dumping_\(pricing_policy\) "Dumping (pricing policy)") duties on rolled Chinese steel.[^233]

China's growing economic and political ties with India are reshaping regional dynamics. India's trade with China has reached $155 billion, significantly surpassing trade between China and its other partners. As India's economy expands rapidly, stalled infrastructure projects and investment risks weaken the strategic alliance with China. This shift highlights a changing power balance and growing economic dependence.[^234]

### Bilateral trade

China is India's [second largest trading partner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_largest_trading_partners_of_India "List of the largest trading partners of India").[^235] [^236] [^237] In June 2012, China asserted that "Sino-Indian ties" could be the most "important bilateral partnership of the century", seemingly substantiated by current Prime Minister Narendra Modi's historic visit to China in May 2015.[^238] [^239] At that pivotal visit, [Wen Jiabao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wen_Jiabao "Wen Jiabao"), the [Premier of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Premier of the People's Republic of China"), and [Manmohan Singh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manmohan_Singh "Manmohan Singh"), the [Prime Minister of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_India "Prime Minister of India") set a goal to increase Indo-Chinese bilateral trade to US$100 billion by 2015.[^240] At the India-China Business Forum in Shanghai, Modi claimed that China and India would pool together their manufacturing capabilities, citing initiative examples, such as building “50 million houses by 2022”, "develop\[ing\] smart cities and mega industrial corridors”, and refining FDI policy and modernizing railway systems together.[^239]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Bilateral_trade_between_India_and_China_Indias_imports_from_China_2010_to_2019.png/500px-Bilateral_trade_between_India_and_China_Indias_imports_from_China_2010_to_2019.png)

Bilateral trade between India and China. India's imports from China — 2010 to 2019.

Following that conference, bilateral trade between China and India touched US$89.6 billion in 2017–18, with the trade deficit widening to US$62.9 billion in China's favor.[^241] [^242] In 2017, the volume of bilateral trade between India & China stood at US$84.5 billion.[^243] This figure excludes bilateral trade between India and Hong Kong, which stands at another US$34 billion.[^244] [^245] Chinese imports from India amounted to $16.4 billion, or 0.8% of its overall imports, and 4.2% of India's overall exports in 2014. Major commodities exported from India to China were: cotton; gems, precious metals, coins; copper; ores, slag, ash; organic chemicals; salt, sulphur, stone, cement; machines, engines, and pumps. Chinese exports to India amounted to $58.4 billion or 2.3% of its overall exports, which made up approximately 12.6% of India's overall imports in 2014.[^246] [^245] Major commodities exported from China to India were: electronic equipment; machines, engines, pumps; organic chemicals; fertilizers; iron and steel; plastics; iron or steel products; gems, precious metals, coins; ships, boats; medical, and technical equipment.[^246] [^245]

In 2018, a Standing Committee on Commerce chaired by [Naresh Gujral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naresh_Gujral "Naresh Gujral") submitted a report on the 'Impact of Chinese Goods on Indian Industry.' [^247] The report pointed out insufficient implementation of existing [anti-dumping laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dumping_\(pricing_policy\) "Dumping (pricing policy)"), reliance on Chinese raw materials in sectors such as pharmaceuticals, reliance on Chinese imports in India's [National Solar Mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Solar_Mission "National Solar Mission"), [Goods and Services Tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goods_and_Services_Tax_\(India\) "Goods and Services Tax (India)") on certain products resulting in increased imports from China, and Indian smart city administrations preferring Chinese bicycles over Indian ones.[^247] India imported $65.3 billion worth of goods from China in the fiscal year ended March 2020, and exported $16.6 billion, according to the commerce ministry.[^248]

In 2020, in a departure from the previous pledges of collaboration, Modi instituted bans on Chinese products, including both intangible apps (TikTok) and tangible goods (toys and furniture).[^249] [^250] [^251] Furthermore, the Modi administration created the Phased Manufacturing Program to encourage India-based cellphone assembly and manufacturing.[^249] Under guidance from this legislation and the accompanying task force to reduce import dependence, India raised import duties on electronic items produced in China, as well as a laundry list of ancillary goods.[^249] [^251]

According to a 2021 survey published in *[ThePrint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ThePrint "ThePrint")*, 43% of Indians did not buy ‘Made in China’ products since the Galwan clash the previous year.[^252] Modi also took notice of Swadeshi Jagran Manch's (SJM) campaigns, launching or reviving 220 out of 370 anti-dumping suits against Chinese items ranging from steels, pharmaceuticals, and chemicals.[^121] [^251]

Due to the [international sanctions against Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_sanctions_during_the_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "International sanctions during the Russian invasion of Ukraine") following the [2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine"), Indian [oil refiners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_refiner "Oil refiner") started using Chinese yuan for payments of Russian oil imports as an alternative to the US dollar.[^253] The Indian government also does not welcome the use of yuan for payments by its companies.[^254] Indian automakers have faced shortages due to export restrictions of [China's rare earth metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rare_earth_industry_in_China "Rare earth industry in China").[^255]

Due to rising trade tensions with the US such as the imposition of 50% [tariffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tariffs_in_the_second_Trump_administration "Tariffs in the second Trump administration") over India's [trade with Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India_and_the_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "India and the Russian invasion of Ukraine") on the majority of Indian goods by President [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump"), which took effect in August 2025, India had a diplomatic shift strengthening its ties with China.[^256] [^257] By September 2025, visa issuances have resumed, and India had begun considering Chinese firms to hold 20% to 25% stakes in its manufacturing, renewable energy, and auto parts sectors.[^257] In December 2025, China filed a case against India with the [World Trade Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Trade_Organization "World Trade Organization") over tariffs.[^258]

The author argues that expectations of India emerging as a global power comparable to China have faded over the past decade. Despite early optimism after economic liberalisation, India did not industrialise or grow at a pace similar to China and remains relatively poor on a per-capita basis. As a result, India is no longer seen internationally as China's peer, and domestic political ambition has shifted toward regional rather than global competition. A second shift, the author says, is the erosion of India's democratic character. While elections remain largely competitive, civil liberties have declined, affecting free expression, religion, academia, civil society, and the rule of law. International assessments have downgraded India from a full democracy to a flawed or partly free system. The author concludes that both diminished economic ambition and democratic backsliding have become widely accepted realities in India.[\[2\]](https://www.heraldgoa.in/edit/why-india-no-longer-competes-with-china/461479/) [^259]

India has eased restriction on Chinese investments, aiming to attract foreign capital to support its economy, which is under pressure due to rising energy prices. The relaxation focuses on simplifying the approval process for investments, while maintaining safegaurds for national security.[^260]

## Public opinion

According to a 2014 [BBC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC "BBC") World Service Poll, 23% of Indians viewed China positively, with 47% expressing a negative view, whereas 27% of Chinese people viewed India positively, with 35% expressing a negative view.[^262] A 2014 survey conducted by [Pew Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pew_Research_Center "Pew Research Center") had 39% of Indian respondents holding an unfavourable view of China compared to 31% holding a favourable view;[^263] 72% of the respondents were concerned that territorial disputes between China and neighbouring countries could lead to a military conflict.[^264]

An [Observer Research Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observer_Research_Foundation "Observer Research Foundation") survey released in August 2021 of Indian youth found that 77% of respondents distrusted China, which was higher than any other country, including Pakistan. 86% also supported the government's decision to ban Chinese mobile apps.[^265] An August 2023 survey by [Pew Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pew_Research_Center "Pew Research Center") found that 67% of Indian respondents had an unfavorable view of China.[^266]

A 2008 poll from the Pew Research Center found that in China, views about India were mixed – 25% said India was a partner, while 24% said it was an enemy.[^267] In 2011, Vikas Bajaj of [The New York Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_York_Times "The New York Times") found Chinese media rarely reported about India and general populace lacks awareness and expertise on Indian matters.[^268] A 2023 [Tsinghua University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsinghua_University "Tsinghua University") survey found that 8% of Chinese respondents had a favorable view of India, 50.6% had an unfavorable view, and 41.5% had a neutral view.[^269] [^270]
