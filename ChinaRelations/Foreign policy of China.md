The [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") emerged as a [great power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_power "Great power") and one of the three big players in the tri-polar geopolitics (PRC-US-USSR) during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), after the [Korean War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_War "Korean War") in 1950–1953 and the [Sino-Soviet split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split "Sino-Soviet split") in the 1960s, with its status as a [recognized nuclear weapons state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_states_with_nuclear_weapons "List of states with nuclear weapons"). Currently, China has one of the world's largest populations, second [largest GDP (nominal)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(nominal\) "List of countries by GDP (nominal)") and the [largest economy in the world by PPP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_\(PPP\) "List of countries by GDP (PPP)"). By law, the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) has authority over the country's foreign policy.

In 1950–1953 it fought an undeclared war in Korea against the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"). Until the late 1950s it was allied with the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") but by 1960 they began a bitter contest for control over the local communist movement in many countries. It reached détente with the United States in 1972. After [CCP chairman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party") [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") died in 1976, [Deng Xiaoping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping "Deng Xiaoping") led a massive process of [reform and opening up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reform_and_opening_up "Reform and opening up") and emphasized trade relations with the world, while maintaining a low key, less ideological foreign policy, widely described by the phrase " [hide one's talent and bide one's time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hide_your_strength,_bide_your_time "Hide your strength, bide your time") ".[^1] The [Chinese economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_China "Economy of China") grew very rapidly giving it steadily increasing power and ambition.

Since [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") became [general secretary of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") in 2012, China has expanded its [foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy_of_Xi_Jinping "Foreign policy of Xi Jinping") ambitions on the global scale,[^2] even as it retains an emphasis on 'periphery diplomacy' or its neighborhood.[^3] China is investing heavily in global infrastructure, citing a desire for [economic integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_integration "Economic integration"). It is also investing in strategic locations to secure its trade and security interests. It calls these programs the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative") (formerly "One Belt, One Road") and the " [Maritime Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/21st_Century_Maritime_Silk_Road "21st Century Maritime Silk Road") ", which it sees as part of its goal of self-sufficiency.[^4]

Since 2017 it has engaged in a large-scale [trade war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%E2%80%93United_States_trade_war "China–United States trade war") with the United States. It is also challenging U.S. dominance in the Pacific and Indian Ocean, expanding its [military naval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army_Navy "People's Liberation Army Navy") and diplomatic efforts.[^5] Part of this is the [String of Pearls strategy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_of_Pearls_\(Indian_Ocean\) "String of Pearls (Indian Ocean)") securing strategic locations in the Indian Ocean and [Strait of Malacca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca "Strait of Malacca") region.[^6]

## Foreign policy institutions

According to the [Foreign Relations Law of the People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_Relations_Law_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Foreign Relations Law of the People's Republic of China"), the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) leads the country's foreign policy.[^7] The main institutions of foreign policy are the CCP's [Central Foreign Affairs Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Foreign_Affairs_Commission "Central Foreign Affairs Commission"), [Ministry of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the People's Republic of China"), the [CCP International Liaison Department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Liaison_Department_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "International Liaison Department of the Chinese Communist Party"), and the CCP [United Front Work Department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Front_Work_Department "United Front Work Department").[^8] Generally, the head of the Foreign Affairs Commission's Office has greater authority than the Minister of Foreign Affairs.[^9]<sup><span title="Pages: 77–78">: 77–78</span></sup>

Historically, the [People's Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") had greater influence in China's foreign affairs, particularly during the early years of the PRC.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 17">: 17</span> </sup> The influence of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs within China's foreign policy establishment has increased since 2009.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 7">: 7</span></sup>

The Central Foreign Affairs [Leadership Small Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leading_Small_Group "Leading Small Group") (FALSG) has historically been a semi-institutional foreign policy coordination body.[^10]<sup><span title="Pages: 177–178">: 177–178</span> </sup> Created in 1958, it was disbanded during the [Cultural Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_Revolution "Cultural Revolution") and restored in 1981 as [Deng Xiaoping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping "Deng Xiaoping") increased the number of stakeholders involved in the development of foreign policy.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 177">: 177</span> </sup> It became a forum for the central leadership in charge of foreign policy to meet regularly with top bureaucrats to discuss priorities, achieve consensus, and prepare recommendations for the [CCP Politburo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Politburo of the Chinese Communist Party").[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 178">: 178</span> </sup> It was the only standing foreign policy coordination body until the aftermath of the [United States bombing of the Chinese embassy in Belgrade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_bombing_of_the_Chinese_embassy_in_Belgrade "United States bombing of the Chinese embassy in Belgrade"), which prompted the creation of the Central National Security Leadership Small Group (NSLSG) in 2000 to coordinate national security crisis response.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 178">: 178</span> </sup> In 2018, the FALSG was renamed as the Central Foreign Affairs Commission, which was a politically significant move because a commission ranks higher than a leading small group.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 8">: 8</span></sup>

China's [media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_media_in_China "Mass media in China") policy on foreign affairs issues is handled by the [Central Leading Group for Propaganda, Ideology and Culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Leading_Group_for_Propaganda,_Ideology_and_Culture "Central Leading Group for Propaganda, Ideology and Culture") in consultation with the Central Foreign Affairs Commission.[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 73">: 73</span> </sup> Long-term and medium-term media policies on foreign affairs matters are announced at the National Propaganda Thought Work Meetings and the annual National Conferences of Publicity Department Directors.[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 74">: 74</span></sup>

To address policy coordination on maritime issues, [CCP general secretary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") [Hu Jintao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hu_Jintao "Hu Jintao") created The Protecting Maritime Rights and Interests LSG in 2012.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 179">: 179</span></sup>

In his effort to build additional institutional capacity for foreign policy coordination, Xi Jinping created the [National Security Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Security_Commission_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "National Security Commission of the Chinese Communist Party") (NSC), which absorbed the NSLG.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 180">: 180</span> </sup> The NSC's focus is holistic national security and it addresses both external and internal security matters.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 180">: 180</span> </sup> Xi introduced the [holistic security concept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holistic_security_concept "Holistic security concept") in 2014, which he defined as taking "the security of the people as compass, political security as its roots, economic security as its pillar, military security, cultural security, and cultural security as its protections, and that relies on the promotion of international security." [^12]<sup><span title="Page: 3">: 3</span></sup>

Although various actors in the Chinese party-state contest foreign policy decision making, Chinese foreign policy is overwhelmingly top-down in decision-making and implementation.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 2">: 2</span></sup>

China traditionally operates separate tracks of government-to-government and party-to-party relations, the latter for example via the CCP's International Liaison Department.[^14]<sup><span title="Page: 137">: 137</span></sup>

On security issues, China generally prioritizes military-to-military exchanges.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 217">: 217</span> </sup> China typically views military personnel as more effective interlocutors on security matters than civil personnel.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 217">: 217</span></sup>

## International relations framework

Chinese scholars observe that the country's international relations differentiate between major powers, states on China's strategic periphery, developing countries, and multilateral international fora.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 4">: 4</span> </sup> These categorizations are not strict and multiple may apply.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 4">: 4</span> </sup> The major powers are the economically developed states, including the United States, Russia, Japan, United Kingdom, and various European Union states as well as the European Union as a whole.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 4">: 4</span> </sup> Strategic periphery countries are generally geographically proximate ones, traditionally including Russia, East Asia, Central Asia, South Asia, and Southeast Asia.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 4">: 4</span> </sup> China began increasing its focus on peripheral diplomacy during the Hu Jintao era.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 35">: 35</span> </sup> during the Xi Jinping era, the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative") is closely associated with China's peripheral diplomacy.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 35">: 35</span></sup>

Generally, China tends to take the side of developing countries.[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 26">: 26</span> </sup> In the broader category of developing countries, China distinguishes between major developing states, newly emerging powers, and other developing states.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 5">: 5</span></sup>

China establishes bilateral [partnerships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_partnerships_of_China "Diplomatic partnerships of China") with other countries that range from broad strategic partnerships to those with more discrete areas of cooperation.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 25">: 25</span> </sup> "Comprehensive strategic partnerships" are the broadest, with "strategic partnerships" and "comprehensive cooperative partnerships" used to describe relations with less broad cooperation.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 25">: 25</span> </sup> China generally avoids signing formal alliances and bilateral treaties.[^17]<sup><span title="Page: 107">: 107</span> </sup> It describes this position as "relationship based on partnership rather than alliance" (*jieban bujiemeng*).[^18]<sup><span title="Page: 1">: 1</span></sup>

Chinese foreign policy documents with countries it views as strategic partners frequently assert the concepts of the validity of different developmental paths, different understandings of democracy or human rights, and noninterference in domestic affairs.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 4">: 4</span> </sup> In its free trade agreements and global engagement on [intellectual property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellectual_property_in_China "Intellectual property in China") issues, it takes a position of non-imposition on intellectual property matters.[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 28">: 28</span> </sup> Although it is willing to accept increased standards itself, China does not generally seek to impose standards in excess of the [TRIPS Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TRIPS_Agreement "TRIPS Agreement").[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 28">: 28</span></sup>

Within the [Global South](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_North_and_Global_South "Global North and Global South"), China focuses particularly on countries it views as hub states in each region.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 5">: 5</span> </sup> Although Chinese diplomatic understandings change over time, hub states have often included Indonesia, Singapore, and Thailand (in Southeast Asia); India and Pakistan (in South Asia); Kazakhstan in Central Asia; Egypt, Ethiopia, and Nigeria (in Africa), Iran and Saudi Arabia (in the Middle East); and Argentina and Brazil (in Latin America).[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 5">: 5</span> </sup> China conducts good relations including with hub states that are regional rivals with each other, often seeking to use [summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summit_\(diplomacy\) "Summit (diplomacy)") diplomacy and regional cooperation forums in an effort to reduce tensions.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 150">: 150</span></sup>

China provides [foreign aid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_foreign_aid "Chinese foreign aid") to advance both foreign policy objectives and [foreign trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade_policy_of_China "Trade policy of China") objectives.[^19]<sup><span title="Page: 18">: 18</span> </sup> Since the 2018 creation of the [China International Development Cooperation Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_International_Development_Cooperation_Agency "China International Development Cooperation Agency") to coordinate aid, China has placed greater emphasis on aid to facilitate foreign policy and a lesser emphasis on aid to advance foreign trade.[^19]<sup><span title="Page: 18">: 18</span></sup>

During the Xi Jinping administration, China seeks to shape international norms and rules in emerging policy areas where China has an advantage as an early participant.[^20]<sup><span title="Page: 188">: 188</span> </sup> Xi describes such areas as "new frontiers," and they include policy areas such as space, deep sea, polar regions, the Internet, nuclear safety, anticorruption, and climate change.[^20]<sup><span title="Page: 188">: 188</span></sup>

### Problems of translation

A 2022 study published by [Leiden University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leiden_University "Leiden University") found that almost half of the translated foreign-policy documents of the PRC from 2013 to 2019 had differences between the English and Chinese versions that might change how a reader views PRC intentions.[^21] [^22]

## Long-term goals

Since the late 1970s, China's primary foreign policy goals are safeguarding its independence, sovereignty, territorial integrity, and shaping an international environment favorable to its modernization and [reform and opening up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reform_and_opening_up "Reform and opening up").[^23]<sup><span title="Pages: 51">: 51</span></sup>

China uses the term [core interests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%27s_core_interests "China's core interests") to define the primary goals that determine its foreign policy choices.[^24]<sup><span title="Page: 193">: 193</span> </sup> This concept was developed by diplomat [Dai Bingguo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dai_Bingguo "Dai Bingguo") during Hu Jintao's administration.[^24]<sup><span title="Page: 193">: 193</span> </sup> The core interests are: maintaining the power of the CCP, continuing China's social and economic growth, and preservation of China's sovereignty and territorial integrity.[^24]<sup><span title="Page: 193">: 193</span> </sup> China also views these core interests as red lines that other countries' behavior should not cross.[^24]<sup><span title="Page: 193">: 193</span> </sup> China's emphasis on national sovereignty arises from its historical experiences following the [Opium Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium_Wars "Opium Wars").[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 51">: 51</span></sup>

During the Xi Jinping era, the [Community of Common Destiny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_of_Common_Destiny "Community of Common Destiny") has become China's most important foreign relations formulation.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 6">: 6</span></sup>

Political scientist Dmitry Shlapentokh argues that Xi and his top leadership are developing plans for global predominance based on rapidly growing economic power. The ideological framework is a specialized blend of [Marxism–Leninism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism%E2%80%93Leninism "Marxism–Leninism"), coupled with China's pre-1800 historic claims to world dominance. China's trade policy and drive for access to essential natural resources, such as gas, are articulated in terms of these ideological approaches. Beijing balances both purely economic goals with geopolitical strategies regarding the United States, Russia and other powers. According to Shlapentokh, balancing those two powers gives China a clear advantage, for its totalitarian government could plan for generations and could change course regardless of the wishes of the electorate or clearly defined interest groups, as is the case with the modern capitalist West.[^25]

Lowell Dittmer argues that in dealing with the goal of dominance over East Asia, Beijing has to juggle its relations with the United States, which has more military and economic power in the region because of close U.S. ties with Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Vietnam, Australia and other countries.[^26]

Analysts argue that Beijing is not yet ready to become a major force in shaping regional politics in the Middle East.[^27] [^28] [^29] While China has major commercial interests in the Middle East and a long history of relations with [Gulf Cooperation Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Cooperation_Council "Gulf Cooperation Council") countries, its involvement in Gulf security affairs is relatively recent.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 30">: 30</span> </sup> China articulates a "zero-enemy policy" in the Gulf region, taking a balanced attitude towards both incumbent governments and opposition forces, Sunni and Shi'a, republics and monarchies, and Iran and the Arab countries.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 32">: 32</span> </sup> Some scholars suggest that China's diplomatic strategy in the Middle East is best understood through “role theory,” which frames Beijing's actions as a negotiated performance between its self-perception and the expectations of others. According to a 2025 review of *China’s Changing Role in the Middle East: Filling a Power Vacuum?*, China avoids military entanglements while expanding influence through infrastructure projects, symbolic neutrality, and adaptive diplomacy that balances ties between rival actors such as Iran and the Gulf States.[^31]

Since the late 1990s, China has articulated its [new security concept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_security_concept "New security concept"), the overarching principle of which is that no single state, even the most powerful, is capable of coping with all security challenges alone.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 71">: 71</span></sup>

China has shown a moderate interest in the Caribbean region in recent years, but not nearly on the same scale as its interest in Asia and Africa. It has been developing ties with Cuba, the Bahamas, Jamaica, the Dominican Republic and Haiti, as well as Colombia. These small countries have not by 2019 noticeably changed their foreign or domestic policies because of their new economic linkages with China. Nevertheless, the governments pay more attention to Beijing's views. On the other hand, China's push into the Caribbean is increasingly resented by the United States and further escalation between the two major powers is a possibility in the region.[^32] [^33]

In 2014, Xi announced the New Asian Security Concept at a summit of the [Conference on Interaction and Confidence-Building in Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conference_on_Interaction_and_Confidence-Building_Measures_in_Asia "Conference on Interaction and Confidence-Building Measures in Asia") (CICA).[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 97">: 97</span> </sup> Implying that Asian countries can handle their security without the involvement of the United States, the core of the New Asian Security Concept is that "Asian issues should be taken care of by Asians, and Asian security should be maintained by Asians." [^10]<sup><span title="Page: 97">: 97</span></sup>

China is generally skeptical about the efficacy of sanctions, often characterizing them as a Western instrument of oppression that escalates tensions.[^23]<sup><span title="Pages: 107–108">: 107–108</span> </sup> China opposes the use of unilateral sanctions and trade discrimination to achieve foreign policy goals and has generally positioned itself as a proponent of global free trade.[^34] China contends that economic interdependence reduces the risk of conflict between countries.[^18]<sup><span title="Page: 61">: 61</span> </sup> China advocates for the role of the WTO as the primary multilateral trade forum.[^16]<sup><span title="Page: 153">: 153</span> </sup> The pace of China's bilateral [free trade agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_trade_agreement "Free trade agreement") negotiations has accelerated since 2007, when free trade agreements were identified as a national priority following the CCP's [Seventeenth National Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/17th_National_Congress_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "17th National Congress of the Chinese Communist Party").[^16]

## Non-interventionism

In its foreign policy, China emphasizes the principle of [non-interventionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-interventionism "Non-interventionism").[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 336">: 336</span> </sup> Its emphasis on non-intervention is based on the [UN Charter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charter_of_the_United_Nations "Charter of the United Nations") -enshrined idea that of respect for the sovereignty of member states.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 71">: 71</span> </sup> China has articulated the principle of non-interventionism since the 1950 [Sino-Soviet Treaty of Friendship, Alliance, and Mutual Assistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_Treaty_of_Friendship,_Alliance_and_Mutual_Assistance "Sino-Soviet Treaty of Friendship, Alliance and Mutual Assistance") and made it central component of foreign policy after its inclusion in the 1953 [Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Principles_of_Peaceful_Coexistence "Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence").[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 56">: 56</span> </sup> China's view is that international intervention, especially the use of force under Chapter IV of the UN Charter, is only legitimate with both authorization of the [Security Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council") and the consent of the parties involved.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 107">: 107</span></sup>

China also contends that many internal crises or conflicts are beyond the scope of the Security Council's mandate.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 107">: 107</span> </sup> Generally, it seeks to avoid siding with unilaterally with a particular camp in regional conflicts.[^18]<sup><span title="Page: 25">: 25</span> </sup> As a corollary to China's principles of non-interventionism, China asserts that other countries must not involve themselves in matters that China deems as its own domestic affairs.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 336">: 336</span></sup>

As part of its non-interventionism, China does not support opposition movements in other countries, particularly those advocating separatism.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 71">: 71</span></sup>

When China provides debt relief, it generally does not require recipient countries to alter their domestic macroeconomic policy.[^19]<sup><span title="Pages: 134–135">: 134–135</span> </sup> China views its position in this regard as consistent with its non-interventionism.[^19]<sup><span title="Pages: 134–135">: 134–135</span></sup>

China's approach to non-interventionism has been particularly well received in [its relations with African countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-African_relations "Sino-African relations").[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 336">: 336</span> </sup> At the 2018 [Forum on China-Africa Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forum_on_China%E2%80%93Africa_Cooperation "Forum on China–Africa Cooperation"), Xi Jinping emphasized the "Five Nos" which guide its foreign policy in dealing with African countries and other developing countries: (1) non-interference in other countries' pursuit of development paths suitable to their national conditions, (2) non-interference in domestic affairs, (3) not imposing China's will on others, (4) not attaching political conditions to foreign aid, and (5) not seeking political self-interest in investment and financing.[^23]<sup><span title="Pages: 108–109">: 108–109</span></sup>

At times, China has modified its approach to non-intervention, particularly because of political issues involving Africa.[^12]<sup><span title="Pages: 336–338">: 336–338</span> </sup> These changes began in 2006, when under pressure from Western governments and the [2008 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics "2008 Summer Olympics") in Beijing, China altered its position on the [Darfur conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darfur_conflict "Darfur conflict").[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 338">: 338</span> </sup> Chinese leader Hu Jintao visited Sudan and met with Sudanese President [Omar al-Bashir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omar_al-Bashir "Omar al-Bashir"), who afterwards accepted the [United Nations-Africa Union peacekeeping mission in Darfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations%E2%80%93African_Union_Mission_in_Darfur "United Nations–African Union Mission in Darfur").[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 338">: 338</span></sup>

China also modified its typically non-interventionist approach during the 2011 Libyan crisis, when pressure from the [African Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Union "African Union") and the [Arab League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_League "Arab League") prompted China to support an arms embargo, travel ban, and asset freeze and to abstain from voting on [United Nations Security Council Resolution 1973](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_1973 "United Nations Security Council Resolution 1973"), which established a no-fly zone.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 338">: 338</span> </sup> The necessity of evacuating over 35,000 Chinese citizens in a period of ten days during the crisis also shaped China's policy approach to conflict zones.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 78">: 78</span></sup>

## South-South cooperation

China's emphasis on [South-South cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South%E2%80%93South_cooperation "South–South cooperation") developed from the [1955 Bandung Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bandung_Conference "Bandung Conference"), where it cultivated ties with other [developing countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country") as a hedge against the United States and the Soviet Union.[^35]<sup><span title="Page: 74">: 74</span></sup>

During the [administration of Hu Jintao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hu%E2%80%93Wen_Administration "Hu–Wen Administration") between 2002 and 2012, China began to increase the importance it placed on relations with Global South countries.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 79">: 79</span> </sup> This trend has continued during the [general secretaryship of Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_secretaryship_of_Xi_Jinping "General secretaryship of Xi Jinping") since 2012.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 79">: 79</span> </sup> As part of its view of South-South Cooperation, China often votes in the United Nations based on the position of regional groups like the African Union or the Arab League.[^23]<sup><span title="Page: 82">: 82</span></sup>

China has a major role in fostering cooperation among the global south countries in the area of climate change and clean energy.[^36]<sup><span title="Page: 18">: 18</span> </sup> China engaged in South-South climate and clean energy cooperation through: (1) bilateral clean energy agreements, (2) multilateral clean energy cooperation, (3) expanding exports of its clean energy technology to other developing countries, and (4) foreign energy infrastructure development via the Belt and Road Initiative.[^36]<sup><span title="Page: 221">: 221</span> </sup> Much of China's overall South-South Cooperation is now explicitly linked to the BRI.[^36]<sup><span title="Page: 226">: 226</span> </sup> The BRI is likewise tied to the concepts of the [Chinese dream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Dream "Chinese Dream") and the Community of Common Destiny.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 35">: 35</span></sup>

The "Ten, Hundred, Thousand" program is China's overarching initiative for South-South cooperation in addressing climate change.[^36]<sup><span title="Page: 224">: 224</span> </sup> As of 2023, China had signed partnerships with at least 27 other developing countries as part of this initiative.[^36]<sup><span title="Page: 224">: 224</span> </sup> Through framing BRI and other economic mechanisms as South-South cooperation, China seeks to position itself as a global South leader and draw a contrast with the global North countries.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 125">: 125</span></sup>

[BRICS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BRICS "BRICS") is a further mechanism for China's cooperation with the global south and functions as a forum for policy coordination among its members.[^37]<sup><span title="Page: 249">: 249</span></sup>

Publishing in 2024, academics Xinru Ma and David C. Kang write that in its South-South relations, China does not attempt to export its ideology or leadership to other countries.[^38]<sup><span title="Page: 181">: 181</span></sup>

## Diplomatic style

The Chinese diplomatic style generally downplays the significance of individual diplomats and their personalities.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 138">: 138</span></sup>

China has generally become more assertive in foreign affairs since 2008, particularly in its relations with the United States, Europe, and the Asia-Pacific countries.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 15">: 15</span> </sup> According to academic [Suisheng Zhao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suisheng_Zhao "Suisheng Zhao"), China is motivated in part by the desire to restore itself from a self-perceived state of victimhood in international affairs.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 15">: 15</span></sup>

### Wolf warrior diplomacy

[Wolf warrior diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf_warrior_diplomacy "Wolf warrior diplomacy") is a confrontational form of [public diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_diplomacy "Public diplomacy") adopted by diplomats of the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") in the late 2010s and early 2020s.[^39] [^40] [^41] The phrase is derived from the Chinese action film franchise *[Wolf Warrior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf_Warrior "Wolf Warrior")* (2015) and its [2017 sequel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf_Warrior_2 "Wolf Warrior 2").[^42] [^41] [^43] This coercive style of diplomacy [^44] has been in contrast both to the narrative of " [China's peaceful rise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China%27s_peaceful_rise "China's peaceful rise") " and to diplomatic practices which emphasize the use of cooperative rhetoric and the avoidance of controversy (often termed *[Taoguang Yanghui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoguang_Yanghui "Taoguang Yanghui")*), financial aid ([checkbook diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Checkbook_diplomacy "Checkbook diplomacy")), the provision of medical supplies such as COVID-19 masks ([medical diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_diplomacy "Medical diplomacy")), and [panda diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panda_diplomacy "Panda diplomacy").[^39] [^45] [^46]

## Status of Taiwan

The People's Republic of China (PRC) considers [Taiwan area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_area_of_the_Republic_of_China "Free area of the Republic of China") administered by [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China") (ROC), part of its inviolable sovereign territory. The ROC was founded in mainland China in 1912 [deposing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1911_Revolution "1911 Revolution") the Chinese monarchy while [Taiwan was under Japanese rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_under_Japanese_rule "Taiwan under Japanese rule") from 1895 and regained in 1945, but Taiwan became the seat of the [ROC central government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_the_Republic_of_China "Government of the Republic of China") since 1949 and later lost its international representation as ["China"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_and_the_United_Nations "China and the United Nations") in the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") in 1971 to the PRC.

Along with the ROC, in PRC's perspective, Taiwan is described a [separatist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_independence_movement "Taiwan independence movement"), [breakaway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_Province,_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Taiwan Province, People's Republic of China") [province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_\(Republic_of_China_province\) "Taiwan (Republic of China province)") that must be [reunified](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_unification "Chinese unification"), by force if necessary. The PRC considers territorial integrity and national unity as the central focus of its cross-strait policies.[^47]<sup><span title="Page: 1">: 1</span> </sup> It has adjusted its cross-strait policies over time based on the economic and foreign policy environments, including domestic politics on Taiwan.[^47]<sup><span title="Page: 1">: 1</span> </sup> The PRC engages more closely with Taiwan when the [Kuomintang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuomintang "Kuomintang") (which ostensibly supports [Chinese unification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_unification "Chinese unification") and favors closer ties with the PRC) are in power in Taiwan, and is more hostile when the [Democratic Progressive Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Progressive_Party "Democratic Progressive Party") (which the PRC construes as independence minded and collaborating with foreign powers) is in power in Taiwan.[^48]<sup><span title="Page: 125">: 125</span></sup>

Under the [One China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China "One China") policy, any polity exerts efforts for countries recognizing either the PRC or ROC to switch their recognition to the other.[^49] [^50] [^51] [^52] [^53] [^54]

In 2005, the PRC has passed the [Anti-Secession Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-Secession_Law "Anti-Secession Law") authorizing the use of military force in the event of unilateral separatist activity.[^55]

## South China Sea

China has staked its territorial claims in the [disputed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_disputes_in_the_South_China_Sea "Territorial disputes in the South China Sea") [South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_China_Sea "South China Sea") with the [nine-dash line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nine-dash_line "Nine-dash line"). Its claims are disputed by other countries.[^56] The contested area in the South China Sea includes the [Paracel Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracel_Islands "Paracel Islands"), the [Spratly Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spratly_Islands "Spratly Islands"),[^57] and various other areas including [Pratas Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pratas_Island "Pratas Island") and the Vereker Banks, the [Macclesfield Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macclesfield_Bank "Macclesfield Bank") and the [Scarborough Shoal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scarborough_Shoal "Scarborough Shoal"). The claim encompasses the area of Chinese [land reclamation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_reclamation "Land reclamation") known as the " [Great Wall of Sand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Wall_of_Sand "Great Wall of Sand") ".[^58] [^59] [^60]

The [United States Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Navy "United States Navy") has conducted [freedom of navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_navigation#United_States "Freedom of navigation") operations asserting its position that some waters claimed by China are [international waters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_waters "International waters").[^61]

On July 12, 2016, an arbitral tribunal constituted under Annex VII to the 1982 [United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea "United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea") ruled that China has no legal basis to claim "historic rights" within its nine-dash line in a [case brought by the Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines_v._China "Philippines v. China"). The tribunal judged that there was no evidence that China had historically exercised exclusive control over the waters or resources within the Nine-Dash Line.[^62]

The ruling was rejected by both Taiwan and China.[^63] [^64] The People's Republic of China and the Republic of China (Taiwan) stated that they did not recognize the tribunal and insisted that the matter should be resolved through bilateral negotiations with other claimants.[^65] However, the tribunal did not rule on the ownership of the islands or delimit maritime boundaries.[^66] [^62]

Scholars have been probing the Chinese motivations and long-term expectations. One approach is to compare trends in multilateral Code of Conduct negotiations between 1992 and 2016. In general, the sovereignty issue regarding contested waters is no longer a central major concern. For three reasons: the inconsistency of China's official claims over time, China's increased bargaining power, and the importance of the shelved sovereignty axiom since the era of Deng Xiaoping.[^67] [^68]
