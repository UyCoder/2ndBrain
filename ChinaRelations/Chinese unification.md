**Chinese unification**, also known as **Cross-strait unification** or **Chinese reunification**, is the hypothetical unification of territories currently controlled, or claimed, by the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") ("China" or "Mainland China") and the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") ("Taiwan") under one political entity, possibly the formation of a [political union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_union "Political union") between the two republics. Together with [full Taiwan independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_independence_movement "Taiwan independence movement"), unification is one of the main proposals to address questions on the [political status of Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_status_of_Taiwan "Political status of Taiwan"), which is the central focus of [Cross-strait relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-strait_relations "Cross-strait relations").

## Background

In 1895, the [Manchu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchu_people "Manchu people") -led [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") of China lost the [First Sino-Japanese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Sino-Japanese_War "First Sino-Japanese War") and was forced to cede [Taiwan and Penghu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_under_Qing_rule "Taiwan under Qing rule") to the [Empire of Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_Japan "Empire of Japan") after signing the [Treaty of Shimonoseki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Shimonoseki "Treaty of Shimonoseki"). In 1912, the Qing dynasty was overthrown and was succeeded by the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_\(1912%E2%80%931949\) "Republic of China (1912–1949)") (ROC). Based on the theory of the [succession of states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Succession_of_states "Succession of states"), the ROC originally lay claim to the entire territory which belonged to the Qing dynasty during the time of its collapse, except for Taiwan, which the ROC recognized as belonging to the Empire of Japan at the time. The ROC managed to attain widespread recognition as the legitimate successor state to the Qing dynasty during the years following the fall of the Qing dynasty.

In the year 1945, the ROC won the [Second Sino-Japanese War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War "Second Sino-Japanese War"), which was intertwined with [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), and took control of [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_under_Japanese_rule "Taiwan under Japanese rule") [on behalf of](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Order_No._1 "General Order No. 1") the [Allied Powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II "Allies of World War II"), following the Japanese surrender. The ROC immediately asserted its claim to Taiwan as " [Taiwan Province, Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_\(Republic_of_China_province\) "Taiwan (Republic of China province)") ", basing its claim on the [Potsdam Declaration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potsdam_Declaration "Potsdam Declaration") and the [Cairo Communique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1943_Cairo_Declaration "1943 Cairo Declaration"). Around this time, the ROC nullified the Treaty of Shimonoseki, declaring it to be one of the many " [Unequal Treaties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unequal_treaty "Unequal treaty") " imposed on China during the so-called " [Century of Humiliation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Century_of_humiliation "Century of humiliation") ". At the time, the [Kuomintang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuomintang "Kuomintang") (KMT) was the ruling party of the ROC, and was widely recognized as its legitimate representative, especially due to the collaboration of its leader [Chiang Kai-shek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek "Chiang Kai-shek") with the Allied Powers.

However, throughout much of the rule of the ROC, China had been internally divided during a period called the [Warlord Era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warlord_Era "Warlord Era"). According to the common narrative, the ROC was divided into many different [ruling cliques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruling_clique "Ruling clique") and [secessionist states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secession_in_China "Secession in China"), which were in a constant struggle following the [power vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_vacuum "Power vacuum") which was created after the overthrowing of the Qing Dynasty. During this period, two ruling cliques eventually came out on top; that of the KMT, backed by the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), and that of the [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP), backed by the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"). The power struggle between these two specific political parties has come to be known as the [Chinese Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War "Chinese Civil War"). The Chinese Civil War was fought sporadically throughout the ROC's history; it was interrupted by the Second Sino-Japanese War.

After the Second Sino-Japanese War concluded, the Chinese Civil War resumed, and the CCP quickly gained a huge advantage over the KMT (ruling the ROC). In 1949, the KMT [evacuated its government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuomintang%27s_retreat_to_Taiwan "Kuomintang's retreat to Taiwan"), its military, and around 1.2–2 million loyal citizens to Taiwan, which had only been ruled by the KMT for around four years by this time. Back in mainland China, the CCP proclaimed the "People's Republic of China (PRC)", effectively creating a reality of [Two Chinas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two_Chinas "Two Chinas"). Following the creation of Two Chinas, the PRC began to fight a diplomatic war against the [ROC on Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_on_Taiwan "Republic of China on Taiwan") over official recognition as the sole legitimate government of China. Eventually, the PRC (mostly) won this war, and ascended to the position of " [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_and_the_United_Nations "China and the United Nations") " in the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") in 1971, evicting the ROC from that position.

As a result, the ROC still governed Taiwan but was no longer recognized as a [member state of the United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_states_of_the_United_Nations "Member states of the United Nations"). In recent years, membership in the United Nations has become almost an essential qualifier of statehood. Most [states with limited recognition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_states_with_limited_recognition "List of states with limited recognition") are not at all recognized by most governments and intergovernmental organizations. However, the ROC is a unique case, given that it has still managed to attain [a significant degree of unofficial international recognition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_Taiwan "Foreign relations of Taiwan"), even though most countries do not officially recognize it as a sovereign state. This is mainly due to the fact that the ROC was previously recognized as the legitimate government of China, providing an extensive framework for unofficial diplomatic relations to be conducted between the ROC and other countries.

In the years following the ROC's retreat to Taiwan, Taiwan has gone through a series of significant social, political, economic, and cultural shifts, strengthening the divide between Taiwan and mainland China. This has been further exacerbated by Taiwan's history as a colony of the Japanese Empire, which led to the establishment of a unique Taiwanese identity and the desire for [Taiwan independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_independence_movement "Taiwan independence movement"). The Taiwan independence movement has grown considerably stronger in recent decades, and has especially become a viable force on the island ever since the ROC's transition to a [multi-party system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-party_system "Multi-party system"), during what has become known as the [Democratization of Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Taiwan#Republic_of_China_\(1945%E2%80%93present\) "History of Taiwan").

The PRC has never recognized the sovereignty of Taiwan. PRC asserts that the ROC ceased to exist in the year 1949 when the PRC was proclaimed. Officially, PRC refers to the territory controlled by Taiwan as [Taiwan area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan,_China "Taiwan, China"), and to the government of Taiwan as the Taiwan authorities. PRC continues to claim Taiwan as its 23rd Province, and the Fujianese territories still under Taiwanese control as parts of [Fujian Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fujian "Fujian"). PRC has established the [one China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China "One China") in order to clarify its intention. The CCP classifies Taiwan independence supporters as one of the [Five Poisons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Poisons "Five Poisons").[^1] [^2] In 2005, the [10th National People's Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10th_National_People%27s_Congress "10th National People's Congress") passed the [Anti-Secession Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-Secession_Law "Anti-Secession Law") authorizing military force for unification.[^3]

Most Taiwanese people oppose joining PRC for various reasons, including fears of the loss of Taiwan's [democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy "Democracy"), [human rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_rights "Human rights"), and [Taiwanese nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwanese_nationalism "Taiwanese nationalism"). Opponents either favor maintaining the status quo of the Republic of China administrating Taiwan or the pursuit of Taiwan independence.[^4] The [Constitution of the Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Republic_of_China "Constitution of the Republic of China") states that its territory includes the mainland,[^5] but the official policy of the Taiwanese government is dependent on which coalition is currently in power. The position of the [Pan-Blue Coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan-Blue_Coalition "Pan-Blue Coalition"), which comprises the Kuomintang, the [People First Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People_First_Party_\(Taiwan\) "People First Party (Taiwan)") and the [New Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Party_\(Taiwan\) "New Party (Taiwan)") is to eventually incorporate the mainland into the ROC, while the position of the [Pan-Green Coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan-Green_Coalition "Pan-Green Coalition"), composed of the [Democratic Progressive Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Progressive_Party "Democratic Progressive Party") (DPP) and the [Taiwan Solidarity Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_Solidarity_Union "Taiwan Solidarity Union"), is to pursue Taiwanese independence.[^6]

In 2024, the Chinese government issued a directive to the courts stating that ["diehard" independence supporters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diehard_%22Taiwan_independence%22_separatists "Diehard \"Taiwan independence\" separatists") could be [tried *in absentia*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_in_absentia "Trial in absentia") with [capital punishment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_China "Capital punishment in China") imposed.[^7] [^8]

## History

### Mainland China

The concept of Chinese unification was developed in the 1970s as part of the CCP's strategy to address the " [Taiwan issue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_status_of_Taiwan "Political status of Taiwan") " as China started to normalize [foreign relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_China "Foreign relations of China") with a number of countries including the United States and Japan.[^9] [^10]

According to the state-run [China Internet Information Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Internet_Information_Center "China Internet Information Center"), in 1979, the [National People's Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_People%27s_Congress "National People's Congress") published the [Message to Compatriots in Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Message_to_Compatriots_in_Taiwan "Message to Compatriots in Taiwan") (告台湾同胞书) which included the term "Chinese reunification" as an ideal for [Cross-Strait relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-Strait_relations "Cross-Strait relations").[^11] In 1981, the chairman of the People's Congress Standing Committee [Ye Jianying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ye_Jianying "Ye Jianying") announced the "Nine Policies" for China's stance on Cross-Strait relations, with "Chinese Peaceful Unification" (祖国和平统一) as the first policy.[^12] According to Xinhua, since then, " [one country, two systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_country,_two_systems "One country, two systems") " and "Chinese reunification" have been emphasized at every [National Congress of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Congress_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "National Congress of the Chinese Communist Party") as the principles to deal with [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong"), [Macau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macau "Macau") and [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan"). "One Country, Two Systems" is specifically about China's policy towards post-colonial Hong Kong and Macao, and "Chinese Unification" is specifically about Taiwan.[^13] Taiwan has also been offered the resolution of "One Country, Two Systems".[^14] [^15]

### Taiwan

Taiwan has a complicated history of being at least partially occupied and administered by larger powers including the [Dutch East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company "Dutch East India Company"), the [Kingdom of Tungning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Tungning "Kingdom of Tungning") (purporting to be a continuation of the [Southern Ming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Ming "Southern Ming")), the Qing dynasty and the Empire of Japan. Taiwan first came under direct Chinese control when it was invaded by the Manchu-led Qing dynasty in 1683.[^16]

The island remained under Qing rule until 1895 when it was ceded to the Empire of Japan under the Treaty of Shimonoseki. Following the [Axis powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_powers "Axis powers") ' defeat in World War II in 1945, the Kuomintang-led Republic of China gained control of Taiwan.[^16] Some Taiwanese resisted ROC rule in the years following World War II. The ROC violently suppressed this resistance which culminated in the [February 28 Incident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/February_28_Incident "February 28 Incident") in 1947.[^17] At the de facto end of the Chinese Civil War in 1950, KMT and CCP government faced each other across the Strait, with each aiming for a military takeover of the other.

From 1928 to 1942, the CCP maintained that Taiwan was a separate nation.[^18] In a 1937 interview with [Edgar Snow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edgar_Snow "Edgar Snow"), [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") stated "we will extend them (the Koreans) our enthusiastic help in their struggle for independence. The same thing applies for Taiwan." [^19] [^20]

The [irredentist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_irredentism "Chinese irredentism") narrative emphasizing the importance of a unified [Greater China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_China "Greater China") area, which purportedly include Taiwan, arose in both the Kuomintang and the CCP in the years during and after the civil war. For the PRC, the claim of the Greater China area was part of a nationalist argument for territorial integrity. In the civil war years it set the communist movement apart from the ROC, which had lost [Manchuria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchukuo "Manchukuo"), the ancestral homeland of the Qing emperors, to Japan in 1932.[^21]

#### Rise of Tangwai and Taiwanese nationalism

From the end of the Chinese Civil War in 1950 until the mid-1970s the concept of unification was not the main subject of discourse between the governments of the PRC and the ROC. The Kuomintang believed that they would, probably with American help, one day retake mainland China, while Mao Zedong's communist regime would collapse in a popular uprising and the Kuomintang forces would be welcomed.[^22]

By the 1970s, the Kuomintang's authoritarian military dictatorship in Taiwan, led by the [Chiang family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_family "Chiang family") was becoming increasingly untenable due to the popularity of the [Tangwai movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangwai_movement "Tangwai movement") and Taiwanese nationalism. In 1970, then-Vice Premier, [Chiang Ching-kuo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiang_Ching-kuo "Chiang Ching-kuo") survived an [assassination attempt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheng_Tzu-tsai#1970_assassination_attempt "Cheng Tzu-tsai") in [New York City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City "New York City") by [Cheng Tzu-tsai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheng_Tzu-tsai "Cheng Tzu-tsai") and [Peter Huang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Huang "Peter Huang"), both members of the [World United Formosans for Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_United_Formosans_for_Independence "World United Formosans for Independence"). In 1976, [Wang Sing-nan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Sing-nan "Wang Sing-nan") sent a [mail bomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mail_bomb "Mail bomb") to then-Governor of Taiwan Province [Hsieh Tung-min](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hsieh_Tung-min "Hsieh Tung-min"), who suffered serious injuries to both hands as a result.[^23] The Kuomintang's heavy-handed oppression in the [Kaohsiung Incident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaohsiung_Incident "Kaohsiung Incident"), alleged involvement in the [Lin family massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lin_family_massacre "Lin family massacre") and the murders of [Chen Wen-chen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chen_Wen-chen "Chen Wen-chen") and [Henry Liu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Liu "Henry Liu"), and the self-immolation of [Cheng Nan-jung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheng_Nan-jung "Cheng Nan-jung") galvanized the Taiwanese community into political actions and eventually led to majority rule and democracy in Taiwan.

The concept of unification replaced the concept of liberation by the PRC in 1979 as it embarked, after Mao's death, on the [reform and opening up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reform_and_opening_up "Reform and opening up") and pursued a more pragmatic foreign policy. In Taiwan, the possibility of the ROC retaking mainland China became increasingly remote in the 1970s, particularly after the [ROC's expulsion from the United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_Resolution_2758 "United Nations General Assembly Resolution 2758") in 1971, the establishment of diplomatic relations between the PRC and United States in 1979, and Chiang Kai-shek's death in 1975.[^21]

#### Majority rule in Taiwan

With the end of authoritarian rule in the 1980s, there was a shift in power within the KMT away from the faction who had accompanied Chiang to Taiwan. Taiwanese who grew up under Japanese rule, which accounted for more than 85% of the population, gained more influence and the KMT began to move away from its ideology of cross-strait unification. After the exposure of [1987 Lieyu massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1987_Lieyu_massacre "1987 Lieyu massacre") in June, [martial law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martial_law_in_Taiwan "Martial law in Taiwan") was finally lifted in Taiwan on 15 July 1987. Following the [Wild Lily student movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wild_Lily_student_movement "Wild Lily student movement"), President [Lee Teng-hui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Teng-hui "Lee Teng-hui") announced in 1991 that his government no longer disputed the rule of the CCP in China, leading to semi-official peace talks (leading to what would be termed as the " [1992 Consensus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1992_Consensus "1992 Consensus") ") between the two sides. The PRC broke off these talks in 1999 when President Lee described relations with the PRC as " [Special state-to-state relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_state-to-state_relations "Special state-to-state relations") ".

Until the mid-1990s, unification supporters on Taiwan were bitterly opposed to the CCP. Since the mid-1990s, there has been a considerable warming of relations between the CCP and Taiwanese unification supporters, as both oppose the pro-Taiwan independence bloc. This brought about the accusation that unification supporters were attempting to sell out Taiwan. They responded saying that closer ties with mainland China, especially economic ties, are in Taiwan's interest.

#### Rise of the Democratic Progressive Party

After the [2000 Taiwanese presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2000_Taiwanese_presidential_election "2000 Taiwanese presidential election"), which brought the independence-leaning Democratic Progressive Party's candidate President [Chen Shui-bian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chen_Shui-bian "Chen Shui-bian") to power, the Kuomintang, faced with defections to the People First Party, expelled Lee Teng-hui and his supporters and reoriented the party towards unification. At the same time, the People's Republic of China shifted its efforts at unification away from military threats (which it de-emphasized but did not renounce) towards economic incentives designed to encourage Taiwanese businesses to invest in mainland China and aiming to create a pro-Beijing bloc within the Taiwanese electorate.

Within Taiwan, unification supporters tend to see "China" as a larger cultural entity divided by the Chinese Civil War into separate states or governments within the country. In addition, supporters see Taiwanese identity as one piece of a broader Chinese identity rather than as a separate cultural identity. However, supporters do oppose [desinicization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desinicization "Desinicization") inherent in Communist ideology such as that seen during the [Cultural Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_Revolution "Cultural Revolution"), along with the effort to emphasize a Taiwanese identity as separate from a Chinese one. As of the 2008 election of President [Ma Ying-jeou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ma_Ying-jeou "Ma Ying-jeou"), the KMT agreed to the One China principle, but defined it as led by the Republic of China rather than the People's Republic of China.

### Military significance

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Bailey_bridge_barge.webp/250px-Bailey_bridge_barge.webp.png)

3D sketch of a bailey bridge barge dock being developed by China to potentially invade Taiwan

Military analysts often point out that control over Taiwan would have [geostrategic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geostrategy "Geostrategy") significance for the PRC, allowing it to break out of the [first island chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_island_chain "First island chain") and increase the country's [strategic depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_depth "Strategic depth").[^24] [^25]<sup><span title="Pages: 138–139">: 138–139</span> </sup> The first island chain is often cited as a natural barrier and perceived constraint to the PRC's access to the Pacific Ocean.[^26] [^24] [Radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar "Radar") stations on the eastern side of Taiwan's [Central Mountain Range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Mountain_Range "Central Mountain Range") would permit the PRC to more readily monitor activity in the [Philippine Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_Sea "Philippine Sea").[^25]<sup><span title="Page: 149">: 149</span> </sup> The PRC's maritime surveillance would be greatly improved.[^27] Control over Taiwan and its deeper eastern waters would also provide the [People's Liberation Army Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army_Navy "People's Liberation Army Navy") 's [ballistic missile submarines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ballistic_missile_submarines "Ballistic missile submarines") with less [detectable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submarine_detection_system "Submarine detection system") access to the wider Pacific where they would serve as an important component of a credible [second strike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_strike "Second strike") capability for the PRC.[^28] [^25]<sup><span title="Pages: 148–149">: 148–149</span></sup>

### "One country, two systems" proposal

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Anti-Taiwan_independence_movement_protesters_in_Washington_DC_20051020.jpg/250px-Anti-Taiwan_independence_movement_protesters_in_Washington_DC_20051020.jpg)

Anti-Taiwan independence protesters in Washington, D.C. during Lee Teng-hui 's visit.

[Deng Xiaoping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping "Deng Xiaoping") developed the principle of [one country, two systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_country,_two_systems "One country, two systems") in relation to Hong Kong, Macau, and Taiwan.[^29]<sup><span title="Page: 176">: 176</span> </sup> According to the 1995 proposal outlined by [CCP General Secretary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") and [paramount leader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paramount_leader "Paramount leader") [Jiang Zemin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiang_Zemin "Jiang Zemin"), Taiwan would lose sovereignty and the right to self-determination, but would keep its armed forces and send a representative to be the "number two leader" in the PRC central government. Thus, under this proposal, the Republic of China would become fully defunct.

In May 1998, the [Central Committee of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Central Committee of the Chinese Communist Party") convened a Work Conference on Taiwan Affairs which stated that the whole party and the whole nation should work together for peaceful unification.[^30]<sup><span title="Page: 11">: 11</span></sup>

Few Taiwanese are in support of the One Country, Two Systems policy while some unification supporters argued to uphold the *status quo* until mainland China democratized and industrialized to the same level as Taiwan. In the 2000 presidential election, independent candidate [James Soong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Soong "James Soong") proposed a [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") -style relation with mainland China (this was echoed by [Hsu Hsin-liang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hsu_Hsin-liang "Hsu Hsin-liang") in 2004) along with a non-aggression pact. In the [2004 presidential election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2004_Taiwanese_presidential_election "2004 Taiwanese presidential election"), [Lien Chan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lien_Chan "Lien Chan") proposed a [confederation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confederation "Confederation") -style relationship. Beijing objected to the plan, claiming that Taiwan was already part of China, and was not a state and, as such, could not form a confederation with it. Developments in Hong Kong have caused the population of Taiwan in recent years to find "One China, Two Systems" to be "unpersuasive, unappealing, and even untrustworthy." [^31]

### Stasis

Unification proposals were not actively floated in Taiwan and the issue remained moot under President Chen Shui-bian, who refused to accept talks under Beijing's pre-conditions. Under the PRC administration of [Hu Jintao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hu_Jintao "Hu Jintao"), incorporating Taiwan lost emphasis amid the reality that the DPP presidency in Taiwan would be held by pro-independence President Chen until 2008. Instead, the emphasis shifted to meetings with politicians who opposed independence.

A series of [high-profile visits in 2005](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2005_Pan%E2%80%93Blue_visits_to_mainland_China "2005 Pan–Blue visits to mainland China") to China by the leaders of the three pan-Blue Coalition parties was seen as an implicit recognition of the status quo by the PRC government. Notably, Kuomintang chairman Lien Chan's trip was marked by unedited coverage of his speeches and tours (and some added positive commentary) by government-controlled media and meetings with high level officials including Hu Jintao. Similar treatment (though marked with less historical significance and media attention) was given during subsequent visits by PFP chairman James Soong and New Party chairman Yok Mu-ming. The CCP and the Pan-Blue Coalition parties emphasized their common ground in renewed negotiations under the 1992 consensus, opening the [Three Links](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Links "Three Links"), and opposing Taiwan's formal independence.

The PRC passed an Anti-Secession Law shortly before Lien's trip. While the Pan-Green Coalition held mass rallies to protest the codification of using military force to retake Taiwan, the Pan-Blue Coalition was largely silent. The language of the Anti-Secession Law was clearly directed at the independence supporters in Taiwan (termed "'Taiwan independence' secessionist forces" in the law) and designed to be somewhat acceptable to the Pan-Blue Coalition. It did not explicitly declare Taiwan to be part of the People's Republic of China but instead used the term "China" on its own, allowing definitional flexibility. It made repeated emphasis of promoting peaceful national unification but left out the concept of "one country, two systems" and called for negotiations in "steps and phases and with flexible and varied modalities" in recognition of the concept of eventual rather than immediate incorporation of Taiwan.

Under both President Chen and President Ma Ying-jeou, the main political changes in cross-straits relationship involved closer economic ties and increased business and personal travel. Such initiatives were met by grassroots oppositions such as the [Sunflower Student Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunflower_Student_Movement "Sunflower Student Movement"), which successfully scuttled [Cross-Strait Service Trade Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-Strait_Service_Trade_Agreement "Cross-Strait Service Trade Agreement") in 2014. President Ma Ying-Jeou advocated for the revitalization of Chinese culture, as in the re-introduction of [traditional Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters "Traditional Chinese characters") in texts to mainland China used in Taiwan and historically in China. It expressed willingness to allow the usage [simplified Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters "Simplified Chinese characters") in informal writing.

Starting in 2017, the [All-China Federation of Taiwan Compatriots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All-China_Federation_of_Taiwan_Compatriots "All-China Federation of Taiwan Compatriots"), a group of Taiwanese residing in the PRC, took on a more prominent role in the CCP's [united front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_front_in_Taiwan "United front in Taiwan") efforts directed at Taiwan.[^32]

## Official stance of the People's Republic of China

The CCP uses the phrase "reunification" instead of "unification" to emphasize its assertion that the island of Taiwan has always belonged to China, or at least that the island Taiwan has been part of China for a long period of time, and that it currently belongs to People's Republic of China, but is currently being sporadically occupied by alleged [separatists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separatism "Separatism") who support Taiwanese independence.[^33]

" [Liberation of Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberation_of_Taiwan "Liberation of Taiwan") " ([Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"): 解放台湾) is a term used in the PRC to garner [public opinion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_opinion "Public opinion") for cross-strait unification with the Republic of China in Taiwan, proposing the use of military force to achieve it. In 1956, CCP Chairman [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") first introduced the term, which was construed to mean a "peaceful" way to unify with the Republic of China. Despite this, both governments have had numerous long-term military confrontations. The CCP has set the unification of China as the most important political goal since the founding of the PRC.[^34]

In January 1979, the [Standing Committee of the National People's Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standing_Committee_of_the_National_People%27s_Congress "Standing Committee of the National People's Congress") issued its first appeal to the KMT, which marked the start of the PRC's "peaceful reunification" strategy.[^35] In March 2005, the [10th National People's Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10th_National_People%27s_Congress "10th National People's Congress") passed the [Anti-Secession Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-Secession_Law "Anti-Secession Law") authorizing military force for unification.[^36] In 2019, CCP General Secretary [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") proposed "peaceful reunification" based on the [one country, two systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_country,_two_systems "One country, two systems") program. The government of the ROC led by President [Tsai Ing-wen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsai_Ing-wen "Tsai Ing-wen") rejected the proposal.[^37]

The PRC does not consider the ROC a sovereign state today, instead believing itself to be the ROC's successor after the PRC's founding in 1949.[^38] [^39]

In 2024, the Chinese government issued a directive to the courts stating that ["diehard" separatists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diehard_%22Taiwan_independence%22_separatists "Diehard \"Taiwan independence\" separatists") could be [tried *in absentia*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_in_absentia "Trial in absentia") with [capital punishment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_China "Capital punishment in China") imposed.[^7] [^8]

In October 2025, [Xinhua News Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinhua_News_Agency "Xinhua News Agency") published a commentary under the pseudonym of Zhong Tai Wen regarding how Taiwan would be governed after a possible unification. It wrote that China would "implement 'patriots governing Taiwan', and a high degree of autonomy", while adding that private property, religious beliefs and legal rights in Taiwan would be protected. It also proposed the establishment of a "cross-strait common market" after unification, saying "Taiwanese goods entering the mainland will all enjoy zero tariffs" and that Taiwan's economy and companies would be integrated with mainland China.[^40]

### Taiwan and Penghu

Officially, the PRC traces Chinese sovereignty over Taiwan Island, allegedly historically known by the Chinese as "Liuqiu" (which is closely related to the name of the modern [Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan") [Ryukyu Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ryukyu_Islands "Ryukyu Islands")), back to roughly around the 3rd century CE ([specifically the year 230 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_Taiwanese_history#3rd_century "Timeline of Taiwanese history")).[^41] However, most [Western](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_world "Western world") sources trace Chinese sovereignty over Taiwan Island back to either 1661–1662 CE (the year(s) when [Koxinga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koxinga "Koxinga") established the [Kingdom of Tungning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Tungning "Kingdom of Tungning") in [southwestern Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tainan "Tainan")) or 1683 CE (the year when the Qing dynasty absorbed the Kingdom of Tungning into its territory and subsequently lay claim to the entire island).[^42]

## Official stance of the Republic of China

Politics in the Republic of China are divided into two main camps, the Pan-Blue and the Pan-Green Coalitions. The former camp is characterized by general Chinese nationalism and ROC nationalism, whereas the latter camp is characterized by Taiwanese nationalism.

ROC official sources note that Qing forces occupied the island of Taiwan's western and northern coasts from 1683, and that Taiwan was declared a Qing province in 1885.[^43]

### Pan-Blue interpretation

The Japanese Instrument of Surrender (1945) is seen by the Pan-Blue camp as legitimizing the Chinese claims of sovereignty over Taiwan Island which were made with the 1943 Cairo Declaration and the Potsdam Declaration (1945).[^44] The common Pan-Blue view asserts that Taiwan Island was returned to China in 1945. Irredentist in nature, those who possess this view commonly perceive Retrocession Day to be the conclusion to a continuous saga of reunification struggles on both sides of the strait, lasting from 1895, the year that Taiwan Island was ceded to Japan, up until 1945, the end of the Second World War. Hence, there is a common view among the Pan-Blue camp that the island of Taiwan was always a Chinese territory under Japanese occupation and never belonged to Japan, neither legally nor in spirit. The Cairo Declaration, Potsdam Declaration, and Japanese Instrument of Surrender are seen as proofs that the Treaty of Shimonoseki was nullified in its entirety in 1945, hence proving that the island of Taiwan always rightfully belonged to China throughout those fifty years of reunification struggles. Shortly following these events, the island of Taiwan was split from mainland China again, according to the common Pan-Blue view, marking the beginning of another reunification saga. Still, the Pan-Blue camp considers both Taiwan and mainland China to be currently under Chinese rule, with the division between the island of Taiwan and mainland China merely being internal, rather than directly the result of outsider aggression; this view is demonstrated through the 1992 Consensus, which some allege to be an agreement reached between officials of both the Kuomintang and the CCP in 1992. The notion of 1992 Consensus is that there is One China and that the island of Taiwan is part of China, but that the legitimate government of China can be interpreted differently by the two sides of the strait.

ROC singer [Teresa Teng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teresa_Teng "Teresa Teng") performed in many countries around the world, but never in [mainland China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland_China "Mainland China"). During her 1980 [TTV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_Television "Taiwan Television") concert, when asked about such possibility, she responded by stating that the day she performs on the mainland will be the day the [Three Principles of the People](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Principles_of_the_People "Three Principles of the People") are implemented there – in reference to either the [pursuit of Chinese democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy_movements_of_China "Democracy movements of China") or reunification under the banner of the ROC.[^45] [^46] [^47]

### Pan-Green interpretation

The views of the Pan-Green camp, though they are diverse, tend to be characterized by Taiwanese nationalism. Hence, most within the Pan-Green camp are opposed to the idea of Taiwan being part of China. Still, most within the Pan-Green camp accept certain historical facts which suggest that Taiwan was part of China. The common Pan-Green view accepts that Taiwan was controlled by a regime in mainland China between 1683 and 1895, though many characterize this as a period of constant rebellion, or suppression of identity (or discovery of a new identity), or colonization by the foreign Manchu people. While most among the Pan-Green camp accept that the transition from Chinese to Japanese rule in 1895 was violent and tragic, many believe that rule under the Japanese was either more benevolent than rule under the Chinese (both KMT and Qing) or more productive. Hence, most Pan-Green do not support the notion that Taiwan was part of China between 1895 and 1945, and neither the notion that there was a strong Chinese unification sentiment in Taiwan at that time. "Dark Green" members of the Pan-Green camp generally do not believe that the Treaty of Shimonoseki was ever nullified. Certain sources claim that attempts were made to nullify the treaty, but that these attempts were either illegal or futile,[^48] whereas other sources claim that the notion that the treaty was ever nullified is a complete fabrication by the KMT in modern times.[^49]

### Tibet and Outer Mongolia

The ROC has the historical claims to [Tibet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibet "Tibet") and [Outer Mongolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Mongolia "Outer Mongolia").

The southwestern region of Tibet was governed by the [Dalai Lama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalai_Lama "Dalai Lama") from 1912 to 1951 as a de facto independent state instead of the [Ganden Phodrang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ganden_Phodrang "Ganden Phodrang"). The ROC government has asserted that "Tibet was placed under the sovereignty of China" when the Qing dynasty (1644–1912) [ended the brief Nepalese invasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Nepalese_War "Sino-Nepalese War") (1788–1792) of parts of Tibet in c. 1793.[^50] while the [Tibetan Government in Exile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_Government_in_Exile "Tibetan Government in Exile") asserts that Tibet was an independent state until the PRC invaded Tibet in 1949/1950.[^51] [^52] By that point, the position of the Republic of China with regard to Tibet appeared to become more nuanced as was stated in the following opening speech to the International Symposium on Human Rights in Tibet on 8 September 2007 through the pro-Taiwan independence then ROC President Chen Shui-bian who stated that his offices no longer treated exiled Tibetans as Chinese mainlanders.[^53] Today, the region is ruled by the PRC-governed [Tibet Autonomous Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region "Tibet Autonomous Region") with parts of the ROC-claimed [Xikang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xikang "Xikang") province.

In the northern region, Outer Mongolia, now controlled by the independent [Mongolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolia "Mongolia") and the [Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia") Republic of [Tuva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuva "Tuva"), it declared independence from the Qing dynasty in 1911 while China retained its control over the area and [reasserted control over Outer Mongolia in 1919](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupation_of_Mongolia "Occupation of Mongolia").[^54] [^55] Consequently, Mongolia sought Soviet Russian support to reclaim its independence. In 1921, both Chinese and [White Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_movement "White movement") forces were driven out by the [Red Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Army "Red Army") of the Soviet Union and pro-Soviet Mongolian forces. In 1924, the [Mongolian People's Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Republic "Mongolian People's Republic") was formed.[^54] Soviet pressure forced China to [recognize the independence of Mongolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1945_Mongolian_independence_referendum "1945 Mongolian independence referendum") in 1946, but the ROC reasserted the claims to Outer Mongolia in 1953. However, the claim was dropped in 2002 as the [ROC Ministry of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(Taiwan\) "Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Taiwan)") opened a representative office in Mongolia in 2002 with reciprocity from Mongolia in the ROC in 2003.[^56]
