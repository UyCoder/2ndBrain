The bilateral relations between the [Kingdom of Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Cambodia "Kingdom of Cambodia") and the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") have been marked by close economic, political and security cooperation.

The first contacts between China and the [Khmer Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Empire "Khmer Empire") occurred in the [Tang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty") period in the 9th century after it was unified in 802 AD by [Jayavarman II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jayavarman_II "Jayavarman II"). Cambodia maintained relations with the [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") during the final years of the Khmer Empire when [Ponhea Yat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ponhea_Yat "Ponhea Yat") dispatched a minister to establish formal diplomatic ties. During the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), the People's Republic of China supported the [Khmer Rouge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Rouge "Khmer Rouge") during the [Cambodian Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodian_Civil_War "Cambodian Civil War") against [Lon Nol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lon_Nol "Lon Nol") 's regime. China also fostered good relations with Cambodian Prince [Norodom Sihanouk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norodom_Sihanouk "Norodom Sihanouk"), who also backed the Khmer Rouge. After Khmer Rouge victory, China provided extensive economic aid to the new government and emerged as [Democratic Kampuchea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Kampuchea "Democratic Kampuchea") 's closest ally. When the Vietnamese military [invaded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Vietnam_relations "Cambodia–Vietnam relations") Cambodia in 1978, China condemned Vietnam and waged a [brief border war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Vietnamese_War "Sino-Vietnamese War") against Vietnam in 1979 to compel it to withdraw from Cambodia. China refused to recognize the [People's Republic of Kampuchea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_Kampuchea "People's Republic of Kampuchea") established by Vietnam, recognizing the [Coalition Government of Democratic Kampuchea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalition_Government_of_Democratic_Kampuchea "Coalition Government of Democratic Kampuchea") government in exile instead.

Relations were normalized following the [1991 Paris Peace Agreements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1991_Paris_Peace_Agreements "1991 Paris Peace Agreements") and the restoration of the Kingdom of Cambodia. Since 1997, China cultivated close ties with Cambodian Prime Minister [Hun Sen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hun_Sen "Hun Sen") and his government. Since then, China provided extensive economic, military and political support to Cambodia, while also providing the country with [foreign aid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_foreign_aid "Chinese foreign aid"). Cambodia has emerged as a close ally of China among [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN") countries, and supporting China regarding [territorial disputes in the South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_disputes_in_the_South_China_Sea "Territorial disputes in the South China Sea") as well as China's internal politics. Cambodia adheres to the [one China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_principle "One China principle") and considers [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") to be "an inalienable part" of China.

## History

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Present_from_Cambodia_to_Qing_Court_%289834761546%29.jpg/250px-Present_from_Cambodia_to_Qing_Court_%289834761546%29.jpg)

Present from Cambodia to Qing court

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Mao_Sihanouk.jpg/250px-Mao_Sihanouk.jpg)

Then-Prince Norodom Sihanouk with CCP chairman Mao Zedong (far left) and Chinese president Liu Shaoqi (far right) in Beijing, 1965.

Although the countries share no common border, China has had a historic cultural and commercial relationship with Cambodia. The 950,000 [Chinese in Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_Chinese "Overseas Chinese") constitute 3-5% of Cambodia's population, and although they were discriminated against by the Khmer Rouge and the Vietnamese, they have re-emerged as a prominent business community.[^1]

The first contacts between China and the [Khmer Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Empire "Khmer Empire") of Cambodia occurred in the [Tang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty") period, and for centuries onward the two countries shared a strong trading relationship. The region had long been "indianized" (influenced by Indian culture, religion, and administrative techniques) since the [Funan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funan "Funan") period (1st–6th centuries AD). While Chinese annals (such as the [New Book of Tang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Book_of_Tang "New Book of Tang")) often mention diplomatic missions and trade, there is no evidence of a distinct "Chinese Cambodian" community in the modern sense during the [8th century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8th_century "8th century") before the unification of the Khmer Empire by [Jayavarman II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jayavarman_II "Jayavarman II") in 802 AD. Instead, the elite were heavily influenced by Indian, Cham, and Javanese culture, adopting Hinduism (specifically Shivaism) and [Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit"). A [Chola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chola_dynasty "Chola dynasty") - [Song](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty "Song dynasty") -Khmer trade axis dominated trade in the east for much of the 11th and 12th centuries. [Yuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_dynasty "Yuan dynasty") Chinese accounts of the Cambodian kingdom proved to be crucial to uncovering the history of the region. Cambodia maintained relations with Ming China as early as 1421 AD during the final years of the Khmer Empire when [Ponhea Yat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ponhea_Yat "Ponhea Yat") dispatched a minister to establish formal diplomatic ties.[^2]

China has used Cambodia as a counterweight to Vietnam. In the mid-20th century, the People's Republic of China supported the [Maoist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maoist "Maoist") [Khmer Rouge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khmer_Rouge "Khmer Rouge") against Prime Minister [Lon Nol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lon_Nol "Lon Nol") 's regime, who [Nationalist China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Taiwan_\(1945%E2%80%93present\) "History of Taiwan (1945–present)") had ties with, during the [Cambodian Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodian_Civil_War "Cambodian Civil War") and then its takeover of Cambodia in 1975. Also, [CCP Chairman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party") [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") had fostered good relations with Prince [Norodom Sihanouk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norodom_Sihanouk "Norodom Sihanouk"), who also fought against Lon Nol and backed the Khmer Rouge. When the [Vietnamese military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_military "Vietnamese military") invaded Cambodia in 1978, China provided extensive political and military support for the Khmer Rouge.[^3] In 1979, China waged a [brief border war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Vietnamese_War "Sino-Vietnamese War") against Vietnam, partly to threaten it into pulling out of Cambodia.[^4] The [Paris Peace Conference on Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1991_Paris_Peace_Agreements "1991 Paris Peace Agreements"), from July 1989 to October 1991, resolved Cambodia–China relations.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Treasure_Boat_Shipyard_-_story_relief_-_P1080085.JPG/250px-Treasure_Boat_Shipyard_-_story_relief_-_P1080085.JPG)

Modern depiction of a visit of Zheng He 's fleet to Cambodia. A relief in Nanjing's Treasure Boat Shipyard Park

Since 1997, China began developing closer relations with the regime of Cambodian Prime Minister [Hun Sen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hun_Sen "Hun Sen"), once a pro-Vietnamese leader and a [defector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defector "Defector") from the Khmer Rouge during Vietnam's occupation of Cambodia.[^3] Although initially backing Hun Sen's political opponent Prince [Norodom Ranariddh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norodom_Ranariddh "Norodom Ranariddh") and his [FUNCINPEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FUNCINPEC "FUNCINPEC"), China was disenchanted with Ranariddh's efforts to build a closer relationship with [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan"), which is claimed by China.[^3] Facing international isolation after the 1997 coup that brought him to power, Hun Sen cultivated close ties with China, which opposed efforts by Western countries to impose [economic sanctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_sanctions "Economic sanctions") on Cambodia.[^3] China filled the gap in grants, aids, and investments formerly filled by Western countries.[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 28">: 28</span> </sup> China's close ties with Cambodia have also served to gain leverage against Vietnamese influence in the region.[^6]

In 2000, [General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") [Jiang Zemin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiang_Zemin "Jiang Zemin") became the first Chinese Head of State to visit Cambodia.[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 28">: 28</span></sup>

In July 2019, Cambodia was among 37 countries which signed a joint letter to the [UNHRC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNHRC "UNHRC") defending China's treatment of [Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghurs "Uyghurs") and other Muslim minority groups in the [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang") region.[^7] In June 2020, Cambodia was one of 53 countries that backed the [Hong Kong national security law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Hong_Kong_national_security_law "2020 Hong Kong national security law") at the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations").[^8]

In November 2022, Chinese Premier [Li Keqiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_Keqiang "Li Keqiang") visited Cambodia for the [ASEAN Summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN_Summit "ASEAN Summit") and stated that China would continue to support and provide aid for infrastructure projects and industrialization efforts in Cambodia.[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 31">: 31</span></sup>

In mid-April 2025, [General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping") [visited Cambodia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_visits_by_Xi_Jinping_to_Vietnam,_Malaysia_and_Cambodia "State visits by Xi Jinping to Vietnam, Malaysia and Cambodia") to reaffirm bilateral relations in response to [United States President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_President "United States President") [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump") 's [Liberation Day tariffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberation_Day_tariffs "Liberation Day tariffs"). During the visit, Xi met with [King](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_of_Cambodia "King of Cambodia") [Norodom Sihamoni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norodom_Sihamoni "Norodom Sihamoni"), [Prime Minister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Cambodia "Prime Minister of Cambodia") [Hun Manet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hun_Manet "Hun Manet") and [Senate President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_presidents_of_the_Senate_\(Cambodia\) "List of presidents of the Senate (Cambodia)") [Hun Sen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hun_Sen "Hun Sen").[^9]

## Cooperation

Generally, Since Cambodia gained its independence in 1953, there has been significant strategic collaboration between China and Cambodia.[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 27">: 27</span> </sup> From the Cambodian perspective, strong relations with China help Cambodia to pushback against Cambodia's larger neighbors, [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Vietnam_relations "Cambodia–Vietnam relations") and [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Thailand_relations "Cambodia–Thailand relations").[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 27">: 27</span></sup>

During the visit of Chinese Premier [Wen Jiabao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wen_Jiabao "Wen Jiabao") between April 7-April 8, 2006 both nations signed several bilateral agreements and a treaty of "Comprehensive Partnership of Cooperation." [^3] China diversified its aid and investments in Cambodia and promised to provide US$600 million in loans and grants.[^3] [^6] China has canceled much of Cambodia's debt and granted a fresh loan of US$12.4 million for the construction of the building housing the Cambodian government's council of ministers and the restoration of the [Angkor Wat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angkor_Wat "Angkor Wat") temple and heritage site.[^10] [^6] About $200 million has been earmarked as a low-interest loan for the construction of bridges spanning the [Mekong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mekong "Mekong") and [Tonle Sap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonle_Sap "Tonle Sap") rivers.[^6] During the visit, Cambodian Prime Minister Hun Sen described China as Cambodia's "most trustworthy friend." [^3] [^6]

China has also funded [Chinese language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language") schools in Cambodia.[^10] Immigration of Chinese workers to Cambodia has also stepped up in recent years and is estimated between 50,000 and 300,000.[^10]

From 2000 to 2014, Cambodia received 132 projects financed by [Chinese aid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_foreign_aid "Chinese foreign aid"), a greater number of projects than any other recipient of Chinese aid.[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 103">: 103</span> </sup> In 2014, Prime Minister Hun Sen requested that China help construct a stadium for Cambodia in advance of the [2023 Southeast Asian Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_SEA_Games "2023 SEA Games").[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 50">: 50</span> </sup> China began building [Morodok Techo National Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morodok_Techo_National_Stadium "Morodok Techo National Stadium") in 2017 at a cost of US$169 million and completed it for Cambodia in December 2021.[^12]<sup><span title="Page: 50">: 50</span></sup>

Within [ASEAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN "ASEAN"), Cambodia is often supportive of China.[^13]<sup><span title="Page: 211">: 211</span> </sup> In 2016, Cambodia took a China-favorable position at an ASEAN meeting which addressed [sovereignty disputes in the South China Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territorial_disputes_in_the_South_China_Sea "Territorial disputes in the South China Sea").[^14]<sup><span title="Page: 185">: 185</span> </sup> Observers generally expected the meeting to produce a statement critical of China's positions, but through Cambodia's objection the meeting resulted in a more general statement not referencing China and speaking generally of concern over on-going developments in the South China Sea.[^14]<sup><span title="Page: 185">: 185</span> </sup> Chinese leaders expressed their appreciation for Cambodia and China's foreign minister stated, "History will prove that the Cambodian side's maintained position is correct." [^15]<sup><span title="Page: 52">: 52</span></sup>

Cambodia follows the [one China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_principle "One China principle") and considers Taiwan to be "an inalienable part" of China.[^16] Cambodia also supports all efforts by the PRC to "achieve [national reunification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_unification "Chinese unification") " and opposes [Taiwan independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_independence_movement "Taiwan independence movement").[^17] In 2025, Cambodia deported Taiwanese citizens involved in telecommunication fraud to China.[^18] In April 2026, China pledged to assist Cambodia in preventing [color revolutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_revolutions "Color revolutions").[^19]

### Defense

In the aftermath of the 1997 coup, China provided US$2.8 million in military aid and has since supplied wide range of military equipment,[^20] training of military and police cadre and naval vessels to combat [drug trafficking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_trafficking "Drug trafficking") and [piracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piracy "Piracy").[^3] In May 2014, Cambodia and China entered into an agreement to increase their defense ties.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 164">: 164</span> </sup> As part of the agreement, China increased the number of scholarships it offered to Cambodian military personnel to study in China.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 164">: 164</span> </sup> In 2018, China provided $100 million to Cambodia for defense spending.[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 103">: 103</span> </sup> In 2022, Cambodia and China signed a memorandum of understanding on defense cooperation.[^11]<sup><span title="Page: 103">: 103</span></sup>

Cambodia-China defense cooperation includes the Chinese-funded expansion of [Ream Naval Base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ream_Naval_Base "Ream Naval Base"), which began in 2022 and was to be inaugurated on 2 April 2025. The project, which included a new pier and dry dock, raised concerns over China's military influence in the region, particularly from the U.S. and its allies. In 2019, reports suggested a secret agreement granting China access to the base, but Cambodia denied such claims. While Chinese warships docked there for months, Cambodia invited Japan's [Maritime Self-Defense Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan_Maritime_Self-Defense_Force "Japan Maritime Self-Defense Force") to use the facility, signaling openness to other nations. In September 2024, China pledged to provide Cambodia with two naval vessels.[^21]

### Health

During the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_Cambodia "COVID-19 pandemic in Cambodia"), China provided major assistance to the [Hun Sen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hun_Sen "Hun Sen") government's vaccination campaign.[^22] As of early November 2021, China had sent more than 35 million vaccines to Cambodia. China provided many of them free of charge.[^22] Vaccines provided by China accounted for more than 90% of total vaccines provided to Cambodia from other countries.[^22] China also provided other health care supplies as well as medical professionals to Cambodia during the pandemic.[^22]<sup><span title="Page: 30">: 30</span> </sup> In part thanks to Chinese contributions, Cambodia had the second-highest vaccination rate in Southeast Asia, despite having the second lowest per capita GDP in the region.[^22]<sup><span title="Page: 30">: 30</span></sup>

## Commerce

Through the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative"), China has a major role in infrastructure development in Cambodia.[^22] In 2017, China financed approximately 70% of Cambodia's road and bridge development.[^22] China built a major expressway between [Sihanoukville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sihanoukville_\(city\) "Sihanoukville (city)") and [Phnom Penh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phnom_Penh "Phnom Penh"), which began operating in 2023.[^22]

There are multiple large-scale Chinese investment projects in Cambodia such as the 400 MW [Lower Se San 2 Dam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_Se_San_2_Dam "Lower Se San 2 Dam") worth $781 million [^23] and a US$3.8 billion deep-water port project on a 90-km stretch of Cambodian coastline.[^24] According to the [Cambodian Center for Human Rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodian_Center_for_Human_Rights "Cambodian Center for Human Rights"), the Cambodian government gave over 4.6 million hectares in concessions to 107 Chinese-owned firms between 1994 and 2012.[^25]

In 2010, Cambodia and China signed cooperation agreements on bridge and road infrastructure.[^15]<sup><span title="Page: 159">: 159</span> </sup> Cambodia and China signed a [free trade agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_trade_agreement "Free trade agreement") in 2020.[^26]<sup><span title="Page: 269">: 269</span></sup>

Effective 1 December 2024, China eliminated [tariffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tariff "Tariff") for goods imported from all of the countries that the United Nations categorizes as [least developed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_developed_countries "Least developed countries") and with which China has diplomatic relations, including Cambodia.[^27]

## Concerns

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Cambodian_Embassy_in_Beijing.JPG/250px-Cambodian_Embassy_in_Beijing.JPG)

Embassy of Cambodia in China

The Cambodian government's suppression of the [Falun Gong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falun_Gong "Falun Gong"), a [new religious movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_religious_movement "New religious movement") (considered by China and other critics to be a cult) banned by China, and [extradition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extradition "Extradition") of two Falun Gong activists to China was criticized by human rights activities and the [United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_High_Commissioner_for_Refugees "United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees").[^3] Suspected preferential treatment for Cambodia-based Chinese firms and the [National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_\(Cambodia\) "National Assembly (Cambodia)") 's guarantee of profits for the Chinese investors in the Kamchay power plant has also provoked criticism from [opposition politicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opposition_\(politics\) "Opposition (politics)") of China's growing political clout in Cambodia.[^10]

From 1994 to 2012 China invested a total of US$9.17 billion in Cambodia.[^28] However, [illegal logging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illegal_logging "Illegal logging") and shady deals involving members of Cambodia's government and Chinese firms were reported to be increasing. Mining projects and government land concessions to foreign and local firms, resulted in people being displaced from their land. According to *[The Cambodia Daily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Cambodia_Daily "The Cambodia Daily")* in 2006, Phong hill tribes in [Mondulkiri province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mondulkiri_province "Mondulkiri province") "claim that the Chinese company has colluded with the Cambodian government to illegally force them from their ancestral homeland".[^29] The [Boeung Kak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeung_Kak "Boeung Kak") and Borei Keila evictions in Phnom Penh were seen by many observers as the Cambodian Government's increasing drift away from the adherence to human rights. The Chinese funded [Sesan Dam project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_Se_San_2_Dam "Lower Se San 2 Dam") in the northeastern part of Cambodia, threaten to lower the fish stocks, and affect the livelihood of many Khmer. "By one estimate, the project will result in a 9 percent drop in fish stocks in the entire [Mekong Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mekong "Mekong") ".[^30]

## Public opinion

According to a 2026 [feeling thermometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeling_thermometer "Feeling thermometer") poll by the [Carter Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carter_Center "Carter Center") and [Emory University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emory_University "Emory University"), Chinese opinion of Cambodia was on average 22 out of 100.[^31]
