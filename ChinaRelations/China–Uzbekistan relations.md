**China–Uzbekistan relations** [^1] were established on 2 January 1992, following the independence of [Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan "Uzbekistan") in 1991. The two countries maintain close economic ties, and China is a major importer of natural gas from Uzbekistan. Both countries are members of the [Shanghai Cooperation Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Cooperation_Organisation "Shanghai Cooperation Organisation").

## History

According to the *[Ming Shilu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_Shilu "Ming Shilu")*, ambassadors from the [Uzbek Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbek_Khanate "Uzbek Khanate") and [Bukhara Khanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khanate_of_Bukhara "Khanate of Bukhara") corresponded with [Ming China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") more than 20 times between 1488 and 1618.[^2]

China recognized Uzbekistan's independence on 27 December 1991 and the two countries established relations on 2 January 1992.[^3] Both countries signed the "China-Uzbek Treaty of Friendship and Cooperation" in 2005, during Uzbek leader [Islam Karimov's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_Karimov "Islam Karimov") meeting with Chinese leader [Hu Jintao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hu_Jintao "Hu Jintao") in Beijing.[^4]

Uzbekistan has cooperated with China in extraditing [Uyghur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghurs "Uyghurs") activists from the country.[^5] In July 2019, UN ambassadors of 37 countries, including Uzbekistan, signed a joint letter to the [United Nations Human Rights Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council "United Nations Human Rights Council") defending [China's persecution of Uyghurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution_of_Uyghurs_in_China "Persecution of Uyghurs in China").[^6] [^7] Uzbekistan was one of 16 countries that defended China in 2019 but did not do so in 2020.[^8]

Uzbek Prime Minister [Abdulla Aripov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdulla_Aripov "Abdulla Aripov") called China Uzbekistan's "closest partner" on a 26 August 2019 meeting.[^9]

## Economic relations

China is the second-largest importer of raw materials from Uzbekistan.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 152">: 152</span> </sup> As of 2024, significant Chinese investment in the country has resulted in approximately 600 Chinese-Uzbek joint enterprises.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 152">: 152</span></sup>

China has also increasing its development loans to Uzbekistan.[^9] China regards Uzbekistan as a critical part of the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_and_Road_Initiative "Belt and Road Initiative").[^11]

As China reduces its reliance on imported natural gas, currently accounting for about 62% of its energy mix but projected to decline due to slowing economic growth and a national carbon neutrality target by 2060, [Central Asian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia") gas exporters, including Uzbekistan, are reevaluating their positions. A May 2025 report by [Eurasianet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasianet "Eurasianet"), drawing on [BloombergNEF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloomberg_L.P. "Bloomberg L.P.") ’s *Energy Transition Supply Chains 2025*, underscores China's dominance in global [clean energy manufacturing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_energy "Sustainable energy"), controlling over 70% of production in solar, wind, and battery technologies. With 76% of global investment in clean-tech facilities occurring in China in 2024 and renewables now supplying around 80% of its new electricity demand, China's softening gas appetite may reportedly limit future energy exports from the region.[^12]

## Taiwan

Uzbekistan follows the [one China principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_China_principle "One China principle"), and recognizes [government of the People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_China "Government of China") as the sole legal government representing the whole of China and Taiwan as "an inalienable part" of China. Uzbekistan also supports all efforts by the PRC to "achieve [national reunification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_unification "Chinese unification") " and opposes [Taiwan independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_independence_movement "Taiwan independence movement").[^13]

## Military relations

[Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_people "Chinese people") – [Uzbek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbeks "Uzbeks") military relations have grown in recent years as part of China's broader effort to expand its role as an arms supplier in [Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia"). Traditionally reliant on Russian military equipment, Uzbekistan has begun diversifying its defense partnerships, partly due to a decline in Russian arms exports following its [2022 invasion of Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "Russian invasion of Ukraine"). In 2024, China sold anti-aircraft systems to Uzbekistan, and by April 2025, reports suggested [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent") was considering purchasing [JF-17 fighter jets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAC/PAC_JF-17_Thunder "CAC/PAC JF-17 Thunder"); a joint China– [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan") project known for its multi-role capabilities. If finalized, the deal would signal a shift in regional defense alignments and underscore Beijing's increasing influence in Central Asia's arms market.[^14]

## Cultural and educational relations

In 2013, Uzbekistan and China signed a cultural exchange agreement to increase engagement in culture, education, science, and technology.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 159">: 159</span> </sup> The first exchange per the agreement occurred in 2017, during which seminars, exhibitions, and performances were held.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 159">: 159</span> </sup> These exchanges have been repeated since.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 159">: 159</span></sup>

China provides support for the preservation of and restoration of [cultural heritage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_heritage "Cultural heritage") sites in Uzbekistan.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 159">: 159</span> </sup> For instance, in 2014, China and Uzbekistan made the decision to collaborate on the restoration of the ancient city of [Khiva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khiva "Khiva"), concentrating on the renovation of [Amir to’ra madrasasi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amir_Tora_Madrasah "Amir Tora Madrasah"), a religious academy, as well as the [Hasan Murad Qushbegi Mosque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasanmurod_Qushbegi_mosque "Hasanmurod Qushbegi mosque"). The restoration project was completed in 2019.[^15]

[China Central Television](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Central_Television "China Central Television") (CCTV) and Uzbekistan's National Television and Radio Corporation (UzTRK) cooperate to produce joint programs and documentaries.[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 159">: 159</span></sup>

Uzbekistan has two [Confucius Institutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius_Institute "Confucius Institute").[^10]<sup><span title="Page: 156">: 156</span></sup>

In June 2025, China and Uzbekistan expanded cooperation in vocational education with the signing of 13 agreements between Chinese colleges, enterprises, and Uzbek institutions at the China–Uzbekistan Vocational Education Cooperation Conference in Chongqing. The agreements covered fields such as telecommunications, new energy vehicles, construction, and manufacturing. As part of the partnership, the Chongqing Vocational Institute of Engineering and [ZTE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZTE "ZTE") announced plans to establish a Sino–Uzbek telecommunication college with the Tashkent Transport Technical College, while Anhui Water Conservancy Technical College and [BYD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BYD_Company "BYD Company") ’s Uzbekistan plant partnered with a local college to open a new energy vehicle industry college. Speaking at the event, Otabek Mahkamov, Uzbekistan's deputy minister of higher education, science and innovation, noted that Uzbekistan was pursuing large-scale reforms in vocational education and valued China's cooperation with major companies such as [BYD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BYD_Company "BYD Company"), [Huawei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huawei "Huawei"), and [ZTE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZTE "ZTE").[^16]

## Resident diplomatic missions

- China has an embassy in [Tashkent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tashkent "Tashkent").
- Uzbekistan has an embassy in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") and consulates-general in [Guangzhou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangzhou "Guangzhou") and [Shanghai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai "Shanghai").
