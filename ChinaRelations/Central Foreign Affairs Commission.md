The **Central Foreign Affairs Commission** is a commission of the [Central Committee of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Central Committee of the Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) that exercises general oversight on matters related to foreign affairs.

The group was first established as the Central Foreign Affairs Leading Group (FALG) in 1958. Disbanded during the [Cultural Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_Revolution "Cultural Revolution"), it was restored by [Deng Xiaoping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping "Deng Xiaoping") in 1981. The FALG was upgraded to the Central Foreign Affairs Commission in March 2018. The main execution body of the commission is the [General Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Office_of_the_Central_Foreign_Affairs_Commission "Office of the Central Foreign Affairs Commission"), with the director of the Office being China's top diplomat, currently [Wang Yi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Yi_\(politician\) "Wang Yi (politician)"). Since 1993, the group has been led by the [general secretary of the Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party") and the [premier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_China "Premier of China") has served as the deputy leader of the group. It is currently chaired by CCP general secretary [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping "Xi Jinping"), with premier [Li Qiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_Qiang "Li Qiang") as its deputy leader.

## History

The Central Foreign Affairs Leading Group, also called the Central Foreign Affairs [Leadership Small Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leading_Small_Group "Leading Small Group") (FALG or FALSG; [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"): 中央外事工作领导小组; [pinyin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin "Pinyin"): *Zhōngyāng Wàishì Gōngzuò Lǐngdǎo Xiǎozǔ*), was first established in March 1958.[^1]<sup><span title="Page: 74">: 74</span> </sup> It was disbanded during the [Cultural Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_Revolution "Cultural Revolution") and restored in 1981 as [Deng Xiaoping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping "Deng Xiaoping") increased the number of stakeholders involved in the development of foreign policy.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 177">: 177</span> </sup> The group has historically been a semi-institutional foreign policy coordination body,[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 177">: 177</span> </sup> and became a forum for the central leadership in charge of foreign policy to meet regularly with top bureaucrats to discuss priorities, achieve consensus, and prepare recommendations for the [Politburo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Politburo of the Chinese Communist Party").[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 178">: 178</span> </sup> It was the only standing foreign policy coordination body until the aftermath of the [United States bombing of the Chinese embassy in Belgrade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_bombing_of_the_Chinese_embassy_in_Belgrade "United States bombing of the Chinese embassy in Belgrade"), which prompted the creation of the Central National Security Leadership Small Group (NSLSG) in 2000 to coordinate national security crisis response.[^2]<sup><span title="Page: 178">: 178</span></sup>

In March 2018, the leading group was upgraded to the Central Foreign Affairs Commission as part of the [deepening the reform of the Party and state institutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deepening_the_reform_of_the_Party_and_state_institutions "Deepening the reform of the Party and state institutions").[^3]<sup><span title="Page: 8">: 8</span> </sup> [^4] It also was put in charge of maritime rights and interests following the disestablishment of the Central Leading Group for Safeguarding Maritime Rights and Interests.[^5]

## Functions

The commission is the top CCP institution dedicated to China's foreign affairs. Through the commission, the CCP leadership makes decisions, assigns responsibilities and oversees implementation on a broad range of foreign-related activities.[^6] The policies handled by the commission include traditional diplomacy, party-to-party diplomacy, external propaganda, external trade, intelligence activities overseas, counterespionage, and internal aspects of Hong Kong, Macau and Taiwan affairs. The meetings of the commission are rarely publicized.[^6] The [Foreign Relations Law of the People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_Relations_Law_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China "Foreign Relations Law of the People's Republic of China") states that the commission is "responsible for policy making, deliberation and coordination relating to the conduct of foreign relations".[^7] [^8] The law further states that the commission "considers and formulates the State's foreign relations strategy and related major principles and policies, and provides guidance for their implementation" and that it is "responsible for top-level design, coordination and holistic advancement of work concerning foreign relations, and supervises its implementation".[^8]

The [Office of the Central Foreign Affairs Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Office_of_the_Central_Foreign_Affairs_Commission "Office of the Central Foreign Affairs Commission") is its administrative agency, operating secretively like its parent body.[^6] The Office is responsible for executing and coordinating the implementation of the decisions and directives of the commission. It additionally conducts research and makes suggestions on international relations, drafts and oversees foreign-related laws and regulations, handles foreign-related inquiries from Party, state and local bodies, and organizes work on maritime rights and interests.[^6] Though the office itself is a ministerial-level institution, the director of the Office has been a member of the Politburo and at the [deputy national-level leader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_service_of_China "Civil service of China").[^6] Generally, the director has greater authority than the [Minister of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(China\) "Minister of Foreign Affairs (China)").[^9]<sup><span title="Pages: 77–78">: 77–78</span> </sup> The director of the commission's Office, currently [Wang Yi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Yi_\(politician\) "Wang Yi (politician)"), is the top diplomat of the People's Republic of China.[^10]

## Membership

The membership of the commission generally consists of China's national leaders, including the CCP general secretary, the premier, the vice president, heads of the [International Department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Department_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "International Department of the Chinese Communist Party") and [Central Propaganda Department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Publicity_Department_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Publicity Department of the Chinese Communist Party"), the director of the [State Council Information Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Council_Information_Office "State Council Information Office"), the [Minister of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(China\) "Minister of Foreign Affairs (China)"), the [Minister of National Defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_National_Defense_\(China\) "Minister of National Defense (China)"), the [Minister of Public Security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_Public_Security_\(China\) "Minister of Public Security (China)"), the [Minister of State Security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_State_Security_\(China\) "Minister of State Security (China)"), the [Minister of Commerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Commerce_\(China\) "Ministry of Commerce (China)"), and heads of CCP and state agencies related to Taiwan, Hong Kong, Macau, and overseas Chinese.[^6]
