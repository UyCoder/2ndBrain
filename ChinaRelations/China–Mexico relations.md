[Diplomatic relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomacy "Diplomacy") between the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") and the [United Mexican States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexico "Mexico") were established in 1972.[^1] Both nations are members of the [Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia-Pacific_Economic_Cooperation "Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation"), [Forum of East Asia–Latin America Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forum_of_East_Asia%E2%80%93Latin_America_Cooperation "Forum of East Asia–Latin America Cooperation"), [G-20 major economies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-20_major_economies "G-20 major economies") and the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations").

## History

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Mendoza-1585-f134.png/250px-Mendoza-1585-f134.png)

The work by the Mexico-based Augustinian Juan González de Mendoza may have been the first book published in Europe (1585) containing (an attempt at a reproduction of) Chinese characters. Here, apparently, Mendoza tries to draw the character 城 ("city"). 2

Chinese-Mexican contacts date to the early days of the [Spanish Colonial Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Empire "Spanish Empire") in the Americas and the [Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines "Philippines"). In the 16th-17th century, people, goods, and news traveling between China and Spain usually did so through the Philippines (where there was a large Chinese settlement) and (via the [Manila galleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manila_galleon "Manila galleon") trade) to Mexico. The first two galleons loaded with Chinese goods arrived from the Philippines to [Acapulco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acapulco "Acapulco") in 1573.[^3]

Of particular significance for the trade between the Spanish Colonial Empire and [Ming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_Dynasty "Ming Dynasty") and [Qing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_Dynasty "Qing Dynasty") China were the so-called " [Spanish dollars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_dollar "Spanish dollar") ", fine silver coins many of which were minted in Mexico from Mexican silver.[^4] Even after Mexican independence, and, later, the Spain's loss of the Philippines, [Mexican dollars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexican_dollar "Mexican dollar") remained important for China's monetary system. During the late Qing, they became the standard relative to which the silver coins that China's provincial mints [started to produce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty_coinage "Qing dynasty coinage") were to be valued.[^5]

This historic connection between the two countries is attested by two important early Spanish-language books (soon translated to Europe's other major languages) that were authored by Spanish ecclesiastics stationed in Mexico: [Juan González de Mendoza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_Gonz%C3%A1lez_de_Mendoza "Juan González de Mendoza") 's *The history of the great and mighty kingdom of China and the situation thereof* (1585) and [Juan de Palafox y Mendoza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza "Juan de Palafox y Mendoza") 's *The History of the Conquest of China by the Tartars* (posthumously published in 1670).[^6]

In December 1899, [Imperial China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") and Mexico formally established diplomatic relations after signing a Treaty of Amity, Commerce and Navigation between the two nations. In 1904, Mexico opened its first diplomatic mission in [Beijing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") and maintained a diplomatic mission in several cities where it was forced to move ([Nanjing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanjing "Nanjing") and [Shanghai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai "Shanghai")) during various wars and instability until the mission was finally closed due to the [Japanese invasion of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War "Second Sino-Japanese War") in 1941. In 1942, Mexico re-opened a diplomatic mission in the city of [Chongqing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chongqing "Chongqing") and in 1943 diplomatic missions between the two nations were elevated to embassies.[^1]

In 1971, Mexico decided to break formal diplomatic relations with the [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China") ([Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan")) after the successful passing of [Resolution 2758](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_Resolution_2758 "United Nations General Assembly Resolution 2758") at the United Nations recognizing the People's Republic of China as the only legitimate representative of China to the United Nations. In February 1972, the People's Republic of China and Mexico established diplomatic relations.[^1] In 1973, Mexican President [Luis Echeverría](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luis_Echeverr%C3%ADa "Luis Echeverría") paid an official visit to China and met with [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") [chairman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Chinese_Communist_Party "Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party") [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong").[^7]

### 2009 swine flu dispute

In 2009, in the wake of fears of a worldwide [swine flu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swine_flu "Swine flu") pandemic, thought to have started in Mexico, relations between the two countries cooled substantially over China's decision to [quarantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quarantine "Quarantine") some seventy Mexican citizens, despite none of them showing symptoms of the virus. The Mexican government responded with outrage and, although China imposed the same measures on four nationals from the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and more than twenty from [Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada "Canada"); dubbed the act discriminatory. Mexican Foreign Minister [Patricia Espinosa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patricia_Espinosa "Patricia Espinosa") used such terms as "unacceptable" and "without foundation", and advised compatriots not to travel to China.

Despite this, a mutual desire to increase bilateral trade and increase shipping of Mexican raw materials into China suggested that diplomatic tensions would be only temporary. "This should not affect the relationship in the medium-term because we are talking about an overreaction on both sides", said [Enrique Dussel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enrique_Dussel "Enrique Dussel"), an expert on Mexican-Chinese trade at the [National Autonomous University of Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Autonomous_University_of_Mexico "National Autonomous University of Mexico") in [Mexico City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexico_City "Mexico City").[^8]

### Post-2016 United States presidential election

After the [election of Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_United_States_presidential_election "2016 United States presidential election"), China and Mexico pledged to deepen their diplomatic ties. On 12 December 2016, [Chinese State Councillor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_State_Councillor "Chinese State Councillor") [Yang Jiechi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_Jiechi "Yang Jiechi") met with Mexican Foreign Minister [Claudia Ruiz Massieu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudia_Ruiz_Massieu "Claudia Ruiz Massieu") to discuss improving transportation and trade between their countries.[^9] In July 2019, Mexican Foreign Minister [Marcelo Ebrard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcelo_Ebrard "Marcelo Ebrard") paid a visit to China to give renewed impetus to trade and investment between both countries.[^10]

### Relations in the 2020s

In 2021, Mexican President [Andrés Manuel López Obrador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Manuel_L%C3%B3pez_Obrador "Andrés Manuel López Obrador") apologized for his country's role in the [Torreón massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torre%C3%B3n_massacre "Torreón massacre") where more than 300 [Chinese Mexicans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_immigration_to_Mexico "Chinese immigration to Mexico") were massacred in 1911 in the northern city of [Torreón](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torre%C3%B3n "Torreón") during an unprovoked act of racism towards Mexico's Asian community.[^11] [^12]

In June 2022, both nations celebrated 50 years of diplomatic relations.[^13] In November 2023, President López Obrador and President Xi held a meeting during the [APEC Summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/APEC_United_States_2023 "APEC United States 2023") in [San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco "San Francisco").[^14] In August 2025, Mexico announced that it plans to increase tariffs on imports from China.[^15] [^16] In September 2025, China opened an anti-dumping investigation into Mexican pecans in retaliation.[^16] In December 2025, Mexico approved tariff increases on a range of Chinese products.[^17] [^18]

## Academic connections

The [National Autonomous University of Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Autonomous_University_of_Mexico "National Autonomous University of Mexico") has established a Center for Chinese-Mexican Studies in the faculty of economics, aimed at increasing the understanding of China and its relationship to Mexico. The center hosts conferences and publishes reports, among other activities.[^20] The university also has an academic office located at the [Beijing Foreign Studies University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing_Foreign_Studies_University "Beijing Foreign Studies University").

## Tourism and travel

In 2019, approximately 170,000 Chinese citizens visited Mexico for tourism.[^24] There are commercial flights between both nations with [China Southern Airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Southern_Airlines "China Southern Airlines") and [Hainan Airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hainan_Airlines "Hainan Airlines").[^25] Several airlines offer direct cargo services between both nations.

## Trade

China is Mexico's second largest trading partner globally. As a result of [nearshoring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outsourcing "Outsourcing") China has invested heavily in Mexico to take advantage of Mexico's geographic proximity and free trade agreement with the United States through [USMCA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States%E2%80%93Mexico%E2%80%93Canada_Agreement "United States–Mexico–Canada Agreement").[^26] In 2023, two-way trade between both nations amounted to US$123 billion (not including trade with Hong Kong or Macao).[^27] From January 1999 to December 2023, Mexico has received a total of US$2.5 billion in Chinese foreign direct investments.[^27]

## Taiwan

Mexico considers Taiwan to be "an inseparable part" of China and considers [Tibet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region "Tibet Autonomous Region") to be China's internal affairs.[^28] There are over 80 [united front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_front_\(China\) "United front (China)") organizations operating in Mexico, some of which issue statements against elements of [Taiwan–United States relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan%E2%80%93United_States_relations "Taiwan–United States relations").[^29]

## Hong Kong

While Mexico was part of the [Spanish Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Empire "Spanish Empire"), trade between Spain and China would traverse Mexico with the [Manila galleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manila_galleon "Manila galleon"). Ships laden with goods from Mexico would make call primarily in [Macau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macau "Macau"), where trade with nearby ports, including [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong"), may have occurred. Those goods would then return to Mexico, before being transferred onwards to Spain.[^30]

In 1962, Mexican President [Adolfo Lopez Mateos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolfo_Lopez_Mateos "Adolfo Lopez Mateos") stopped on an overnight visit to [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Hong_Kong "British Hong Kong") (a British colony at the time) and was met by representatives of the local government.[^31] In 1966, Mexico opened a consulate-general in Hong Kong.[^32]

In October 2002, Hong Kong's Chief Executive, [Tung Chee-hwa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tung_Chee-hwa "Tung Chee-hwa"), paid a visit to Mexico to attend the [APEC Summit in Los Cabos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/APEC_Mexico_2002 "APEC Mexico 2002"). In April 2013, Mexican President [Enrique Peña Nieto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enrique_Pe%C3%B1a_Nieto "Enrique Peña Nieto") paid a visit to Hong Kong, the first visit by a Mexican head of state to the special administrative region.[^33] During his visit, President Peña Nieto met with Chief Executive [Leung Chun-ying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leung_Chun-ying "Leung Chun-ying") and discussed increasing trade between Mexico and Hong Kong. President Peña Nieto also met with businessmen and monetary and financial authorities of the city.[^33] In August 2015, Hong Kong's Secretary for Commerce and Economic Development, [Gregory So](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregory_So "Gregory So"), paid a visit to Mexico.[^32]

### Bilateral agreements

Mexico and Hong Kong have signed a few bilateral agreements, such as an Agreement for the Suppression of visas for holders of Ordinary Passports (2004); Agreement on Air Transportation (2006); Agreement to Avoid Double Taxation and Prevent Tax Evasion with respect to Income Taxes (2012); and an Agreement for the Promotion and Reciprocal Protection of Investments (2020).[^34]

### Trade relations

In 2023, two-way trade between Mexico and Hong Kong amounted to US$1.6 billion.[^35] Hong Kong's main exports products include: electronic integrated circuits and various other electronic equipment; motor vehicles, aluminum, iron and steel products, chemical based products, clothing, jewelry, and food based products. Mexico's main export products include: telephones and mobile phones, electronic integrated circuits, beef and fish, minerals, motor vehicles, scrap metals, clothing, and vegetables.[^35]

## Macau

First contact between Mexico and [Macau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macau "Macau") took place when ships from the Manila galleon would visit the port of [Portuguese Macau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Macau "Portuguese Macau").[^30] Soon after the [Torreón massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torre%C3%B3n_massacre "Torreón massacre") and the expulsion of [Chinese Mexican](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_immigration_to_Mexico "Chinese immigration to Mexico") men (accompanied by their Mexican born wives and children) in the 1930s from northern Mexican states of [Sonora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonora "Sonora") and [Sinaloa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinaloa "Sinaloa");[^36] many from the community were sent to [Guangdong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangdong "Guangdong") Province before settling in Macau which appeared familiar to many Chinese Mexicans as the territory was administered by the Portuguese and the Portuguese language was similar to Spanish, as well as the predominant Catholic religion in the territory. It would be at least 30 years before those expelled would be allowed to return to Mexico.[^36]

The consulate-general of Mexico in Hong Kong maintains a presence periodically in Macau and there are channels for dialogue with local authorities.[^32]

### Bilateral agreements

Since 2008, Mexico and Macau have had an Agreement for the Elimination of Visas for holders of Ordinary Passports.[^32]

### Trade relations

In 2023, two-way trade between Macau and Mexico amounted to US$26.7 million.[^37] Macau's main exports products include: electrical parts, parts and accessories for motor vehicles, plastic, clothing, and antibiotics. Mexico's main exports products include: telephones including mobile phones and video game consoles.[^37]
