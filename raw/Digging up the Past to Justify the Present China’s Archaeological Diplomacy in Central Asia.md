On a windswept plain near Quva in Uzbekistan’s Fergana Valley, a joint team of Chinese and Uzbek archaeologists carefully brushed soil from a set of ancient walls, revealing the outline of a long-forgotten Silk Road settlement dating from the third century BCE. The discovery, reported with enthusiasm by Chinese state media, was framed not simply as a scientific breakthrough, but as a moment of shared historical recovery—evidence of civilizational exchange stretching back millennia.[^1] For Beijing, such scenes have become increasingly common across greater Central Asia: Chinese archaeologists at work in Xinjiang’s Kazakh-populated Ili Valley, Kyrgyzstan’s Chui Valley, Tajikistan’s Zarafshan Valley, and the fertile basins of Uzbekistan’s Fergana Valley. Each excavation is presented as a collaborative scholarly endeavor. Yet, taken together, these projects form something larger and more strategic: a sustained campaign of heritage diplomacy.[^2]

Over the past decade, China has dramatically expanded its archaeological footprint across the Silk Road countries of Central Asia. These initiatives are embedded within broader frameworks of cultural diplomacy, heritage cooperation, and the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), which China launched in 2013. Xi Jinping, when announcing BRI in Kazakhstan in 2013, framed it as a continuation of the past, stating that “throughout the millennia, the people of various countries along the ancient Silk Road have jointly written a chapter of friendship that has been passed on to this very day.” [^3] They are conducted through joint expeditions, shared laboratories, UNESCO nominations, museum alliances, exhibitions, and training programs.

While often described in technical or academic terms, these efforts also serve clear political and narrative functions.[^4] They promote a China-centric interpretation of Eurasian history, reinforce claims about China’s long-standing civilizational presence in regions bordering Xinjiang, and challenge Western- and Russian-dominated historiographies of the region.

---

## Archaeology as Heritage Diplomacy

The concept of heritage diplomacy provides a useful analytical framework for understanding China’s activities. Introduced into international relations scholarship by Tim Winter a decade ago, heritage diplomacy refers to the ways shared history and cultural heritage are transformed into mechanisms of cooperation, governance, and influence.[^5] Unlike traditional diplomacy, which relies on formal political channels, heritage diplomacy operates through cooperation between museums, joint excavations, funding for restoration projects, academic exchanges, and collaborative cultural exhibitions.[^6] Its power lies in its apparent neutrality: archaeology and heritage protection appear apolitical, even as they shape collective memory and identity and serve the interests of those involved.

Chinese leaders have explicitly embraced this logic. In a 2016 speech, Xi Jinping emphasized that “Chinese culture is both historical and contemporary, belonging both to the Chinese nation and the whole world,” highlighting the outward-facing dimension of China’s cultural policy.[^7] This framing aligns with Beijing’s broader ambition to be recognized not merely as an economic or military power but as a “geocultural power”—a state capable of shaping how history itself is understood.

Most recently, China has tied its heritage diplomacy to the Global Civilization Initiative (全球文明倡议), which seeks “progress for humanity and harmony for the entire world.” [^8] Speaking at the launch in 2023, Xi stated that China should “let cultural exchanges transcend estrangement, mutual learning transcend clashes, and inclusiveness transcend any sense of superiority.” Between 2021 and 2025, over 7,700 archaeological excavations took place within China. [^9] At the same time, China significantly expanded global archaeological cooperation, conducting 44 joint archaeological projects in 24 countries over the past decade, including at least 9 in Central Asia.[^10] In Central Asia, heritage diplomacy is particularly potent.[^11] For China, the Silk Road, although only coined as a descriptor in the nineteenth century by German geographers, occupies a near-mythic status as a symbol of peaceful exchange, cosmopolitanism, and mutual learning. The idea of a Silk Road has also become an important concept for the five Central Asian countries to position themselves at historical and contemporary global crossroads. By positioning itself as a primary steward of Silk Road heritage, China taps into shared historical imagery that resonates across the region.

---

## Reinvigorating the Silk Roads

Another core objective of China’s archaeological diplomacy is to engage in epistemic competition. Chinese scholars and media explicitly argue that Western-centered interpretations of the Silk Road marginalize China’s contributions.[^12] By generating new data, leading excavations, and publishing findings through Chinese channels, Beijing seeks to reshape the historiography of Eurasia. UNESCO plays a central role in China’s heritage diplomacy strategy. Of the 33 World Cultural Heritage sites along the Silk Roads, 7 are in Central Asia.[^13] The 2014 inscription of the Routes Network of Chang’an-Tianshan Corridor, a trade network active from the second century BCE to the seventeenth century CE, as a World Heritage site jointly nominated by China, Kazakhstan, and Kyrgyzstan exemplifies how shared heritage can be mobilized for diplomatic purposes. By anchoring Silk Road routes to a framework beginning in Chang’an (modern-day Xi’an), the nomination subtly reinforced a Sinocentric geography of Eurasian exchange.

Modern Chinese archaeological engagement with the Silk Roads is frequently framed, implicitly and explicitly, through the figure of Zhang Qian, the Han dynasty envoy whose missions to Central Asia in the second century BCE are conventionally credited with opening the routes that later became known as the Silk Road.[^14] Archaeologists such as Wang Jianxin—who organized expeditions from Xi’an to Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, and Kyrgyzstan—explicitly describe their work as “following in Zhang Qian’s footsteps.” [^15] In contemporary Chinese discourse, Zhang Qian functions as more than a historical emissary: he is a civilizational origin point. Speaking at the launch of BRI in 2013, Xi Jinping declared that “his journeys opened the door to friendly contacts between China and Central Asian countries as well as the Silk Road linking east and west, Asia and Europe.” [^16] By invoking his departure from Chang’an, Chinese scholars and institutions subtly anchor the genesis of the Silk Roads in China’s political and cultural heartland, positioning Xi’an as the narrative starting line of Eurasian connectivity.

As part of a recent study on how Chinese academics view Central Asia, we surveyed 10,563 publications on the region to see how “soft” historical topics feed into the political projects emerging from Beijing.[^17] Some of this outgrowth in historical research follows political imperatives, with much archaeological work along the Silk Roads being funded in relation to BRI and the narrative China seeks to advance about its place in the world.[^18] References to the Silk Road by Chinese academics surged tenfold between 2010 and 2015, peaking at over 4,000 references as China sought to justify its new BRI project.[^19] The growth in these topics follows China’s diplomatic efforts after 2013 to focus on the region and find common histories to bridge China and Central Asia as relations started to deepen. When narrating its history of interactions with Central Asia over the millennia, official Chinese historical narratives portray China’s role as constructive and peaceful.

In the upper reaches of the Surxondaryo River valley, where the foothills of southern Uzbekistan open toward the routes once linking the Silk Road, archaeologists recently began peeling back layers of earth at the site of Chinar-Tepa.[^20] What emerged was not a single monument or isolated find, but the outline of an entire lived landscape: more than 30 house foundations, scattered domestic artifacts, and dozens of burials dating to the era of the Kushan Empire (30 BCE–375 CE). The evidence fills a long-standing gap in the archaeological record of the eastern Surxondaryo region, demonstrating sustained settlement during a period when the Kushan realm served as one of the Silk Road’s most important hubs of connectivity.

The excavation is part of a wider constellation of Chinese-led projects in Uzbekistan that began in 2012, when joint teams first turned their attention to sites such as Mingtepa in the Fergana Valley—a fortified city whose walls, gates, and towered entrances have been compared by Chinese scholars to both medieval Central Asian urban forms and the ancient capitals of Luoyang and Chang’an.[^21] Drawing on textual references during the Han dynasty to Dayuan alongside material evidence from the ground, Chinese archaeologists argue that such sites reveal the deep entanglement of Central Asia with China’s early imperial world.

Long before the Silk Road took a recognizable shape, patterns of settlement were already emerging across Eurasia, and it is this deep prehistory that Chinese-led archaeology increasingly seeks to connect. In eastern China, the ruins of Liangzhu, first identified in the early twentieth century, preserve a dense landscape of villages, tombs, and ritual altars that chart the rise of complex urban life between 3300 and 2300 BCE. More than a millennium earlier, a parallel process was unfolding thousands of kilometers to the west in the Zarafshan Valley, where communities began to coalesce during a transitional phase between mobile pastoralism and settled agriculture.[^22] Excavated jointly by Chinese and Tajik archaeologists, the ruins of this proto-urban settlement, among the oldest yet identified in Central Asia, have been presented as a mirror to Liangzhu, revealing comparable rhythms of early social organization across distant parts of Asia.

In the wide Chui Valley of northern Kyrgyzstan, where the Tian Shan foothills flatten into grassland, the ruins of Ak-Beshim lie scattered across the terrain—mudbrick walls, eroded foundations, and the faint outlines of what was once Suyab, a major Silk Road settlement and former political center of Inner Asia.[^23] Today, the site has become the focus of a new phase of Chinese-Kyrgyz cooperation. Through a joint initiative led by Kyrgyzstan’s Ministry of Culture and China’s Dunhuang Academy, the two countries have established a dedicated laboratory for the preservation of cultural heritage, transforming Ak-Beshim into both a conservation project and a living research space. The site’s layered history lends itself naturally to this collaborative framing: Chinese sources emphasize traditions suggesting that the Tang dynasty poet Li Bo may have been born in Suyab.[^24]

“As China-Uzbekistan joint archaeological efforts continue to produce impressive results, the real key to reviving these cultural relics is fostering a sense of cultural identity,” Tang Yunpeng, the leader of the Chinese archeological team in Uzbekistan, told the *Global Times*.[^25] Archaeologists such as Tang have collaborated with their counterparts in ten countries along the Silk Road, conducting more than 70 archaeological and cultural heritage preservation projects. These include joint archeological teams in Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, and Kazakhstan.

## Institutionalizing Heritage Diplomacy

In spring 2023, the ancient city of Xi’an once again assumed its familiar role as a point of convergence. Delegations from across Asia gathered there for the General Assembly of the Alliance for Cultural Heritage in Asia, where participants issued a joint declaration committing to collaborative archaeological research on the origins of civilizations and the Silk Road, as well as coordinated engagement with global heritage frameworks. Only weeks later, Xi’an hosted another carefully choreographed moment of convergence: the China–Central Asia Summit, at which China and five Central Asian states formalized new agreements on joint excavation, conservation, and cultural heritage restoration.[^26]

On the ground, Chinese archaeologists describe the effects of this alignment in human terms. “Local people greet us with *nihao* and smiles,” noted Wang Jianxin, recalling the reception Chinese teams receive at overseas sites.[^27] For Central Asian partners such as Gulmira Mukhtarova, the value lies in reciprocity: joint excavation, shared methods, and the accumulation of practical experience that extends beyond any single site.[^28] Framed within the broader logic of BRI, these encounters recast archaeology as a form of people-to-people diplomacy—cultivating goodwill, expertise, and historical narrative in tandem, and actively reassembling the Silk Road around Xi’an as its living center.

China’s archaeological diplomacy is underpinned by a dense institutional ecosystem. At its core are academic and research bodies such as the Chinese Institute of Archaeology, the Shaanxi Academy of Archaeology, and Xi’an’s Northwest University. These institutions have spearheaded dozens of joint projects across Central Asia since the early 2010s. Shaanxi Province, in particular, has emerged as a key subnational actor. Home to Xi’an—ancient Chang’an, the eastern terminus of the Silk Road—Shaanxi leverages its historical prestige to justify a leading role in overseas archaeological engagement.

Beyond individual institutions, China has established multilateral platforms designed explicitly to internationalize its heritage agenda. The International Alliance of Museums of the Silk Road, founded in Beijing in 2017 with support from the Ministry of Culture and Tourism and the State Administration of Cultural Heritage, now includes roughly 180 member institutions.[^29] Its stated mission is to “forge bonds of friendship” among Silk Road peoples through museum cooperation, exhibitions, and exchanges.[^30] Similarly, the Belt and Road Cultural Heritage Global Alliance, launched in Xi’an in 2019 under the auspices of Northwestern Polytechnical University, brings together dozens of heritage institutions across Eurasia.

These alliances do more than facilitate technical cooperation. They standardize interpretive frameworks, circulate Chinese methodologies, and reinforce Beijing’s role as a convenor and agenda-setter. Through conferences, declarations, and joint statements, such as those issued at the Alliance for Cultural Heritage in Asia, China embeds its vision of Silk Road history within regional and global heritage governance structures. Institutions of heritage diplomacy include the following:

- Chinese Institute of Archaeology
- Shaanxi Provincial Research Institute of Archaeology
- Xi’an’s Northwest University
- International Alliance of Museums of the Silk Road
- Belt and Road Cultural Heritage Global Alliance
- Northwestern Polytechnical University
- Dunhuang Academy
- Collaborative Research Center for Archaeology of the Silk Roads
- Shaanxi Academy of Archaeology
- Chinese-Kyrgyz Joint Laboratory for the Protection of Cultural Heritage

Such museum-based cooperation is increasingly visible across Central Asia. One illustrative example is “Silk and the Silk Road: From China to Kazakhstan,” which opened at the National Museum of the Republic of Kazakhstan (NMRK), serving as a flagship cultural event for Kazakhstan’s 2025 Year of China Tourism.[^31] At the opening ceremony, the China National Silk Museum and the NMRK signed a memorandum of cooperation establishing an overseas exhibition space for the Chinese museum within the NMRK.[^32] This initiative forms part of the Silk Museum’s “Global Showcase” program, which, since 2023, has mounted exhibitions in seven countries, including Hungary, France, Uzbekistan, and the United States.

Institutional cooperation has also extended beyond exhibitions into permanent research infrastructure. On October 21, the Issyk branch of the Collaborative Research Centre for Archaeology of the Silk Roads was inaugurated at Kazakhstan’s State Historical-Cultural Museum-Reserve Issyk, marking the first branch established outside China.[^33] The new center will concentrate on research related to the “golden man” burial complex and other archaeological sites in the Issyk region, integrating scientific investigation with training programs, public outreach, and joint conservation initiatives.[^34] Similar patterns of cooperation are visible in China-Uzbekistan engagements. On May 10, the digital exhibition “Yu Hong’s Era: A Cultural Confluence from 1,400 Years Ago” opened at Fergana State University, cohosted by Chinese archaeological institutions and the university.[^35] The event coincided with the launch of a joint archaeological center and a new laboratory dedicated to the conservation and restoration of cultural relics.

Exhibition-based collaboration has likewise taken on a sustained, serial form. On December 6, the China National Silk Museum and the Samarkand State Museum-Reserve jointly opened “Silk and Silk Roads: From Hangzhou to Samarkand,” the third installment in an ongoing exhibition series.[^36] Focusing on silk art from the Tang dynasty (618–907 CE), the exhibition explores historical artistic connections between China and Central Asia through four thematic sections, combining restored historical garments with contemporary designs and interactive presentations to link Silk Road heritage with modern cultural expression.[^37]

Joint heritage protection projects further illustrate the practical dimensions of this cooperation. A recent reporting meeting at the National Historical Museum of Kyrgyzstan reviewed progress on a preliminary survey conducted as part of a Chinese-Kyrgyz initiative to protect the Ak-Beshim settlement.[^38] The site forms part of the UNESCO-listed “Silk Roads: the Routes Network of Chang’an-Tianshan Corridor,” jointly nominated by China, Kazakhstan, and Kyrgyzstan. Led by the Dunhuang Academy in partnership with Kyrgyzstan’s Ministry of Culture, the project has completed baseline assessments of site conditions, analyzed degradation processes, and developed a conservation plan to guide future preservation efforts, with both sides agreeing on priorities for the next phase of work.

## Integrating Xinjiang

Beijing’s claims to a peaceful order built on trade belies its deep history as a colonial power, epitomized in its name for the Turkic lands known today as Xinjiang, meaning “new frontier.” Its claims to rule Xinjiang go back two thousand years to the Han dynasty, which had its capital in modern-day China’s Henan Province, although formal annexation only came in 1884.[^39]

While China’s archaeological diplomacy is outward-facing, it is deeply entangled with domestic concerns in this westernmost region. In recent years, Chinese officials and scholars have increasingly framed archaeological work as a means of countering “untrue narratives” that separate Xinjiang culture from Chinese civilization. “There is an untrue narrative in the international community that separates Xinjiang culture from Chinese culture and even sets it up in opposition,” Pan Yue, deputy director of the Chinese Communist Party United Front Work Department, said in June 2025. “But a large amount of archaeological evidence tells us that Xinjiang has been an important part of the Chinese cultural circle since ancient times.” [^40] Excavations, museum exhibits, and historical claims are mobilized to portray Xinjiang as an ancient melting pot that has always been integrated into a broader Chinese cultural sphere.[^41]

Claims that a seventh-century Chinese empress ordered the construction of Buddhist temples in Xinjiang, or that Silk Road cities in Central Asia demonstrate enduring Chinese influence, are not merely academic arguments.[^42] They serve to legitimize Beijing’s governance of a region that is internationally scrutinized for human rights abuses. By projecting narratives of historical belonging outward into Central Asia, China reinforces them inward at home. China does not view itself as a historically colonial power. This “imperial innocence” stems from the idea that China expanded naturally and peacefully, incorporating new peoples into its multi-ethnic state.[^43] Like the Silk Road metaphor, this selective reading of history seeks to justify China’s present rule over Xinjiang and dampen any criticism of its policies.

---

## Conclusion

China’s expanding archaeological presence across Central Asia reveals how the past has become an arena of strategic competition. Through joint excavations, museum alliances, UNESCO nominations, and institutional platforms anchored in Xi’an, Beijing is not merely uncovering ancient settlements; it is shaping the interpretive frameworks through which Eurasian history is understood. By positioning itself as the steward of Silk Road heritage, China embeds contemporary geopolitical ambitions within narratives of civilizational continuity, peaceful exchange, and shared destiny. In doing so, archaeology becomes more than scholarship: it becomes infrastructure for influence, reinforcing China’s regional role while recasting the historical map of Eurasian connectivity in subtly Sinocentric terms.
