**41:54** · Han tombs tell us as much about everyday life as any written text. The Han believed deeply in the afterlife and wealthy families equipped their dead with everything they might need. Food, wine, lacquer, silk, bronze vessels, clay models of houses and servants and farm animals.

**42:16** · Most remarkable are the jade burial suits. garments made from thousands of jade pieces, each sewn together with gold, silver, or bronze wire, depending on the deceased's rank. Jade was believed to preserve the body. The suits could contain over 2,000 individual pieces. They are one of the most striking artifacts of any ancient civilization.

### The Collapse of the Eastern Han

**42:44** · When Emperor Jang died in 88 AD, the Eastern Han entered a period from which it never truly recovered. The problem was structural, and it had been building since the dynasty's founding. The system was designed to be run by capable adult emperors, and it kept producing children.

**43:06** · Emperor He came to the throne at age 10.

**43:09** · Emperor Shang, who technically was an emperor, died at age 1. Emperor Anne took the throne at 13 and preferred drinking to governing. He handed real power to the palace unics who surrounded him. Emperor Shang, Emperor Chong, Emperor Xi. None of them ruled long enough or lived long enough to matter.

**43:35** · Between 88 and 146 AD, Han had seven emperors. Most of them were children.

**43:43** · Most real power was held by Empress Daagers, their male relatives, and increasingly by the palace unuk.

**43:52** · The Unuks deserve some explanation.

**43:55** · In the Han court, Unix, men who had been castrated and therefore could safely live within the inner imperial quarters, served as personal attendants to the emperor. They were trusted with physical access that no other officials had. They knew the emperor's private thoughts, controlled his household, managed who could see him. As the emperors became younger and more dependent, the Unix became more powerful.

**44:25** · By the 2n century AD, senior unics were appointing officials, issuing edicts in the emperor's name, and accumulating enormous personal wealth.

**44:36** · This created a three-way power struggle that paralyzed the court. The Unix, the Consort clans, and the Confucian educated official class who considered both of the other factions corrupt and illegitimate.

**44:51** · When officials and students organized to protest the unuk's power, emperors Juan and Ling instead of acting against the Unix had the protesters arrested.

**45:05** · In 166 AD and again in 169 AD, hundreds of scholars and officials were blacklisted, imprisoned or executed in what became known as the Dangu Ji Huo, partisan prohibitions.

**45:22** · The intellectual class was alienated.

**45:26** · The best people in the empire were being punished for trying to fix what was broken. Outside the palace, conditions were deteriorating.

**45:36** · Droughts and floods hit the empire repeatedly through the 170s and 180s.

**45:42** · Crops failed. Peasants who couldn't pay their taxes had their land seized, usually by powerful local families who were already wealthy enough to weather the crisis. The landless poor flooded the cities or wandered the countryside.

**45:58** · The divide between the very wealthy and the desperately poor had never been wider. Into this situation stepped a man named Jang Xiao.

**46:08** · Jang Xiao was a faith healer and Tauist teacher who had been traveling the countryside for years, offering healing to the sick through a combination of prayer, confession, and herbal medicine.

**46:21** · His movement called the way of great peace promised that the era of Han symbolized by the color blue was ending and that a new age of great peace symbolized by yellow was about to begin.

**46:36** · He and his brothers organized followers across eight different provinces simultaneously.

**46:43** · In the spring of 184 AD they rose. The followers tied yellow cloth around their heads. Yellow for the new age, yellow for the earth, yellow to identify each other when the fighting started.

**46:57** · Hundreds of thousands of people rose at almost the same moment across multiple provinces.

**47:04** · Granaries were seized. Local government offices were attacked. Han army units were rooted. The Han government's response was the decision that finished the dynasty. Unable to deploy enough regular troops fast enough, the court authorized regional governors and powerful local figures to raise their own armies and deal with the rebellion themselves.

**47:28** · The yellow turbans were eventually crushed. Jang Xiao himself died before the main campaigns ended, possibly from illness. The rebellion was over within a year.

**47:42** · But the warlords didn't disband their armies afterward. Why would they? They now had troops, weapons, and regional authority. The center had proven it couldn't protect them. So, they kept what they had. In 189 AD, the Han court fell into another succession crisis when Emperor Ling died.

**48:03** · A general named Dong Jaw marched his army into Lu Yang, deposed the designated heir, and installed a child of his choosing on the throne. He then proceeded to sack Luo Yang, burning parts of the city, terrorizing the population and forcing the court to relocate to the old western Han capital of Changan.

**48:31** · The warlords who opposed Dongja formed a coalition, but the coalition quickly dissolved as each warlord realized he was more interested in carving out his own territory than in restoring the Han.

**48:45** · China fragmented.

**48:47** · The emperor, Emperor Xian, who came to the throne as a child in 189 AD, became a porn passed between powerful men who used his name to legitimize their own authority.

**49:01** · The most capable of those men was Chao Chowo. He was not a warlord in the simple sense. He was a genuinely talented administrator, a skilled general, a poet, and a ruthless political operator who understood that controlling the emperor was more valuable than replacing him. From 196 AD onward, Chow Chow kept Emperor Xian as a figurehead while effectively running the empire's northern half.

**49:29** · By 208 AD, Chowo Chow had consolidated control of most of northern China and set out to complete reunification by conquering the south.

**49:43** · He assembled a massive fleet on the Yangze River. His opponents, the warlord Sun Kuan in the southeast and Liu Bay in the west, formed an alliance against him. Their combined fleet met Chow Chows at a place called Red Cliffs in what is now Hube Province. What happened next is one of the most famous engagements in Chinese military history. Chowo Chow's northern troops were not accustomed to fighting on water.

**50:14** · The opposing fleet, led by the admiral Joe Yu, used fire ships, vessels loaded with dry reeds and fat, set a light and sent sailing into Chaochows anchored fleet with the wind behind them. The Han fleet burned.

**50:32** · Chowchow barely escaped with the remnants of his army. The Battle of Red Cliffs in 208 AD shattered his hope of reunification.

**50:43** · China would remain divided.

**50:46** · For the next 12 years, the fiction of the Han dynasty continued. Emperor Xian held the throne in name. Chowo Chowo held the power. When Chowo Chowo died in 220 AD, his son Xiao Pi made a decision.

**51:03** · He informed Emperor Xian politely but unmistakably that the time had come to step down. Emperor Xi Anne, who had been a puppet emperor for 31 years, who had watched everything he ruled turn to ash around him, agreed. He surrendered the imperial seal. Xiao Pi declared the way dynasty. Emperor Xian was given an estate and a title and lived quietly for another 14 years. He died in 234 AD at age 54.

**51:40** · In the south, Liu Bay, who claimed descent from the Han royal family, declared his own Han dynasty, known to history as Shuhan, refusing to recognize the transfer of the mandate.

**51:54** · Sunun Kuan eventually declared his own state of Wu. China was now three kingdoms.

**52:02** · The Han dynasty, the real one, was over.

### Why The Han Dynasty Never Really Ended

**52:08** · The Han dynasty lasted from 202 BC to 220 AD, 422 years in total with a brief interruption.

**52:19** · Today, 1.4 billion people call themselves Han Chinese, not as a political statement, not because a government told them to. The term Han stopped being about a dynasty somewhere in the last 2,000 years and became the word for a shared cultural identity, a language, a set of values, a way of writing, a way of thinking about history and society and family.

**52:49** · That kind of cultural persistence is genuinely rare in human history. The dynasty that produced it ended almost 2,000 years ago.

**52:59** · The political model the Han established, a centralized empire governed by an educated bureaucracy selected through examination operating under Confucian principles became the template for every Chinese dynasty that followed. The Tang, the Song, the Ming, theQing, all of them, different as they were, were working within a framework that the Han had built. Paper invented or perfected during the Han changed the world.

**53:29** · It traveled west through central Asia, reached the Islamic world by the 8th century and from there spread to medieval Europe where it replaced expensive parchment and made books affordable which made literacy possible at scale which eventually made the printing press meaningful. The Silk Road opened under Emperor Wu, secured by generals like Banchcha functioned as the primary artery of Eurasian commerce and cultural exchange for over a millennium.

**54:05** · The Taiishaong Shu or Simarian's Shiji written during the depths of his personal humiliation completed despite everything became the model for historical writing across East Asia.

**54:20** · Every official dynastic history that China produced after that followed the structure C ma chian established.

**54:29** · Japan, Korea, and Vietnam adopted similar historioggraphical traditions.

**54:36** · The way billions of people think about the past today owes something to a man who wrote in the first century BC. The Han wasn't a perfect dynasty. It had brutal emperors and incompetent ones. It had corruption and famines and rebellions.

**54:54** · But it built something durable enough that when the dynasty itself ended, what it had created did not. The name survived. The culture survived. The values survived.
