China’s rapid advances in military technology, growing assertiveness in the Indo-Pacific, and expanding ability to project power globally make the capability of its People’s Liberation Army a central question in international security debates. Wuthnow and Saunders, two preeminent scholars of the Chinese military, have joined forces in this authoritative study that is both accessible and deeply informative. They trace the evolution of the PLA’s organizational structure, doctrine, capabilities, and threat environment from the era of Deng Xiaoping that began in the late 1970s to the present and conclude that under Xi Jinping’s leadership, the PLA has become stronger and more capable than at any point in its history. At the same time, the PLA’s command structure, logistics, and training regimens remain flawed in ways that not only produce significant vulnerabilities but also make it harder for China to transform from global actor to global power. Although the PLA is resourced for combat in the Indo-Pacific, for example, it could not manage a sustained global conflict. The authors suggest that closing this gap will be difficult but not impossible. They believe it is only a matter of time before the PLA addresses these deficits and becomes the U.S. military’s true peer competitor.
