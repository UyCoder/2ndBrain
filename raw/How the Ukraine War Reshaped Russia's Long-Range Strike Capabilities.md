Russia’s long-range strike arsenal was supposed to be running on empty by now. At the end of 2022, Ukrainian intelligence assessed that Russia had fired through most of its pre-war Iskander stockpile, and analysts widely expected its precision missile stocks to be exhausted within months. That prediction was wrong. Rather than exhausting this supply, Russia rebuilt it. The arsenal that exists in 2026 is now larger and more varied than the one Russia took to war. What stands out is that Russia produces almost all of these weapons. The strike complex now terrorizing Ukrainian cities is the output of a Russian defense industry rebuilt around three priorities: extending range, increasing warhead lethality, and saturating defenses.

### Russia’s Wartime Arsenal

Russia’s long-range strike inventory now spans three operating tiers:

- Mass-produced one-way attack drones
- Theatre-range cruise and ballistic missiles
- Intermediate-range and air-launched systems with continental or global.

Each tier addresses a different operational problem, and the boundary between them is increasingly blurred as drone payloads and ranges grow.

Russia’s strike strategy layers cheap drones with cruise and ballistic missiles in coordinated packages, exploiting the mismatch between threats and countermeasures, and forcing Ukrainian defenders to choose in the terminal seconds what to engage and what to let through.

### Russia’s One-way Attack Drones

The Shahed-class drone is the clearest case of Russian industrial adaptation during the war. Initially, Moscow was purchasing Iran’s Shahed-136 loitering munition. By November 2022, Russia and Iran had [agreed](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national-security/2022/11/19/russia-iran-drones-secret-deal/) on a $1.75 billion deal for Russia to build a domestic production line at the Alabuga Special Economic Zone in Tatarstan, with Iran transferring production documentation, software, engine manufacturing procedures, and training for Russian personnel. The original contract called for 6,000 drones by September 2025. Alabuga [met](https://edition.cnn.com/2025/08/08/europe/russia-drone-factory-iran-intl) that target roughly a year early and has since moved well past it. It is [estimated](https://politicstoday.org/russia-drone-manufacturing-iran/) that around 90% of Russian Shahed-136 production is now domestic, and the unit cost has fallen from around $200,000 to approximately $70,000. Russia has upgraded the airframe, guidance systems, and warhead beyond what Tehran supplied, and [current output](https://www.cnn.com/2025/08/08/europe/russia-drone-factory-iran-intl) at Alabuga is running at over 5,500 units per month.

Each generation of drone systems has been modified in response to Ukrainian countermeasures:

- **Geran-2** (Shahed-136 equivalent). Delta-wing airframe, with a cruise speed of 185 km/h. Range up to 2,500 km, GPS-guided. It carries a 90kg warhead. It constitutes the primary platform of Russia’s mass saturation campaign.
- **Geran-3** (Shahed-238 equivalent). [Jet-powered](https://www.pravda.com.ua/eng/news/2025/09/16/7531118/) variant of the Geran-2 airframe, equipped with a Chinese turbojet engine. Cruis speed up to 370 km/h, and an operational range up to 1,000 km. The speed increase places it beyond the interception envelope of the propeller-driven interceptor drones Ukraine developed to counter the Geran-2.
- **Geran-5**. [First used](https://www.analisidifesa.it/2026/01/in-azione-nel-conflitto-in-ucraina-il-nuovo-geran-5-russo/) in combat in January 2026. A jet-powered, fixed-wing airframe reaching 600 km/h with a 90 kg warhead and a range of approximately 1,000 km. Notably, it can be released from Su-25 bombers, shifting the launch point away from fixed ground sites and adding 200-500 km of range, making its approach vector far harder to anticipate.
- **Gerbera**. Decoy UAV costing around $10,000, designed to replicate the radar and thermal signature of a Geran-2 within a mixed strike package.

Ukrainian intelligence [assesses](https://www.pravda.com.ua/eng/articles/2026/05/19/8035370/) that Russia intends to make jet-powered drones half of its long-range output, which would further degrade interception rates already slipping through 2025.

### Russia’s Ballistic and Cruise Missiles

The [systems](https://militarnyi.com/en/articles/from-kalibr-to-kinzhal-how-much-do-russian-missiles-really-cost/) in this tier are the core of any Russian strike against hardened fixed infrastructure. Unlike the drone tier, these missiles cannot be absorbed in large numbers by a defender running on limited interceptor stocks: each one requires a dedicated high-end engagement, and each costs the defender far more to stop than it costs Russia to fire. Every system listed here can reach a European capital from its current deployment position.

- **Iskander-M (9M723)**. Short-range quasi-ballistic missile. Road-mobile, launched from a wheeled launcher. Carries a warhead of around 480 kg. It has a range of up to 500 km. Some variants carry penetration-aid decoy canisters that emit thermal and radar signatures to confuse interceptors during the terminal phase. Unit cost is approximately $3 million.
- **Iskander-K (9M729)**. Ground-launched cruise missile. Russia claims a range of under 480 km, while [Western intelligence](https://missilethreat.csis.org/missile/ssc-8-novator-9m729/) assesses a maximum range of 2,500 km. Carries a 500kg warhead, conventional or low-yield nuclear. Ukraine [confirmed](https://united24media.com/latest-news/russia-uses-9m729-missile-that-triggered-us-inf-treaty-exit-to-strike-ukraine-12997) at least 23 combat uses since August 2025. From western Russia, the assessed range puts Berlin, Paris and Rome within reach. Unit cost varies from $1.5 to $7 million.
- **Kalibr (3M14)**. Sea- and submarine-launched land-attack cruise missile. Surface ship variants (3M14T) launched via vertical launch system; submarine variants (3M14K) fired from torpedo tubes. It has a range of up to 2,500 km. Unit cost is approximately $2 million.
- **Kh-47M2 Kinzhal**. Air-launched aeroballistic missile, derived from the Iskander-M airframe and adapted for air launch. Carried externally by the MiG-31K and, in limited numbers, the Tu-22M3. Range 1,500-2,000 km from the launch aircraft’s release point. Warhead 480 kg, conventional or nuclear. Unit cost is approximately $4.5 million. A Patriot battery in Kyiv [intercepted](https://www.npr.org/2023/05/06/1174505616/ukraine-patriot-defense-russian-hypersonic-missile-kinzhal) a Kinzhal in May 2023, the first confirmed intercept of the system.
- **Kh-101.** Air-launched conventional cruise missile, with a range of 3,500-5,500 km. The airframe incorporates radar-absorbent materials and a reduced cross-section to lower detectability, and later production batches carry decoy dispensers and an electronic-warfare module. Launched from Tu-95MS (up to 8 missiles) and Tu-160 bombers (up to 12) operating from bases well outside Ukrainian or [NATO](https://www.geopoliticalmonitor.com/tag/nato/) airspace. Unit cost is approximately 2 million.

Two further systems round out the tier. The 3M22 Zircon is a scramjet-powered sea-launched cruise missile carried on Admiral Gorshkov-class frigates and Yasen-M submarines. Russia also fields the Kh-22 and its Kh-32 upgrade, which are Soviet-era heavy missiles carried by Tu-22M3 bombers.

### The Strategic Tier

The strategic systems are fired in smaller numbers but carry the heaviest political signal. This is also where Russia advertises capabilities it cannot yet field at scale.

- **Kh-102**. The thermonuclear variant of the Kh-101 airframe, carrying a reported 250 kt warhead. It shares the parent missile’s range of 3,500-5,500 km, its radar-absorbent construction, and the same Tu-95MS and Tu-160 launch platforms, but is not expended in the conventional strike campaign.
- **Oreshnik**. Intermediate-range ballistic missile, road-mobile. Range assessed at 3,000-5,500 km, and a MIRV warhead. The [Dnipro strike](https://english.nv.ua/nation/what-is-the-oreshnik-missile-and-can-air-defense-intercept-it-50610643.html) of November 2024 marked the first confirmed combat use of a MIRV. Interception requires exo-atmospheric engagement. Systems capable of this include SM-3 Block IIA and Arrow-3, neither of which is widely deployed in Europe.
- **[Burevestnik](https://www.geopoliticalmonitor.com/russias-burevestnik-launch-an-exercise-in-strategic-communication/)**. Nuclear-powered cruise missile, still in development and testing. Propelled by a nuclear thermal jet engine designed to provide theoretically unlimited range. Russia [claimed](https://www.twz.com/nuclear/skyfall-nuclear-powered-cruise-missile-long-range-test-claimed-by-russia) a successful 14,000 km, 15-hour test in October 2025. Putin ordered the preparation of deployment infrastructure following the claimed test.

### Russia’s Industrial Base

The strike volume Russia generates rests on industrial capacity built up since 2022, sanctions-evasion networks operating through third countries, and external supply from China, Iran, and North Korea. Ukrainian [assessments](https://english.nv.ua/russian-war/expert-assesses-russia-s-missile-stockpiles-and-ability-to-launch-mass-strikes-50565639.html) put monthly replenishment at 120 to 150% of expenditure. By June 2025, Ukrainian military intelligence [assessed](https://euromaidanpress.com/2025/11/27/russias-growing-missile-stockpile/#google_vignette) Russian capacity at roughly 2,900 cruise and ballistic missiles a year against about 2,061 launched in 2024, leaving hundreds in reserve each year. That output rests on a war economy now in its fourth year. Russia’s military spending [has risen](https://www.themoscowtimes.com/2025/12/26/did-russias-defense-sector-boom-peak-in-2025-a91555) nearly 300% since 2021, reaching almost $150 billion in 2025, the highest share since the Soviet collapse. Defense Minister Andrei Belousov [affirmed](https://tass.com/politics/2060339) that this production architecture is being built toward confrontation with NATO in the 2030s.

Production has not grown uniformly. It has grown where Russian commanders found their weapons working and contracted where they did not. For instance, Russia [ramped up](https://defence-blog.com/russia-expands-kh-101-and-kinzhal-missile-production/) the Kh-101 while cutting the Kalibr, a reallocation that suggests that the air-launched cruise missile earned its keep and the naval one did not.

The Alabuga complex shows how far the reconstitution has gone. The plant met its original contract, 6,000 drones by September 2025, about a year early, and has moved from screwdriver assembly of Iranian kits to a domesticated line that now modifies the design itself. The dependency on Tehran that started the program has inverted: Russia builds the drones, improves them faster than Iran does, and no longer needs Iranian permission to do either.

One potential bottleneck can be found in Russian reliance on Western microelectronics. The Kh-101 that struck Kyiv’s Okhmatdyt children’s hospital in July 2024 [contained](https://euromaidanpress.com/2024/07/11/western-tech-in-russias-missiles-15-companies-whose-parts-fuel-kh-101-missile-that-hit-kyiv-childrens-hospital/) 28 foreign components from 15 Western firms, with chips traced to US, European, and Japanese manufacturers. Of course, because of the sanctions, none of those firms sells to Russia directly. Yet the parts still arrive through [intermediaries](https://2021-2025.state.gov/office-of-the-spokesperson/releases/2025/01/sanctions-to-disrupt-russias-military-industrial-base-and-sanctions-evasion/) in China, Turkey, the UAE and Kazakhstan, purchased as civilian dual-use goods and then rerouted into missile plants.

### The View from Europe

Almost every European capital sits within the range of Russian missiles, and the defensive arithmetic is fraught. Aster 30 production, the European interceptor for the SAMP/T system, [ran](https://norskluftvern.com/2026/01/02/europes-long-range-air-defense-decision-samp-t-ng-versus-next-generation-patriot/) at 80 to 100 units in 2025, with output targeted to reach 300 by 2028. Patriot PAC-3 MSE production sits around 500 per year, climbing toward 650 by 2027. Patriot deliveries to non-Ukrainian customers are running [four to five years](https://militarywatchmagazine.com/article/severe-patriot-air-defence-shortages-netherlands-european-rival) behind schedule. Critically, Russia produces more ballistic missiles in a year than Lockheed Martin produces PAC-3 MSE interceptors. The gap [widens](https://defence-industry.eu/europes-missile-defence-gaps-and-dependence-on-patriot-systems-highlighted-by-cepa/) further when one includes Shahed-class drones, which absorb interceptors designed for far more capable threats.

The Russian long-range strike complex has become a structural feature of the Russian state, built and tested under fire, and designed to scale further. A few developments are worth tracking closely over the next year.

The first is the jet-powered share of long-range drone output. If it crosses 50%, current Ukrainian interception rates erode further, and European defense planning against drone saturation becomes considerably harder.

The second is Oreshnik: serial production and forward deployment would force European air defense planning to confront a class of threat that Patriot and SAMP/T architectures were not designed to intercept. Both are optimized for short-range ballistic missiles below 1,000 km range, and neither can reach the mid-course phase above 100 km where a pre-separation intercept is possible. By the time Patriot or SAMP/T can engage, the Oreshnik has already released its warheads in space, arriving as six or more simultaneous objects, mixed with decoys, faster than either system can handle.

The third is what Russia does with its production lines after the [Ukraine war](https://www.geopoliticalmonitor.com/tag/ukraine-war/) ends. If the military-industrial sites continue at wartime output rates once fighting stops, considering that [roughly 5%](https://www.themoscowtimes.com/2025/12/26/did-russias-defense-sector-boom-peak-in-2025-a91555) of the workforce is absorbed by the defense sector, the stockpile that is currently kept thin by daily expenditure will begin compounding. Within a few years at current production rates, Russia would hold a ready arsenal that has no proportionate operational purpose against Ukraine but would represent a formidable first-strike inventory against NATO. Ukraine has served as a four-year stress test of every system in this inventory. Whatever ceasefire arrangement eventually emerges, Russia retains the factories, the doctrine, and the data accumulated under fire. Europe’s window to close its own production gap is closing at roughly the same rate.
