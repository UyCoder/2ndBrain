## Introduction

Halford Mackinder made a famous comment: “Who rules East Europe commands the Heartland; who rules the Heartland commands the World-Island; who rules the World-Island commands the world” (Mackinder, [2026)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?l2jAsc). Today, Central Asia (Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, etc.) contains vast reserves of oil, natural gas, uranium, and rare-earth minerals. In a **Heartland framework**, whoever monopolises these resources can emerge as an industrial empire, capable of challenging the rest of the world [*(Ranked: Natural Gas Reserves by Country, n.d.)*](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?YQNPP1).

This paper argues that China’s Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) is a modern realisation of Mackinder’s railway hypothesis. China seeks to integrate the “World Island” physically, thereby enabling it to circumvent maritime chokepoints, such as the Strait of Malacca, currently controlled by the U.S. Navy [(Raymond, 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?gKla5J). On the other hand, India does not share a direct border with Central Asia, but Central Asia is officially classified as India’s “extended neighbourhood”. India’s engagement with the five Central Asian Republics (Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, and Uzbekistan) is part of a grand strategy, resource economics, and continental security.

This study emphasises a qualitative geopolitical analysis of secondary data, employing a comparative and interpretive framework to examine the strategic behaviour of India and China in Central Asia.

## Historical Evolution And Theoretical Framework

Central Asia has for centuries been the battleground for a “Great Game”, a cold war between the British and Russian Empires, competing for control of territories between Iran and China to protect British India. This game for control of the region was supposedly motivated by the now-infamous Halford Mackinder “Heartland” theory of 1904, which posited that control of a large, inland region (which included much of Central Asia) could be used to effect world domination.

Following the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991, a “New Great Game” emerged, shifting the focus from military-political territorial conquest to the exploitation of the Caspian region’s vast oil and natural gas reserves, prompting Western politicians to label it the “New Middle East”. China is playing the best game of integration into the region through its explosive economic growth. This relentless foreign intervention prioritises resource extraction over local development. Through the lens of dependency theory, the imbalance in the relationship between the core (foreign powers) and the periphery (heartland) will lead to continued poverty and rising extremism across Central Asia [(Bhat, 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?RJKdE7).

While a nation like India seeks to maximise its influence in this region through soft power diplomacy, developmental projects, cultural exchange, resource management, etc. From the very beginning, India espouses policies such as “Vasudhaiva Kutumbkam”, mutual respect for other sovereign nations, and civilisational roots [(Biswa, 2024)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?UezbgA).

The geopolitical and economic dynamics of Central Asia can be deeply understood through the lens of **geopolitical realism**. The “Heartland” is crucial for global dominance, where China uses the BRI to circumvent the region physically, and India counters this to protect continental security and safeguard its territorial sovereignty. **Hegemonic theory** further explains China’s actions, focusing on how a dominant state projects power to shape the international system to its advantage and counter rivals by showing massive power projection. Scholars argue that by leveraging “debt-trap diplomacy” and building dual-use ports, China effectively encircles rivals like India through a “String of Pearls”. Finally, **dependency theory** highlights the exploitative economic dynamics at play, arguing that resources flow from underdeveloped peripheries to a wealthy core, systematically stunting the periphery’s development.

## Civilizational Ties Of Central Asia With India And China

India and Central Asia have a deep historical and civilisational connection that dates back thousands of years. Even though physical distance separates the two regions today, centuries of interaction in the domains of trade, cultural exchange, religious conflict and political settlements have sustained a civilisational bridge between them.

![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%201024%20767'%3E%3C/svg%3E)

Figure no. 1 – The black line depicts Uttarapath, and the red line depicts Dakshinapath. Source, (Uttarapath – History Cafe, n.d.).

Long before the emergence of modern nation-states, the peoples of India and Central Asia were linked by a vast network of commercial, religious, and educational ties, facilitated primarily by the ancient Northern Road or the “Uttarapath” (see figure no. 1) – a vast network of caravan routes that linked the Gangetic plains of India (from Bengal) and Central Asia (to Bactria/Balkh) [(Centre, n.d.)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?CqUNJW).

Today, there is interest in reviving the International North-South Transport Corridor and the Chabahar port, which seek to revive some of the historic links that once worked well. However, the memory of old interactions survives through language, music, cinema, folklore and historical accounts [(Reconnecting India and Central Asia, 2010](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ud6Yr2)). Recognising its deep historical ties, India has formulated a multi-faceted Connect Central Asia policy that considers education, culture, people-to-people contacts and civilisational ties as the pillars of engagement (Sen, 2017).

On the other hand, the communication and interaction between China and Central Asia is also a historically continuous process. Archaeological evidence points to much earlier connections. Cultural and demographic exchanges existed before the official opening of the “Silk Road”. China’s contacts with Central Asia were a two-way process of political relations, economic exchange, cultural influence, and population movements [(Zhang, 2023)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?28gXvY). But the historical ties are the base for the modern-day “Belt and Road Initiative” of China, the ambitious project of Beijing to connect Europe and Africa with the Asian markets.

## Belt And Road Initiative Of China In Central Asia

The Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) of China in Central Asia presents a complex landscape of economic opportunities, intertwined with significant structural and geopolitical risks. Although Central Asia has served as the geostrategic hub of the entire project, there is still scepticism about this initiative due to China’s hidden motives and geopolitical risks [(Jash et al., 2024)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?FDnEJj). In this chapter, there will be an analytical and data-oriented assessment of the BRI projects in the five Central Asian countries: Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, and Turkmenistan. The major focus will be on economic intentions, geopolitical dynamics, and local socio-political realities (Zhang, 2023).

The region is landlocked and historically reliant on Soviet-era logistics. But through this project, China has invested tens of billions of dollars in this region to develop its infrastructure. Flagship projects such as the Khorgos Dry Port, located near the China-Kazakhstan border, the Shalkar-Beyneu railway, and the upcoming China-Kyrgyzstan-Uzbekistan (CKU) railway are fundamentally transforming the logistics systems across the entire region [(Aminjonov et al., 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?iTUAFq). Nowadays, on the verge of the Russia-Ukraine war and subsequent Western sanctions on Moscow, the demand for alternative transit routes, and therefore the Trans-Caspian International Transport Route, or the “Middle Corridor”, which is heavily supported by the BRI logistics, is noticing heavy usage for transportation, effectively turning Central Asia into an important connecting point for Asian manufacturing to European markets (Morris, 2024).

Central Asia is not only strategically important to China’s national security but also critical to its energy security. A 1,800 km-long, 3,300 mm-diameter buried pipeline, called the Central Asia–China Gas Pipeline, has been constructed and operated on the Chinese mainland to receive and pipeline gas imports from Turkmenistan and Kazakhstan. The pipeline does not use the Malacca Strait or other sea transportation to acquire China’s energy supplies [(Jash et al., 2024)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?iehlnX). Besides trade and energy security, the BRI project has acted as a catalyst for the geopolitical shift in Eurasia, challenging the traditional hegemony of Russia and also competing with other major actors like the USA, the European Union (EU), and other regional actors like India, Turkey, Iran, etc [(Morris, 2024)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?jzmevI).

Actors such as India, Japan, South Korea, and Turkey are actively engaged with Central Asia, but none can individually match the financial weight of the BRI. They are all crucial alternatives for bilateral trade, diplomatic ties, and economic vectors to prevent over-reliance on China (Taliga, 2021).

There are a total of 261 Chinese BRI and bilateral projects in Central Asia. Total recorded investment across all sectors amounts to roughly $136.2 billion USD [(Aminjonov et al., 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?A2ABLX).

These were publicly reported as BRI projects, financed by designated BRI institutions, such as the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB), China Development Bank, Export-Import Bank of China, or the New Silk Road Fund.

Despite investing billions of dollars around the world in railways, ports and power plants as part of its Belt and Road Initiative, China’s biggest foreign foray faces criticism of being a heavily lopsided deal, with host countries bearing most of the risk. The most frequent criticism of the BRI has been the spectre of a “debt trap” [(Council on Foreign Relations, 2023)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?YHMNl2). The economic partnership reinforces the core-periphery relationship. Central Asian countries mostly export raw materials, such as hydrocarbons, uranium, and agricultural goods, to China. However, they buy high-value manufactured goods and electronics from China. This dynamic could keep these countries stuck at the bottom of the global supply chain, making it harder for them to industrialise [(Jash et al., 2024)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?zJlK4a).

While Central Asian governments warmly welcome Chinese investments, public sentiment is often much more complex. Anti-China protests have periodically erupted in Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan over the past decade. Chinese investment is very attractive to local elites who see political gain in hosting Chinese projects. Central Asian governments are caught in the middle: eager to accept Chinese BRI investment, they are increasingly impatient and unhappy with the status quo (Karibayeva, 2020).

## Connect Central Asia Policy Of India

India’s “Look North” policy, which was more passive, has been replaced by the “Connect Central Asia” (CCA) policy, launched in 2012. The new policy also reflects a unique set of geopolitical, economic and security considerations that guide India’s approach towards the region and its attempt to establish itself as a credible counter-weight to other players in the neighbourhood and as a stakeholder in the long-term national interest [(Aryal, 2024)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?uLyvGv). The underlying objective of this stance would be to achieve a Russian-backed strategic alliance to consolidate its position on the geo-strategic, political and energy fronts. Central Asia, with its border to China, Russia and the Islamic world, is an important region for India. Due to the region’s volatility and present instability, India has certain strategic interests in this part of the world because the region is a potential source of religious extremism, terrorism and drug-trafficking threats to India’s security paradigm, especially with regard to its controversial Kashmir Policy and the volatile Afghanistan-Pakistan region [(Roy, 2001)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?O1qGsH).

As one of the world’s emerging economies, India cannot afford to meet its energy needs from the volatile Middle East. The Central Asian region, with its vast hydrocarbon reserves and equally large uranium deposits, is emerging as an alternative energy source for India. Since the challenges ahead demand a non-hegemonic approach, India is compelled to adopt a more constructivist, soft-power approach. History, culture, and spirituality are woven into the Indian way of life and form the foundation of its foreign policy. Buddhism and Sufism, which have shaped the age-old civilisational dialogue between India and the region, are being rediscovered and wisely deployed.

While rejuvenating old links, India is also building the region’s capacity through knowledge sharing in information technology, telecommunications, pharmaceuticals, and education. The efforts should be normative, sustainable and positive [(Yadav & Wahlang, 2021)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?Oyb3qs). While efforts must be directed to containing Chinese encirclement, simultaneous efforts must be made to reach out to democracies in the subcontinent that share space with China and are willing to join hands with India to stem the Chinese tide [(Kumar & Chatterjee, 2021)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?r6IiU0).

The proposed CCA policy from the Indian side certainly has a strategic rationale, but upon closer examination, it reveals several operational and conceptual drawbacks. The tyranny of geography means the two countries do not share a land border. Therefore, any connection has to be maritime. The proposed route from Chabahar in Iran through the International North-South Transport Corridor (INSTC), though declared game-changing, is complex, multimodal and has some structural issues rooted in the limited connectivity due to geopolitics. The US sanctions on Iran and the steeper-than-usual freight charges that Indian shipping lines would have to pay would spoil this agreement. Given that India is a latecomer in this part of the region, it would not be able to wield the same leverage and influence that could have been possible had it been the prime mover. But it gets even worse for India – it is geographically closest to China, which has a huge economic advantage.

In addition, India’s goals are hampered by the state of its infrastructure and a lack of implementation. Current connectivity projects, like the planned TAPI pipeline, face significant financial and safety challenges. Even if India builds a bridge across the geographic gap, the internal pipelines in Central Asia still rely on Soviet-era infrastructure that isn’t sufficient to efficiently send resources south. Even though high-level diplomatic talks have become more common in the last ten years, actual project execution on the ground is still very slow compared to how quickly Russian security umbrellas or Chinese state-owned businesses can get things done. CCAP positions itself as a cultural partner that people appreciate rather than as a major geopolitical power until it can address its geographic challenges and accelerate investment execution.

## Conclusion

Ultimately, India’s Connect Central Asia Policy (CCAP) and China’s Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) reflect fundamentally different approaches to statecraft. While China uses the BRI to aggressively build the region’s “hardware” through massive, state-backed infrastructure investments, India’s CCAP focuses on the “software”—leveraging soft power, capacity building, and historical goodwill to overcome its geographic and financial limitations. India cannot match Beijing dollar-for-dollar, but its strategic value lies elsewhere. India presents the Central Asian “stans” with a balancing act, allowing them to avoid being entirely economically absorbed by China.
