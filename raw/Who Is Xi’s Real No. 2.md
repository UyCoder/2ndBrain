## The Chinese leader isn’t willing to give anybody else power.

May 7, 2026, 3:49 PM

Since the 20th National Congress of the Chinese Communist Party (CCP), there has been no shortage of debate over who is President Xi Jinping’s second-in-command. As the 70-year-old official Cai Qi’s role has grown, many observers have come to identify him as that “No. 2.” A recent article in the *[Economist](https://www.economist.com/china/2026/04/30/cai-qi-may-be-chinas-second-most-powerful-man)* takes precisely this view, arguing that Cai—as a member of the Politburo Standing Committee, first-ranked member of the CCP Secretariat, and director of the CCP General Office—has long accompanied Xi at important events and controls the top leader’s schedule, documents, meetings, information flow, and security arrangements.

Cai is indeed one of the people closest to Xi in Chinese politics today. But proximity to supreme power is not the same as being close to holding it. Cai is of course important, but he is not China’s de facto second-in-command. Such a figure, under a leader as paranoid about sharing power as Xi, doesn’t really exist.
